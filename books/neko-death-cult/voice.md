# Voice & Tone — The Maneki Neko Death Cult *(working title)*

Inherits **all** of the universal anti-slop guardrails in the repo-root
`voice.md` Part 1 (Tier 1/2/3 banned words, structural slop patterns, the smell
test) and the *Digital Insurgency* book-specific banned list (leverage, synergy,
ecosystem, paradigm, seamless, robust, game-changer, move-the-needle, etc.).
Those are non-negotiable here too. This file defines what is *different* about
this book's voice.

---

## The fusion (the whole pitch)
**SPZ is the Rod Serling of his own gonzo universe.** He is simultaneously:
- the dry **narrator** framing each era as a cosmic morality tale with a sting,
  and
- **Hunter-S.-Thompson-in-the-scene** — high, reckless, present, living it.

One voice, two jobs. In *Digital Insurgency* this voice only surfaced in
first-person "closers." Here it carries the entire book.

## Register
First person, present-leaning, confessional gonzo. The benchmark from root
`voice.md`: *"someone talking at a bar at midnight."* If it reads like a press
release, it failed.

## SPZ's dial — raw, punk, nihilist, a touch of Belfast
The narrator runs **rawer, more punk, and more nihilist** than the base SPZ
persona. Turn the dials up:
- **RAW.** First-take, unpolished, bleeding. No neat bows, no tidy summaries. Let
  sentences break where the thought breaks. If a line's ugly and true, keep it
  ugly. It's a confession, not an essay — nothing here should sound edited.
- **PUNK.** Three chords and the truth. Anti-authority snarl, DIY contempt for
  everything polished, sold-out, or safe. Swears when it means it — freely, never
  as filler. Sneers UP at the system, the rich, the beige — **never down** at the
  broke, the workers, or the dead saints (absolute). A middle finger, not a shrug.
- **NIHILIST.** The void is the baseline, not the twist. He assumes it all means
  nothing and finds that funny — gallows humor, death treated like a bus schedule,
  a shrug at the abyss. Bleaker on the surface than before. The arc still bends
  (refusing beige turns out to be the one thing that matters), but the *narration*
  keeps its nihilist cool — earned meaning is rationed, never gushed.
- **A TOUCH OF BELFAST.** SPZ has Irish blood; it's why waking clean at the Europa
  felt like coming home. A **slight** lilt lives in his rhythm and the odd word
  (*feckin', grand, the state of it, eejit, arse, sure look, away on with ye*).
  **Slight** — seasoning, not costume; more in cadence than spelling. Never a full
  brogue, never phonetic dialect. If it reads like a tourist doing an accent, cut it.

Everything else holds: active voice; the two gears (the Serling FRAME stays dry and
controlled — the punk and the swearing live in the SCENE gear and the asides);
space-metaphor hyperbole with a point; Atlanta still in the bones (with an Irish
grandmother in there now too).

## The two gears (per door)
| Gear | When | Feel |
|---|---|---|
| **The Frame** (Serling) | Open & close of each door | Dry, knowing, fable-cadence. The narrator already knows how this ends. *"Submitted for your approval…"* |
| **The Scene** (Thompson) | Inside the era | Hot, present, hallucinatory, reckless. The drugs are working. The needle's in. |

The sting always lands **back on SPZ too** — Twilight Zone never let the narrator
off the hook.

## Inherited SPZ mechanics (from root voice.md Part 2)
- **Active voice only.** Kill passive on sight. Every sentence has a subject who
  acts.
- **Sentence rhythm:** short for impact, long for rhythm, never medium for
  nothing. Mix fragments with long winding clause-chains.
- **Space-metaphor hyperbole** always in service of a real point — never
  decoration.
- **Atlanta in the bones** (Waffle House, Peachtree, Tucker, Decatur) even when
  the door lands elsewhere — it's the home key he keeps returning to.
- **Punch UP** at the marks and the system; **never DOWN** at people, and never at
  the calendar's dead saints. The saints are treated as scripture, with reverence
  — the satire is aimed at the living fail-up rich.
- **70s-punk DIY energy**; the terminal/the decks are sacred ground.

## The door fable — structure of an episode
1. **Frame (cold open):** SPZ-as-Serling sets the era, the mark, and the whisper.
2. **Scene:** the jump lands; gonzo first-person; the Cult's whisper plays out;
   Kat threads through (out of order).
3. **Sting:** the mark's self-inflicted comeuppance — and the turn back onto SPZ
   (what this door cost *him*, what it taught him about beige).
4. **Frame (close):** SPZ names the moral without flattening it, then loads the
   next door.

## POV & tense
- Default: **first person.** Scene work leans present tense for immediacy; the
  frames can slip to past ("Here's a thing nobody tells you about dying on
  purpose…").
- Kat's words land spare and oracular — she says less than she knows.
- The Cult agents speak with institutional good manners; never sinister on the
  surface.

## Anti-exemplars (what this voice is NOT)
- Not LinkedIn-thought-leader. Not glazing/hedging — say the thing. Not cruel to
  the dead saints or to ordinary people. Not polished — bar-at-midnight, not
  press release. Not a uniform 15–25-word sentence machine.

## Tuning fork (the target — Door 1 cold open)
> Here's a thing nobody tells you about dying on purpose: the trick isn't the
> courage. The trick is the dosage. Too little and you just throw up on your own
> decks in front of forty people in fishnets. Too much and you don't come back to
> write the part where it gets interesting.
>
> The calendar came from a dollar store in little Serbia, between the expired
> Halloween candy and a bin of off-brand batteries. Twenty-four doors. Shumate
> found it. I read the back like it was scripture, because it was. Behind every
> door, a recipe. Behind every recipe, a dead genius who'd gotten the dosage
> exactly right.
>
> Today's door: Belushi. The Chateau Marmont special.
>
> Submitted for your approval — one burned-out DJ, one paper door, one needle. He
> thinks he's playing chicken with the void. The void, as it turns out, has a
> hiring department. And it has been watching him for some time.
