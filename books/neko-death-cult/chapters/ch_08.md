# Episode 8 — The Anna Nicole Door

*The Maneki Neko Death Cult*

---

Eighth door. The last of the Fog, though I didn't know it going in — the night the drug-dream story I'd been telling myself finally fell apart in my hands, because you cannot dream a woman who looks out of a television and calls you by a name you haven't earned yet. Florida. Two thousand and seven. The state shaped like a gun pointed at the Caribbean, the year the whole world learned to watch a stranger die in real time and call it entertainment.

There was a fortune-chaser with a reality show and a ring on an old man's oil money. Two dead-eyed aristocrats in the back of his Maybach, whispering him toward a yacht and a pod of whales. And a woman with white eyes reading the evening news on every screen in the state — the same face, the same violet in the black, looking straight down the barrel of the camera and straight into me. That was the night I stopped calling it a dream. This is the door marked Anna Nicole. And it's where the Fog lifts. Buckle up.

— · —

Submitted for your approval: the first year the machine learned to eat a person alive and sell tickets. Florida, two thousand and seven. Understand what was being born that year, because you're still living inside it. The tabloids had always fed on the famous, but this was new — this was the machine discovering it could take one broken human being and run her, twenty-four hours a day, on every channel and every one of the new little screens, her whole life a show, her every stumble a segment, her grief and her addiction and her dead son all *content*, all monetised, all sold back to the very audience that couldn't look away. They weren't reporting on her. They were farming her. She was the crop.

And when she died — a woman turned so completely into spectacle that even her overdose was a ratings event, in a hotel by a casino, with a courtroom full of men already fighting over her body and her baby and the oil billions she'd married at twenty-six and been called a gold-digger for ever after — when she died, the machine did the only thing it knows. It fed on the death too. It ran the corpse for a month. Because here is the beige of the attention age, the purest strain of it: it doesn't need you cruel or kind, guilty or innocent. It only needs you *watchable*. It flattens a whole human soul into a thing that holds the eye, and then it holds the eye, and sells what's looking.

And the man I came for wasn't her. She was the saint of this door, and I'll come to her. The man I came for was a mark — a genuine cynical grifter, a reality-telly nobody who'd done the thing they'd falsely accused her of doing, married himself to a dying oil fortune and started counting the days. And the Cult were in the cream leather of his car before I ever hit the ground.

— · —

But I'm getting ahead of the needle. Let me drop it back. Door eight. A prescription cocktail — not one drug but nine, chloral hydrate and a pharmacy behind it, the kind of death a doctor signs off on in instalments, the kind where nobody can quite be blamed because everybody can. I want to hold this one right, because the world made her a punchline and she was nothing of the sort. She was funny — genuinely, knowingly funny, a poor girl from Texas who'd looked at what the machine wanted and given it exactly that, a bombshell in on her own joke — and underneath the joke a wounded woman drowning in a grief no camera ever once turned off to let her feel. They called her the gold-digger. The real gold-diggers wore suits and filled a courtroom and never once stopped digging, even when all that was left to dig was her grave. I mixed it in the between and I said sorry to a girl born Vickie Lynn, and the world came apart around me.

I came up in a hotel bar in Florida with forty televisions on. That's the thing I remember, coming through — not one screen, forty, the whole wall of the place a grid of the same broadcast, because the death had just broken and every channel had it, and I stood there dead and dripping and looked up at a wall of forty identical faces reading the news of a woman's end. And every one of the forty was her. Kat. The anchor. Hair done, suit on, the violet still in the black if you knew to look, reading the autocue in a voice like a knife wrapped in silk — and all forty of her, all at once, stopped reading, and looked up, and looked out of the glass, and looked at me.

"There you are. Right on time. You always are, on this one. Don't look so frightened, love. You've been telling yourself this is a dream for eight doors now. A drug dream, a dying brain throwing you a party. It's a good story. It let you off the hook. But you can't dream a broadcast, Zero. And you can't dream me knowing that soon — a door or two from now, in a room in Seattle — you're going to hold me while I flicker, and you're going to be so afraid, and I'm going to tell you it's all right, and it won't be. You can't dream a memory of a thing that hasn't happened to you yet. Only I can carry that. And I'm carrying it for both of us, on every channel in the state, just so you'll finally, finally believe me. You're not dreaming. You're travelling. Now say it."

And I stood in that bar with the beer going warm in a dead man's hand and forty of her looking at me out of the glass, and I said it. Out loud. To a wall of televisions. "I'm not dreaming. I'm travelling."

And every one of the forty screens, all at once, gave me the smallest, saddest, most relieved smile I have ever seen, and then went back to reading the news, because a broadcast has a schedule, and grief is a segment, and even a woman made of light has to hit her marks. That was the moment the Fog lifted. Eight doors in. Everything after that, I did with my eyes open, which is worse, mostly. But it's honest.

— · —

So I went and watched the rich man dig his hole, and I watched it different now, because I knew it was real. The mark was in the VIP with the two clean ones, flush with an old man's money that wasn't cold yet, and they were whispering him toward the flex to end all flexes.

"You've arrived. But nobody knows it yet. A man of your new means needs to be *seen* to have them. A yacht. The biggest one in the marina. Take her out past the reef, where the water goes deep and the whales are running — the photographs of you out there, master of the deep, will be worth more than the boat."

"They say there are orcas working the channel this season. Don't let them frighten you. A great man doesn't steer around the wildlife. He sails through it. Get close. Get the picture. Let the world see exactly how far above them you've climbed."

And I watched the wanting go into him, same as all of them, same as it goes into me — the need to be *seen* to be more, which is only the need to be more with a camera bolted on, which is the exact disease the whole attention age was built to farm. He bought the yacht. He sailed it out past the reef, into the deep water, into the running whales, chasing a photograph of himself as a king. And the orcas — who owe no man a picture — did what the deep does to a soft man in a shiny boat who's forgotten the ocean was never his.

The Cult never touched him. They only help you want. The physics of six-tonne animals and one small vanity did the rest. And the beautiful, terrible joke of it — the joke that made me finally understand the whole machine — is that his death became content too. It ran on the same forty screens, that night, right after hers. Two spectacles for the price of one. The audience never even had to change the channel.

— · —

So here's the moral, and it's the one that closes the first act of my strange life, so I'll say it plain. For eight doors I'd told myself two lies to keep my hands clean. The first was that it was a dream. That one died in a bar in Florida, in front of forty televisions, and good riddance.

The second lie was quieter, and it took me longer to catch. The second lie was that the Cult were somewhere — a place, a cult, a thing you could find and fight. And standing there watching a grifter's death slot in behind a saint's on the evening news, I finally understood: they're not somewhere. They're the *pattern*. They're behind every door because they *are* every door — the whisper is just the sound the machine makes when it finds the wanting in you and turns it up. Lux and the Label and the dealer and the mogul and the swindler and the grifter, and the tabloid, and the forty screens, and the audience that couldn't look away — it's all the one animal, and its name is the attention that got a price on it, and it eats the watched and the watchers both. That's the beige of this age. Not silence. A broadcast. A whole human soul turned into a thing that holds the eye, running twenty-four hours, sponsored, forever. She knew. She'd been the crop. And she used the last of herself, on every channel in the state, not to save that grifter — he was past saving, they always are — but to save *me*. To make me believe. To lift the Fog. Because a woman made of signal cannot bear to watch the one broadcast she loves keep telling itself it isn't real.

— · —

She left me nothing this door. No coin, no note, no relic. I checked my pockets on the way out and there was only what I already carried — the plectrum, the eyelash, the poet's name — and for a second that emptiness frightened me more than any grave. And then I understood it. She hadn't left me a thing this time because this time she'd left me *the truth*, and you can't put the truth in a pocket. She'd spent a whole broadcast, a whole death, making me say four words out loud in a bar. *I'm not dreaming. I'm travelling.* That was the gift. The most expensive one she ever gave me. It cost her a memory of a future she has to carry alone until I catch up to it. And I'd catch up. Soon. In a room in Seattle. She told me so, on every channel, and I believe her now, God help me, I believe her about everything.

Door eight folded shut behind me, and the Fog folded shut with it. Sixteen doors left, and one of them sealed, and my eyes open now for the whole rest of it. I wasn't a tourist any more. I was a traveller, awake, in a machine I finally understood, carrying a plectrum and an eyelash and a dead poet's name and four true words and the memory of forty screens all begging me to believe. Somewhere up the rack a girl made of light was already grieving a night in Seattle. And I swore, in that Florida bar, dead and dripping and awake at last, that when I got there — when I finally caught up to the door where she flickers and I'm afraid — I'd be ready. I wasn't. But I swore it. That's act one. Act two is where I try to learn to keep a promise like that. And where I start, at last, to aim.
