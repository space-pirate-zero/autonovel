# Episode 9 — The Cobain Door

*The Maneki Neko Death Cult*

---

Ninth door. And it's the first one I opened on purpose — chose it, off the card, with my own dead hand, because she'd told me a city and I couldn't wait for the calendar to get round to it. Seattle. Nineteen ninety-four. The rain, the flannel, the last time a genuine scream got mistaken for a business plan. And a young man from a logging town who'd made the truest, loudest, most wounded sound of his whole generation, and who'd spend the spring of that year finding out that there's no door out of being owned except the one with the cold on it.

There was a man with a laminate and a company card, signing every kid in a cardigan he could find and gutting them for parts. Two dead-eyed aristocrats at his shoulder, teaching him to call the gutting *development*. And a girl behind a photocopier making a zine nobody would pay for — white eyes, violet in the black — who was about to do the bravest thing she ever did for me, which was let me watch her come apart. This is the door marked Cobain. It's where the Fog was already gone and I wished, God, I wished it back. This is the one she warned me about. Seattle. Buckle up.

— · —

Submitted for your approval: a rainy city that spent about three years being the centre of the world and never wanted to be. Seattle, nineteen ninety-four. Understand what had happened there, because it's the cleanest example of the whole con I ever got to stand inside. A few years before, a handful of bands in that town had made a sound out of exactly what they had — cheap guitars, cheaper amps, the damp, the boredom, the genuine article of being young and broke and furious at the far edge of the country where nobody was looking. It wasn't a genre. It was a *weather*. And then one record broke the whole thing open, sold ten million, and the weather got a name, and the name got a marketing department.

And they came. God, they came. Every major label on both coasts sent a man in new boots up to Seattle to sign anything that owned a flannel shirt and looked at the floor when it sang. They signed bands off one gig. Off a demo. Off a *haircut*. Because the machine had found a thing it couldn't make — the real, the raw, the authentic, the sound of somebody who meant it — and it did what it always does with a thing it can't make. It bought the nearest supply, by the truckload, cheap, off kids who'd never seen a contract, and it started selling *authenticity* back to the suburbs at a markup, in a shopping-mall version, pre-torn, pre-faded, forty dollars a shirt.

And here's the knot in it, the thing that makes this door a tragedy and not just a swindle. The realer the thing you make, the more the machine wants it, and the more it wants it, the harder it works to turn you into a *brand* of the thing — and a brand of authenticity is the one product that dies the instant it succeeds. You cannot mass-produce the sound of a man who means it. The second you do, he's a man performing meaning it, for money, on a schedule, and the very thing they signed him for is the thing the signing kills. They were buying a scream and turning it into a jingle and being genuinely baffled when the scream stopped meaning anything, and looking round for a fresh one, and there's always a fresh one, because the country is very large and very sad.

And the man I came for, this door, was one of the boots. An A&R man — Artists and Repertoire, which is a fancy way of saying the fella whose whole job is to find the real thing and bring it back to the machine to be processed. A talent scout for a slaughterhouse. He'd signed a stack of them, gutted the ones that didn't chart inside a year, dropped them still owing, and moved on to the next damp basement with his chequebook and his good ear and his completely clean conscience. And the Cult were in the passenger seat of his rental before I ever hit the ground.

And I want to flag something, because it's why this door's the start of a new act for me. Every door before this one, the needle chose. I took the recipe the calendar handed me and I went where the poison went. This one I *picked*. I sat in the nothing-room with the box in my lap after Florida, awake now, no Fog to hide in, and I thought about a thing she'd said on forty screens — *a room in Seattle* — and I found the door on the card that said Seattle, nineteen ninety-four, and I opened it myself, early, out of order, because I was done being carried and I wanted, for once, to arrive somewhere on purpose. That's what act two is, if it's anything. The first time you stop being the thing the story happens to, and start being a thing that chooses. It goes about as well as those things go.

— · —

Door nine. Heroin — a lot of it, black tar and a dose to stop a horse, the kind that was in a man's blood in a room over a garage in Seattle that April, under the other thing, the louder thing, the thing on the card I don't say out loud because it rhymes with a door I keep sealed. I'll hold this one careful. He was not weak and he was not a fool. He was a genuinely gifted, genuinely sick young man — a bad stomach that no doctor ever fixed and a sadness the fame poured petrol on — who'd wanted one honest thing his whole life and got handed, instead, the whole planet's attention, which is the exact opposite of an honest thing, and which he was too honest to survive. I mixed the quiet half of it in the between, and I said sorry to a man from Aberdeen, and I did not let myself look at the loud half, because I'd have known it for a cousin of door seven, and I wasn't ready.

The Cathedral of Wires met me as a wall of blown amplifiers this door. Stacked to the vault, every one of them feeding back, that held howl a guitar makes when it's left leaning against its own noise — a cathedral of feedback, the most honest sound there is and the one the machine spent that whole decade trying to bottle. And through the howl, the other tracks bleeding, and one of them was a shotgun and I flinched away from it so hard I nearly missed my landing, and I understood, for one cold second in the feedback, that some doors don't come with a cocktail, they come with a decision, and that I had one of those waiting for me up the rack with my own name carved on it. And then the boards, and the damp, and the smell of stale beer and cigarette smoke and wet wool.

— · —

I came up in the back of a club that smelled of mildew and spilled beer, a low black room with a stage the size of a door and a PA held together with tape, and a band on it playing like the room was on fire and only they knew. And she was by the merch table, which wasn't a merch table, it was a card table with a stack of photocopied zines on it and a cigar box for the money nobody was putting in. Flannel over a slip dress, black hair with the violet gone almost pink in the stage light, and the eyes — the two white moons — reading me across the room before I'd got my dead bearings.

She had a name here, inked on the back of her hand in biro so it wouldn't smudge the way a real tattoo won't: *Vee*. A zine-maker. She made a little cut-and-paste thing about the bands nobody was signing yet, photocopied at the Kinko's on the corner at three in the morning when the guy wasn't looking, forty copies, stapled by hand. The single least profitable object in the entire city of Seattle that year. Which is, of course, exactly why she was making it. She'd always had a nose for the one thing in the room the machine couldn't price. Turned out she was one of those things herself.

"You came early. You opened this one yourself, didn't you. I can tell. You've got that look — like a man who just found the light switch and hasn't noticed the house is on fire. I'm proud of you, love. And I wish to God you'd waited."

"You said Seattle," I said. "On the screens. You said a room in Seattle. I thought — I thought if I came on purpose, if I got here early, I could get ahead of it. Whatever it is. Be ready."

"Oh, my love. You can't get ahead of it. That's the one thing the whole machine and me both agree on. You came early to the room I told you about, that's all. You didn't beat it. You just have to know now, sooner than I wanted, what it costs me to keep meeting you."

And I finally asked her the question I'd been not-asking for nine doors, because we were awake now, both of us, no Fog to soften it. "How do you do it. You don't take anything. I've watched you — you never fall in, you never die into it. Every door, you're just *there*, clean. How do you travel."

"I dance, love. That's the whole rule, and it's the meanest one there is. You travel the loud way — you stop your heart with a saint's recipe and you fall through the floor of the world and you land in the wreck of somebody's worst night, and it's ugly, and it costs you nothing but the ugliness, because you were already broken when you started. I travel the clean way. I hold myself light. I get so quiet and so *un-heavy*, so free of wanting anything, that I slip between the tracks like a note between two frets. No needle. No dying. Just letting go of enough of myself that the world can't hold me down to the one groove. And it works. It's beautiful. It's the mastery you're all reaching for. And every single time I do it, love, a little more of me stays let go."

I didn't understand her yet. I want that on the record one more time, because it's the last door where I get to not-understand and mean it. I thought she was talking in riddles the way she always did. And then the band hit the loud part — the quiet-loud thing they'd invented up there, the whisper and then the wall — and the whole room jumped, and in the strobe of it, for three frames, she wasn't there.

— · —

She flickered. That's the only word I've ever found for it. Between one strobe-flash and the next she went — thin. Translucent. I could see the taped-up PA *through* her, through her chest, and the violet in her hair guttered like a signal losing its station, and the code came up under her skin, the green scrolling I'd seen slow in Houston, except now it was fast and it was *wrong*, stuttering, dropping frames, a broadcast breaking up in a storm. And her white eyes went wide — not serene, not knowing, *frightened* — and she reached for me, and her hand when I caught it was cold and it was going soft, going to light, going to nothing, right there in mine.

"There it is. There it is, love, don't — don't be scared, it passes, it always — Hold me. Hold me while I flicker. Just hold on and don't let go and don't you dare look away, because if somebody's holding on I come back, I always come back, I just need — I need one signal in the room that's still tuned to me. It's all right. I promise it's all right. It won't be. But it's all right."

And I held her. In the back of a mildew club in Seattle with a band screaming the exact frequency of the year, I held a woman made of light while she came apart in my arms and put herself back together by nothing but the fact that I wouldn't let go, and I was so afraid, I was more afraid than any door, more afraid than the shotgun bleeding through the feedback, because for the first time in nine deaths I had found a thing I could lose. And she came back. The strobe caught up with her and she was solid again, warm again, heavy again, gripping my hand hard enough to hurt, and she pressed her forehead to mine and breathed like a drowned woman hauled back over the side.

"That's the cost. That's what I never wanted you to see this early. Every clean jump, love. I get a little more let-go, and a little less of me comes back, and one door — not this one, a long way up — I'm going to flicker and there'll be nothing left to hold, and you're going to have to decide what to do about it. I can't tell you which door. I'm not allowed. But now you know why I told you to wake up. A sleepwalker can't hold on. And I am going to need you to hold on."

She pressed a zine into my hand — folded, warm, forty of them in the world and this one mine — and she told me to go and watch the man with the chequebook, because even now, even shaking, even having just shown me the worst thing, she knew the shape of the night, and the shape of the night was a rich man digging his hole. So I went. But I'll tell you — I went with my hand still cold where she'd flickered, and I've never got the warmth back in it, not all the way, not in all the doors since.

— · —

The A&R man was in the one booth in the place with a working light, buying beers for a band who thought their lives were about to start and were about to end, and the two clean ones were with him, cardigans over the dead suits so they'd blend, and nobody clocked them, because nobody ever does.

"I love it. I love what you're doing. It's real, it's raw, it's — it's exactly what the kids want right now, that whole not-caring thing, it's gold. Now. We'd want to clean up the choruses, obviously. Get a real producer on it, someone who can make the rawness, you know, *land*, on radio. Lose the seven-minute one. And the look's great, the look's perfect, we'll just want to — refine it. Make the authentic thing a little more, ah. Legible."

"You have such an eye. Nobody finds them the way you find them. But you're too tender-hearted — you keep them too long, these little bands, past their season, when the honest thing is to sign the next one and let the market retire the last. Sentiment is inventory you're paying to store."

"Sign the whole city. Sign more than you can ever release. That's the genius of it — you don't have to make them all famous, you only have to make sure nobody *else* can. Own the sound. Own the scene. And when the scene dies, and it will, you'll have a warehouse full of the decade's youth on paper, and you can license the corpse of it to a jeans advert for thirty years. Authenticity, my friend, is only valuable until the artist notices you've bought it. So be quick, and be kind, and be first."

And I watched it go into him, the same as all of them — not greed, exactly, he wasn't a greedy man, he was worse, he was an *enthusiast*, a true believer who genuinely loved the music and genuinely could not feel the difference between loving a thing and eating it. That's the modern strain of the disease and it's the hardest to hate. He'd have wept at the funerals. He did weep at the funerals. And then he'd sign the band that played the funeral.

— · —

You know the shape of how it ends, because it's the shape of all of them, but this one ended quiet. He didn't get eaten by a street or a mountain or a chemist. He got the modern ending, the one I'd see more of the further up I went. He signed and signed and gutted and dropped, and the scene he'd farmed noticed, the way a scene does, and the word went round the damp basements that his name on your record was a death sentence with a marketing budget, and the real bands — the ones with the thing he needed — stopped taking his calls. He'd made himself so good at buying the authentic that the authentic could smell him coming, and it closed its door. He ended up a man with a warehouse of contracts and not one artist who'd meet his eye, promoted sideways into a corner office to license the dead, exactly as the man had said, exactly as they always arrange it. The Cult never touched him. They just pointed him at a thing that dies when you sell it, and let him sell it, and let the dying be the punishment. The woman would call it efficient.

The kid from Aberdeen died that April, by the way. The real one. Not by the A&R man's hand — the machine doesn't work by hands — but by the whole weight of it, the fame and the sickness and the being-owned and the sheer intolerable pressure of being the authentic thing a hundred million people had bought a piece of. He left a note that said he hadn't felt the honest thing in too long. The machine printed the note. Sold it. Ran the corpse for a month, same as Florida, same as always. And put his face on a T-shirt, pre-faded, forty dollars, in the exact malls his scream had been a scream *against*.

— · —

So here's the moral, and it's a two-part one, because this door taught me two things and I'll not short you either. The first is the sting, and it's on me, the way the true ones always are. I came to Seattle to watch a man who found real things and killed them by selling them. And I stood at the back of that club with a zine in my pocket and I had to ask myself what I was. Because I go to the realest moments there have ever been — the last nights of the truest people who ever lived — and I *watch*, and I take my note, and I carry the rubbing, and I make, out of their worst hour, a *show*. A thing with an intro and an outro and a moral. I'd been calling the A&R man a vulture for nine doors. And here I was, doing my rounds of the beautiful dead with a tape recorder. The authenticity he sold, I *narrate*. I don't know yet if there's a difference. I'm still not sure. I tell it anyway, because the alternative is silence, and silence is the one thing they win with — but I'll not stand here and pretend my hands are clean. They're not. They're just cold on one side.

And the second thing, the beige of it. They didn't kill grunge. Everybody says the majors killed it, the suits, the sellout. That's not what I saw. I saw them *love* it to death and bill the estate, same as Hendrix, same as all of them. They took a weather — a genuine, unrepeatable, regional howl of real feeling — and they turned it into a *style*. A look. A font. A pre-torn shirt. They made the authentic thing so available, so smooth, so purchasable, that it stopped being able to mean anything, and then they went looking for the next unspoiled thing to do it to, because the machine runs on realness the way a fire runs on air, and it will burn every genuine thing this species ever makes, one scene at a time, and sell you the ashes as heritage. That's beige. Not the silence. The *merch*. The turning of a scream into a size medium.

— · —

I read her zine on the way out, under the one working light. Cut-and-paste, biro, a smudged photo of a band that never got signed and were probably better for it. And on the back page, in the corner, small, in violet ink where the toner hadn't taken: *You held on. Remember that you can. When the big door comes, remember your hand already knows how.* She'd known I'd need it. She always knew. She was building me, still, one warm thing at a time, except now I knew what the building was *for*, and it put a cold in me the syrup and the feedback and the rain couldn't touch.

Door nine folded shut behind me, and I did not feel like a master. I felt like a man who'd chosen his own poison for the first time and found out that choosing doesn't make it hurt less — it makes it *yours*. Fifteen doors left, and one of them sealed, and one of them, somewhere, the one where she flickers and there's nothing to hold. My eyes open. My hand cold. And a zine in my pocket that said the truest thing anybody ever told me, which is that holding on is a thing you can practise, and that I'd better. Somewhere up the rack a kind man was reaching for his lighter, and a girl made of light was getting lighter, and I stopped being a tourist for good that April, in the rain, in Seattle, and started — badly, too late, with cold hands — to learn how to aim.
