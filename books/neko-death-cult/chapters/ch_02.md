# Episode 2 — The Joplin Door

*The Maneki Neko Death Cult*

---

The second time you die on purpose, you stop bracing for the white light. You start bracing for the paperwork. Because I didn't get heaven, and I didn't get hell. I got 1967. San Francisco. The summer they put on all the posters, the one with the flowers in the hair.

And I got a rich boy who'd decided to become God. Two of the deadest, poshest killers you ever heard, stood at his elbow, whispering him up a ladder with no top to it. And a girl with no colour in her eyes, painting the walls with light and grieving a man she hadn't met yet. That man was me. This is the door marked Joplin. Somebody is about to get everything they ever wanted, and it is going to end them — and the worst part, the part that kept me up across a dozen lifetimes after, is that I understood exactly why they did it. Buckle up.

— · —

Submitted for your approval: a city that's mistaken itself for a garden. San Francisco, the summer of 1967. Half a million kids pouring in off every bus in America on a rumour of kindness, with a guitar and no return ticket. Flowers, and free food in the park, and strangers who'd give you the coat off their back and a tab under your tongue. It was the realest thing a generation ever felt. And that, in my long and rotten experience, is exactly when the men in the good shoes turn up. Because a real thing, a thing people love, is the one commodity the machine hasn't figured out how to price yet. And the machine cannot abide a thing it can't price.

You want to know what they were incorporating? Let me paint it, because in about eighteen months it's gone and they've put a fence round the grave. The Haight-Ashbury. A few streets in San Francisco where, for one summer, a lot of frightened kids tried to build the thing every religion's promised and every government's throttled: a place where you didn't have to pay to exist. There was a shop called the Free Store where everything was free — you walked in, took what you needed, left what you didn't. There were the Diggers, feeding a couple hundred people a day in the park out of dustbins and sheer stubbornness, on principle, for nothing. Music you could get into for the price of a smile. Light shows that turned a dance hall into the inside of a lava lamp. And a general agreement that the point of a life might not be the accumulating of it. It was naive as a newborn and it was real, and I've been to a lot of years, and I'm telling you the realness is the rarest element there is. Rarer than the drugs. Rarer than the love. And the men in the good shoes can smell it from a decade away.

They weren't there to ban it. That's the mistake everybody makes about that summer — they think it got shut down. It didn't. It got *incorporated*. And the man doing the incorporating, that summer, in a velvet room behind a ballroom, was a soft-handed beautiful boy who'd burned a name to get there. Born Chip Ostrander, of the Newport Ostranders, a trust fund the size of a small county sitting under him like a trampoline. Out here he was just *Lux*. Light. He said it in a way that made you feel cheap for having two whole names.

By the time I landed, mind, the rot was already in the flower. That's what *curdling* means. For every kid handing out free bread there was a tour bus crawling down Haight Street with the windows up and the tourists pointing, like it was a safari. For every acid test there was a fifteen-year-old three days from home with no money and a man in a nicer coat than he could afford telling her he'd look after her. The speed came in behind the acid the way it always does, and the speed doesn't want to hold hands — the speed wants things. And the papers back east were already writing the obituary before the body was cold, because a dream's only news when it's dying. The summer wasn't murdered. It was hospice. And the Cult are very, very good at hospice.

And the Cult was already at his elbow when I fell out of the sky. They usually are. By the time you clock the whisper, it's three sentences deep.

— · —

But I'm getting ahead of the needle. Let me drop it back. I woke at the Europa, in Belfast, the morning after the first door, and the bearded man was gone and the breakfast was cold and the calendar was on the nightstand where it had no business being, one door torn open and twenty-three shut. And I did what any reasonable man does when he's been handed proof the universe is a jukebox and he's got a fistful of tokens. I sat with it for about a day. And then I opened door two.

And I'll be straight about why I opened that second door so quick, because it wasn't courage. It was the clean. That first landing in Belfast — no shakes, no comedown, head scrubbed, a stranger who knew my name and a breakfast going cold — it was the first morning in years I'd woken up feeling like a person instead of a crime scene. I didn't understand it. I thought maybe the next door would hand me that again. It wouldn't. Turns out a clean landing's a thing you earn, not a thing you're given, and the bearded man had handed me one for free the way a dealer hands you the first one for free. I was chasing that feeling. I'd chase it a long, long way.

And aye, I know what you're asking, because I asked it too, sat on that clean Belfast bed with a box of dead geniuses in my lap. Why did the Joplin door drop me in *her* year — nineteen sixty-seven, Monterey just up the coast, the exact summer a scared girl from Port Arthur, Texas got up in front of the world and opened her throat and became Janis? Here's the only navigation the calendar ever gave me, and it took me a dozen doors to trust it: you arrive how you died, and you arrive *where the death belongs*. Take the saint's recipe, you land in the saint's weather. Belushi put me in a bungalow-shaped hole in the world. Janis put me in the summer she caught fire. I don't steer it. I just pick the poison, and the poison knows the way home.

Joplin. Southern Comfort and a little brown saint's worth of the other thing, mixed exactly the way she'd got it right at the very end, in a motel in Los Angeles with the door locked. I want to be clear how I held that one. Some of these doors I opened like a sneer. Not hers. You don't sneer at Janis. You say thank you, and you try to do the sums with steady hands, and you mind that a voice like that only comes along when somebody's been hurt in exactly the right shape.

The warmth came up. The cold came after. And the most-bombed hotel in the world peeled off the walls and went to the Cathedral of Wires — the broken moons, the stained glass made of screens, the sermon in static — and I heard, just for a second this time, the other tracks bleeding through, a million versions of a million nights, and one of them had a girl in it, painting with light. And then hands on my shoulders, and warm boards under me that smelled of patchouli and spilled wine and the particular sweat of people who've been dancing since a Tuesday that might've been last week.

I came up into a wall of colour. A girl was throwing it. Stood behind a projector with two glass clock-dishes pressed together, oil and water and dye swimming between them, and she tilted them with her fingertips and the whole back wall of the ballroom bloomed and ran — great slow continents of red and violet pulling apart and folding back. A liquid light show, they called it. The Avalon Ballroom, nineteen sixty-seven, and the crowd had their hands up in the colour like they could hold it. Maybe they could. The rules were softer that year.

I should tell you about that room, because it's the kind of thing that gets sanded flat in the telling and it deserves better. The Avalon wasn't a venue, it was a nervous system. No stage worth the name, no barrier, no bouncers built like fridges — just a big sprung floor and a band and the light on the walls and five hundred people agreeing, for a few hours, to be the one animal. The posters alone were worth the ticket, hand-drawn things you could barely read, the letters melting into each other like they'd been left in the sun. Nobody was performing *at* you. Everybody was *in* it. And I stood there dead and dripping in my black club clothes, a rivethead ghost forty years early, and for about a minute — before I remembered what I'd come to watch — I felt the thing those kids had come for. The thing the machine can't price. And God help me, I wanted it.

I knew her before she turned round. Course I did. Different costume, same skin. Henna up her arms this time, and a name inked on the inside of one wrist — Indra — that wasn't hers, because she didn't have one of those, not a fixed one. But her eyes were the same. All white, no pupil, two wee moons pointed at me and at something a long way behind me at once. In a room full of people who'd taken something to see the universe, she was the only one actually looking at it.

And here's the world-building they don't put in the brochure, so have it for free: two of us were tuned to that summer. Me, falling in on a dead woman's recipe. And her, who I'd work out much later didn't fall anywhere — she *danced* in, clean, no needle, no dying, just a body that knew how to hold itself light enough to slip. She was the disciplined one. I was the battering ram. We were the same animal going opposite ways, and every single time we met, it was somebody's first and somebody's last.

And that's the physics of the only love story I've got, so hold onto it. Two people who've slid together get *tangled* — tuned to each other so tight they keep colliding across the whole rack of years, no map, no reason, just signal finding signal. Which sounds romantic until you do the sums. Because we were coming at it from opposite ends of the tape. Every reunion, for her, was a goodbye she'd already lived. Every one, for me, was a stranger I was falling for from scratch. And she was paying for her grace, though I couldn't see it yet. The clean slip, the dancing, the holding-yourself-light — it costs. Every time she did it clean, a little more of her went to signal and a little less stayed girl. She was further up the road than me in every way that mattered, including the one where the road runs out. But that's later. That's a lot of doors later. Christ, I'm doing it again — telling you the end to make you believe the middle.

"You're early."

"I'm dead, actually," I said. "Pretty sure. Recently."

"You're early, and you've got it backwards, and that's all right. Everybody does at the start. I keep meeting you at the wrong ends. I said goodbye to you so recent I can still feel it. And here you are, not even knowing my face."

I didn't understand a word of it. I want that on the record. I sat on the sticky boards with half an orange going warm in my hand — a nineteen-year-old in a fringe waistcoat had given it me and told me to look at it till it made sense, which is a genuinely good system, I recommend it — and I watched a beautiful woman grieve a thing that hadn't happened to me yet, and I decided she was just very high. Because that was easier. Easy was the only muscle I had left.

"Do I know you?" I said.

Something went across her face. If you've ever told someone you loved that you didn't recognise them, you know the exact shape of it. I was looking at it from the wrong side of the worst thing I'd ever do to her, which was only this: to keep turning up younger, brand new, at the funeral of everything we hadn't done. She didn't cry. She reached into the wrap of her skirt and took out a square of paper folded down small and hard, and she pressed it into my dead fingers and closed them over it.

"Not yet. You can't read it yet, and if you could it wouldn't take. Put it somewhere you won't lose it. There's going to be a kind man with a clean breakfast. And a long time from now you're going to want to remember that I told you first. Now go and watch the rich boy become God. It's why the needle put you here. You should see how they do it. It'll save your life, eventually. If anything does."

And she went back to her colours. And I didn't chase her, because she'd told me to go and watch, and watching was the one thing I was ever any good at.

I tried to go home first, mind. I want you to know I tried. Stood in that ballroom and reached for it the way you reach for a light switch in a dark room you grew up in — reached for Belfast, for the clean sheets, for the booth at the Vault, for any night I already knew the shape of. And there was no switch. There was no back. I understood it in my body before my head caught up: the door behind me wasn't shut, it was *gone*. You don't rewind the tape. You only ever go on. And going on, that night, meant a rich boy, and a ranch in the gold hills, and a summer with a knife hidden in the flowers.

— · —

You should see how they do it. She was right. You should. Lux had a room behind the ballroom, velvet and candle-smoke, and he held court in it like a man who'd read about courts. And the kids loved him. I want to be fair to the kids — they weren't fools, they were *tired*, they'd hitchhiked across a whole cruel country on a rumour of kindness, and here was a soft, beautiful man telling them the rumour was true and that he, specifically, was where it lived. You'd love him an' all. That's the trick. That's always the trick.

And the two clean ones stood at the edges of the candlelight, too pressed for the room, too still, taking turns to lean into that beautiful ear. I'd heard their pitch in a motel. Now I got the second verse in a ballroom.

"You're being modest, Lux. Modesty is a kind of theft. You have something these children are dying for. Literally dying, some of them, out in the parks. Wouldn't it be *cruel* to keep it small."

"A movement is only a moment that didn't think big enough. You could incorporate this. Make it permanent. A church pays no tax, you know. A church can hold land. You could give them a *home*. All you have to do is let them call you what they already want to call you."

And I watched the importance go into him like dye into water. You could see it bloom. Here was a man who'd come west to lose Chip Ostrander, to be nobody's son and nobody's trust — and they'd found the one thing he wanted more than nothing, which was to be Everything. They didn't argue him into it. They never do. They just turned up the volume on a song already playing in his chest. The exact song that'd been playing in mine the night I knelt down in the booth. Maximum stakes. Maximum seriousness. All importance, no chill. I could've hummed along.

I asked them once — not that night; a few doors on, when I'd stopped being frightened enough to be polite — how long they'd been at it. And the man looked at me with those dead eyes and said they were older than money's own name for itself. That they'd stood at the elbow of the first man who ever dug a granary bigger than his neighbour's and whispered *bigger*. That they'd done the pharaohs and the railroad barons and the men who owned the ships that owned the people, and they'd do the ones who weren't born yet — the ones with the rockets and the bunkers and the little cold empires made out of everybody's attention. Same job every century. Find the man who's forgotten the ground is even down there. Tell him he's right to reach for more. And let the height do the killing. They don't hate the rich — that's the thing nobody believes. They *farm* them. The paw in the window was never waving the luck in. It's counting the house.

And that's the machine, laid bare, if you ever want to see it working: they don't sell you a lie. They find the truest, hungriest thing in you and they agree with it, warmly, until it's heavy enough to sink you. The whisper isn't "you're wrong." The whisper is "you're right, and you should have more."

— · —

They threw the coronation at the end of August, up a ranch in the gold hills, and called it the Luminous Convergence. And Lux stood on a flatbed truck in white with the whole curdling summer at his feet and told four thousand exhausted children that the old world had ended and he was the door to the new one, and that they should sign the papers being passed, very gentle, through the crowd. The papers were the part the posters always leave out.

And here's what I learned about how the Cult collects, watching from the back of that crowd with the dust in my teeth. They never lay a hand on anybody. They'd told me that in the motel and I'd thought it was a brag. It isn't. It's just the physics. You pour importance into a man till he's so heavy with it he tips, and then the world does the rest — because the world cannot abide that much pressure in the one place, and it will always, always send something to even it out.

For Lux, the world sent three things, more or less at once, the way it does when it's serious. It sent the bad batch — acid cut with something mean, so his new dawn came up screaming and a third of his congregation spent the holy night on their knees in the dirt, very afraid of the stars. It sent the follower — a kid named Denny who'd given Lux his guitar, his savings, and the better part of his mind, and who climbed up onto that flatbed at three in the morning to ask his god a simple question about his bus fare home.

"You said we were family. You said the money didn't matter. I just want to go home, man. I just want my fare home."

And Lux gave him an answer. And Denny didn't like it. And Denny had a knife.

Lux lived, by the way. I need to tell you that, because it's the part that's truly the Twilight Zone, the part the woman in the clean suit would call *efficient*. He didn't get the saint's ending, the clean one, the one I'd borrowed off Janis to get there. He got the other one. They seized the ranch. They seized the trust — the trust reached out all the way from Newport to settle the family's exposure, and Chip Ostrander, who had crossed a continent to stop being Chip Ostrander, got dragged home at last by the very money he'd run from, to spend his thirties in a panelled room signing documents while a lawyer explained, gently, that he was nobody. That he'd always been nobody. That the most interesting thing he'd ever do was already behind him, and it had a lien on it.

The man who tried to become Everything got turned, with great precision, into beige.

— · —

So here's the moral, and I'll hand it to you the unflattering way, because by now I'd started to suspect the unflattering way was the only true one. I went to nineteen sixty-seven thinking I was a tourist. Thought I was there to watch a rich kid get what was coming, and feel that clean wee spark of justice you get watching a bully trip on his own laces. And I did feel it. For about an hour.

Then I understood I wasn't watching a stranger. I was watching a mirror with better hair. Lux and me were the same animal. We'd both decided the only honest way to live was to hold your whole life over your head like a stone and dare the world to make you put it down. He wanted to be God. I wanted to be gone. Same disease, pointed at different doors. And the universe has the one move for men like us, and it is patient, and it does not miss.

And the other thing. The thing that's stayed longer. They didn't kill the summer of love. Everybody says they did — the squares, the speed, Altamont round the corner. That's not what I saw. I saw them *incorporate* it. I saw the realest thing a generation ever felt get a logo and a board and a tax status. That's the actual murder weapon. Not hate. Hate would've been a fair fight. What kills the real thing is somebody loving it just enough to put it in a box and sell it back to you at a markup.

The Diggers had a phrase that summer — everything free, do your own thing. And within two years you could buy a poster of it. That's the whole trick in one sentence. They didn't outlaw *do your own thing*. They trademarked it. Put it on a mug. And a thing on a mug has stopped being a threat and started being a revenue line. That softening, that flattening, that turning of the fire into a lifestyle you can subscribe to — that's beige. It's the only weapon that's ever actually won a war, and it wins them all.

Sixty years on they'd be running the same con with the same smiling cat on the label, selling the kids a watered-down, no-stakes, no-needle version of the realest physics in the universe. But I didn't know that yet. That door was a long way up the rack.

— · —

The girl was gone when I went back to the ballroom. They're always gone. That's its own rule, and I'd learn it properly later: signal finds signal, but never on a schedule you get to set. But the paper was still in my pocket, folded down small and hard where she'd closed my fingers over it. I worked it open under the marquee lights with hands that had stopped being dead and started being just tired. Her writing inside. Violet ink, pressed hard.

WHEN THE KIND ONE LIGHTS UP, COUNT THE DOORS.

I read it four times. Meant nothing. A kind one. Lighting up — a joint, a cigarette, a stage. Counting doors — I'd twenty-two of them left on a bit of cardboard, so maybe that. I thought about the bearded man in Belfast, the soft pack, the smoke going up through the clean light, and something turned over in me and then lay back down, because I was tired, and the connection was ten doors away and I was stood at the start of them in the wrong year with an orange rind in my other pocket and no way home.

I didn't understand it. I told you I wouldn't. She'd told me I wouldn't. *You can't read it yet, and if you could it wouldn't take.* I put it somewhere I wouldn't lose it. I have it still. I had a lot of years to find out she was right about every word, and the worst of them was *first*. She told me first. Remember that, when we get there.

The needle lifted. The Avalon went to static. Door Two folded shut behind me, gone, a record I'd never play again. Twenty-two to go. And somewhere up the rack, a kind man was already reaching for his lighter.
