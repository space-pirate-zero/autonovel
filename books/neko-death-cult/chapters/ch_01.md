# Episode 1 — The Belushi Door

*The Maneki Neko Death Cult · Season One: The Fog*

---

Before we start, let me tell you how it ends, because it's the only way you'll
swallow the middle. I've died. Properly died — no pulse, sheet over the face, the
lot — more times than I've fingers to count on. And every single time, I woke back
up. Not in heaven. Not in hell. In another year. Another city. 1967. 1994. A hotel
in Belfast they'd bombed thirty-odd times and never quite killed. Same me. Wrong
decade. And a woman I hadn't even met yet, already stood there saying goodbye.

There's a crowd runs this universe like a bin collection, and somewhere along the
line they'd gone and put my name on a list. This is the night they found me. The
night I learned that dying, for me, was just changing the record.

So buckle up, would you. It's going to get worse. And then — God help the pair of
us — it's going to get a lot stranger.

— · —

Here's the thing nobody tells you about killing yourself on purpose: the guts are
the easy part. They hand those out at the door, free, like flyers for a band that
already broke up. The hard part's the dosage. Too little and you're on your knees
puking on your own decks in front of forty kids in fishnets, and you've to keep the
tune going, because nobody came to watch you feel your feelings. Too much and you
don't come back to tell the good bit.

I'd got very good at the dosage. That's the whole tragedy of me, if you're
collecting tragedies. Took the one gift I ever had and pointed it at the fecking
floor.

This is the night I found out the floor had a basement. And the basement was
hiring.

— · —

Submitted for your approval: one burnt-out DJ, in a dead club, in a dying scene, in
a city too busy paving over everything it used to love to notice either of us
going. Atlanta. 2003. The kind of summer where the air comes at you like a wet dog
and the kudzu eats a parked car a week. I was meant to be spinning industrial down
the Vault — a club the way a shipwreck's a boat. Bare brick. Rusted pipes sweating
over your head like the building had a fever it couldn't kick. And a booth in the
corner, lit that hospital green you only get off old monitors and off the faces of
people who've quit sleeping for good.

I wasn't really DJing. The tracks ran themselves off a playlist I'd built the way
some fellas build a noose — careful, and only the once. My job was to stand there
and be a mood. A knackered wee lighthouse warning ships off a coast nobody sailed
to any more. I was good at it. Last thing I was good at, if we're being honest, and
we might as well be.

You'd have to understand the scene to understand how easy it was to vanish inside
it. Atlanta industrial-goth, the arse-end of it — a couple hundred of us in the whole
city who owned more black than colour and treated a fog machine like a sacrament.
Rivetheads and baby vampires and art-school casualties, dancing to drum machines that
sounded like a factory having a nervous breakdown. We had the two clubs, the one
photocopied zine, and a shared understanding that the mainstream was a slow death you
paid rent on. It was beautiful, mind — the way a condemned building's beautiful. You
could see the light through the holes in it. And it was dying. The rents were coming
for Ponce, the lofts were coming for the artists, and every month another one of us
cut their hair and got a badge on a lanyard and stopped returning calls. I watched
the thing I loved get turned into a car park, one haircut at a time, and I hadn't the
word yet for what was doing it. I've got the word now. It's coming.

And then there was the calendar.

— · —

Shumate found it. Course he did. Shumate could walk into a pound shop on the stretch
of Buford Highway everybody called little Serbia — past the out-of-date Halloween
sweets, past a bin of knock-off batteries that were somehow already dead — and come
out holding the single strangest thing in the building, grinning like he'd robbed a
chapel.

A chocolate advent calendar. Cardboard. Cheap print, a wee winter village on the
front traced off a nicer one. Twenty-four doors. Only, behind the doors, where the
waxy chocolate should've been, somebody'd pressed in blister packs and folded cards.
By hand. Neat as you like. Every card a recipe. Every recipe a name.

I read the back of that thing like scripture, because that's what it was. Behind
every door, a measurement. Behind every measurement, a genius who'd got the dosage
exactly, perfectly, beautifully right. Not the ones who stumbled. The ones who
*aimed*. Walked up to the edge of the world and stepped off with their chins up and
their sums correct.

I read every door that first week, front to back, the way other men read the racing
pages. Behind door two, a lassie who drank Southern Comfort and sang like her ribs
were on fire. Door nine, a man in a cardigan who put a shotgun where the cardigan
should've been. Door eleven, a voice that gave out in a hotel bathroom. Door eighteen
— gonzo, a pistol, a note. Twenty-four saints, and every last one of them had walked
up to the same cliff I was living on and gone over it on purpose, and the calendar
loved them for it. Whoever built the thing — and I've had a lot of years to wonder,
and I still don't know — they weren't grieving. They were keeping score.

And there was the one door I never opened. Door seven. The card behind it hadn't a
cocktail on it at all — just a name, a date, the shape of a shotgun, and a feeling in
my gut that said: not that one. Not ever. That one's a full stop, and there's no
groove on the far side of it. Turned out I was right. But that's a long way up the
road.

"It's a countdown," Shumate says, in the car, windows down because the AC died back
in '99. "Twenty-four days. Open one a day till Christmas."

"Or," I said.

He gave me a long look at the lights on Ponce. That look a mate gives you when he's
deciding whether to pretend he didn't hear the *or*. Light went green. He drove. He
pretended.

That was Shumate. Hand you the loaded thing and look the other way while you made
your mind up. He found the box. He drove me home. That's the whole of what Shumate
is to this story, and it's plenty.

— · —

Today's door was Belushi. You know the recipe even if you don't know you know it.
The Chateau Marmont special, March of '82. A speedball — smack and coke up the one
needle, an upper and a downer having a knife fight with your heart stood in the
middle holding the coats. Mixed by a man who had the whole world. Every door in it,
open. And he bet the lot, in a bungalow, on one more.

And I respected it. I want to be dead honest about how sick I was, because the
sickness is the whole point of where this is going. I looked at a man who had
everything and flung it off a cliff, and I didn't think *what a waste*. I thought:
finally. Somebody who took it seriously.

Because that was my disease, and I'd no name for it yet. Everybody else I knew had
learned to want things a normal amount. A car. A girl. A Tuesday. And I couldn't do
it. I held everything like it was life or death, because to me it *was*, so I'd
decided the one honest move left was to make it actually be. Maximum stakes. Maximum
seriousness. All importance, no chill, both hands round the throat of my own life.

There was a physics under all that, I'd learn — and the physics was cruel about it in
a way I'd have found funny if it hadn't cost me the whole world. The trick of moving,
of slipping the groove clean, is to want it light. To hold your own life like a coat
you'd not mind losing. And I was the exact opposite of that. All grip, all weight, all
importance. I'd made a religion out of taking everything as deadly serious as a
coronary, and it turns out that's the precise thing that pins you to the one track and
won't let you off it. The only reason I ever moved at all is I'd found the one lever a
man like me could pull. I couldn't learn to hold my life light. So I put it down the
hard way. Over and over.

Turns out that's a frequency. Turns out something was listening on it.

— · —

She was on the floor that night. She was always on the floor. Cosmic Swing Kat.
Never meant to be there, and yet she always was. That night she'd come as some thing
she'd have called the Tribal Nomad Signal Dancer if you were daft enough to ask —
henna glowing up both arms, a dozen layers of sari that moved a half-second after
she did, coin belts jingling in a rhythm the music kept lunging at and missing.
Black hair with violet and fuchsia run through it, and I swear to God the hair drank
the strobe and gave it back warmer. And her eyes. All white. No pupil, no iris. Two
wee stage-lights pointed at everything at once — at me, at the door, at some spot
about nine feet behind my own skull.

And here's the bit I couldn't square, stood there in the green. I'd seen her before.
Seen her at a warehouse rave in '98, same skin, same violet run through the black.
Seen her once in a photograph from a club that shut before I was old enough to drink —
and told myself it was a lookalike, a mother, a coincidence, because the alternative
was mad. She turned up wherever the music got desperate enough, in a new costume and
the same face, and nobody else ever clocked that she didn't age, didn't leave, didn't
belong to whatever year you were standing in. She wasn't from the scene. I'd work it
out eventually: she wasn't from any scene, any year, at all. She was tuned to the same
dying frequency I was. Just coming at it from the far end.

And she was watching me. Two white lights dead on me, and her face doing a thing I
hadn't the equipment to read. Looked like grief. Looked like somebody saying
goodbye. Which made no sense, because you don't say goodbye to a stranger.

That night I just thought: pretty girl, looking sad at me. And I went down on my
knees in the booth, in that green light, needle already loaded — a wee glittering
spear with a saint's name on it — gave the crowd one last helping of nothing, and I
aimed it at my own heart. Last thing I saw with the old eyes was Kat stopping. Just
stopping, mid-phrase, coins gone quiet. Stood dead still in a room full of moving.
Watching me leave.

— · —

The cocktail goes in like liquid sun and then the sun sets all at once. There's a
warmth — the first honest warmth I'd felt in months — and hard behind it the cold, a
black frost crawling up the inside of you, and the music slowing and smearing and
coming apart at the seams. The brick peeled back into something huge. The banjaxed
disco ball over the bar didn't fall — it multiplied, a dozen smashed moons turning
on nothing, throwing broke light on walls gone to stained glass, only the glass was
screens, and the screens were running a sermon in a language made of static. A
Cathedral of Wires. It's the place you pass through. Nobody lives there.

I've spent more time in that cathedral since than I have anywhere with an address.
It's what sits between the grooves — the place the needle crosses when it jumps the
track. Every religion that ever put the fear in a child got the one detail right, and
the detail was this room: the wires, the broken light, the sermon you can nearly
follow in a language made of static and old modem noise. It's not heaven. It's not
hell. It's the switchboard. And if you listen — I didn't, not that first time, I was
far too busy dying — you can hear all the other tracks bleeding through the walls.
Every version of every night, playing at once. Loudest quiet there is.

And then a hand on my shoulder. And another.

— · —

I came up into the wrong light. Not the strobe. Not the green. A dull yellow lamp
the colour of a sun that had given up. Room stank of disinfectant and the bottom of
a coffee pot. A motel. And two people sat across from me in chairs that didn't
belong to the room, because the chairs were clean and the room wasn't, and *they*
were clean — clean in a way nothing in my life had ever been. Two black suits. A man
and a woman. Forgettable in a way that takes effort. You'd look straight at them and
your eye would slide off, like they'd been edited in. And nothing moved in either of
their faces. Nothing at all. Like two folk who'd read a manual on being people and
decided most of it was optional.

"Welcome back to the land of the living, Space Pirate Zero," the woman said. Flat.
No music in it anywhere.

My throat felt like I'd been screaming across a car park the size of Texas. "Who," I
said, "the fuck are you two."

"We are representatives of the Maneki Neko Death Cult," the man said, in the voice
of a man reading a serial number.

I laughed, because I was still up past Jupiter somewhere, and because it was the
funniest thing I'd ever heard. "Maneki Neko Death Cult. What, you stick one of them
wee waving cats on the dash and pray for a parking spot at the end of the world?
That how you cosmic weirdos get your kicks?"

"Not quite." The woman didn't smile. She didn't do anything. "We are capitalism's
feedback loop. The correction. The Darwinian function that comes due on the men who
fail upward forever — the greedy, the gilded, the ones who have never once heard the
word *no* and lived."

"Corporate hitmen," I said. "The reaper with a business plan."

"We do not kill. Killing is messy. And inefficient. We whisper. We are the rust in
the pin. The small voice that tells a man who has everything that he ought to have
one more thing, and that the clever way to get it is the stupid way. We do not push
anyone off the cliff, Zero. We stand near the edge and we wait for them to lean."

And the man, in the same dead level: "The yacht that sails a touch too close to
where the orcas are working. The submarine that goes down to look at the wreck. The
mountain climbed in April on March's gear. We never touch them. Physics touches
them. We only help them want."

"So you're the capitalist Darwin," I said. Words felt borrowed. "Pruning the fat
bergs. Trimming the dead wood off the money tree."

"We redistribute," the woman said. "We are the cat with the raised paw — the one in
every restaurant window, waving the luck in. We wave it out. Out of the hands that
have far too much of it and have forgotten what it is for. We are a bin collection.
A blessing wearing a scythe."

I should've been scared. I was mostly fascinated, which is its own brand of thick.
"How long," I said. "How long have you lot been at this."

"Longer than the money has had names for itself," the man said. "We were there when
the first man who had enough decided he wanted more, and we leaned in close, and we
said: go on. Build the bigger barn. Sail the further sea. We are older than the
yacht, Zero. We will outlast the rocket."

"Think of it as pruning." The woman didn't blink. "Every system that grows rich
enough grows a rot in it — men who have failed upward so long they have forgotten the
ground is even down there. They become heavy. And a heavy thing, held up by nothing
but its own belief, does not want pushing. It wants permission. We are the
permission. We are the small round of applause at exactly the wrong moment."

And here's the part that's stayed with me every year since — the sickest thing I ever
heard or the truest, I still couldn't tell you which. They weren't cruel about it. No
relish. No reaper's grin. They talked about culling the richest men on earth the way
you'd talk about emptying a bin that had started to turn — a chore, a tidiness, a
thing that simply wanted doing. That's what the wee cat in the window means, the one
with the raised paw. Everybody thinks it's waving the luck in. It's not. It's
counting you.

Behind them the logos started to flash — a reaper grinning, scythe up; a beckoning
cat, paw raised. Red, then a sick rainbow, then a plaid. A tartan.

"You're full of shite," I told them, "and I'm off my head, and you can both away
back to whatever skip you crawled out of. What do you want."

"Nothing," the woman said.

That landed worse than a threat.

"We want nothing from you, Zero. This is a courtesy. You stumble into timelines and
you hack them like jukeboxes and you never once rewind the tape, and that makes a
particular kind of noise, and we are the sort who listen for that noise. You are not
a recruit. You are not a target. You are a data point. A rogue transmission in a
very quiet band. We only wanted you to know somebody is receiving."

"A data point," I managed, the drugs pulling me under. "I'm a typo in your
spreadsheet."

"A lovely one," the man said, with no love in it at all. "You have a gift for being
in the wrong place at exactly the right time. Your whole life is a downbeat nobody
else can hear. Do not lose it."

And the woman's voice, the last of it, following me down, cold as a filing cabinet:
"Don't let the bastards grind you down to beige."

— · —

Now, here's what took me a dozen deaths to work out, so I'll just hand it to you and
save you the needle. Reality's not a river you float down. It's a jukebox. Every
moment that could ever happen is already pressed onto a record and racked inside the
machine — every year, every version, all of it spinning at once. Most people only
ever hear the one track their whole life. Me, I'd found the trick of punching a new
number. And the way in was death. Every overdose skipped the needle to a different
groove — '67, '94, wherever the drug fancied dropping me — and there's one rule, the
rule that ruins your whole life: you can never rewind the tape. You don't get to go
back. Not to a night. Not to a person. Not to a groove you've already worn through.
Only forward. Only stranger.

And it's not time travel, before you go getting clever. There's no nipping back to
fix things, no standing on a butterfly. Every year that ever was or will be is already
pressed and racked in that machine, all of it turning at once, right now — 1967 isn't
behind me, it's just a different record on the same jukebox. I don't travel through
time. I change the record. The only clock that runs the one way is mine: I get older,
I remember, I never un-know a thing. But the years come up in whatever order the drug
fancies. And which door I take decides where I land — die like Belushi, you wake
somewhere Belushi-shaped; die like the lassie behind door two, you wake in her
weather. Arrive how you died. That's the only bit of navigation I ever got.

The suits called me a rogue transmission, and for once they weren't just being poets.
A person can be a signal, turns out. Burn bright enough and wrong enough, and other
things tuned to your frequency start finding you across the whole dial — no map, no
schedule, just like calling to like. That's how she kept finding me. That's how they
did an' all. Signal finds signal. It's the nearest thing this universe runs to a love
story, and the nearest thing it runs to a horror, and in my experience the two were
mostly the one thing in different shoes.

And the crowd in the black suits — they're not the reaper. They're worse than the
reaper. They're the clean-up crew for a system that eats its own. They lean on the
rich and the reckless till the poor eejits delete themselves, then they sweep up
what's left and pour it back in the ground. Capitalism's own antibodies, wearing
good shoes. And the thing they're really at war with — the thing I'd spend the rest
of my dying learning to fight an' all — it isn't death. It's beige. The slow
grinding-down of everything that ever made a sound into something quiet and safe and
dead.

Beige isn't a colour, by the way. It's a verb. It's what happened to my scene, one
haircut at a time. It's the meeting shifted to make room for a bigger meeting. It's
the band that signs the deal and the deal sands the teeth off them till they sound
like everyone else. It's a whole life spent with your head down and your CV tidy so
that one fine day, safe at last, you can die of nothing in particular in a house the
exact colour of a bandage.

They told me not to let the bastards do it. Turned out to be the only decent
advice I ever got, and it came from a monster in a motel.

— · —

I woke up in heaven, and it put the fear in me worse than hell ever had. Clean
sheets. A big white duvet, sunlight — actual sunlight, sharp and honest. And the
smell wasn't disinfectant. It was bacon. Toast. Coffee somebody'd made because they
wanted it to taste of something.

My hands were steady. That was the first wrong thing. After a speedball your hands
have a lot to say in the morning, and mine were saying nothing. No shake. No sweat.
No black anvil of a comedown sat on my chest. Head scrubbed clean. Like somebody'd
taken the version of me that did all that damage and quietly swapped him for the
floor model.

"Morning, sleepyhead," a man said.

A man at a wee table by the window. Kind eyes. A neat beard going grey in a way that
looked earned. Buttering a scone like it was minor surgery and he was the last
surgeon on earth who still gave a damn.

"Where," I said. "Where am I."

"The Europa Hotel. Belfast." He nodded at the window, the grey-gold city beyond it.
"Most-bombed hotel in the world, this. Funny old place to wake up clean."

He never told me his name. Never would, and I'll spare you the suspense, because I
pulled on that thread for years: I never found out who he was, where he came from,
or who he answered to, and I came to understand the not-knowing was the whole point
of him. He shook a cigarette out of a soft pack and lit it right there at the
breakfast table, in the sun, like it was the most natural thing in the world, and
let the smoke climb. He turned up at the edges of a lot of what happened to me
after. Always that same calm. Always already there. A man waiting, with all the
patience in the world, for a bus he happened to own.

The whole night came back at me, and the last reasonable man left in me stood up and
said: it was a dream. Eat the eggs. Go home. It was a good theory. I held onto it a
long time. Explained nearly everything.

Explained everything except what was in my pocket.

— · —

So here's the moral, and I'll give it to you the unflattering way, because I've
since learned the unflattering way's the only one worth keeping. I went looking for
an ending. And the universe looked at my big, serious, life-or-death,
maximum-importance death wish, my white-knuckle grip on my own throat, and it didn't
give me oblivion. It gave me a meeting. Clean sheets, a stranger, a plate of beans.
Took the one door I was dead sure led out and turned it into a hallway.

That's the joke the whole thing turns on, and it's on me: I was so sure that wanting
it badly enough was the way through. And badly enough was the exact thing in my way.
I just didn't know yet it was a physics. I thought it was a bad night.

— · —

The bearded man tapped ash into the saucer and pushed the plate an inch toward me.
"Eat," he said. "You've a long story ahead of you. And a new chapter, by the look of
things, just started."

I didn't eat. Not yet. I put my hand in the pocket of a jacket I hadn't been wearing
when I knelt down in Atlanta — because I'd died in Atlanta, hadn't I, or near enough
— and my fingers closed round something wee and warm and round. Brought it out into
that honest Belfast light.

A coin. Brass, worn smooth, the kind that hangs in a row of a dozen off a dancer's
belt. A hole punched in the top for the thread, and the thread still in it, snapped
clean, like somebody'd torn one single coin free in a hurry and pressed it into the
pocket of a man on his way out the door. You can't bring a coin out of a dream.

And I sat there in the most-bombed hotel in the world, with my steady hands and a
gone woman's coin warm in my fist, and I understood the one thing, the first stair of
a staircase I'd be climbing the rest of whatever this was now: she'd been saying
goodbye. But she'd handed me a hello.

And somewhere up the groove, in a year I hadn't reached and a self I hadn't become,
she was already waiting on a song I'd apparently played for her a long time from now.
I picked up the fork. Door One was open. Twenty-three to go.

*— end of Episode 1 —*
