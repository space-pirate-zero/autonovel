# Episode 14 — The Sid Vicious Door

*The Maneki Neko Death Cult*

---

Fourteenth door. And this is the one that came for my own throat, because this is the door where the dead boy was one of *mine* — my tribe, my noise, my whole strutting nihilist act — and I had to stand in a room in New York and watch the machine do to punk what it does to everything, and understand that it had already done it to me. New York. Nineteen seventy-nine. A boy of twenty-one, dead of heroin at a party thrown to celebrate his release on bail, a boy who could barely play the instrument he was famous for, who'd been sold to the world as the living face of anarchy and was, in the cold fact of it, the most manufactured product the music business had ever shipped.

There was a man who'd built him — a shopkeeper, an impresario, a genius of a particular filthy kind — who'd worked out you could sell rebellion itself, by the yard, off a rail, and had a slogan for it. Two dead-eyed aristocrats at his shoulder, delighted, because he'd saved them a century of work. And a girl with a camera and white eyes, taking the one photograph in that whole scene that told the truth. This is the door marked Sid. It's the one where I found out my own leather jacket came from the same factory as everything I ever sneered at. Buckle up. Or safety-pin your mouth shut. It's that kind of door.

— · —

Submitted for your approval: the most brilliant swindle the machine ever ran, which was to take the one thing that was supposed to be its enemy and sell it back to the enemy's own kids at a healthy markup. Punk. Understand what it was meant to be, because I believed in it, I still do, some nights, God help me. It was supposed to be the *no*. The scream. A few kids in London and New York with no talent and no future and no permission, deciding that the whole rotten edifice — the record industry, the prog-rock dinosaurs, the polite dead culture — could get stuffed, that anybody could pick up a guitar and mean it, that the rage was the point and the competence was a trap. Three chords and the truth, and if you can't play the third chord, better still. It was the realest, rawest *no* a generation had in it.

And the machine looked at that *no* — at a movement whose entire selling point was that it could not be sold — and it did the most audacious thing I ever watched it do. It didn't fight it. It didn't ban it, or bankrupt it, or wait for it to curdle. It *manufactured* it. From the very start. Because there was a man, this door, a boutique owner off the King's Road, a situationist with a till, who understood before anyone that the appearance of unsellability is the single most sellable thing there is. If the kids want rebellion, sell them rebellion. If they want anarchy, put anarchy on a T-shirt and price it at nine quid. He didn't co-opt the punk band. He *built* one, from scratch, cast it like a film — the pretty one, the angry one, and the one who couldn't play a note but had a face like a switchblade and would do anything for the camera. And that last one was the boy behind this door.

And the slogan — because he had one, they always have one, and this one told you the whole of it if you were listening. *Cash from chaos.* He said it out loud, proud, like a magician explaining the trick mid-performance and knowing you'd applaud anyway. Cash. From chaos. Manufacture the anarchy, bottle the riot, sell the end of the world to the kids who were bored of the world, and bank the difference. He turned a genuine scream into a product line inside eighteen months, and the terrible genius of it, the thing I have never got over, is that it *still felt real to the kids buying it*, because the machine had learned to manufacture not just the product but the *feeling of authenticity* around the product. You could buy the pre-ripped shirt and the safety-pin and the sneer and feel, genuinely feel, that you were sticking it to the man, while the man rang up your purchase and smiled. And the Cult were in the back of the boutique before I ever hit the ground, taking notes, because he'd just handed them the master key to the next fifty years.

And I want you to sit in *how* he did it, because the how is the whole horror. He didn't find a band and sign it. He held auditions for a *movement*. He'd read his Situationists — the French lot, the ones who'd worked out that modern life had become a spectacle, a screen you watch instead of a life you live — and where they'd meant it as a warning, a call to tear the screen down, he read it as a *business plan*. If it's all spectacle anyway, he reasoned, then be the best spectacle. Cast it. So he picked a pretty frontman for the sneer, and he took a sweet, dim, beautiful boy who'd been hanging round the shop and could not play a single note, and he made him the bassist — not despite the fact he couldn't play, *because* of it. Because incompetence read as authenticity. Because a boy who obviously couldn't do it proved that anybody could, proved the whole three-chords-and-the-truth promise, and that proof sold records. He manufactured the *proof of un-manufacturedness*. Read that twice. It's the trick the whole century turns on.

— · —

Door fourteen. Heroin, and I have to hold this one careful, because there's no myth to it, no glamour, nothing but a boy and his mother and a spike. He'd been in prison, out on bail on a charge of killing the girl he loved — stabbed in a hotel room, and whether he did it or the smack did it or somebody else did it, nobody will ever know, because they never let it come to trial. And they threw him a party the night the bail came through, and his own mother, they say, went and scored the gear, the good gear, the strong gear, for her boy, to celebrate — and it was too strong, or he was too clean from prison, and he went out on the floor of an apartment at twenty-one, a manufactured rebel who never got to find out if there was a real person under the product. I mixed it in the between, and I said sorry to a boy named John — because that was his name, before they gave him a viciouser one — and the safety-pins came off the walls of the world.

The Cathedral of Wires met me as a boutique this door — a long shop, racks to the vault, and on every rack the rebellion of a different decade hung up and tagged and sale-priced: the leather jackets and the tie-dye and the flannel and the ripped everything, every genuine *no* a generation ever screamed, laundered and hung and marked down, and mannequins wearing all of it with the dead eyes of the truly incorporated. And I understood, walking the length of it, that this is the machine's masterpiece, its endgame, the thing it had been building toward through every door: not to silence the rebellion, not even to sell the rebellion, but to make the rebellion *indistinguishable from the merch*, so that the kids can't tell, will never be able to tell, whether they're screaming or shopping. And then the boards, and the smell of piss and amphetamine and cheap vinyl and New York in the winter.

— · —

I came up in the back room of a club that was really a toilet with a stage, the kind of place I'd have killed to play in my own life, sticky and black and loud and *mine*, my scene, my noise, forty years early and an ocean over but unmistakably the room I came from. And on the little stage was the manufactured boy himself, alive this door, shirtless and cut and beautiful and utterly lost, playing a bass that — I could hear it, any musician could — wasn't plugged in. Wasn't even plugged in. They ran the real bass from offstage. He just stood there and *was the image*, threw the shapes, bled for the cameras, a boy paid to impersonate a rebellion he was too young and too wrecked to know wasn't real.

And she was down the front, but turned around — facing away from the stage, into the crowd, with an old camera up, because she'd worked out the same thing I was only now understanding: the show wasn't the show. The real photograph was the *faces*, the kids watching, the genuine hunger on them, the thing the machine was harvesting. Her name that door, biro on her knuckles, was Vex. A photographer. White eyes behind the viewfinder, violet in the black under a shredded scarf, and she was documenting the one true thing in a manufactured room, which was the realness of the wanting, even when everything it was pointed at was fake.

"There you are. In your natural habitat, for once. Look at you, you love it, don't pretend you don't. It's your church, this. And that's the whole horror of tonight, love, so brace up: they built a fake one. They took your church and they franchised it, and the kids in here can't tell the difference and neither, if you're honest, can you. That boy on the stage isn't even plugged in. And they're screaming for him like he's the truth. Same as they'll scream for you, someday, if you're not careful. Space Pirate Zero — that's a brand too, love. Ask yourself who's holding the till."

And that landed like a boot in the chest, because she was right, and I'd spent fourteen doors so pleased with myself for seeing the machine that I'd never once turned round and checked whether I was standing on a shop floor. Space Pirate Zero. The leather, the sneer, the nihilism, the whole raw punk-nihilist act I do for you, right now, in your ears — where did I get it? Off a rail. Same as the boy on the stage. The machine sold me my rebellion when I was fifteen and angry in Belfast, sold me the jacket and the records and the pose, and I wore it so hard and so long I mistook it for a self. My *no* came pre-manufactured. My anarchy had a barcode. I am, it turns out, exactly as bought as the boy who wasn't plugged in — I just play the part with more conviction, and conviction, she was telling me, conviction's just the premium model. The machine loves a rebel who really means it. Means it sells better.

I stood in that toilet of a club a long time with that in me, and I'll tell you what it did, because it's the most useful thing this whole rotten pilgrimage ever did to me. It didn't make me stop loving the noise. That's what I'd been afraid of, all fourteen doors — that if I ever really *saw* it, saw that my rebellion was a product, the seeing would kill the loving, and I'd be left with nothing: a man in a costume he can't believe in and can't take off. But it didn't. I looked at that manufactured boy throwing shapes over a bass that wasn't plugged in, and I looked at the kids screaming for him with genuine fire in their faces, and I found I loved the kids *more*. Because the con was real and their wanting was real, both at once, and that's not a tragedy, that's just Tuesday, that's just what it is to be a person in the machine: everything you love came off a rail, and you love it anyway, and the loving is yours even if the object was sold to you. The jacket's bought. The warmth in it is mine.

"There it is. That's the one thing they can't reach. They sold you the jacket, love. They sold all of us the jacket. But they never once worked out how to sell you the *moment you notice it's a jacket* — and keep wearing it on purpose, eyes open, for the warmth and not the pose. That's the only free thing left. Not taking the costume off; you'd only buy another. Wearing it *awake*. Keep your eyes open in the shop. That's the whole of it. It's all I've ever been trying to teach you, backwards, one relic at a time."

— · —

So I went and watched the impresario, and I'll say this for him over all the marks I'd met — he wasn't in denial. He knew exactly what he was. He was in the back with the two clean ones and a cash box, counting, and he was almost admirable in his honesty, the way a shark is admirable if you're not in the water.

"People think I'm the villain. I'm the only honest man in the building. That lot out there — the labels, the radio, the polite pop men — they sell the kids a dream and pretend it's real. Me? I sell 'em chaos and I tell 'em to their faces it's a con, and they love me *more* for it. That's the joke, that's the beautiful joke — you can tell the mark it's a swindle, right out loud, put it on the poster, "cash from chaos," and they'll queue round the block, because what they're buying isn't the truth, it's the *feeling*, and the feeling's realer to them than any fact. The boy? The boy's perfect. Can't play, don't matter, never mattered. He's not a musician, he's a *logo*. And a logo don't need to be plugged in."

"You've done in eighteen months what took us the whole of history. We spent millennia learning to sell people comfort, status, belonging. You've cracked the last market. You sell them *dissent*. You've found a way to monetise the very act of refusing to be monetised."

"And it is, of course, perfectly self-cleaning. When the boy dies — and he will, they always do, it's practically in the contract — his death won't hurt the brand. It will *complete* it. A live rebel is a liability; he might grow up, sell out, embarrass you, learn to play. But a dead one at twenty-one is a fixed asset, a saint, a poster, forever twenty-one and furious and *authentic*, precisely because he never lived long enough to be revealed as the product he was. You're not manufacturing a band. You're manufacturing a martyr. Do try to enjoy it."

And I watched the pleasure of it move through the impresario, the pure joy of a man who's seen the whole machine and decided to be its most gifted operator rather than its victim, and I hated him, and I understood him, and I could not, standing there in my own bought jacket, claim to be one thing he wasn't. He'd just read the contract I'd signed at fifteen and never bothered to. The boy died on schedule. The brand never sold better. Forty years on you can still buy his face, furious and vacant, on a shirt, in a shop, nine quid, and every purchase is a tiny act of rebellion that funds the exact machine it thinks it's fighting. The woman would call it efficient. She'd be underselling it.

— · —

So here's the moral, and it's the one that finally came for me, personally, by name, so I'll not hide behind the third person. For thirteen doors I'd been the guy who *sees* it — the awake one, the punk, the nihilist, the one who couldn't be fooled because he was already too disillusioned to fool. And door fourteen turned me round to the mirror and showed me that being disillusioned is not the same as being free. That my whole knowing, sneering, seen-it-all act — the Space Pirate Zero of it — was itself a product, maybe the machine's favourite product, because a rebel who's given up is a rebel who's stopped fighting and calls it wisdom. The most owned man in the building isn't the true believer down the front. It's the cool one at the back who thinks he's above it. That's me. That's who they sell the deluxe rebellion to. The ones too clever to be sold — those are the easiest sale of all, because they never check their own jacket.

And the beige of it — the deepest cut of the whole rack. They don't just sell you comfort and status any more, the machine. Lenny showed me they'd sell you the pose of the truth-teller. But this boy, this door, showed me the endgame: they'll sell you the *revolution*. The whole apparatus of resistance — the anger, the jacket, the anti-everything, the very stance of standing against the machine — is available now, pre-packaged, in your size, and buying it feels exactly like fighting, which is why nobody fights any more, we just *shop antagonistically*. That's the masterpiece. That's beige at its most total: not the absence of rebellion, but rebellion turned into a *consumer choice*, a flavour, a fit, so that the most furious kid in the room and the machine he's furious at are, at the register, the same transaction.

And it went everywhere, of course it did, the way all of it goes everywhere. Look at it now, with the dead boy's eyes. Every revolution's got a merch table before it's got a manifesto. Every protest movement gets a branded hashtag and a corporate sponsor and a range of tote bags inside a fortnight. The rage of a generation gets A/B tested and turned into an advert for a razor. The machine learned, off a boy in New York who couldn't play the bass, that the safest possible thing it can do with dissent is *become the vendor of it* — because a customer who thinks they're a revolutionary is the most loyal customer there is; they'll defend the purchase like it's their soul. They don't fear your anger any more. They've got a department that manufactures it, packages it, and sells it back to you at the exact temperature that feels like fighting and changes nothing. Cash. From chaos. He wasn't a swindler. He was a prophet. He just got there fifty years early, and only had the one boy to spend.

And I'm part of it. This show's part of it. You're maybe buying a little anti-machine feeling off me right now, and it's real, and it's also merch, and I can't fully untangle the two, and the only honest thing I can do is what I'm doing: tell you it's a rail, and let you check your own jacket, and hope the checking is the one thing they never worked out how to sell.

— · —

Vex found me after, in the alley behind the club, and she pressed something into my hand — a photograph, still wet from whatever she developed it in, and it wasn't the boy on the stage. It was me. Caught from the side, at the back of the crowd, in my bought jacket, watching, with a face on me I've never been able to describe except to say it was the face of a man realising his armour is a uniform. On the back, violet ink: *This is the real one. Not the pose — the moment you saw the pose. Keep it. That look is the only thing on you that didn't come off a rail. It's the most punk thing about you: the second you doubted the jacket.* And I put it in with the plectrum and the eyelash and the poet's name and the played string and the chapel card and the stenographer's shorthand and the calendar with my name on it, and I understood she'd handed me the antidote to the whole door: that the one un-sellable thing, the only genuine rebellion left, is the moment you turn round and doubt your own costume.

Door fourteen folded shut behind me, and I did not feel above anything, for the first time in a long time, and it was the closest thing to free I'd felt since I died. Ten doors left, and one of them sealed, and one of them mine. I took off the sneer for a minute — just a minute, I put it back, a man needs his tools — and I stood in a New York alley in a jacket I finally knew the price of, and I decided that if I couldn't get out of the shop, I could at least keep pointing at the tills. That's the job now. Not to be un-bought — nobody is, that's the lie that sells the most — but to keep naming the price out loud, in a bought jacket, with a doubting face, until somebody young enough to still get out actually checks the label. Somewhere up the rack a kind man was reaching for his lighter, and a girl made of light was photographing the truth in a room full of fakes, and I loved my ruined tribe more, not less, for finding out it was manufactured — because the wanting in those kids' faces was real, even if everything they pointed it at was a con, and the wanting's the thing. The wanting's always been the thing. Keep it pointed somewhere true.
