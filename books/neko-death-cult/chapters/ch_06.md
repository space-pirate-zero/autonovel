# Episode 6 — The Morrison Door

*The Maneki Neko Death Cult*

---

Sixth door. And this is the one that taught me you cannot cheat the Balancing, not by dying, not by faking your death, not by any door there is — because the Cult, it turns out, are patient in a way that makes the grave look impulsive. Paris. Nineteen seventy-one. A poet who'd run three thousand miles to stop being a product died in a bathtub in the Marais at the age of twenty-seven, and they buried him in a city of the beautiful dead, and within a week his grave was already the most-visited advertisement in the whole cemetery.

There was a man in that city who'd faked a fortune and was about to fake a funeral. Two dead-eyed aristocrats at his elbow, telling him the second lie was the way out of the first. And a girl with white eyes and violet in her hair, kneeling at a poet's grave with a sheet of paper and a stick of charcoal, taking a rubbing of a name, waiting for a dead man to fall out of the sky. That man was me. This is the door marked Morrison. Buckle up.

— · —

Submitted for your approval: the most beautiful city on earth, and the con men who go there to disappear. Paris, nineteen seventy-one. Understand why they all ran there — the poets, the painters, the tax cheats, the deserters, the broke aristocrats and the fake ones. Paris was the last place in the West where you could still be *nobody in particular*, magnificently, over a coffee, for hours, and nobody would ask you what you did or what you were worth. It was a city built, block by beautiful block, to let a person be unaccounted for. Which makes it, of course, the exact place a certain kind of man goes to hide from a ledger.

They'd been running there for a hundred years by the time I got there, and always for the same reason under the reasons. The painters said it was the light. The writers said it was the cheap wine and the cheaper rent. The deserters said it was the one city that wouldn't hand them back. But under all of it was the same hunger: to be *un-filed*. To walk into a café where the waiter didn't know your father, your debts, your draft board, or your price, and sit there three hours being nothing but a man with a coffee and an unlit cigarette. Every other city on earth had learned, by then, to keep a ledger on you. Paris was the last one that still politely declined to. And a man being hunted by a ledger will always, always run to the one country that refuses to keep one.

And the man I came for, this door, was one of those. An expat. A charmer. A financier, he'd have called himself, though the word for what he actually did is older and shorter. He'd built a fortune the way they always build them — by taking money off the new believers to pay the old ones, and calling the gap between them genius, and living, in the meantime, extremely well in an apartment in the Marais on a river of other people's trust. A Ponzi, in the flat English of it. A beautiful, doomed pyramid with a charming man asleep at the top of it. And the Cult were pouring his Pernod before I ever hit the ground.

I should say how the fortune actually worked, because it's the same shape as everything else in this show, just drawn in money. You promise the new believer a return no honest thing on earth could pay. He hands you his savings. You don't invest a penny of it — you *can't*, there's nothing that pays what you promised — you simply hand a slice of it straight back to last year's believer and call it his profit, and he's so delighted he tells his brother, his priest, his widowed mother, and they all line up with their envelopes. Round and round, the new paying the old, the pyramid climbing, the charming man skimming his beautiful life off the top of the churn. It is not investment. It's a machine for turning trust into rent, and it has exactly one moving part — the supply of fresh believers — and the supply of fresh believers, in this world, is the only thing more finite than patience.

Because a pyramid like that has only the one ending, and everybody in the world knows it except the man on top, and the ending is: it runs out of new believers, the whole thing folds inward in an afternoon, and the charming man goes to prison for a very long time. And this particular charming man had just worked out that there might be a door out of it. A trick. The oldest trick a cornered man knows.

Now I have to teach you a word, because the whole of this door turns on it, and it's the strangest law in the machine I live inside. The *Balancing*. Here's the shape of it. The universe behind all these doors — it hates an excess. Any excess. Too much love pointed at one thing, too much fear, too much importance, too much *want* — it all bends the world out of true, like a heavy man sat on one end of a rowboat. And the universe, patiently, without any malice at all, always sends a weight to the other end to level it back. Push too hard toward a thing, and forces you can't see assemble, quietly, to push it away from you. Grip a thing too tight, and the gripping itself becomes the reason you lose it. The mystics I read between the bathroom tiles called it a law of balance — surplus potential, they said — and it does not care about your cleverness, and it cannot be argued with, and it never, ever forgets what it's owed. The swindler was about to test it. Everyone tests it. It has never once lost.

And for the ones just tuning in — quick, then I'll let it lie. I'm dead. Atlanta, two thousand and three, too much of the wrong thing, and instead of the dark I got a door, and then another, up a stack of overdoses like a needle climbing a jukebox — always forward, never back, arriving in each new body in the shape it died in. Signal finds signal. Two clocks: the one on the wall, and mine, and mine's always further along. And the thing that arranges it, the beckoning cat in the shop window, the Maneki Neko, never once lifts a hand against you. It just waits by the one law even it has to obey — the Balancing — and lets you overreach your own way into its arms.

— · —

But I'm getting ahead of the needle. Let me drop it back. Door six. A bathtub, and a heart that stopped, and a bottle — the official story says heart failure and the unofficial one says the other thing, and the truth got buried with him because the people who loved him wanted him left in peace and the people who owned him wanted the mystery, because mystery sells. I want to hold this one right. He was a genuine poet who got sold as a sex symbol, a man who wanted to be taken for his words and got taken for his leather trousers, and who ran to Paris to try and be a writer and got about six months of peace before his heart, or the bottle, or the sheer weight of being *looked at*, put him under the water for good. I mixed it in the Europa bathroom and I said sorry to a man named Jim, and the most-bombed hotel in the world came apart around me.

The mixing's a matching, if you're new to me — I make the borrowed body into the shape the next one died in — and this one's shape was murky on purpose. Heart, said the certificate, signed by a doctor no one could ever afterward find. The bottle, said everyone else. So I made myself the right kind of stopped, on the cold Europa tiles, and went under thinking about the trousers. Because that's the joke that killed him, and it's this whole show in a single image: a man writes his soul out in verse, real verse, and the machine looks at all of it and decides the sellable part is the leather trousers. It put him on the posters in the trousers. It sold a million of him in the trousers. And he ran three thousand miles to a city that might read the verse instead — and got six months of grey Paris peace, and then his heart, or the bottle, or the sheer accumulated weight of a decade of being stared at, stopped him in a bath at twenty-seven.

The Cathedral of Wires met me as an arcade this time — one of those long glass-roofed Paris passages, gone to ruin and static, every shopfront a dead grey screen, and behind each pane a poet at a small table, writing and writing, the ink turning to modem-hiss as it touched the paper. Every true line anyone ever wrote, and then priced, and then forgotten, scrolling up into the vaulted dark. I walked the length of it in a heartbeat that lasted a decade, and I came out the far end knowing — the way you know things in the between and can never afterward keep — that the writing was never the thing they were selling. The writing was only ever the bait on the hook they used to land the man.

— · —

I came up in a cemetery. Père Lachaise, in the grey Paris morning, a city of the dead more beautiful than most cities of the living — cobbled lanes and leaning tombs and chestnut trees dropping their leaves on a hundred thousand marble names. And she was there, kneeling at a fresh grave that was already half-buried in flowers and bottles and scrawled devotion, and she had a sheet of thin paper pressed to the headstone and a stick of charcoal in her hand, and she was taking a rubbing of the name, slow, the way you'd trace something you were trying to memorise.

If you've never been up there — it's a proper city, for the dead, on a hill in the east of Paris, with named streets and numbered divisions and a hundred thousand tombs leaning on one another under the chestnuts, the famous and the forgotten crammed in together the way the living never are. Chopin's up there. Oscar Wilde, under a stone kissed to ruin. And there, in a modest plot that a week ago meant nothing to anybody, a fresh mound already vanishing under flowers and bottles and metro tickets scrawled with lines of his own songs. They'd found him in a week. They'd worn a path to him in a week. Nobody organised it. Nobody advertised it. It was the one kind of gathering the machine has never once managed to manufacture — and that, I'd learn, is exactly why it frightens them.

"They come already. Did you see? A week in the ground, and they've worn a path to him. That's the trick the machine can't do and can't stand, love. It can sell a man his whole life and never once touch the thing that makes strangers walk across a city to sit by his stone in the rain. It doesn't understand devotion. It only understands attention — which is devotion with the soul scooped out and a price put where the heart was."

"Do I know you," I said. And she didn't look up from the stone, and she smiled at the name under her charcoal, and I understood — dimly, the way you understand things in the Fog — that she wasn't answering me. She was answering all the versions of me at once, the whole scattered rack of me, and this particular young fool kneeling in a Paris graveyard was only the one she happened to be able to see this morning.

"You know me the way this city knows him. Not yet, and always, and too late, and forever. Here. Take this. It's not for now. It's for a door a long way up, where you'll need to remember that a name outlives the man, and a rubbing outlives the name, and the love outlives the rubbing, and that's the only thing in all of creation the good shoes have never once managed to price. Now go and watch a coward try to cheat a debt that can't be cheated."

"What is it you're building," I asked her — because by the sixth door I'd started to notice the pattern of the small warm things. "You keep handing me pieces. A pick, an eyelash, now a dead man's name. What's it going to be, when it's finished."

"A reason, love. There's a door coming — you feel it already, you cross the room in the box to avoid it — and when you finally open it there's going to be one moment, just the one, where you'll have every excuse in the world to become the very thing that's been chasing us, and no reason at all not to. Except these. A pocketful of proof that people made true things, and were loved for them, and that none of it could ever be bought. I can't be there for that door. I'm going the other way; I'll have already gone. So I'm sending it on ahead of me, one relic at a time, into your coat. I'm packing you a conscience you can carry — for the day I can't hand you one in person."

And she pressed the folded rubbing into my dead hand, and I put it in my pocket next to a plectrum and a false eyelash and a message that said stay off-camera, and I went and watched.

— · —

The swindler was in a café on the Île Saint-Louis with the good coffee and the two clean ones, and he was planning his own funeral. The pyramid had a week left in it, maybe less, and he'd worked out the door: fake the death. A body — there are always bodies, in a city, for a price. A grieving widow, well rehearsed. A closed casket, a quiet burial, a moving obituary, and then a new name and a new face in a country with no extradition, living out his days on the last skimmed millions while the believers he'd ruined wept at an empty box. Death, he'd decided, was the one debt collector you could dodge by pretending you'd already paid.

"You look at me like I'm a thief. I'm an *optimist* — that's the whole of my crime. I promised people a better tomorrow, and I paid them, on time, with a smile, for years. Did their banks? Did their governments? I gave a widow in Lyon eleven per cent and a reason to get out of bed, and yes — all right — the arithmetic was always going to arrive. But arithmetic is only pessimism with a pencil, and I have never once in my life had the time for it. No. I'll not sit in a cell for the crime of being *liked*. There's a cleaner exit, and a clever man takes it."

"It's elegant. I'll grant you that. The dead don't stand trial. The dead don't repay. You simply step out of your own life, like a man stepping out of a coat, and leave the ruin to attend your funeral."

"And why be modest about it? A man clever enough to die on paper is clever enough to do it *twice*. A new fortune, in the new country, off new believers — and if that one folds, well. You've learned the trick now, haven't you. You need never truly answer for anything again. Death is a door, and you've found the key. Think how free you'll be."

And I watched the relief go into him, warm as the coffee — the particular relief of a drowning man who's been handed, he thinks, a rope. Same whisper, dressed for the occasion: not *reach for more*, but *you can escape the bill entirely, you clever thing, you special thing — the rules that hold ordinary men were never really meant for you*. And that, I've come to think, is the deepest flattery the machine has got in the whole of its bag. Not *you deserve more*. *You deserve to be exempt.*

And they helped him plan it, the clean ones, down to the florist — which is how I should have known. Because the Cult never once help you toward a door that leads *out*. They only ever hold open the ones that lead further in. Every step of that beautiful escape — the borrowed body, the rehearsed widow, the closed box, the new name waiting in a country with no extradition and no old friends — every single step walled off one more person who might, on some worse night down the road, have talked him back from the ledge. They weren't building him an exit. They were building him a solitary cell with excellent soft furnishings, and letting him believe the key was in his own pocket.

— · —

So he did it. The body, the widow, the closed box, the moving words. He stood in the back of his own funeral in a false beard, the swindler did, and watched a hundred people he'd ruined weep over an empty coffin, and he felt — I saw it on him — he felt like a god. He'd cheated death and the ledger in a single afternoon. He'd stepped out of his own coat.

And the Cult let him. That's the part that took me years to understand. They *let* him fake it. They helped. Because here is the thing about the Balancing that a cornered man can never quite bring himself to believe: it is not in a hurry, and it cannot be fooled by a costume. The debt he'd built didn't care whether he was legally alive. It only cared that it was owed. And a man who's faked his own death has cut himself off from every friend, every name, every soul who ever knew him and might have helped — he's alone, now, completely, in a strange country under a strange name with a suitcase of guilty money and no one on earth he can call. They didn't need to kill him. They'd helped him wall himself in alive. The pyramid found him in the new country inside the year — a ruined believer with a long memory and nothing left to lose — and there was no widow this time, and no closed box, and no obituary, and no one to grieve, because as far as the whole world knew, he was already dead. The one death nobody mourns is the second one. The woman would call it efficient.

— · —

So here's the moral, and I got it in a graveyard so it's a graveyard kind of moral. You cannot cheat the Balancing. Not with a needle, not with a costume, not with a clever door. I know, because I'd spent five doors trying — I was a man who'd made a whole career out of stepping out of my own coat, out of every consequence, every morning-after, by simply dying and waking somewhere the bill hadn't followed. And watching that swindler stand in a false beard at his own funeral, feeling like a god, I recognised him. He was me. Of course he was. They're always me. He thought he'd found the door out of the debt, and the door out of the debt does not exist, and the only thing you buy by pretending to have paid is a lonelier place to finally pay.

And I'll tell you why it landed on me as hard as it did, standing there in the chestnut leaves. Because I am the world champion of stepping out of my own coat. Think about what I actually *am*. A man who, every single time the bill for a life comes due — the wreckage, the people I've hurt, the morning after — simply overdoses and wakes up in a fresh body in a fresh year where none of it can find him. I don't pay. I've never paid. I've built a whole *cosmology* out of not paying. So the swindler in the false beard, feeling like a god because he'd dodged the ledger — that wasn't a man I was watching. That was a mirror I was standing in front of. And the Balancing — patient, unhurried, uncheatable — was already quietly assembling the weight for the far end of my rowboat too. It always is. That's the part the cornered man can't believe and the dead man only learns slow: the door out of the debt does not exist. There is only the debt, and the choice of a lonelier or a less lonely place to sit down and pay it.

But here's the other half of it, the half she gave me at the stone, and it's the only warm thing in this cold door so hold it close. Two men, two graves, one city. The swindler bought the most elaborate funeral money could counterfeit, and not one living soul stood at it and *meant* it, because the funeral was a lie and the man had spent his whole life taking. The poet got a heart attack in a bathtub and a cheap plot and no ceremony worth the name — and half a century on, strangers who weren't even born when he died still climb a hill in the rain to press their hands to his carved name. That's the whole ledger, in the end. Not money. Not fame. Attention is what the machine sells, and attention evaporates the instant the selling stops. But devotion — the real thing, the walk-across-a-city-in-the-rain thing — is what you get for making something true out of your one wild unrepeatable life, and it is the single asset the good shoes have never found a way to price, or short, or steal. Beige is a funeral nobody comes to. And the cure — the only cure there ever was — is to make something real enough that strangers will cross a city, a century later, just to touch your name.

— · —

I still have the rubbing. The thin paper, the charcoal name. She was right that I'd need it a long way up the road, at a door I can't tell you about yet, where the only thing that kept me on the right side of a very final decision was a scrap of paper that said, in a dead poet's own carved letters, that a name outlives the man. But that morning, in the grey and the chestnut leaves, all I knew was that I had a poet's name in my pocket, and a plectrum, and an eyelash, and a warning, and that the girl with the white eyes was building me, slow, out of small warm things, into someone who might one day be worth the trouble she'd already decided I was.

Door six folded shut behind me. A record I'll never play again. Eighteen to go. And I'd noticed, by now, that I was halfway through the first act of my own strange life and I still hadn't opened door seven — the sealed one, the one with no cocktail, just a name and a date and the shape of a shotgun — and that I crossed the room to avoid it, in the box, the way you cross the street to avoid a man you owe money to. Somewhere up the rack, a girl was gathering me together one relic at a time, and a kind man was reaching for his lighter, and door seven waited, patient as the Balancing, for the day I'd finally run out of the courage to keep it shut.
