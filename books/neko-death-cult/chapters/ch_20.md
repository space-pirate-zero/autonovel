# Episode 20 — The Dubai Door

*The Maneki Neko Death Cult*

---

Twentieth door. And it's the one where, for the first time in a whole death of watching people get taken, I looked at a man the machine had lined up for the taking — a man who'd earned it, mind, earned it a hundred times over — and I turned around and walked away and left him alive. Not out of mercy. He deserved no mercy. Out of *refusal.* Because after Davos I finally knew what a reap was for, and I would not be the one to file another death, not even his, not even the easiest, most satisfying, most deserved death the whole box ever dangled in front of me. Dubai. The present. A gold city built on a swamp of borrowed water and buried labour, where the richest men alive have stopped trying to fix the world and started, quite openly, engineering their exit from it.

There was a billionaire with a rocket and a bunker and a plan to be somewhere else when the bill came due. Two dead-eyed aristocrats at his shoulder, holding a door open for him with enormous patience. And a woman I loved, near-gone now, barely a shimmer, who stood beside me while I made the hardest small decision of the whole box — not to take the win. This is the door marked Dubai. It's where I stopped being a reaper. Buckle up. It's shorter on saints, this one, and longer on the thing that comes after you finally understand the machine: the question of what you do with the understanding.

— · —

Submitted for your approval: a city that is a brochure for the end of the world, and doesn't know it, or knows it and has priced it in. Dubai. The present. Understand what it represents, because it's the machine's dream of its own future made out of glass and slave-wage concrete. A place with no history it didn't purchase, rising out of a desert on a river of oil money and the bodies of the men who built it — migrant workers, a whole invisible nation of them, flown in, passports taken, worked in fifty-degree heat for a wage they can't send home fast enough, dying on the scaffolding of towers named after the men who'll never learn they existed. It is the purest thing the machine has ever built: a monument to a handful of people, standing on the erased lives of a multitude, air-conditioned against a climate the whole enterprise is helping to cook. And at the top of the tallest tower, the men who own it look out at the burning world they're burning and they do not think *how do we stop this.* They think *how do we get out.*

And I have to stop on the workers, because they're the whole moral of the door, and the machine spends fortunes keeping them off your screen. They come from the poorest corners of the earth on a lie and a loan — recruiters back home charge them a year's wages for the job, so they arrive already in debt, and the first thing the employer does is take the passport, "for safekeeping," which means they can't leave, which means the debt and the heat and the twelve-hour days in fifty degrees stop being a job and become a *sentence.* And the sentence built every gleaming thing you've ever seen a photo of and envied. Men die on those sites and the cause gets written up as "cardiac arrest" — true, in the way a heart does stop when you work it to death in an oven — and the bodies go home in boxes while the towers go up regardless, named after sheikhs and brands, and not one plaque anywhere carries a single name of the men who are, quite literally, mixed into the concrete. That's not a flaw in the gold city. That's the design. The machine's dream of its own future turns out to be the oldest arrangement in human history — the pyramid, the many carrying the few — only now with better air-conditioning and a marketing department. I climbed in on one of those erased men. I wore his death up the tower. It was the truest passport I ever carried.

Because that's the new thing, the thing this door is really about, the beige of the apocalypse. The very rich have quietly stopped believing the world can be saved — their own models tell them so, they've read the science their PR departments deny — and they've made a rational, monstrous, entirely characteristic decision: not to prevent the catastrophe, which would cost them their position, but to *survive* it, separately, in style. The bunkers in New Zealand. The seasteads. The Mars fantasy, the orbit fantasy, the escape-velocity dream of leaving the wrecked planet to the multitude who built the rockets. They're not trying to fix the ship. They're building lifeboats for first class and selling the rest of us tickets to watch. And the man I came for was the prophet of that exit. And the Cult were holding his lifeboat door open before I ever hit the ground — because a machine whose owners have decided to *leave* is a machine with nothing left to restrain it at all.

And I couldn't reach him the old way — he wasn't a dead genius, he was a living cancer, no card, no calendar. So I did what Davos taught me. I authored my own door again. And I chose, on purpose, the death that sat directly beneath his tower: a migrant worker, one of the invisible nation, dead of the heat and the debt and a fistful of cheap oblivion on a dormitory floor, a man whose name is on no monument and whose labour is in all of them. I rode his erased death up into the gold city, because there's a justice in it the machine will never see coming — that the only way into the oligarch's heaven is through the body of the man who built it. And the towers came apart around me.

— · —

Door twenty. Another blank one, authored, mine. The cheap despair-oblivion of a worked-to-death man in a country that imported him to be disposable — the most common death on the whole planet and the one the machine is most careful never to name, because to name it is to admit the towers stand on it. I mixed it, and I said sorry to a man ten thousand miles from a family who'd never be told the truth of how he died, and I kept his name too, with the cleaner's from Davos, in the part of my coat the machine can't reach. And the gold city peeled off the walls of the world.

The Cathedral of Wires met me as a construction site this door — a vast one, endless, a forest of cranes and rebar under a poison-orange sky, and on every level, building and building, the invisible nation, the men who raise every monument and are written out of every plaque, and above them, floor by floor, the structure getting shinier and emptier and more exclusive until the top was pure gold and pure vacancy. And I understood, climbing it, that this is the shape of the whole thing, has always been the shape: a pyramid, real this time, built by the many, inhabited by the few, and the few at the top no longer even pretending the arrangement is temporary or the ascent is open. Then the marble under me, and the smell of oud and chlorine and money so new it still had the shrink-wrap on.

— · —

I came up in a penthouse in the sky, the whole burning coast spread out below through floor-to-ceiling glass, and it was a party, the machine at its ease among itself again, and she was by the window, my girl, my signal, so faint now that the sunset came through her like she was the glass and not the person in front of it. Twenty doors of watching her dissolve and it still took the breath I don't have to see how little of her was left. She turned, and the two white moons found me, and she smiled, because even now, even nearly gone, she was glad, and that gladness is the bravest thing I've ever seen.

"There he is. The man who reads contracts now. You feel it, don't you — the pull. There's a death set up in this tower tonight, a good one, a clean one, the best-deserved one you've ever been offered. And every part of you that's still the tourist, still the one who wanted the mogul eaten in Los Angeles, wants to stay and watch it land. I'm not going to tell you what to do. I'm done telling you things. You know it all now. This is the first door where you get to *choose,* really choose, with your eyes all the way open and no fog and no excuse. So I'm just going to stand here, in the last of my light, and watch what you do when the machine hands you a win you've wanted for twenty doors. Go and look at it. Then decide."

— · —

So I went and looked, and God, she was right, it was the sweetest reap the box ever offered. The billionaire was the worst man I'd met in twenty doors — not a broken artist, not a weak doctor, not even a swindler with a spark of desperation, just pure, sleek, planetary appetite in human form — and he was in his glass office with the two clean ones, and he was, of all the perfect things, about to be undone by his own exit.

"The launch is Thursday. Test flight, but I'll be on it — optics, you have to lead from the front. And once the orbital platform's proven, the bunker network's the fallback, but honestly between us the bunker's for the nervous. The future's *up.* Let them have the surface. I've done the math, we've all done the math, the surface is a write-off by mid-century and no amount of — of *degrowth* or whatever they're calling surrender this year — is going to change the curve. So you don't fight the curve. You get above it. Literally. I'm not abandoning humanity; I'm *saving* the part of it worth saving, which is, let's be honest, the part that can afford the ticket. That's not villainy. That's stewardship. Somebody has to make it to the other side."

"Beautifully put. And you needn't worry about the launch — we've reviewed it, and the risk is... acceptable. To us. Let's say the door is open, whichever way Thursday goes. A man of your vision, martyred in the reach for the stars, is worth even more than a man of your vision safely in orbit. Either outcome, you win. Either outcome, we're paid."

"And you. The door that reads contracts. We wondered when you'd come. Go on, then. You know how this works better than anyone alive now. His launch has a flaw. We know it; he doesn't. All you have to do is nothing — witness, the way you always do, take your little note — and Thursday a monster who'd cook a planet to save himself goes up in a very expensive fireball, and the world is marginally better, and you get the cleanest, most righteous, most *deserved* reap of your entire death. We're even offering it to you. A gift. Payment, if you like, for twenty doors of good attendance. Just stay. Just watch. Just let it happen. It's the easiest win you'll ever be handed."

— · —

And I stood there, and I wanted it. I'll not lie to you at door twenty. Every cell I had left wanted to stay and watch that man go up in fire, wanted it the way I'd wanted the mogul eaten in nineteen sixty-two, the clean righteous spark of it, the *good.* He deserved it. The planet would be better. And they were handing it to me, gift-wrapped, no cost, the easiest justice in the world. And that — the *they were handing it to me* — is exactly what turned my stomach and cleared my head. Because Marilyn taught me the trap of cheering the machine for eating the right man, and Davos taught me what the eating is *for,* and standing in that gold tower I finally put the two together and saw the whole con. This reap wasn't justice. It was *maintenance.* His death, deserved or not, wasn't a crack in the machine — it was the machine, working, culling an asset that had outlived its use, and paying itself in my applause. They didn't want him dead because he was monstrous. They wanted him dead because he was *finished,* and a martyr's worth more than a has-been, and they wanted *me* to bless it, to be the witness that makes a murder a tragedy, to file it, to be, one more time, their quality control. And I said no.

I said it out loud, to the two clean ones, in a penthouse in the sky. "No. I'm not staying. I'm not watching. I'm not taking the win." And the man — the monster — I did the thing that cost me most of all: I walked over to his flawed and murderous launch plans, and I did not sabotage them further, and I did not fix them either, I *told* him. I put my dead hand on his shoulder and I said the flaw out loud, the one they'd hidden, the one that would kill him Thursday — I gave a monster the information that would save his monstrous life — because his death was a contract and I would not fulfil it, would not close that door the way it was paid to close, would not be their instrument even against a man I'd have happily seen dead an hour before. I refused the reap. I left the worst man in the world alive, on purpose, and I have never done anything harder or more certain in the whole of my strange death.

And I want to be honest about the cost of it, because a refusal that costs nothing is just a preference. Saving him didn't feel righteous. It felt *obscene.* Every instinct I had screamed that I'd just used the one scrap of power I'll ever have to protect the exact kind of man the whole box exists to mourn the victims of. I gave a planet-killer his life back, and I'll carry the wrongness of that as long as I carry anything. But that's the trap, you see — that's the last and cleverest turn of the screw: the machine makes doing its work *feel* like justice and refusing its work *feel* like betrayal, so that a good man's own conscience becomes the final whip. I had to refuse the reap while it still felt like a sin — because it only counts if it costs, and the thing it cost me was the sweetest feeling the box had left to offer: being on the right side of a righteous death. I gave that up. I'll never get it back. It's the price of not being their instrument. And it still feels like a wound, and that's exactly how I know it was real.

— · —

So here's the moral, and it's the strangest one yet, because it's about refusing to do a good thing. That billionaire should be dead. The world would be better without him. And I saved him, and I'd do it again, and here's why, and it took me twenty doors to be able to say it: *you do not get to be the machine's justice.* The moment you let them hand you a death — even a deserved one, even a monster's, even one that helps — you've agreed to the whole logic of the box: that there's a list, that people go on it, that the only question is who holds the pen, and that a well-aimed death is a solution. And it *never* is. It's just maintenance in a hero's mask. The machine doesn't fear your anger at the monsters; it *farms* it, it hands you the monsters specifically so you'll cheer the culling and never notice you've become the culler. The one thing it cannot survive, the thing it has no defence against, is a reaper who lays down the scythe. Who refuses to close the door. Who looks at the easiest, most righteous win ever offered and says: not by my hand. Not even him. Because the day I decide even one death is acceptable, I'm theirs, and every door after it is just me negotiating the list.

And the beige of it, the escape, the thing I climbed a tower of dead men to see. They've stopped trying to save the world. That's the quiet catastrophe under all the loud ones. The people with the most power to prevent the ending have run the numbers and decided their exit is cheaper than the cure, and they're building it in the open, and calling it innovation, and selling us the rockets and the story that we're all going together when the lifeboats have a guest list and we're not on it. Nobody gets out — that's the truth they can't afford to know — you cannot orbit your way off a dying planet you're still dying with, you cannot bunker your way out of being a person, the exit is a lie the same as all the others, the same as the needle and the shotgun and the rewind, one more door onto nothing dressed up as salvation. But they'll spend the whole ending believing it, and cooking the rest of us to fund it, unless somebody down here refuses to keep the machine's books.

And you think I'm exaggerating — the exit, the lifeboats — I'm not, it's the one part of this whole death you can go and verify without me. They really are building the bunkers; there are firms that do nothing else, luxury silos with hydroponics and blast doors and a five-year larder, sold to men whose favourite private dinner-party question — the real one, the one they ask the futurists when the cameras are off — is *how do I keep my security guards loyal after the money's worthless?* That's the actual question at the top of the pyramid. Not how do we save it. How do I keep my guards from turning on me once civilisation, the thing that made me a god, stops issuing the paychecks. They've war-gamed the collapse and cast themselves as the survivors and everyone else as the mob at the blast door, and they call it realism — and it is the single most beige idea in the entire universe: to look at a burning house full of people and spend your genius, your fortune, your one wild life on a fireproof room for one. The exit isn't just a fantasy. It's the final confession. It's the machine admitting, in reinforced concrete, that it was never going to fix anything, that it only ever meant to *win,* right up to and past the end. And a species whose most powerful members are visibly packing to leave is a species being told, in the only language that class speaks — money, spent — that it has been written off. So refuse that. If you refuse nothing else, refuse the write-off. Stay on the surface with the rest of us and build something, anything, forward. Nobody gets out. So we may as well be here, together, awake, while it's still here to be in.

So. I refused. It's a small thing. It's the biggest thing I've ever done.

— · —

She was waiting by the window when I came back, so faint by then I could barely find her against the burning coast, and she didn't say anything, because there wasn't anything to say — I'd finally done it, the thing all her relics had been teaching me toward, the refusal, the laying-down of the scythe. She just reached up, with a hand that was more light than hand now, and she touched my face, and where the other doors she'd left me a thing to carry, this door she left me nothing but that touch, because I didn't need a relic any more. I'd become the lesson. I was carrying it in the choice itself. The two clean ones didn't rage, didn't threaten — they just watched me go, with something on those dead faces I'd never seen, and it took me a moment to name it, and when I did it frightened me more than any threat: it was *curiosity.* A door had refused to open. They'd never seen that. They didn't know what came next. Neither did I.

Door twenty folded shut behind me — or rather, I let it stay open, the worst man in the world alive behind it, the first door I ever declined to close on the machine's terms — and I was something now that the box had no word for, because I'd stopped being its reader and its instrument and even its witness, and become the one thing it never planned for: a refusal that walks. Four doors left. One sealed — and quieter now, strangely, because a man who won't reap a monster is a man who's less likely to reap himself. One mine, with a buyer I'd deny. And one, so close I could feel the cold of it, hers — her last solid night, coming, and after that only signal. Somewhere up the rack a kind man was reaching for his lighter, and a girl made of light had almost nothing left and had spent it standing at a window to watch me lay down the scythe, and I walked out of the gold tower into the poison heat, past the invisible nation still building the exit they'd never board, and I made them a promise too, the dead workers whose names I keep: that a door that won't close for a monster won't close for a machine, either, and that the refusal doesn't stop at one. It's only just started. Four doors. Refuse well. It's nearly time.
