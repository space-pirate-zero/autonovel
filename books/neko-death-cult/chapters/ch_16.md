# Episode 16 — The Amy Winehouse Door

*The Maneki Neko Death Cult*

---

Sixteenth door. And it's the one I'd been walking toward without knowing it, because it's a twenty-seven, the last and the saddest of them, and the door where she finally told me how the story ends. London. Camden. Two thousand and eleven. A girl of twenty-seven — that number, that cursed club, Joplin and Hendrix and Morrison and the Seattle boy and now her — dead of the drink in a little house in the one part of London that ever felt like it belonged to the kids, a voice like a hundred-year-old wound and a wit like a razor, filmed falling for years by a machine that mourned her the very day she finally hit the ground.

There was a man who'd built a business out of watching people die slowly and selling tickets by the frame. Two dead-eyed aristocrats at his shoulder, calling it engagement. And a woman in a Camden pub with a beehive and white eyes who was about to sit me down and tell me, at last, the one true thing she'd been protecting me from for sixteen doors: that she had one jump left in her, and one only, and what that meant for the both of us. This is the door marked Amy. It's the end of the second act of my strange death, and it's where the timer stopped being a metaphor. Buckle up. Hold something.

— · —

Submitted for your approval: a girl who sang like the pain was already antique, in an age that had learned to livestream the pain and sell the ads against it. Camden, two thousand and eleven. Understand what she was, because they turned her into a hairstyle and a punchline and I'll not let that stand. She had a voice out of another century — a proper one, a jazz voice, a blues voice, the kind that sounds like it's remembering something that happened to somebody else a hundred years ago and breaking its heart about it fresh. She wrote her own ruin down before it happened, put it in the songs, sang a hook about refusing the very help she needed and watched the whole world sing it back at her like a chant, like a taunt, "no, no, no," until the joke and the prophecy and the grave were the same three notes.

And I want you to feel how they did her, because the how is the whole indictment. When the drink and the wrong man and the grief pulled her under, the machine didn't look away, and it didn't help, and it didn't even have the honesty to be cruel to her face. It made her a *bit*. A running gag. Her name became shorthand for a mess, a punchline on the panel shows, a tabloid serial with a new grim instalment every week — "look at the state of her" — a whole nation tutting and clicking, tutting and clicking, and every tut worth a fraction of a penny to somebody. They booked her, wrecked, onto a stage in Belgrade in front of twenty thousand people knowing she couldn't stand, and let her fail in front of the world, and filmed it, and sold the failing. She was a woman drowning in public, and the entire apparatus of her culture arranged itself into grandstand seating. And the cruellest part, the part I can't get past: she'd *told* them. She'd written the ending down first, sung it to them in advance in that hundred-year-old voice — and they'd danced to it. They made a number-one hit out of her refusal of the help that might have saved her, and played it at parties, while she died to the beat of it, on schedule, in Camden.

And the age she died in had a new machine in it, newer than the tabloids that killed the girl in Florida, sharper than the studio that owned the voice in Beverly Hills. This was the age of the *feed*. The always-on, everyone-a-camera, refresh-for-more age, where a famous person's collapse wasn't a story that ran and ended, it was *content*, a serial, a live one, updated hourly, a girl coming apart in real time on a million little screens with a comments section underneath doing half the cruelty for free. They didn't hound her the old way, in a pack, for a photo. They hounded her the new way, ambient, constant, monetised, every stumble out of a Camden pub a thousand frames a thousand ways, sold and re-sold, and the more she fell the more they earned, until her disintegration was, in the cold ledger of it, the single most profitable thing she ever produced. She made the machine more money falling than she ever did singing. That's the age. That's the new thing. And the man who ran that particular racket was at his desk before I ever hit the ground, watching her on six monitors, deciding which frame of a dying girl would get the most clicks.

— · —

Door sixteen. Not a needle, not a pill. The drink — the oldest, most legal, most patient poison there is, the one they advertise. She'd got clean, that's the part that guts me, she'd stopped the hard stuff, and it was the *drink* that got her in the end, a lethal tide of it in a body that had done without so long it had forgotten how to survive the flood. No dealer to blame, no doctor, nothing anyone could be arrested for. Just the legal poison, and a girl alone in a house in Camden, and a level in the blood that stops a heart, at twenty-seven, on a Saturday, while three streets away the market she loved was busy being sold as a brand. I mixed it in the between, and I said sorry to a girl named Amy, and Camden came apart around me.

And the Cathedral of Wires, this door, was full. That's the only way I can say it. Every other door I'd crossed the between alone. This time, as the broken moons turned and the static-sermon dropped, the between was *crowded* — with them, the twenty-sevens, the whole cursed club I'd been walking through without counting: the girl with the Southern Comfort and the girl with the hard hands and the poet in the bath and the boy in Seattle with the green eyes, all of them there in the wiring, all of them turning to watch her come in, the newest and the last of them, and there was no cruelty in it, no triumph, just a terrible tender recognition, the dead making room. And I understood, passing through that crowded holy static, that the machine hadn't invented a thing. It had just got faster. It had been harvesting twenty-sevens since before there were cameras, and it would go on doing it, and the only thing that changes, century to century, is the resolution.

And I owe you a word on the number, because it's haunted this whole box and it deserves better than superstition. Twenty-seven. The girl with the Southern Comfort, the hands that made weather, the poet in the bath, the boy in Seattle, and now her — and a dozen more the calendar keeps. People treat it like a curse, a spooky coincidence, a club with a velvet rope. It isn't. It's a *statistic with a cause*. Twenty-seven is roughly how long it takes — how long from the moment the machine finds a genuinely gifted, genuinely raw young person and starts feeding: the contracts at nineteen, the fame at twenty-one, the ownership, the pressure, the chemical management of a human being run at industrial speed. That's about how long that fire burns before it burns out. It's not mystical. It's a *shelf life*. The machine has measured, across a century, the average interval between ignition and ash for its most flammable product, and it comes out around twenty-seven years, and it plans around it, and it keeps the tribute package ready. The club isn't cursed. It's *scheduled*. That's so much worse, and it's the true thing, and she was the last one I'd have to watch check in. Then the cobbles, and the smell of stale ale and cigarettes and rain on a Camden night.

— · —

I came up in a Camden pub, the back room, low ceiling, a tiny stage, the kind of room where the real ones cut their teeth before the machine found them — sticky and warm and human, the good version of the thing the boy in New York's club was the fake of. And there was a woman on a stool with an acoustic guitar and a beehive gone half-collapsed and cat-flick eyeliner smudged by the night, singing to nobody, to eight people and a dog, a torch song so quiet and so true it stopped the pint halfway to my mouth. And when she looked up, the eyes were white, the two moons, and the violet was in the black under the bleach, and I knew her, and something in her face this door was different, and it frightened me before she said a word.

Her name on nothing, this door — no call sheet, no tag, she was just singing in a pub — but she told me to call her Cass, and then, halfway through the second song, she stopped, mid-line, and set the guitar down, and looked at me across that little room the way you look at someone when you've finally decided to stop protecting them from a thing, and she said, "come outside, love. It's time you knew. I've put it off as long as the physics let me."

— · —

So we went out the back, into a Camden alley in the rain, and she leaned on the wet brick, and she was so thin now, so *faint*, I could see the neon of the kebab shop across the way glowing through her shoulder, and she took my dead hand in both of hers, and she told me.

"I've got one jump left, Zero. One. You've watched me spend myself for sixteen doors — the dancing, the standing-in, the clean way, the going-to-light a piece at a time. Well. This is the piece where I tell you there's nearly nothing left to spend. The girl at the bright end of the tape is almost all paid out. One more jump, one more clean slip between the tracks, and there won't be a Cass or a Vera or a Marlo or a Kat to come back — there'll just be signal, everywhere and nowhere, the frequency with the girl finally burned all the way off it. I'm not telling you to frighten you. I'm telling you because you've earned the truth, and because you're about to spend the rest of your doors trying to work out how to save me, and I need you to hear, from me, while there's still a me to say it: *you can't.* It's not a puzzle you failed to solve. It's the price of the way I travel, and I paid it gladly, door by door, because every instalment bought me another meeting with you."

"Then don't jump," I said. Stupid, the stupidest, the same stupid I always was with her. "Stay. Right here. Don't take the last one. Stop the tape."

"Oh, love. You can't stop the tape, you of all people know that — it only ever goes forward. If I don't jump, I don't stop, I just fade where I stand, slower, sadder, alone, in a year that isn't yours. The jump's not the thing that ends me. The jump's the thing that lets me choose *where*. And I know where. I've always known where. There's a door coming — you've felt it, you've dreaded it since Seattle — a small room, and rain, and you, and me flickering, and it's the one I've been travelling backwards toward this whole time. That's where I spend the last of it. With you. On purpose. Not fading alone in some grey nowhere, but flickering out in the arms of the one broadcast I ever loved, having said everything, having got here, at last, at the right end for once. That's not a tragedy I need saving from, Zero. That's the only happy ending the physics allows me, and I chose it before you were born, and I'd choose it again, every wrong-ended meeting, the whole backward tape, to get you one more time. Don't you dare rob me of the one death I actually get to pick."

"There's another way," I said — and I heard the sealed door in my own voice before I'd even finished the thought, and hated it, and said it anyway. "Door seven. The shotgun. If you're going, if the physics won't let you stay, then I don't have to *watch*. I could go first. Open the sealed one, be in the dark waiting when you flicker out, so you're not the one left calling a dead channel."

"No. No. You listen to me, Zero, because this is the one they'll dress up prettiest, and it's the one that damns you. You will not beat me to the dark to spare yourself the grief. That is not love. That's the machine wearing love's coat — it's the finish, the exit, the beige completing itself, the same lie they sold the old man in Ketchum. And if you take it *for me*, they win the whole board, and I spent myself, door by door, for nothing. The most I will ever ask of you — the whole and entire of it — is the single hardest thing there is. Outlive me. Stay. Keep transmitting after I've gone quiet. Be the broadcast that doesn't go dark just because the one tuned to it did. I'm choosing to flicker out in your arms because your arms will still be warm, and here, and *alive* — that's the gift, love, that's the only gift, a real death held by a living hand. Go cold first, and you take even that from me. So swear it. Not that you'll save me — you can't. Swear you'll *survive* me. That's your act three. That's the only lesson left in the whole box."

And I swore it. In a Camden alley, in the rain, to a woman I could see straight through, I swore the one thing I had no earthly idea how to do — to be the one who stays, the signal that keeps broadcasting into the dark long after the answer's gone silent. It's the hardest promise a person can make, and I made it like breathing, because she asked. And I've been trying to keep it ever since. This — all of it, this whole show, every door I've narrated into your ears — this *is* me keeping it. Still transmitting. Still here. That's the secret at the bottom of the box, if you want it: the reason there's a show at all is that a dissolving woman in an alley made me swear not to go dark, and I am a man of my word about exactly one thing in all of creation, and it's her.

And I stood in a Camden alley in the rain and held a woman I could see the kebab-shop neon through, and I did not say anything clever, because there wasn't anything clever, there was only the thing, the plain unbearable thing, which is that the person I loved had one move left and had decided to spend it on me, and that the whole of my third act was going to be the war between letting her have the death she chose and the animal in me that would burn down every door in the box to keep her here one more rotten faded minute. She knew it. She'd known it before I did. She always did.

"There now. There. Go and watch the sad man count his clicks. I know, I know, not now, but yes now, because the show is the show and she deserves a witness even tonight, especially tonight. And you're the only one in this whole city who came here for *her* and not for the fall. I'll keep. I've got one jump in me. I'm not spending it in an alley. Go on."

— · —

So I went, wrecked, and I watched the content man, and after everything she'd just handed me he barely registered as a person — just a final, perfect specimen of the thing I'd been naming for sixteen doors. He was in an office above the market with six monitors and the two clean ones, and every screen had her on it, the dying girl, angles and angles, and he was choosing.

"Right, so — engagement's through the roof on the pub footage, she's a machine, honestly, best performer we've got and she doesn't even know she's working for us. Push the stumble-out-the-door clip, that's doing numbers, and hold the sad-eyes one, we'll want that in the bank. Look, I'm not a monster, I want to be clear, I don't want anything bad to happen to her. But if something does — and let's be realistic, the trajectory's the trajectory — we need to be ready, because the day she goes will be the biggest traffic day this business has ever had, and grief is the highest-converting emotion there is. Have the tribute package built. Have it ready to go. Respectfully. Obviously. But ready."

"You've refined it to a science your grandfathers with their long lenses could only dream of. They had to chase her. You've built a machine she walks into for free, that films her better the worse she gets, and that will make more from her death in a day than her label made from her genius in a decade. And no one will ever call you the killer. You never touched her. You only ever pointed a lens."

"And note the elegance — you don't even need her to be *falling*, ultimately. That's the old model. The mature model is that everyone films everyone, all the time, the whole species pointing lenses at itself, monetising its own slow disintegration frame by frame, and you simply own the platform they do it on. She's not the product. She's the proof of concept. The product is the entire watching world, and it's already bought. Do have the tribute ready. And do try to seem sad; it converts better."

And I watched the man nod and drag a clip of a dying girl into a folder marked *hold*, and I felt something I hadn't felt in a long stretch of doors, which was not the old clean spark of wanting the machine to eat him. I was past that; Marilyn cured me of that. What I felt was tired. Bone-tired, and clear. Because I finally, fully understood that there is no eating him. He's not a man who did a wrong thing. He's a nerve ending of a thing with no centre, and the thing will grow another him by Monday, and the girl three streets away will die on Saturday, and both of those facts are just the weather now. The woman would call it efficient. She didn't even bother. It went without saying.

— · —

So here's the moral, the one that closes the second act, and it's got two halves like a broken heart. The first half is the machine's, and you know it by now, I've been building it door by door: they will monetise your dying. Not just the famous — that was the prototype, the girl in Florida, the girl in Beverly Hills, this girl in Camden, the three of them one lesson taught three times, louder each time. The full version is the one the clean man said out loud: the whole species, pointing lenses at itself, filming its own slow fall, selling the frames to each other, and a machine at the centre owning the platform and taking its cut of every tear. That's the endgame of the attention age. Not that they'll kill you. That they'll livestream you dying and split the ad revenue with the crowd that came to watch. And call it connection. And mean it.

And the second half of the moral is mine, and it's smaller, and it's the only thing I actually care about tonight, God forgive me, with a dead girl three streets away and a species disintegrating on six monitors. The second half is: she's got one jump left. The woman I love has one move on the whole board, and she's chosen to spend it dying in my arms in a room in Seattle that's coming for us both like a train, and she's asked me — twice now, she never asks twice — not to disappear after her. To stay a person. To not method myself into the beautiful grief the machine would crown me for. And I don't know if I can. That's the honest end of act two. I've learned to see the machine, to sit still, to keep my hand in my pocket, to doubt the jacket, to keep a room with no camera in it. Sixteen doors of mastery. And none of it, *none* of it, has taught me how to watch her flicker out and not follow. That's act three. That's the only lesson left. And I'm going to fail it, probably. But she asked. So I'll try.

— · —

She left me nothing this door, and this time I understood it straight away, no fear. She left me nothing because she'd given me the only thing left to give, which was the truth of the ending, out loud, in the rain, while there was still a mouth to say it. You can't fold that into a pocket. It just goes in you and stays, cold and warm at once, forever. Though when I got back to the pub she'd gone — of course she'd gone, they always go — and the eight people and the dog were still there, and somebody had left her guitar leaning on the stool, and on it, scratched in the dust on the body with a fingernail, small, three words: *worth every door.* Worth every door. The whole backward tape, every wrong-ended meeting, every time I turned up young and stupid and didn't know her face — worth it, she said, to her, who paid for all of it in the only currency she had, which was herself, a piece at a time, down to the one jump she was saving to die on me with. Worth every door. I'll spend the rest of mine trying to be.

Door sixteen folded shut behind me, and the second act of my strange death folded shut with it, and I was not the tourist who fell out of the sky in Atlanta, and I was not even the traveller who counted the doors in Belfast. I was a man in love with a signal that had one flicker left, walking toward a room in Seattle I'd spent the whole box dreading, carrying a promise I didn't know how to keep. Eight doors left. One of them sealed — heavier than ever now, because the sealed door is the one exit that would let me beat her to the dark, and God help me it's started to look less like a horror and more like a mercy, which is exactly, precisely how they'll try to take me. One of them mine. And one of them hers, coming, in the rain, in Seattle, soon. Somewhere up the rack a kind man was reaching for his lighter, and a girl made of light had one jump left and a smile on her about it, and the second act ended not with mastery but with a countdown, and the countdown, for the first time, was hers. Worth every door. Hold something. Act three is where it costs.
