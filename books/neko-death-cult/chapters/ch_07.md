# Episode 7 — The Hemingway Door

*The Maneki Neko Death Cult*

---

There's a door I've never told you about, because I've never opened it. Door seven. It hasn't got a cocktail behind it. It hasn't got a saint's recipe or a measured dose or a way home. It's got a name, and a date, and the shape of a shotgun, and a cold that comes off the card even through the cardboard.

Every other door in the box is a jump — a death you walk away from, into another year. Door seven is the other kind. The real kind. The one with no groove on the far side. And this is the story of the night, six deaths deep and tired all the way to the bone, that I nearly opened it. No new city, this one. No costume, no era, no rich man to watch get eaten. Just me, and a box, and the quietest, most patient argument of my whole strange life. Buckle up. Or don't. It's that kind of door.

— · —

Submitted for your approval: a tired man in a room that isn't anywhere. Because between the doors there's a waiting place — not the Cathedral, the loud between, but a quiet one, a nothing room the shape of every cheap motel and every hotel bar at four in the morning I'd ever drunk in. The slipstream parks you there sometimes, between jumps, when you're worn too thin to move. And it was there, on the little laminate table under the dead yellow lamp, that I finally let myself really look at door seven.

Let me tell you about the nothing-room, because you'll not have heard of it, and I hope to God you never see it. It isn't the Cathedral. The Cathedral's a cathedral — it has grandeur, it has that terrible holiness, it roars. The nothing-room is where the slipstream leaves you when you're too worn even for the Cathedral. It's every four a.m. hotel bar folded down into one. Dead yellow lamp. Laminate table with a cigarette burn in the corner that's always in the same corner. A window with nothing behind it — not black, not a view, just *nothing*, the honest kind, the kind that doesn't even bother pretending to be a wall. And no clock. That's the worst of it. Every other room I'd ever died in at least had a clock. The nothing-room's taken even that, because in the nothing-room there's nothing left you could still be on time for.

And I was carrying six doors by then. People think the watching's the easy part. It isn't. Every man I'd stood in the dark and watched get whispered off his own edge — the drummer, the singer I kept meeting at the wrong ends, the screwed-down soul, the hands that made weather, the girl on the edge of the light, the poet who couldn't cheat the debt — I hadn't left a single one of them behind. That's not how it works. You don't pass through a ruin. You take a rubbing of it, and you carry the rubbing, and by six doors deep your coat is so heavy with other people's endings you can barely lift your arms to close it. The woman was right about that, curse her for it. It's heavier being the audience than being the act. The act only has to die the once. The audience has to carry every death that ever moved them, forever, and hasn't got a door of its own to set any of it down behind.

The name on the card was Hemingway. Papa. The great man himself, who'd done everything a man was supposed to do and done it bigger — hunted the big game, fished the deep water, drove the ambulances and drank the cafés and wrote sentences so true and so clean they made every other writer sound like they were gargling. The Nobel. The myth. The most alive man of his whole century, by his own careful design. And on the second of July, nineteen sixty-one, in a house in Ketchum, Idaho, in the early morning before his wife woke, he took the shotgun down off the rack and did the one thing the myth was built to make impossible. That's door seven. The great man's last door. And the calendar, God help it, had kept it.

You have to understand what he'd built, to understand why his was the door that could kill me. He wasn't only a great writer. He was the first man to work out that in the new century you could be a *brand* of aliveness — that the living itself could be the product. The boxing and the bullfights and the marlin and the wars, all of it performed, photographed, sold; the beard and the bravado, Papa on the cover of the magazines. He half-invented the modern thing, the being-famous-for-how-you-live thing, and he did it fifty years early and better than anyone's done it since. And the trap folded into it — the trap that finally closed on him — is that once your *aliveness* is the product, you're never again allowed to be tired. To be frightened. To be a soft, failing, ordinary man in a failing body. The brand doesn't permit it. The brand needs Papa. And there came a morning when there wasn't enough Papa left in him to meet the demand, and no one — least of all him — had ever thought to build a way for the man to survive the death of the myth.

— · —

They came, of course. The two clean ones. They always know when you're thin. I looked up from the box and they were in the chairs across the laminate table, pressed and patient, and for once the man wasn't selling me anything. That's how I knew it was serious. When the Cult stop selling, that's when you want to keep your hands where you can see them.

"You look tired, Zero."

"I'm always tired. It's the job."

"Six doors. Six men we've walked to the edge, and you've watched every one, and taken your little note, and told yourself you were only passing through. But you're not passing through, are you. You're accumulating. You carry all of it now. Every ruin. Every grave nobody came to. It's heavy, being the audience. Heavier than being the act."

"Think of the arithmetic, Zero. Six doors carried. Seventeen still to come — and they get worse. You know they get worse. You've seen the sealed one on the card; you can feel the shape of the ones past it. The boy in Seattle with the green eyes. The ones we won't name yet. You'll stand in the dark for every last one, and take your little rubbing, and add it to the coat, and there is no door at the end of the row marked *home* — we saw to that, it was the very first thing we saw to. There's only more watching, and a heavier coat, and the girl you never once get to keep, met at the wrong end, forever. Or there's the card, on the table. And quiet."

"There's a man, up the rack — this century, your century. A great man, in the modern style. Bought his greatness rather than earned it, but he wants the earning so badly he can taste it. So he climbs the mountains with the wrong gear, and dives the wrecks in the tin cans, and hunts the animals that hunt back, chasing the one thing all his money can't buy him: the feeling of having done a real thing with his own soft hands. We're with him now. He'll die on a mountain, in April, on March's equipment, believing right up to the cold that he's finally proved he was always Papa underneath. It's rather beautiful, in its way. A man dying to feel alive."

"We're not asking you to do anything, you understand. We never ask. We're only... noting that the door is there. And that you're tired. And that no one — not one soul, in any year — would blame you. That's all. We'll sit with you. Take whatever time you need. Time is the one thing the nothing-room has in surplus."

"And there's the door, Zero. Right there on the table. You could stop. Like the old man stopped. Not a jump — a *finish*. You've done more than any of them. You've earned the right to put the gun down at last. The great man knew when the story was over. Don't you?"

And here's the thing I have to tell you honest, because the whole of the rest of my life turns on it. I wanted to. God, I wanted to. Six doors of watching, and no way home, and a woman I kept meeting at the wrong ends and could never once keep — and the tiredness in me that night was the oldest thing I had, older than the calendar, older than the Vault, the tiredness that had put the needle in my arm in the first place, back when I still thought oblivion was a place you could get to. And there it was. The real one. The finish. And two quiet voices telling me I'd earned it. And my hand — I watched it happen, the way you watch a stranger's — my hand moved across the laminate toward door seven.

And the terrible thing — the thing I've never once said out loud until now — is that it didn't feel like their voices. That's their whole genius, I keep trying to tell you. They don't put a single thing into you that wasn't already there. Every word the woman said reached me in my own voice. *You've earned the rest. You've carried enough. The girl's a wound you keep reopening — let it scar. Go quiet. Go dark. It's the one thing in the whole of this that's actually yours to choose.* And it was true. That's what nobody warns you about the edge — how much of what calls you over it is *true*. The tiredness was true. The no-way-home was true. The unkeepable love was true. They didn't have to lie to me. They only had to sit quiet in the dead yellow light and let the truth do the pulling, and watch my hand move toward the cold coming off the card.

— · —

"Don't."

She was in the room. I don't know how — she doesn't come to the between, it's not a place you can dance to — but she was there, in the dead yellow light, white eyes wide for once, not knowing, not serene, just frightened, and I'd never seen her frightened, and it stopped my hand more than any argument could have.

"I can't tell you not to. You know I can't — I meet you out of order, I don't get to change the tape, I only get to ride it. But I can tell you what's behind that door, because I'm further up the road than you and I've seen the rack with you not on it. There's no you, love. That's what's behind it. Not peace. Not rest. Not the old man's dignity. Just a rack of years with a hole in it exactly your shape, and me, in every one of them, still tuned to a frequency that's stopped transmitting. Signal finds signal — but only if there's a signal to find. You open that door and I spend forever calling a dead channel."

"Listen to me. Listen. I have been up the road you're pointing that hand toward, and I have stood in the version of the rack where you did it — where you opened it — and I need you to know what it is actually like. Because it is not rest, love. It is not the old man's dignity. It is not quiet. It's a house with the radio left on in an empty room. It's me, in every year I've got left, tuned to a frequency that used to be you, turning the dial through the static — past sixty-one, past oh-three, past all of it — and there's nothing. There's *nothing*. There's just the hiss where the broadcast used to be, and a woman refusing to believe it's gone, night after night after night, calling a channel that went dark on purpose. You want to talk to me about a debt that can't be cheated. *That's* the one you'd leave me. Not grief — grief ends. This is meeting a man out of order for the rest of time and arriving, every single time, one door too late. You're not the audience, Zero. You never were. You're the only broadcast I've ever loved. Don't you dare go dark."

And the two clean ones didn't move, and didn't argue, because they don't, they never do — they only ever help you want, and they'd shown me the wanting, and now they sat and waited to see which want won. That's the whole of their religion. They don't push. They let the height do the killing. And the height was right there, and so was she, and my hand was between them on the cold laminate, shaking.

— · —

I want to tell you I chose life because I'm brave. I'm not. I chose it because she was frightened, and I'd have crawled across broken glass to un-frighten her, and the only way to do it was to take my hand off the door. That's all it was. Not courage. Love, doing the job courage couldn't. I closed the box. I closed it slow, and I pressed door seven shut with my thumb till the cardboard creased, and the cold came off it, and then, when it was shut, went quiet. And the two clean ones stood up, unbothered as ever, and the woman looked at me with those dead eyes and said the only kind thing I ever heard her say, which is also the cruellest thing anybody's ever said to me, both at once.

"Interesting. Most of them lean, in the end. You didn't. We'll be watching to see how long that lasts."

— · —

So here's the moral, and it's the heaviest one in the whole box, so I'll set it down gentle. I'd spent my whole life, and six deaths on top of it, believing that the shotgun — the needle, the door, the finish — was the one move the machine couldn't touch. The last free act. The great man's dignity, the door out that no landlord could ever put a rent on.

And I want you to hear how they actually ground him down, the old man, because it's the whole argument of this door. It wasn't sudden. It never is. First the body went — the plane crashes in Africa that he walked away from twice and never really walked away from at all, the liver, the blood, the pain. Then the nerve went — the certainty that he was being watched, followed, his phone tapped, which everyone around him called paranoia and which the files, opened long years after, showed was at least in part, and horribly, *true*. And then, to fix the fear, the kindest men available strapped the truest sentence-maker of the century to a table at the Mayo Clinic and ran the current through his brain until the fear went quiet — and took his memory out with it. He came home from the cure unable to remember his own stories. Unable to build a single clean sentence. They'd reached in and switched off the one light that was actually *him*, meaning only to help, and left the myth standing in a body that could no longer produce it. That's the grind. That's the beige-ing of the most alive man alive. And three weeks after they sent him home cured, in the early morning, before his wife woke, he finished the little bit of work they'd started.

And that night at the laminate table I understood I'd had it exactly backwards. The finish isn't freedom from the machine. It's the machine's cleanest win. They didn't shoot the old man. They never do. They ground him to beige and handed him the gun and called it his dignity — and he did their last stroke of work for them, with his own hands, in the quiet, and thanked the idea of the exit for the privilege. The machine *loves* a man who'll grind his own self down to nothing and call it a decision. It saves them the overhead.

I used to think there was a nobility in it. The exit. The one door with no rent on it. I'd built my whole death around the idea — that oblivion was a country you could emigrate to, that the needle was a passport, that if the life got heavy enough you could always, always put it down. And what I learned at that table, with my hand shaking between a frightened girl and a cold card, is that the machine *adores* that idea. It's their favourite thing a person can believe. Because a population that thinks the exit is theirs — that going dark is the last free act — is a population that will do the machine's culling for it, quietly, one at a time, in the early mornings, before the wives wake up, and thank the machine for the dignity of the option. The exit is not the door out of the beige. The exit is the beige, arriving on schedule, wearing the mask of your own free will.

The cure isn't the gun. I know that now, and it cost me a shaking hand on a cold table to learn it. The cure is to stay a signal. To keep transmitting — however tired, however ground down, however far from home — because the second you go dark they've won, and worse, far worse, somebody who's tuned to you spends forever calling a dead channel. Don't go dark. Whatever it costs. Don't you dare go dark.

— · —

There was a man at the door of the nothing-room when I looked up. Bearded, kind-eyed, a soft pack in his hand, watching me the way he always watches — like a man waiting for a bus he owns. He'd been there the whole time, I think. He didn't say if I'd done right or wrong. He never does. He just lit his cigarette, and let the smoke climb in the dead light, and gave me the smallest nod, and I still can't tell you, all these years on, whether it was approval or disappointment or simply a man ticking a box.

I've wondered, since, whose side he's on. The bearded man. Whether he's a Cult man in a softer coat, or something older than the Cult, or just the janitor of the whole machine — the one who sweeps up after all of it and doesn't much care who wins, because he's seen too many rounds to bet. He was there the night I nearly went, and he didn't stop me and he didn't push me. Afterward he just... noted it. Lit the cigarette. I've come to think he's the one who keeps the actual record — not the woman's ledger of who leaned and who held, but the true one, the one nobody living gets to read. And I think the nod he gave me meant *logged*. Nothing warmer than that. Nothing colder. Just: logged. The universe had seen. Make of it what you can.

Door seven stayed shut. It's still shut, as I tell you this — but it's closer than it was, and it calls, on the bad nights, in a voice I know too well, and there's a day coming, a long way up the rack, where I'll have to answer it for good. But not that night. That night I closed the box, and I put my shaking hand in my pocket and held a plectrum and an eyelash and a poet's carved name, and I waited for the slipstream to move me on. Seventeen doors left, and one of them sealed. And a girl made of light, somewhere up the road, who'd crossed into a place she couldn't reach just to tell me not to go dark. I owed her every door after that. I still do. I always will.
