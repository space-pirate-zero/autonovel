# Episode 10 — The Elvis Door

*The Maneki Neko Death Cult*

---

Tenth door. And this is the one that taught me the cruellest trick the machine's got, the one it saves for its very favourites: it doesn't just kill you. It keeps you working after. Memphis. Nineteen seventy-seven. The King of the whole thing, the man who'd stolen a Black sound and a swivel of the hips and lit the back half of a century on fire with it, dying at forty-two on a bathroom floor in a mansion he'd bought and could no longer leave, a fat sad prisoner of the very crown he'd won.

There was a man who called himself a Colonel and wasn't, who owned half of the King and had for twenty years, and was about to lose his meal ticket and already planning to sell the ghost. Two dead-eyed aristocrats in the wings of the showroom, teaching a carny how to strip-mine a corpse. And a woman singing backup in a white jumpsuit chorus — white eyes, violet in the black under the stage wig — who caught my eye across a Vegas stage and didn't miss a note. This is the door marked Elvis. It's where I learned what beige really is, at the end of the road: not silence, not death, but a man doing an impression of himself, forever, for money, in a cape. Buckle up.

— · —

Submitted for your approval: a golden mansion with a chain on the gate and a man inside it who owns everything he can see and none of the hours of his own life. Memphis, nineteen seventy-seven. Understand what he'd become, because he half-invented the thing you're all trapped in now. He wasn't just the first rock star. He was the first *product* built to that scale — the first human being taken all the way from a truck-driver's boy in Tupelo to a global brand, and the man who built him understood, before anybody, that the trick wasn't the music. The trick was the *owning*. Get the whole of him on paper, early, cheap, off a kid too grateful to read the small print, and then run him like a mine until the seam gives out.

And I want to be honest about who he was, because the machine flattened him in both directions — worshipped him into a saint, then later sneered him into a thief, and he was neither, he was the more complicated third thing that actual people always are. He was a poor white boy from the wrong end of Tupelo who grew up in and around Black churches and Black radio and loved that music with his whole chest, genuinely, and had the gift — a real one, don't let anybody tell you different — to carry it. And the machine looked at a music made by people it refused to sell to, and it found a beautiful white boy who could sing it, and it did the oldest robbery in the American book: it took the thing and left the people. That's not his crime. He'd have shared any stage with any of them, said so, loud. It's the machine's crime, and he was the machine's instrument for it — and also, at the very same time, a kid who just really loved the songs. Hold both. The truth's always both.

And the man who did the owning called himself Colonel Tom Parker, and he was not a colonel, and his name was not Tom Parker, and he was not, it turned out, even from this country. He was a carny — a genuine, literal carnival man who'd worked the midways, learned every angle a mark can be worked from, and then found the greatest mark the world would ever produce and married himself to fifty per cent of him. Fifty. Half of everything the King ever earned, for the crime of being the man who answered the phone. And here's the detail that tells you the whole of it, the detail I think about more than almost any: the Colonel never once let the King tour outside America. The biggest star on earth, and he never played London, never played Paris, never played anywhere the man who owned him couldn't follow — because the Colonel was in the country illegally and had no passport and could not leave, and so the King couldn't either. He caged the most famous man alive inside the exact size of his own manager's secret. That's not a metaphor. That's just what happened.

He'd found him early, in fifty-five — a nobody with a swivel and a truck-driver's day job — and he'd done the one genuinely brilliant thing a manager can do, which is *see* it: the whole future in a nineteen-year-old, the scale of it, before anyone. And then he spent the next twenty-two years making certain that future belonged to him. Fifty per cent, rising. Merchandising so cannily cruel he'd sell you an "I love Elvis" badge and an "I hate Elvis" badge from the same booth, because he'd worked out that indifference was the only thing that didn't pay. He didn't own a piece of the King. He owned the *concept* of the King, and he rented the man back to himself, by the hour, forever.

And so the King, who could have had the world, got Vegas instead. Two shows a night in a showroom, the same set, the same jumpsuit, the same karate moves gone slow and heavy, a residency that was really a sentence, playing a character called Elvis to rooms of people who'd come to see the character, not the man — because by then there wasn't much man left to see. They'd worn him down to the costume. And the Cult were in the Colonel's ear before I ever hit the ground, because a man who'll cage a king for a passport is a man who's already ninety per cent theirs.

And I came to this one further along in myself than the last, so let me flag it. Nine doors I'd been a watcher, and one door I'd started, badly, to aim. By Memphis I'd got a dangerous idea in my head — that if I was awake now, and I understood the machine now, maybe I could *do* something. Reach in. Change a note. I was wrong, and this door's where I found out exactly how wrong, and it cost a woman made of light a piece of herself to teach me. But I didn't know that walking in. Walking in, I thought I was getting good at this. That's always when it takes the most off you — the moment you think you've got its number.

— · —

Door ten. Not one drug — fourteen. A whole pharmacy, prescribed, signed for, delivered: codeine and Quaaludes and a fistful of things ending in -barbital, the polypharmacy death, the kind a doctor writes out in a steady hand and calls medicine, the kind where the King's own physician had scripted him something like ten thousand doses in the last year of his life and never once, apparently, thought to add them up. That's the modern way to kill a man you love and profit from: not a needle in an alley, but a prescription pad and a respectful silence. I mixed it in the between, and I said sorry to a boy from Tupelo, and the gold records came off the walls of the world.

The Cathedral of Wires met me as a hall of gold records this door — thousands of them, stacked to the vault, each one a framed and certified sale, and behind every glinting disc a man playing the same song for the ten-thousandth time with the light going out of his eyes. A cathedral of achievement that was really a cathedral of repetition, the sacred turned to product turned to muscle-memory, and I understood in the between that the machine's idea of heaven for an artist is exactly that: to have made one thing people loved, and then be made to do it, and only it, until you die. And then the smell of it — sweat and hairspray and fried food and the particular chemical chill of a Vegas air-conditioning unit fighting the desert — and the roar of a showroom, and the boards under me.

— · —

I came up at the side of a stage the size of a car park, in the wings, in a forest of cables and quick-change rails, and out there under the lights was the King himself — the real one, this door, alive, huge, sequinned, sweating through a cape, halfway through a song he'd sung a thousand times, and you could see it, even from the wings, even at that distance: the man was somewhere else. The voice was doing the work on its own, muscle and memory, while whatever was left of the actual person stood a little behind his own eyes, watching his hands do the karate chops, a passenger in his own show. And the crowd loved it, roared for it, because the crowd hadn't come for a man. They'd come for an Elvis. And he was giving them a very good one.

I stood in those wings a while, before any of it, and I watched the crowd more than the King — because the crowd's where the beige actually lives. They *knew*. That's the thing that undid me. They weren't fooled; they could see he was heavy and slow and gone behind the eyes, everybody could — and they didn't want him fixed. They wanted the show. They'd paid for the cape and the sweat and the old moves, and a well man, a growing man, a man doing difficult new songs, would've been a disappointment. The tribute act was the product they'd come for, and the fact that it was being performed by the original, dying, wasn't a tragedy to them — it was authenticity. They were watching a man impersonate himself to death and calling it the real thing, and they were right, that's the horror of it, by then it *was* the real thing. He'd become his own covers band, and the covers band sold out every night.

And she was three feet from me, in the backup line, in a white jumpsuit like the rest of them, a gospel singer's grip on a harmony — but the eyes over the fixed smile were the two white moons, and the hair under the stage wig had the violet in the black, and she was watching me the way she always watched me, from everywhere at once. Her name on the call sheet that door was Della. She had the best voice on that stage, and nobody in the building knew her name, which was exactly how she liked it. Too particular to sell. She'd hidden herself in the one place the machine never looks: the chorus.

"Don't. I can see it on you, love. You've got a plan. You're going to try and fix it — warn him, or slip him something, or knock the pills out of a doctor's bag, some brave stupid thing. You can't. I need you to hear me before you break your own heart on it. The record's already pressed. You're standing inside a night that already happened. You can walk through it, you can grieve in it, but you cannot change one groove of it, and if you try — if you throw yourself against a thing that's already been — it's not him you'll hurt. It's the two of us. Every push against the pressed track costs, and I'm the one the bill comes to."

But I didn't listen. That's the whole of door ten, and I'll not dress it up: she told me plain, and I didn't listen, because I thought being awake was the same as being able. So when the King came off for a costume change, swaying, grey under the tan, and his doctor moved in with the little paper cup, I stepped out of the wings. Dead man in black clothes in a room of sequins, and I put my hand on that doctor's arm, and I said — I don't even remember what I said. Something true. Something useless. *He doesn't need it. Let him breathe. You're killing him by the dose.*

And the doctor looked straight through me, because I wasn't there, not to him — I was a draught, a bad feeling, a thing you shrug off. And the cup went down the King's throat the way it always had, the way it always would, because it already had, forty years and a whole mythology ago. And behind me, in the chorus line, I heard her gasp — a small sound, wrong, and I turned and Della was gripping the shoulder of the singer next to her, white-knuckled, and for half a second, under the stage lights, she *flickered* — thin, stuttering, the code coming up fast and wrong under the jumpsuit — because I'd pushed against the record, and the world had balanced it, and it had taken the payment out of her.

— · —

"I told you. Don't you ever — don't do that again. Not for him, not for anyone, not until you understand what it costs. You want to save somebody, Zero? There's exactly one person on the whole rack you're allowed to save, and it isn't a dead king, and it isn't a stranger, and God help me it isn't even you. But you're not ready to know who it is. So put your hand back in your pocket, and watch, and learn the one lesson this door has got, which is that the wanting to fix it is good, it's the best thing in you, and it is exactly the handle they'll use to move you, if you let it out before you've learned to aim. Now go and watch the carny sell the ghost. And I'll be all right. I'm always all right. You know the drill."

And for the ones keeping the physics — because this is the door where I tested it and lost — the reason you can't change a pressed record isn't a rule somebody made up to spoil your fun. It's the Balancing, the same law the swindler broke himself on in Paris. The night already happened. It has a weight, a settled place in the rack. And when you shove against it — try to make a thing that *was* into a thing that *wasn't* — the universe doesn't argue, it just quietly finds the nearest loose thing and spends it to keep the books level. And the nearest loose thing, when I push, is always her — because she and I are tangled, tuned together, and she's the one travelling light enough to be cheap to spend. That's the trap folded into my own good intentions: my mercy has a price, and she's the one the bill comes to. Learn that early, if you ever get powers. The reach is never free, and it's rarely you who's billed.

— · —

So I went, with my hand back in my pocket and my heart in bits, and I watched the Colonel work. He was in the back office of the showroom with the two clean ones, and the King wasn't even cold yet in the storyline of the night, and they were already three moves ahead.

"Now don't look at me like I don't grieve. I grieve. That boy was the best thing ever happened to me, God's honest. But grieving's for after, and business is for now, and the two don't mix, son, they never have. Here's the beauty of it — and it is a beauty, you'll see it in a minute. A live act, you gotta feed. Hotels, band, jumpsuits, the doctor, the whole circus, and he was eating the profits faster than he made 'em there at the end. But a *dead* act? A dead act's got no overhead. Doesn't drink, doesn't bloat, doesn't miss a show. You put him in the ground and the estate's worth ten times the man ever was, because now you can sell him forever and he can't spend a dime of it or embarrass you once. I've been doing this a long time. Dead Elvis is the best client I ever had."

"You understand it better than anyone alive. Most men in your trade mourn the artist and mismanage the estate. You've never once made that mistake. You saw, from the very beginning, that the man was only ever the mine, and the myth was the ore."

"License the silhouette. The sunglasses. The lip. Put him on a stamp, a slot machine, a plate. And the impersonators — do not fight the impersonators, *charter* them. A thousand men in a thousand capes, in a thousand chapels, keeping the brand warm, paying you a fee to be him. You'll have built the first perpetual-motion machine in the history of fame: a dead man, working, forever, in ten thousand bodies, and every one of them wiring you a cheque. It would be almost a crime *not* to."

And I watched the delight go into the Colonel, and it wasn't even greed by then, it was *craft*, the pure pleasure of a carny who's found the last, best, most inexhaustible mark: a corpse that sells. He did all of it. Every word they said, he did, and it worked, and the estate outgrew the man a hundredfold, and somewhere out there right now, tonight, as I tell you this, a man in a cape is being the King for a wedding, and a fraction of a cent of it is still, somehow, running downhill to the ghost of a carny who never had a passport. The Cult never touched the King. They just taught the man who owned him that death is a business model. The woman would call it efficient.

— · —

So here's the moral, and it's the one that named the whole disease for me, so I'll take it slow. I'd been calling it beige for ten doors — the flattening, the smoothing, the turning of a wild thing into a safe one. But I'd been thinking of beige as an ending. A death. Silence. And standing in that showroom, watching a living man do a flawless impression of himself for a room that preferred the impression, I finally understood that beige isn't the silence *after*. Beige is the *performance* that replaces you while you're still breathing. It's the day the machine has so completely turned you into your own product that you spend the rest of your life doing a tribute act of the person you used to be — the same song, the same cape, the same swivel, the light gone out behind the eyes, the crowd happier than ever because a tribute act never disappoints, never grows, never bites. The King didn't die on that bathroom floor. The King died the first night he walked out and *did Elvis* instead of being him, and the body just took a few more years to get the memo.

And it isn't only kings it does this to — that's what I truly carried out of Memphis. The tribute-act thing, the doing-an-impression-of-yourself thing: the machine learned it on the famous first and rolled it out to the rest of us later, same as it does everything. Give it thirty years and it'd have every ordinary soul on earth doing a tribute act of themselves all day on the little screens — a curated, brand-safe, best-of version of a human being, the same poses, the same lines, the light going quietly out behind a billion sets of eyes, performing "me" for an audience instead of being anyone at all. The King was just the first to get the full treatment, because he was the first product built to that scale. He was the prototype. We're all in the residency now. We all do two shows a night.

And here's the sting, and it's on me, because this door I'd finally done more than watch — I'd *reached*, and got a woman I love hurt for it. So I had to ask the hard one. What am I, doing this? I go to the last nights of the beautiful dead, and I take my notes, and I do my bit — the intro, the voice, the moral, the same swivel every episode. Am I a witness? Or am I an impersonator of a witness, a tribute act to my own conscience, doing a very good *caring* for an audience, night after night, while the light goes out behind my eyes too? I don't know. I still don't. But I know that she flickered because I couldn't keep my hand in my pocket, and I know that the wanting-to-help is the last real thing I've got, and I know the machine knows it, and wants it, and is waiting for me to spend it stupid. Aiming, it turns out, is mostly learning when *not* to fire.

— · —

She'd left me something, even this door, even after I'd hurt her with my good intentions. When I checked my pocket on the way out there was a guitar string — a single one, wound and worn, coiled small and warm, off a real guitar somebody had actually played till it frayed. No note. It didn't need one. A worn string is a thing that got used, hard, by a hand that meant it, right up until it broke — and that's the opposite of a tribute act, the exact opposite, the realest little object in a town built on fakes. She was telling me, I think, to stay a played string. To fray. To break, if it came to it, from use and not from repetition. To be a man and not a monument.

Door ten folded shut behind me, and I did not think I was getting good at this any more. I thought I was a man who'd been handed a lesson at somebody else's cost, which is the only way I ever seem to learn one. Fourteen doors left, and one of them sealed, and one of them, still, the one where she flickers and there's nothing to hold — and I'd just been shown, in a Vegas showroom, that I could make that day come *sooner* if I couldn't learn to hold still. So I'd learn to hold still. God help me, I'd try. Somewhere up the rack a kind man was reaching for his lighter, and a girl made of light had a played guitar string's worth less of herself because of me, and I put my hand in my pocket, round the string, and I swore I'd earn it. I'm still trying. It's act two. Earning it is the whole of the job.
