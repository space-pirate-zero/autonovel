# Episode 5 — The Marilyn Door

*The Maneki Neko Death Cult*

---

Fifth door. And this is the one where the Cult scared me for the first time — not because they were cruel, but because, for one long ugly hour, I agreed with them. Los Angeles. Nineteen sixty-two. The dream factory at the exact height of its powers, a machine that took frightened girls off buses and turned them into gods and then billed the gods for the manufacture. And the god it broke best, the brightest and the saddest, went out on a Sunday in August in a little house in Brentwood with a bottle of yellow pills and a telephone in her hand.

There was a man who ran that factory who had never in his life heard the word *no* and lived. Two dead-eyed aristocrats at his elbow, whispering him toward the edge of his own machine. And a contract girl with white eyes and violet in her platinum, standing on a soundstage under a light designed to erase her, waiting for a dead man to fall out of the sky and see her. That man was me. This is the door marked Marilyn. And it's the door where I nearly became one of them. Buckle up.

— · —

Submitted for your approval: a factory that manufactures dreams and disposes of the dreamers. Hollywood, nineteen sixty-two. Understand what a studio was in those years, because they've spent every year since making sure you forget. It wasn't a company that made films. It was a machine that made *people* — that bought a scared kid's contract for seven years, changed her name, capped her teeth, dyed her hair, taught her how to walk and laugh and want, and then owned every second of the result, body and face and voice, like a farmer owns a field.

Seven years. I want you to feel the length of that. Seven years in which they owned your name — the one they'd given you, having binned the one your mother chose — and your face, and your hair, and the twenty pounds they told you to lose, and the smile they told you to fix, and, criminally, your *time*: every hour of it, loaned out to whatever picture they pleased. And if you turned down a part you thought was beneath you, they didn't fire you. That would have been mercy. They *suspended* you. Stopped your pay, froze the clock, and stitched the frozen months back onto the far end of the seven years — so the sentence only ever got longer the more spirit you showed. A prison that punished you for being worth imprisoning.

And to keep the product running they kept it medicated, which is the polite word for it. There was always a studio doctor — a kindly man, a black bag, and no scruples worth the name — and he had a pill to wake you for the five a.m. call, and a pill to carry you through the afternoon, and a pill to bring you down off the working pills at night, and a pill for the nerves the other pills had given you. By the time a girl had been a star five years she was a pharmacy with a hairdo, chemically incapable of a single unmanaged hour. And they didn't do it to hurt her. That's the part you have to swallow whole about the machine — nobody in it was ever trying to hurt anybody. They were trying to protect the *schedule*. The girl was just the soft thing the schedule got printed on.

And here's the beige of it, the purest strain of beige I ever saw, so drink it in: the whole genius of the machine was *sameness*. It took a thousand specific, particular, unrepeatable girls and it ran them through the same mill and it made them all into the same handful of interchangeable dreams, because a dream you can predict is a dream you can price, and a dream you can price is a dream you can sell twice on a Saturday. They weren't making stars. They were making a product that happened to have a pulse, and the pulse was the part they found most inconvenient.

And nineteen sixty-two is the hinge of the whole thing — she died right on it. Behind her, the old machine, the seven-year contracts, the studio system that owned people outright, cracking apart at last under its own weight. Ahead of it, the new machine — the agents, the deals, the freedom that would turn out to be its own kind of cage — not yet built. She died in the gap between two machines, which is the most dangerous place in the world a person can stand, because in the gap you have, for one terrifying moment, no owner — and a machine can forgive you anything except being briefly unowned. They don't kill you for it. They just stop catching you. And it turns out all those years you thought they were holding you up, they were only holding you *in*, and the day they let go you find out there was never any floor.

And the man who ran the machine, this door, was a mogul. A studio boss of the old school, a little emperor in a big office, a man who'd started with nothing and built a kingdom and somewhere along the way stopped being able to tell the difference between the two. And the Cult were in his private dining room, cutting his steak, before I ever hit the ground.

And if you've come in late — if you don't yet know why a dead man is narrating a Sunday in sixty-two he wasn't alive for — here it is, fast, and then I'll never mention it again this episode. I died in a club in Atlanta in two thousand and three, too much of the wrong thing, and I didn't stop. I jumped. Up a stack of lives like a needle up a jukebox, from one overdose to the next, always forward, never back, arriving in each new body in the exact chemical shape it died in. *Signal finds signal.* Overdose to overdose. There are two clocks — the one on the wall that says nineteen sixty-two, and the one behind my eyes that's just been counting, door after door, since the floor of that club — and I am always, always older than the room. And the thing that arranges the jumps, that waves me in like a cat in a shop window, is the Maneki Neko Death Cult, and it has never once needed a weapon, because everything it wants it gets by agreeing with you at exactly the wrong moment.

— · —

But I'm getting ahead of the needle. Let me drop it back. Door five. Nembutal. Barbiturates, a whole bottle of them, in a body already worn to paper by a decade of the studio doctor's little helpers — uppers to work, downers to sleep, and a machine that ran on both and called it care. I want to hold this one right, because the world has spent sixty years turning her into a poster and a punchline, and she was neither. She was a genuinely gifted comedienne and a genuinely wounded woman who read Dostoevsky between takes and wanted, more than the fame, more than the money, just to be taken *seriously* by a machine that had decided her seriousness was bad for the brand. I mixed it in the Europa bathroom and I said sorry to a girl born Norma Jeane, and the most-bombed hotel in the world came apart around me.

The mixing's a matching, if you're new to me — you have to make your borrowed body into the shape the next one died in, and this one died of Nembutal, so on a cold bathroom floor in Belfast I counted out a handful of yellow and became the right kind of dying, and the tuning fork went off behind my teeth and the tiles went to water. And I'll tell you what I thought about, going under, because it matters for this door. I thought about the name. Norma Jeane. There was a real, particular, frightened, funny girl called Norma Jeane, and the machine took her and built a goddess called Marilyn on top of her like a set built over a graveyard, and then spent twenty years insisting the graveyard wasn't there. And the awful joke, the joke that finally killed her, is that it was Norma Jeane who did all the acting, and Marilyn who took all the credit, and neither of them, in the end, was ever once allowed to just be a person.

The between was all mirror, this door. The Cathedral of Wires does that — it reads the room you're falling toward and dresses itself to match — and for the dream factory it built me a cathedral out of dressing-room mirrors, ten thousand of them, each framed in those cruel little bulbs, and in every single mirror a different girl leaning in to paint on a face she'd been told was more bankable than her own. A choir of reflections, all of them somebody's Norma Jeane, all of them being slowly turned into the same three saleable dreams. It lasted a heartbeat or a hundred years, no way to tell which. And then the mirrors went to smoke, and there were cables under my feet, and one blazing island of light in a sea of patient dark.

— · —

I came up on a soundstage at two in the morning, the way they were always lit — a cathedral of black space with one blazing island of light in the middle of it, and cables like vines, and forty exhausted people stood in the dark keeping absolutely silent so that one manufactured goddess could stand in the burning centre and pretend to be happy. And she was on the edge of the light. A contract girl, waiting for her mark, platinum and painted, and the violet still somehow in the roots of the black, and the eyes — those two white moons — the only thing in the building the make-up department hadn't been allowed to fix.

Forty people in that dark, and I counted them while she waited for her mark. Grips and sparks and a script girl and a man whose entire job was to watch the continuity of a lit cigarette. Forty skilled, tired, decent people — and not one of them cruel, and not one of them free — arranged in a perfect silent circle around the one burning spot where the product got manufactured. That's the picture I can never quite put down. Not a monster with a whip. A room full of kind professionals, all facing inward, all keeping quiet, so the light could do its work. That's how the beige gets made. Not by villains. By good people, being quiet, in a circle, so the schedule can be kept.

"Careful of the light, love. It's not there to show you. It's there to burn the *you* off you, till there's only the face left. They've a light like it in every factory the machine ever built. You'll learn to walk around the edge of it. That's the whole trick of surviving one of these places — stay just out the reach of the thing that wants to make you shine."

"Do I know you," I said. And she gave me the look — the tired, tender, forgiving look, the one that told me she'd answered this exact question a hundred times and would answer it a hundred more, because that was the shape of loving a man who kept arriving before he'd learned her name.

"You will. You do. You did. All three, love, all at once — that's how it is for me, you'll get used to it or you won't. Now go and watch. There's a man about to be eaten by his own machine, and you need to see it, because the day's coming when you'll have to decide whether the machine eating a monster is the same as justice. It isn't. But you'll want it to be. Go on."

"How do you do it," I asked her. "Stay off the reach of it. Everyone else in this room's already half burned."

"By being too specific to sell, love. The light can only catch the smooth parts — the parts that match the poster. So you keep the odd bits. The laugh that's too loud. The book nobody your kind's meant to read. The one crooked tooth you won't let them cap. You stay *particular*. And a thing that's truly particular can't be mass-produced, and a thing that can't be mass-produced is no use to a factory, and being no use to the factory — she smiled — is the only freedom anybody's ever smuggled out of one of these towns alive."

"And listen. This thing you're about to feel, watching that man get eaten — you're going to like it. I'll not tell you not to. I'm just going to stand here while it passes, so that after — when the clean one puts her mouth to your ear and tells you you're one of them now — there's a person in the room who still knows that you're not. That's my whole job, some doors, love. Not to save you. Just to witness that you were something better right up to the second you weren't, so there's a record of it somewhere. Somebody has to keep the record."

So I went and watched. And here's where it got frightening.

— · —

The mogul was a genuine monster. I'll not soften it. He'd spent forty years treating human beings as inventory — breaking the ones who wouldn't sign, blacklisting the ones who talked, keeping a stable of frightened kids on contracts that owned everything about them but the fillings in their teeth, and helping himself to whatever he wanted from any of them, because who were they going to tell, and what with, when he owned the newspapers too. He was the machine made flesh. And the two clean ones stood at his shoulder and whispered.

"I made this town out of orange groves and other men's daughters. I have signed the cheque for every dream you've ever had in the dark. And I have never once — not one single time in forty years — been *told* what to do inside my own gates. Now they want a union. They want a *committee*. In my studio. Boys, when I came up, a man who forgot who owned him got reminded. Loudly. And the whole street learned the lesson off his back. Perhaps this town's gone and forgotten there's a cost to forgetting me."

"You built this town. And now a pack of agents and unions and *actors* think they can tell you how to run it. You've been too patient. A man of your stature does not negotiate with the help."

"Make an example. The biggest one you have. The girl who's been late, who's been difficult, who's been *seen* by too many important people. Fire her. Publicly. Ruin her. Remind this town whose town it is. They'll never forget it — and they'll never forgive you — and forgiveness was always going to be beneath you anyway."

And notice the shape of it, because it was new. In the doors before, the whisper had always been *more* — more genius, more money, more of the beautiful thing you already had too much of. Reach further. Want bigger. This was the first door where the whisper was the other one, the darker one, the one I'd learn they keep especially for the powerful: not *reach*, but *punish*. Not your hunger they fed, but your fear. And a monster's fear is an enormous thing, because a monster knows better than anybody alive exactly what the machine does to soft things, having fed it so many of them himself. So when they told him the way to be safe forever was to make an example of the softest thing he owned, it slid into him like a key into a lock that had been cut for it forty years before.

And I watched the cruelty go into him like it had found a home it recognised. Same whisper, and yet not — because this was the first door where the whisper wasn't *reach for more*, it was *punish*. And it worked, because the fear in a monster is the same fear that's in everyone: that one day the machine will notice you're just a person too, and grind you like it grinds the rest. So he did it. He made his example.

And here's the part that put the cold in me. Watching that man — that genuine, unrepentant monster — get whispered toward his own ruin, I felt something I hadn't felt in the earlier doors. I felt *glad*. I wanted it. I stood in the dark at the edge of the burning light and I thought: good. Do it. Feed him to the thing he built. Let it hurt.

And a very quiet, very clean voice, right at my ear, said —

"There. Now you understand us. It's not so complicated, is it. It's only justice — when it's aimed at someone you've decided deserves it. We simply never let ourselves decide."

— · —

And the mogul's ruin came, the way it always comes when they overreach — the example he made was one cruelty too many, and the town that feared him found, all at once, that it hated him more, and the agents and the unions and the actors he'd sneered at turned out to be the future and he turned out to be the past, and they took the kingdom off him piece by piece and left him alive in a big house to watch it happen, which is the version of the ending the woman calls efficient. He got the Lux ending. The beige one. The man who'd turned a thousand people into inventory became, himself, obsolete stock.

The god died too, by the way. The real one. The girl on the edge of the light. Not by his hand — the machine doesn't work by hands, I keep telling you — but by the whole weight of it, the pills and the doctors and the decade of being owned, on a Sunday in Brentwood with a telephone in her hand and nobody, in the end, on the other end of it. The monster and the angel, the same August, the same machine. It doesn't sort by innocence. It never did.

— · —

So here's the moral, and it's the one that cost me the most to learn, so take it careful. That night I found out that the thing I'd been travelling with — the Cult, the whisper, the patient culling of the cruel — is *seductive*. Because it feels like justice. Because sometimes the man it eats really is a monster, and standing in the dark wanting the monster eaten feels righteous, feels clean, feels like being on the right side at last.

Here's the mechanism of the trap, laid out flat, because it's the most important thing I'll say all season. The Cult do not recruit. They don't hand you a robe and a pamphlet and a mug. All they ever need is for you to sign off — once, in your heart, in the dark, where you think nobody's counting — on a single death you've decided was deserved. That's it. That's the whole of the initiation. Because the instant you do, you've conceded the only point that ever mattered: that there's a list, and that people go on it, and that the only real question is who gets to hold the pen. And once you've agreed there's a list, you're not fighting the machine anymore. You're just haggling with it over names. And haggling with the machine over names, patiently, in a good suit, forever — that *is* the machine. That's the entire job. I'd been one whisper away from applying for it and calling it a conscience.

And the reason it frightened me worse than any monster had — I'd been dead four doors by then, and I'd started, somewhere I hadn't been watching, to enjoy the view. The distance of it. Watching from the dark, knowing the ending, feeling myself become a man who *understood* the machine instead of a man it was still able to hurt. That's the first step into the good shoes, that little cool distance, that connoisseur's eye for a well-run ruin. I'd been growing it without noticing. And the clean voice at my ear that night wasn't recruiting me. It was *congratulating* me. On how far along I already was.

And it's a trap. It's the deepest trap in the whole rack. Because the second you let yourself cheer the machine for eating the right man, you've agreed to the machine. You've agreed that some people are inventory and the only question left is which ones. The Cult don't need you to join them. They just need you to *approve*, once, of the right death — and after that you're theirs, and you'll spend the rest of your doors quietly sorting the living into the ones who deserve the whisper and the ones who don't, which is exactly, precisely, the job they do, in the good shoes, forever. The mogul was a monster. The machine that ate him was worse, because the machine was still hungry in the morning. That's the beige. Not the villain. The appetite.

The mogul was a monster; I'll say it a third time so nobody writes in. He earned every inch of his ruin twice over. But the machine that ate him didn't eat him *because* he was a monster. It ate him because he'd become inefficient, and it was hungry, and he happened to be standing there — and it would have eaten a saint on the same Tuesday, with the same appetite, and filed them both under *restructuring*. That's the horror I carried out of door five and never quite set down. Evil, at least, has tastes. It wants specific things; it can be reasoned with, bribed, resisted. The machine has no tastes at all. It only has an appetite. And an appetite doesn't hate you. It doesn't even notice you. It just gets to you, eventually, in the order you happened to be standing in.

— · —

She'd left me something. When I came back up there was a single false eyelash in my pocket, absurd and delicate and warm, and folded inside it a scrap of studio letterhead with three words in violet ink: *stay off-camera*. And I've thought about those three words more than almost anything she ever gave me. Because that's the whole survival guide, isn't it, for a person trying to keep a soul in a world of machines. Stay off-camera. Stay just out the reach of the light that wants to burn the you off you. Don't let them frame you. Don't let them price you. Be too particular to sell.

Door five folded shut behind me. A record I'll never play again. Nineteen to go. And somewhere up the rack a girl was teaching me to walk around the edge of the light, and a kind man was reaching for his lighter, and I'd just found out the most dangerous thing about myself — that some nights, God help me, I wanted to be one of the monsters in the good shoes. And the wanting was the whole of the danger.
