# Episode 4 — The Hendrix Door

*The Maneki Neko Death Cult*

---

Fourth door. And this one taught me the difference between the men who kill the music and the men who kill the musician, which turns out to be no difference at all, only a nicer coat. London. Nineteen seventy. The winter after the summer of love, when the flowers had all been sold and the bill came due, and the greatest pair of hands the guitar ever grew went quiet in a basement flat in a city that had already started to monetise his ghost.

There was a man in Mayfair turning genius into money and money into more money and never once making a sound himself. There were two dead-eyed aristocrats at his shoulder, whispering him toward a scheme that would choke him. And there was a girl in a gallery with white eyes and violet in her hair, standing in front of a painting she could see was a lie, waiting for a man she hadn't met to fall out of the sky. That man was me. This is the door marked Hendrix. Buckle up.

— · —

Submitted for your approval: a city with a hangover the size of an empire. London, the tail end of nineteen seventy. For about four years the whole of Britain had convinced itself it had invented the future — the music, the clothes, the idea that a kid from a council flat might get to be a king for the length of a single. And then the calendar turned, and the future turned out to have a landlord, and the landlord wanted his rent in full.

You have to understand what a comedown looks like at the scale of a nation. It doesn't look like sadness. It looks like accountancy. All those beautiful long-haired boys who'd been told they were the revolution woke up one grey morning and found that the revolution had been incorporated, floated, and sold back to them at a markup, and that somebody they'd never met owned the word for what they were. The dream didn't die — that's the part nobody warns you about. The dream got *bought*. And a bought dream keeps right on smiling from the poster long after the eyes behind it have gone out.

And I'd had a front-row seat to how they do it, because I'd watched the exact same trick pulled on a whole genre of sound in a door or two behind me. It works like this. You find the wildest, most uncontainable thing a human being can do — a voice, a riff, a body that moves like it's arguing with gravity — and the first thing you do is not enjoy it. The first thing you do is *sign* it. You get it on paper. You get a percentage of it, then a bigger percentage, then the name of it, then the *masters* of it — the actual physical thing the light got pressed into — and then, quietly, you own the light. And the beautiful part, from the machine's side of the desk, is that the kid signs gladly. Grinning. Because you've come to him at the one moment in his whole life when somebody finally said the word *yes* to him, and a starving man will sign anything that's handed to him with a smile and a pen still warm from the last one.

And nowhere did the comedown taste more expensive than in Mayfair, where the money had never once believed in any of it, and had spent the whole decade quietly buying up the paintings while the children danced. Because here's a thing about real art, and I'd watched it in two doors already so I knew the shape of it by now: the surest way to launder a dirty pound is to spend it on a beautiful thing, hang the beautiful thing on a wall, and let the beautiful thing do your lying for you. Art doesn't ask where you're from. That's what the money loves about it.

Now — if you've just tuned in, if some algorithm coughed you up onto this particular frequency and you don't yet know who's talking, here's the shape of it, quick, because I hate a recap as much as you do. I'm dead. Have been for a while, in the way that matters least. I took too much of the wrong thing in a club in Atlanta in two thousand and three, and instead of ending I *jumped* — sideways, up the rack, into another body in another year that had also just been switched off the same way. That's the rule of the box I'm in. You don't rewind. You can't go home. You only ever move forward through the machine, one dead genius at a time, arriving the way they left. A door is a death. And every one of them is a stop on somebody else's line.

And I know how that sounds. Believe me. First few doors I was sure I'd just lost the last of my mind in the usual way and this was the light show on the way out. But it holds together, if you let it. Picture time not as a river but as a jukebox — a wall of records, every one of them a life, already pressed, already finished, just waiting on a needle. You don't *change* the record. You can't. You only get to choose, or have chosen for you, which one drops next. And the needle only ever moves forward across the stack. That's the one law with no exception in the whole rotten machine: no going back. Whatever you did, wherever you left somebody standing, it's cut into the vinyl now, and the vinyl does not un-press.

There's two clocks running, is the thing nobody tells you. There's the clock on the wall — nineteen seventy, tick, tick, the wine going warm in the glass. And there's *my* clock, the one only I can read, that's been counting up since a club floor in Atlanta and doesn't care in the slightest what year the wall says. I'm always older than the room. I'm always the only person in the building who's already seen how it ends. And when the two clocks disagree hard enough — when I want a thing badly enough to argue with the record that's already been pressed — that's when the machine notices, and that's when the cat starts to wave. The Maneki Neko. The little raised paw in the window that says *come in, come in.* Because the surest way to walk a man exactly where the vinyl already says he'll walk is to convince him it was his own brave idea.

They call the between the Cathedral of Wires, and I'll get you there in a minute. They call the smear I move through the Fog, capital F, and it isn't weather — it's *me*, the blur I leave because I'm living my own story out of order, meeting the same people at the wrong ends. And *they* — the Maneki Neko Death Cult, the beckoning cat — they're the ones who arrange the doors. Not with a gun. Never once with a gun. With a whisper. Hold on to that. The whole of this show is a story about how the worst thing that ever happened to you probably arrived dressed as a compliment.

— · —

But I'm getting ahead of the needle. Let me drop it back. Door four. Wine and Vesparax — red wine and German sleeping pills, nine of them, about eighteen times a safe dose, taken by a man so tired of being a product that he forgot he was also a body. I want to hold this one right. This wasn't a man chasing oblivion. This was the most gifted human being of his entire generation, so ground down by managers and contracts and being *owned* that he took a fistful of somebody else's sleeping pills in a stranger's flat and never woke, and choked, quiet, in the dark, at twenty-seven. The saddest door in the box is always the one where the genius didn't even mean it. He just wanted to sleep. I mixed it in the Europa bathroom and I said sorry to a man named James Marshall, and the most-bombed hotel in the world came apart around me.

I should say how it works, the mixing, because people write in and ask. It's not magic, and it's not just a needle in the arm. It's a *matching*. Every door in the box is a death by the same door — an overdose, a body switched off by too much of the thing it loved. And to get from one to the next you have to re-key the lock: put your own borrowed body into the exact chemical shape the next one died in. Wine and nine Vesparax, this time. So I sat on a cold bathroom floor in the Europa in Belfast and I did the sum on a stranger's death and I made myself into the right kind of dying, and the tuning-fork hum started up behind my teeth, and the tiles went soft under me, and the signal found the signal. That's the phrase for the whole business. *Signal finds signal.* Like tunes to like. Overdose to overdose, all the way up the stack, and me the poor bent wire the current runs down.

The Cathedral of Wires had a soundtrack this time. That's the only door it ever did. As I came through the between, as the broken moons turned and the sermon dropped its static, I could hear a guitar — not a record of one, but the thing itself, somebody up the rack bending a note so far it stopped being music and became weather, and I understood for one slowed second exactly what the machine had been so desperate to own, and exactly why it could never, ever make it itself. And then the boards under me, and the smell of turpentine and cold champagne and money.

People ask me what the between actually *is*, and the honest answer is I don't have the words, only the picture. Picture a cathedral built by something that had the concept of a cathedral described to it once, over a bad phone line, and had to reconstruct the holiness out of copper and dial-tone. Vaulted static. Stained glass made of test-patterns. A choir that's really just every note that was ever cut off too soon, held forever in the wiring, still reaching for the end of the phrase. You pass through it every time you die into the next one. It lasts four seconds or a small eternity, no way to tell which, and you come out the far side knowing one more true thing than you went in with and never once able to say what it was. This time the true thing had six strings. This time it *sang* at me. And I have never been more certain that some things are not for sale — nor more certain that the machine will die trying to sell them anyway.

— · —

I came up in a gallery. White walls, black frames, a room built to make you whisper. And she was in it. Of course she was. A London girl this time — a model, they'd have called her, a face the scene passed around — in a silver dress, henna gone, but the hair still had the violet in the black and the eyes were still those two white moons, pointed at a painting on the wall and through it and past it at me. Somebody's arm candy at a private view, and she was the only living thing in a room full of expensive corpses.

"It's a fake, you know. The painting. Lovely, isn't it. Most of the beautiful things in this room are fakes. That's rather the point of the room."

"Do I know you," I said. It's the question I always asked her in the Fog, the stupid one, and every time it cost me something to watch it land.

"Not yet. You keep asking me that, in these early ones, and I keep forgiving you for it, because you can't help the order we come in. You're moving through me backwards, love. To me this is an old song. To you it's the first time you've heard it. So I'll teach you the words slowly, and you'll teach them to me quickly, and somewhere in the middle we'll both know the whole thing, and that'll be the day I've been dreading and living for since before you were born."

I want to stop the record here a second, because you deserve to know who she is before you fall for her the way I did — out of sequence and against my will. She isn't a victim and she isn't a predator. In a show full of both, she's the third thing. She's a *witness*. She rides the same rack I do, only she's awake for all of it, and she's travelling the other way down the line — so every time I meet her I know a little less and she knows a little more, and she has to sit there and love a man who forgets a fraction more of her each time he sees her. That's the cruellest seat in the house. And she took it on purpose. Ask me later why. I still don't entirely know, and the not-knowing is most of what I've got left of her.

"You're doing the thing again. Where you narrate me. It's all right. I like being the part of the show you can't quite explain. Only — when you go off to watch the sad rich man dig his hole, remember it isn't him you came here for. It never is. You came for the plectrum. You just don't know it yet."

"How many," I asked her. It's the other question, the one that lives underneath the stupid one. How many doors. How many of these do we get.

"More than you'd want, and fewer than I'd beg for. Don't do that, love — don't start counting. The counting is how they get you, in the end. The machine adores a man keeping score, because a man keeping score can always be sold one more. Just — be here. In the ugly gallery, with the fake on the wall and me in the borrowed dress. This one's real, even if the painting isn't. That's the whole trick I keep trying to teach you, backwards, one door at a time: how to know a real thing while you're still standing inside it — instead of only ever after, in the reverb."

And she reached up and fixed my collar, the way you'd straighten the collar of a man going in to do something stupid, which I was, and her hand was warm — warm the way her things were always warm — and for one second the whole cold beautiful lying room went quiet and told the truth.

I didn't understand her. I never did, in the Fog. But I understood the room, because the room was the same con I'd watched in a ballroom and a screw session, just dressed for a colder climate. So I went and watched the rich man dig his hole.

— · —

The dealer was in his back office with the good brandy and the two clean ones, and he was in the middle of the con of his life. He'd got hold of a dead painter's estate — a real genius, dead broke and dead young, the way the machine likes them — and he'd worked out that a dead man can't tell you how many paintings he made. So the plan, the beautiful, greedy plan, was to have a few more of them made. Quietly. By a clever forger in a cold studio in Notting Hill. And to sell the dead man's genius by the inch, forever — an inexhaustible supply of the one thing that was supposed to be the rarest thing there is.

"It isn't forgery. I do wish people would stop reaching for that ugly little word. The man is dead. His *vision* is not. I am merely... completing the catalogue. Finishing the sentences he'd have finished himself, had the world been a shade kinder to his liver. Call it a continuation. Call it a resurrection, if you've the stomach for the word. The market calls it the finest thing it has seen in a decade — and the market, gentlemen, does not lie."

"And the forger — *my* forger — he's better than the dead man ever was, if we're honest, and we're never honest, that's the trade. He's got the eye and none of the ego. He can be a starving Montmartre ghost before lunch and my dead young genius by tea, and he signs none of them, which is rather the point. A signature is only a confession with good handwriting. No. We let the *provenance* do the signing. A letter here. A gallery stamp there. A widow who needs the money and won't ask which canvas. Paper marries paper, and the market believes paper the way a drowning man believes a plank."

"Nobody will ever know. The forger is discreet. The provenance is watertight. And the beauty of it — the real beauty — is that the paintings will be *good*. Better than good. Who is harmed, exactly, by more beauty in the world?"

"And why stop at the one dead man? You have the technique now. The market has the appetite. You could be the sole supplier of the twentieth century's finest ghosts. Think bigger. A man of your eye. It would be almost a crime *not* to."

"You see? He understands us already. It was never about the paint. It was always about the *story* the paint is wearing. And stories, unlike painters, never run out, never overdose, never disappoint you in a stranger's flat at twenty-seven. A story you can own clean, forever."

And I watched the greed go into him like brandy into blood. Same whisper. Every door, the same whisper. Not *you're a crook* — never that. Always *you're an artist, really, the truest kind, and the world owes you more than it's paying*. They found the soft, starving thing in the middle of a rich man and they fed it until it couldn't stop.

Here's what took me four doors to see clearly, so I'll hand it to you for nothing. The Cult don't deal in force. Force is loud, and force is traceable, and force leaves a body with a bruise shaped like a motive. The Cult deal in *permission*. They find the exact person who's one small yes away from ruin, and they don't push. They *agree* with the worst voice already in the room. They're the friend who says *go on, you've earned it*. The manager who says *sign here, it's only a formality*. The two beautiful people at your shoulder at the party who make the greedy thing sound like the brave thing, and the brave thing sound like the only thing. And then they step back, and they sip the champagne, and they let physics do the rest. Because they know what I know now — nobody is ever really talked into the pit. They're only ever *agreed with*, all the way down to the bottom.

And here's the part that took me the longest to swallow, so brace up for it. They aren't lying to him. That's the whole horror of the thing. Everything the two clean ones say is *true*. The forger is good. Nobody probably will know, not for years. The paintings will be beautiful. More beauty in the world, technically, yes. The Cult never once has to lie, because the pit was always dug out of true things — a true talent, a true grievance, a true hunger — and all they ever do is hold the lantern up helpfully at the very edge of it and murmur, *my, what a view.* The devil doesn't need to lie to you. He just needs to agree with you at exactly the wrong moment, and let your own true nature carry you the rest of the way down.

— · —

You know how it ends. You've heard it twice already in a different accent. He got greedy, and greed is a volume knob with no ceiling. He flooded his own market. He sold one too many miracles to one too many careful men, and careful men talk, and a chemist somewhere ran a test on a pigment that hadn't been invented when the dead genius was still alive to grind it. And the whole tower came down on him at once — the buyers, the lawyers, the taxman, the ruin. He'd built a machine to turn a dead man's air into gold, and the machine turned round and asphyxiated him with it. Choked on his own supply. The Cult never touched him. They only ever help you want. Physics — and a careful chemist — did the rest.

"Efficient."

— · —

And here's the moral, the unflattering one. I felt the spark. A parasite who'd never made a thing got buried under the weight of the things he'd faked. Grand. Justice, of a kind.

But then I made myself finish the sum. Because the dealer was never the disease. He was a symptom the machine grew on purpose — the same as the Label, the same as Lux — a middleman, an antibody in a nice suit, and the machine that made him had already grown a dozen more in a dozen mews the length of the city. They'd go on selling the dead by the inch. They always do. That's the whole of the art world, when you strip the varnish off it: a beautiful, patient engine for buying genius cheap off the living and selling it dear off the dead.

What they did to Hendrix, they'd done to every one of them. Found a boy who could do the one thing the machine can't — make weather with his hands — and they *managed* him. Contracted him. Owned his masters and his name and the light coming off him, and worked him three hundred nights a year until the only room left in the world where nobody wanted a piece of him was a stranger's dark flat with a bottle of red and somebody else's pills. They didn't kill the music. They loved it to death and billed the estate. That's beige. Not silence. Ownership. The turning of a wild, unrepeatable thing into a catalogue number that pays a dividend forever.

Three hundred nights a year. Sit in that a second. The man who could make a guitar cry like a person and scream like a jet engine and pray like a whole congregation — and they had him in a different grey city every single night, playing the hits, the *product*, until the wonder went to piecework and the miracle punched a clock. They owned his masters — the actual tapes, the light itself. They owned the name on the marquee. Some of them, by the end, in the small enough print, owned the *shape of his hands* in a photograph. And when a man is owned as completely as that, the only thing left in all the world that's still his, the last unrepossessed square inch, is the dark. Is the going-out. Is a stranger's flat and a bottle of red and a fistful of somebody else's sleep. They'd bought everything else off him. Death was the last room in the house with his own name still on the door — so of course. Of course that's the room he went and sat down in.

I keep using that word and I owe you the meaning of it, because it's the nearest thing this show has to a villain. Beige isn't grey. Grey is honest — grey is just the colour of a hard day. Beige is the colour they paint a thing *after* they've taken the life out of it, to make the corpse presentable for the lobby. Beige is a boxset of a revolution. Beige is your favourite scream, remastered, cleared for use in an advert for a bank. The Cult's whole product, door after door, is the conversion of a thing that could never happen twice into a thing that can be sold forever — and the residue that process leaves behind, the beautiful safe nothing hanging on the wall where the wild thing used to be, *that's* beige. And once you can see it you can't stop seeing it, and I'm sorry, genuinely, because now you'll see it too.

— · —

She'd left me something. Course she had. When I came back up, there was a guitar plectrum in my pocket — worn thin on one edge, warm the way her things were always warm, tortoiseshell gone soft as skin. No note on it. It didn't need one. I've carried it through more doors than I can count, and I've never once learned to play, and I keep it anyway, because it's a small hard piece of proof that the most beautiful thing a human being ever did with their hands was real, and happened, and no chemist's test and no careful lawyer can ever quite un-happen it.

Door four folded shut behind me. A record I'll never play again. Twenty to go. And somewhere up the rack a girl was teaching me a song backwards, and a kind man was reaching for his lighter, and I still hadn't learned the only chord that ever mattered — how to hold a beautiful thing without gripping it hard enough to kill it.
