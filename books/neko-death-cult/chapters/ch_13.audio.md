@NARRATOR: Submitted for your approval: a country that had just invented the modern idea of free speech and was busy inventing the modern way around it. Los Angeles, nineteen sixty-six. Understand what he was, because the machine has spent sixty years making him safe and I'll not let it. He was a stand-up comic, which undersells it the way calling the sea "some water" undersells it. He was a street-corner prophet with a microphone, a man who'd worked out that the dirty words weren't the dirty part — that the actual obscenity was the hypocrisy, the war, the way the respectable people lied, and that you could get at all of it, expose all of it, if you were willing to say the small forbidden words out loud first and watch which powerful men flinched. He wasn't telling filthy jokes. He was doing surgery, with a laugh for an anaesthetic, on the body of a country that did not want to be opened.

@NARRATOR: [dry] And a machine cannot allow that. A real truth-teller, one who's genuinely funny, genuinely fearless, and genuinely can't be bought — that's the single most dangerous object the machine ever encounters, more dangerous than a bomb, because a bomb it can sell you the fear of, but a man laughing at the emperor's tackle just *dissolves* the spell, and the spell is the whole product. So they had to stop him. But here's the genius, the thing that makes this door the hinge of the modern age: they'd just learned they couldn't ban him. The law had turned, free speech was the new religion, you couldn't put a man in prison for words any more without the whole country noticing. So they didn't. They found the better way. The quiet way. The one they still use, on everyone, right now, tonight.

@NARRATOR: [dry] And I want to give you the mechanics, because the beauty of the thing is always in the mechanics. It started with a word — one word, said on a stage, and a plainclothes cop in the audience with a notebook, writing it down. And from that one word: an arrest. And from the arrest: a trial. And because he was who he was, he didn't plead it out and pay the fine and move on — he *fought* it, because he was right, because the principle mattered, because a man who's built his whole life on saying the true thing cannot suddenly agree to stop. And that was the trap, and they knew it would be, because a coward you can silence with a single arrest, but a brave man you can only silence by giving him *more* to be brave about, endlessly, until the bravery itself bankrupts him. They didn't beat his courage. They *weaponised* it. They took the best thing about him — that he would not back down — and made it the exact mechanism of his ruin. Fight every charge, they knew, and the fighting will cost you everything the charges never could.

@NARRATOR: They arrested him anyway. Over and over. Not to convict — the convictions kept getting overturned, that was fine, that was almost the point. They didn't gag him. They *invoiced* him. They put him on a treadmill of process and let him run himself to death on it, broke and obsessed, spending the last two years of his life not doing comedy but acting as his own lawyer, boring audiences to tears reading legal transcripts aloud because the trial had eaten the man and left only the case. <break time="0.5s" /> And the man doing it — one of the men, there's always a keen one out front — was a prosecutor. @NARRATOR: So here's the moral, and it's the one aimed straight at me, so I'll not flinch from it. They will never ban this. Any of it.
