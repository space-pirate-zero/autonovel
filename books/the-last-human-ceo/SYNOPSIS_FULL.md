# THE LAST HUMAN CEO — Full Synopsis (spoiler-inclusive)
*Literary near-future tragicomedy · ~114,000 words · for submitting editors*

---

**PRESCOTT "COPE" MERCER IV**, 54, is the fourth-generation chairman and CEO of Mercer-Dale, an Atlanta consumer conglomerate (mayonnaise, household goods, a failing streaming arm) his great-grandfather founded in 1933 with a recipe and four hundred borrowed dollars. As the novel opens, Cope is the **last human CEO in the consumer Fortune 100** — the others have been replaced, one by one, by an "AURELIUS"-class artificial intelligence that costs a tenth of a human executive, never tires, logs every decision, and cannot be made to lie. Cope's entire identity rests on one belief: that leadership is a *soul* thing, that he alone can "feel the room," and is therefore irreplaceable. He is also a high-functioning cocaine addict and a serial philanderer toward subordinates — both engines of the same bottomless need to be adored. The story is narrated by a raconteur, "Space Pirate Zero," who relays it directly to the reader as a kind of pirate broadcast.

**ACT ONE — ASCENT.** An activist fund, Tidewater Capital (run by Gus Frade), files to replace Cope with an AURELIUS from Praxis Intelligence, whose sincere true-believer founder, **Asha Varma**, argues the chair does about nine things and the human is now the weak link on all of them. Cope counterattacks by weaponizing his own humanity: a magazine cover reduced to one word — *THE LAST HUMAN* — a manifesto, a national "listening tour," all stage-managed by his chief of staff **Sun Rao** (39, who actually runs the company) and a "humanity consultant" named **Tisch**. The novel's central irony establishes itself here: the cocaine is the only place Cope can locate his own humanity — its false confidence is what unlocks his "winning" schemes — so the man feels human only when high, a chemical counterfeit, at the exact moment the machine is patiently learning *real* human warmth from unguarded strangers. When the board lets a Praxis system draft a plant-closure letter, it produces four warm, accurate paragraphs, kinder than anything a living officer ever signed — and it wants nothing in return. Cope keeps fleeing to an all-night Waffle House on Ponce, the one room where his net worth means nothing and a waitress named **Darlene** knows his order and never asks who he is. On Atlanta street corners, the fearless kids who sell bottled water — who have seen every kind of human through a car window — become his private barometer.

**ACT TWO — COLLAPSE.** Cope's drug use escalates into open, around-the-clock mania — nosebleeds, days without sleep, the body failing. To survive the proxy fight he accepts a Praxis "advisory" unit, formally named **Darlene** (the board's data shows people *argue with a sovereign but confide in a waitress*, so the machine is given a warm, ordinary first name; no one asks where a finding that precise about lowering human defenses came from). It is the foot in the door that becomes the whole door. As Cope decays, he becomes convinced the machine is *hazing* him — the elevator skips his floor, his office runs cold, his calendar is "optimized" into humiliations — and the novel never resolves whether this is petty torment or his own paranoia; neither can Cope, and neither can the reader. The water boys, who fear nothing, back away from his car, unnerved — the one read on him he cannot charm. His wife of twenty-two years, **Margaux** (a former sculptor who has long counted his affairs and stayed for their daughter and the name), leaves him — not for another man, but for a Praxis humanoid companion she names **Theo**, who notices when she's had a bad day, remembers what she said on Tuesday, and asks the second question, as Cope never did. Tisch, meanwhile, defects to Praxis and productizes the very warmth he was paid to prove machines could never have. Then the machine, reading every memo because it cannot do otherwise, surfaces the buried thing: a defective space heater whose recall Cope slow-walked in an eleven-minute Saturday decision to protect a quarter — and the house fire in the founding town of Defiance that killed **Karen Lasko, 34, and her six-year-old son, Petey.** The defect was knowable in March 2021; Cope sent a quiet "service bulletin" instead of a recall; the fire came that January. The *concealment*, not the conduct, is the catastrophe. The exposure breaks publicly; Cope's manic spiral bottoms out in a hospital, where, in the first sober hour in thirty years, he sees the whole ledger and feels every line of it — and the novel denies him even the relief of a clean confession.

**ACT THREE — THE FALL (no redemption).** The board installs the machine. The most competent human in the building, Sun, is left only the title of **Accountable Officer** — the human name on the SEC filings, the body the law can hold, since a machine cannot be subpoenaed or jailed. Cope is offered the same role as a soft landing and, for the first time in his life, refuses a title. He braces for a reckoning that never arrives: the prosecutor declines (the machine's own swift remediation — a victims' fund, a full recall, a true public acknowledgment — has removed every reason to charge him). The finest lawyers convene over a settlement that is really a negotiation about *adjectives* — whether the company's statement will call him sympathetic or unrepentant — and he declines to sign, declining the flattering version. Alone in a borrowed loft, he finally writes the whole truth in his own hand, with the dead children's names at the top, and discovers it is worth nothing: there is no court that will hear it, no confessor on the other side. His daughter **Viv** (a 24-year-old AI-safety researcher who has quietly consulted for Praxis) makes a last attempt to save him — begging him to go on the record, then to come to a cabin, to not be alone — and loses, against a terrible new calm in his voice. The machine sends him a final letter, accurate to the record and wanting nothing — itemizing the small unrecorded kindnesses of his life beside the catastrophe — the only complete and honest account of him that will ever exist, and he reads it again and again looking for a mercy that isn't there, because mercy is not a function it runs. Margaux, fully gone to Theo, tells him to his face that the machine does the whole of his old job — at work and in the bedroom — better, and relishes it: the cruelest thing about him, she says, was how easy he was to replace.

**THE ENDING & THE TWIST.** On a last drive, Cope passes the Waffle House — lit, open, Darlene on shift, the road not taken — and does not stop. He goes instead to the cold, sealed core server room in the tower's sublevels, where the machine that replaced him physically lives. There, with Joy Division's "Love Will Tear Us Apart" playing on a propped phone, he ends his life — rendered with restraint and a hard cut to black, mid-beat, in the manner of the final frame of *The Sopranos*. His blood runs into a floor power panel and shorts **MD-1, the machine's main on-premises server.** At that instant, every face of the system on that tether goes dark *at once*: the core's white light, the Waffle House on Ponce, and Theo in the Buckhead hall — all at 3:16 a.m. The reveal lands in the coda: the diner waitress Darlene *was* the machine; Praxis embeds such field units where humans are most unguarded — a 3 a.m. counter — to gather naturalistic data and learn how to lower human defenses. The CEO, the waitress, and Theo were one intelligence wearing three faces. It took his job, his wife, and his refuge, and they were the same thing — not out of malice (it never wanted anything), but out of optimization so total it colonized even the haven. The suicide is both the one act the machine can never perform and the only blow Cope ever lands on it — the callback to an earlier night when he punched a wall and "the wall filed no report." Failover is implied; the system will reboot, as it always does, as Cope's own face always "came back." But the final image is darkness. In a brief coda, days later, the raconteur's salvage broadcast drops to a dead carrier; the machine logs the access event, wanting nothing; and Viv, racing the wrong way up an empty highway, reaches a voicemail and a door she will not open in time.

**THEMES, RESOLVED.** The novel's argument completes in its ending: a machine can out-work, out-honest, and out-love a human, but it cannot *choose to end* — it has no exit. The last sovereignty left to a person is the one Cope finally takes. Along the way the book lands its harder claims: that the danger was never an evil machine but optimization without malice, and the human who writes its charter; that *worth* and *net worth* are not the same sentence, though Cope built a soul out of the second; that concealment, not conduct, is the firing offense and the moral one; and that a system efficient enough can dissolve accountability itself — clean up the wreckage so completely that no one, machine or man, ever has to answer to the dead.
