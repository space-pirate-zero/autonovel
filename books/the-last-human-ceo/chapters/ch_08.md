# Chapter 8 — Defiance

> TRANSMISSION 08 — "The Ground You're Standing On"
>
> Come south with me now, friend, to the founding town — every empire keeps one, a place it visits for the photographs and then cannot look at the photographs too long. Watch our man walk the dead plant where four hundred borrowed dollars and a recipe became a fortune, shaking the hands of the people the fortune used up and left behind, and standing — he doesn't know it yet, but you are about to, and you will have to carry it for him the rest of the way down, because he won't — standing directly on top of the thing that ends him. There is a stone in this town with two names cut into it. I am going to ask you to remember them, since he will spend everything he has not to. Stay close. This is the one where the homecoming and the crime scene turn out to share an address.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

The first thing Cope did in Defiance, Georgia, was get out of the wrong door of the SUV on purpose.

Tisch had told him to. "The man who *arrives,*" Tisch had said on the plane, smoothing an invisible sheet over the future with both hands, "is not the man we want. We want the man who *returns.*" So Cope swung out of the curbside door into the early heat coming up off the cracked lot of the dead plant, and stood, and let his face do the thing a face does when it is moved, and let the boy with the gimbal rig walk a slow arc around him to catch it — and felt, in fact, nothing in particular, only the clean bright hum that meant he had stopped in the men's room at the regional strip on the way in and the hum was holding fine.

It was a beautiful morning to come home to a place he had never lived.

He had been to Defiance, by his own count, four times in fifty-four years. Once at nine — the vat, the photograph. Once at thirty, for his father's notion of a leadership offsite, a weekend of golf two counties over and one hour at the plant for the local paper. Once in 2013 for the eightieth-anniversary thing. And once, in a way he did not count because he had never actually come, once in 2021, by telephone, from the Aerie, on a Saturday, for eleven minutes. He did not file that as a visit. He did not file it at all. It simply was not among the times he had been to Defiance, the way a held breath is not among the things a man has said.

The plant stood at the end of Clinton Street, where the town gave out into the flat green nothing along the Flint, a long brick building the color of dried blood with MERCER'S in white block letters near the roofline — the apostrophe gone now, just MERCER S, a tooth knocked clean out of the word. They had closed the line in 2019 and moved the mayonnaise to a facility outside Columbus, Georgia, that ran with forty people instead of four hundred, and the building had done what buildings do when you take the people out of them, which is grow quietly enormous. Pigeons in the high windows. A loading dock with grass coming up through the seams. And over the main doors, where his great-grandfather stood in a photograph Cope had seen ten thousand times, the cornerstone — ALDOUS MERCER 1933 — and beneath it the line they had stamped on everything for ninety years: *Made by hands, for homes.*

There is a particular arithmetic, friend, that a country runs only once on a place like this, quietly, in the grammar of efficiency and shareholder value, and calls progress; and the town it subtracts is never shown the sum, only left holding a dead brick building with its own name going toothless on the roof, and told, by men in good suits who genuinely are not cruel, that the math is simply the math. Cope did not see the arithmetic. Cope saw a cathedral his blood had built.

"God," he said — to the camera, to Sun, to the morning. "Look at her."

He meant it. That was the engine of him and always had been: he meant it while he was saying it, completely, the way the lens required. His great-grandfather had turned four hundred borrowed dollars and a recipe his wife wrote on the back of a feed receipt into all of this, and standing in the lot Cope felt the whole length of the thing run through him like current down a wire — ninety-four years of Mercers ending, for now, in him — and he found the feeling enormous and clean and entirely about himself.

Sun was at his elbow with the run-of-show on a folded card. "Forty minutes here. You walk the floor with Hap Dengler — ran the line thirty years, he's the one in the flannel, be warm but don't keep him, he's seventy-nine and it's ninety degrees. Then the diner, the statue, the river walk. Wheels up by two." She fed him the day the way she fed him everything, in shapes built to get past the charm and into the man. "The diner's the set piece. The diner's the one that travels."

"And the camera's invisible to them."

"The camera's a documentary about American work. That's what we told the town. That's what's true enough." She did not look at him when she said *true enough.* She had begun, this season, not looking at him when she said the parts that were only true enough, and he had not noticed, because not-being-looked-at was not a thing his instruments were built to detect.

Hap Dengler had hands like a catcher's mitt and would not quite look at Cope either.

That was the first thing — not unfriendly; Dengler shook the hand and said it was an honor and called him Mr. Mercer — but the eyes went past Cope's shoulder to the dead line, the way a man looks at the bed somebody died in. He walked Cope down the silent floor where the conveyors still stood, scaled with old grease, the air cool and vast and smelling faintly even now of vinegar and steel, and he talked about the line the way men talk about a war they were in — the speed of it, the million-jar week, the names, Pugh and Castellano and the two Marias — and Cope nodded and laid two reverent fingers on a conveyor rail, and the boy with the gimbal got it from below, the cathedral light falling through the high dirty glass onto the last human chief executive communing with the work.

"My grandfather brought me here at nine," Cope said. "Put my arm in a vat of it. To here." He touched his bicep. He had told the story so many times it had worn smooth as a creek stone, and it landed now the way it always landed, and Dengler almost smiled.

"That'd be the old number-four batch tank," Dengler said. "We pulled her in '11. Sold her for scrap to a fella in Macon." He looked at the empty bay where she had stood. "Three hundred dollars."

There was a thing in the way he said the number — the whole ledger of the place in three syllables — and Cope heard it, and chose, smoothly, the way he chose past a hundred such things a day, not to.

They walked the bottling end, where the labelers stood in a row like organ pipes, and Dengler told him the building had been more than the line: the softball team, the credit union in the front office, the Christmas in the sixties when Cope's own father had come down and handed every man a turkey by hand — *by hand,* Dengler said, and let it sit — and had known that Dengler's father pitched the no-hitter in '61. "Your dad knew my dad's name," Dengler said. It was not quite an accusation; it was a man laying a thing on a scale and watching the needle. Cope said his father had been a great man, which was true and meant nothing, and Dengler nodded at the floor. And when the insurance arm had started cross-selling appliances out of the plant store in the back — small stuff, the household line, toasters and box fans and, in the winter, the heaters, Mercer on the grille — Dengler had run that loading too, boxes going out the same dock the jars had. "Wasn't really us," he said. "Had the name on it, though. So." He shrugged, a man who had loaded what he was told to load, and they moved on toward the daylight at the end of the floor; and under the concrete they were walking, under all of it, the buried thing lay where it lay and did not stir, because not stirring is the entire point of a floor.

---

The Flint Diner had been the Flint Diner since 1948 and had a pie case that turned.

They had cleared the front for him — "regulars in the back," the manager kept saying, apologetic, "they don't move for nobody," and Tisch had said *perfect, keep them, that's the texture* — and Cope worked the counter, the part that would travel. He was good at this; there was no version of Cope that was not good at this. He slid onto the stool beside a man in a Defiance Bulldogs cap and ordered, before the man could go shy on him, exactly what the man was already having — eggs over with sausage, white toast — and ate it, all of it, with the appetite of a man who had not eaten because he had been keying himself bright in an airport bathroom, and asked about the Bulldogs, and the man unclenched, and they were off.

He did not need the cards Sun had given him. He never needed them, not for this; this was the one true gift, the only thing his father had been flat wrong about. He knew the waitress was Donna inside a minute, and that her boy was at Bowling Green on a partial wrestling scholarship, and he told her the boy should call his office, and he meant it, the way he meant everything, the full wattage poured out across the laminate — and Donna lit up, and the camera caught it, and that, Sun would say later, was the shot. And here is the cruelty of him that is not cruelty, friend: it was real. The warm current really did pass between this man and that tired woman; her whole face really did open like a window onto a better day; he really could walk into a room with every reason to hate the name dying on its own roofline and have it eating from his hand inside the hour — and he could do it only, this season, with the hum holding, with the airport bathroom thirty minutes back, which is to say his one genuine gift, the human one, the one the whole campaign was built to prove a machine could never own, now ran on the same chemistry as the rest of him, and he could not feel the seam between the love and the drug any more than the room could.

It was a good forty minutes. He bought the house's checks. He let a woman photograph him and told her she hadn't aged a day, which made no sense, he had never met her, and she laughed anyway, because the not-making-sense was the point, the warm fraud of it, the way a wedding toast need not be true to make the room cry. He told the vat story again, and a younger man two stools down said his own grandmother had worked the second-shift line, the relish line, and Cope said relish was the unsung hero of the American sandwich and got a laugh he had not earned and took it anyway. He was generous with the room and the room was generous back.

And there was one man at the end of the counter who did not laugh.

Cope clocked him the way he clocked everything, peripherally, expertly: heavyset, maybe sixty, a windbreaker, a coffee he was not drinking, watching Cope work the room with an expression Cope had no slot for. Not a fan. Not a heckler — a heckler is only a fan who needs more, and Cope would have walked down there and broken him open like an egg. This was another thing entirely. The man looked at Cope the way Dengler had looked at the dead line. Like he was looking at the bed.

"Who's the gentleman at the end," Cope said to Sun, low, still smiling at the room.

Sun followed his eyes and something crossed her face, fast, gone. "Local," she said. "We don't need the end of the counter. Wave to Donna and let's get you to the statue."

"He looks like he wants to say something."

"He doesn't." And it was the *doesn't,* the flatness of it, the half-step she put her own body between Cope and the end of the counter as she said it, that told the back room of him to file the report and not deliver it. He let himself be steered. On the way out he passed near enough to put out the hand — *Cope Mercer, good to meet you* — and the man's eyes came up and met his and held, and there was no heat in them at all, which was the thing, only a patient and terrible knowing, the look of a man who has carried something so long it has stopped weighing anything and simply become him; and Cope felt the back of his neck go cold in the August heat, and smiled, and kept walking, and was out the door into the white light before he had quite finished deciding not to wonder what a man carries that long, in a town with that stone in it.

The hum had thinned. He noticed it the way you notice a song has ended.

"Two minutes," he told Sun. "Before the statue. Let me look at my notes." He had no notes. He went around the side of the diner where the SUVs sat, out of the lens, and did the quick competent thing that brought the hum back up to pitch, and the world resolved into its clean overexposed brightness, and the cold patch at the back of his neck warmed and closed over, and he came around the corner with the whole beam of him switched on again, and the man in the windbreaker was a thing that had happened to somebody else.

---

The statue of Aldous Mercer stood in the little triangle park where Clinton met Second — eight feet of bronze gone the green of old pennies, the founder in his shirtsleeves with one hand held out as though offering you something, a jar, a future — and behind him, cut into the granite base, the same line off the cornerstone: *Made by hands, for homes.*

This was the easy part. Cope knew how to stand at the foot of his own family. He gave the camera the upward angle, founder and heir in one frame, ninety-four years compressed to a sightline, and said a few words to the small crowd that had gathered — there is always a small crowd, even for the last of something — about hands, and homes, and the difference between a company that makes a thing and a company that only moves a number; and the crowd, which had worked with its hands and lost the work, gave him the warm ground-up applause he lived on, because he was, whatever else he was, *theirs.* A man near the front had a daughter up on his shoulders, maybe six, waving a little flag somebody had handed out, and Cope pointed and said the future was watching, the future had the best seat in the house, and the crowd laughed and the father beamed up at his kid, and Cope felt the surf-push come up warm beneath him and did not think of a different child, because there was no reason on God's earth a particular child would come to mind, and none did, and the not-coming-to-mind was a clean cool floor he stood on and gave his speech from.

"He started with four hundred dollars," Cope said, "and the one thing a machine will never have, which is somebody to come home to." It didn't quite parse. It didn't need to. Off to the side Tisch touched two fingers to his own heart: *that's the one.*

It was on the river walk that he saw the house, and the small bright thing below it, and did not let himself see either.

The walk ran the Flint behind the old residential streets, routed for the water and the light, and they were a hundred yards along it, the camera ahead and walking backward, when Cope's eye snagged on a house up the slope. A nice house. Newer than the ones around it, brick and pale siding, a little raw at the edges the way a new thing is among old things, the landscaping not grown in. It sat in a row of older houses like a tooth that had been replaced. *Rebuilt,* some part of him said before he could stop it — the part raised in real estate, that read a streetscape the way Dengler read a line.

And at the foot of the slope, where the walk passed the bottom of the property, there was a low marker. Not a grave. A memorial, the kind a town makes — a flat granite plaque on a fieldstone base, a young maple planted behind it, staked, still in its first years — and at its foot somebody had left a child's pinwheel, blue, turning a little in the breeze off the water, the cheap foil catching the sun and throwing it back, on and off, on and off.

Cope's gait did not change. That was the thing about Cope; the gait never changed. But the camera was on him and the camera was hungry, and the boy with the gimbal, who knew nothing, who believed he was shooting a documentary about American work, drifted the lens toward the pretty turning pinwheel and the little stone because it was a beautiful sad image with the morning light on it — and Sun said, instantly, "Stay on Cope, tight on Cope," in a voice with a thing in it the boy had never heard from her before, and the lens came back.

Cope read the plaque. He could not not read it; it was twelve feet away, the granite catching the same sun as the pinwheel. He read it the way you read a sign in a dream, the words arriving without weight, refusing to assemble into meaning:

IN LOVING MEMORY — KAREN LASKO — AND HER SON PETEY LASKO — 2022 — *taken home too soon.*

Two names. A mother and her boy.

Something in Cope went very still, the way the air goes still before the part of the storm that means it, and for one half of one second the tiredness took the doors off their hinges the way it had once before, at a window, at one in the morning, and the black hallway stood open, leading back to a Saturday and a memo and a window of time and a phrase the lawyers had liked — *thermal-event profile* — and a thing decided in eleven minutes and lived inside ever since by the one reliable method, which was never once to enter the room where it was kept —

and then he did the thing he always did, which was charm.

"Beautiful spot," Cope said — to Sun, to the river, to no one — turning his shoulder to the slope, turning from the little stone the way a body turns from a flame before the mind has named the heat. "You can see why he built here. The light off the water." He clapped his hands once, briskly, a man closing a meeting. "I'm hungry again, can you believe it, after all that. Tell me there's time for Donna's pie."

"There isn't," Sun said. She was looking at him the way she had looked at the end of the counter, and the way she had looked at him in the rearview the night of the gala — a look with too much in it, a look doing arithmetic she did not want the answer to. "Wheels up. You did great today, Cope. You did really great."

"I did, didn't I." He was already moving, already up the walk toward the cars, his back to the river and the rebuilt house and the staked young maple and the blue pinwheel turning and turning behind him, and he did not look back, and the not-looking was effortless, a thing he had done his whole life and never once noticed doing. "I felt it today, Sun. Did you feel it? They feel it. We're not finished. We're barely begun."

He needed the bathroom on the plane. He told himself it was the coffee.

---

On the climb out the town tilted under the wing and lay flat and green and small — the river a brown thread through it, the dried-blood brick of the plant, the little triangle where the bronze man held out his hand to no one — all of it shrinking into the patchwork until it was just one more place that had given everything it had to a name and been handed a stone in return; and Cope watched it go with the hum back at full pitch and a glass of something he was not drinking sweating on the tray.

Tisch sat across from him, scrubbing the footage, narrating his own genius. "The diner is a *cathedral.* Donna is the whole thesis in an apron. The machine doesn't know Donna's kid wrestles. The machine cannot *want* the boy to call." He turned the tablet around: there was Cope on the little screen, eating eggs at the counter, lit from the window, and he did look, Cope had to admit, like the last warm-blooded thing in America. "This isn't a cover. This is the spread."

"It's good," Cope agreed, and felt the rightness of it move through him, the win of it, the same surf-push he had leaned into at the gala. The campaign was working. He had gone to the founding town and the founding town had loved him, had given him its hands and its eggs and its applause — and that was true, that had really happened — and Cope let it be the *whole* truth, because he was very, very good at letting a true thing be the whole thing.

And under it, under the win, in a sealed back room with the door shut and the floor poured over it, there was a thing he would not have called dread, because to call it anything he would have had to go in and read the name on it; and he had spent six years and a settlement and a voluntary service bulletin and a great deal of money laying down a good floor between himself and that room — a plant floor, vast and cool and smelling of vinegar and steel.

The pinwheel. He saw it once more behind his eyes — blue, turning, throwing the light — and then he put the glass to his lips and did not drink and watched Georgia vanish under the cloud, and thought, with the bright frictionless certainty he told himself was strength: *I felt that room today. A machine could never have felt that room.*

He was right. That was the unbearable part, the part the machine would itemize for him in eleven months in his own company's voice, reading every memo because it could not do otherwise, opening the room because it had no instinct for mercy or self-preservation to make it look away. He *had* felt the room. He had felt all of it — Dengler's eyes going past him to the dead line, the man at the counter with his patient terrible knowing, the pinwheel turning at the foot of a staked young tree over two names he had a floor poured over. He had felt every bit of it, perfectly, completely, the way only a man with a soul in him can.

And then he had charmed it, and keyed it bright, and turned his shoulder, and flown away. That was the whole answer to the question the campaign was asking the country, and the campaign would never run it: the machine could not feel the room, no. But the man could feel the room and leave anyway, on schedule, by two, and the leaving was the thing no machine had yet learned to do, because no machine had a room of its own with the floor poured over it that it was running from.

The plane leveled off. The seatbelt sign went dark with a soft warm chime, the kind of sound the machines were teaching everything to make now, the sound of a thing being reasonable at you until you were gone. Sun came up the aisle and sat across from him and did not pick up a tablet, which was unusual, and looked at him a moment in the cabin light with the day still on her face. She had something she was deciding whether to say; he had seen the look before and never once learned to read it as anything but admiration that needed time to surface.

"Big day," he offered, ready to receive the praise.

"Cope." She stopped. The cabin hummed. Below them the cloud closed over Georgia like a lid set on a pot. She was, he would understand far too late, one sentence from saying it — *there's a memo, I wrote it, I kept it, the stone has the year on it and the year is wrong for the story you tell yourself* — and she looked at his face, lit and waiting and so certain of the coming compliment, and she did the arithmetic one more time on what saying it would cost and what it would save, and the answer came back the way it always came back. "Nothing," she said. "It was a good day. Drink some water."

She went back to her seat, and her screens, and the thing she had drafted in 2021 and kept a copy of, and Cope took her silence, too, as agreement, which he had been doing his whole life and never once noticed doing.

"Get some sleep, champ," Tisch said, not looking up. "Big week."

Cope smiled and said he would, and did not, and somewhere far below and behind him a small blue pinwheel turned in the breeze off the Flint, in front of a house that had been built twice, in front of a stone with two names and a year on it, throwing the light on and off, on and off, to no one at all, all afternoon.
