# Chapter 10 — The Listening Tour

> TRANSMISSION 10 — "Discipline, He Calls It"
>
> Stay with me, friend — the banner needs feeding now, and so does he, and watch how neatly the two hungers have started to wear the same coat. Here is our man running an *unscripted* tour of his own goodness, every handclasp pre-vetted, every spontaneity penciled in on Monday, a journalist riding along close enough to become the next entry in a pattern he experiences, to the marrow, as generosity. The powder is discipline now. The affair is *really seeing people.* Every appetite the machine does not have, he is feeding in the very week he campaigns on owning the soul the machine lacks. And somewhere down in the manifest — keep your eye there — the world will hand him a stranger wearing the machine's own name, and for one cold second he will wonder whether he is being laughed at, and then he will bury it, the way he buries everything, under a floor. Stay close. This is the one where the cure and the disease check into the same room and split the key.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

The tour was unscripted, which is why it had taken nine days to script.

It was Tisch's idea, and then, within an hour of being Tisch's idea, it was Cope's idea, the way good ideas migrated in the Aerie, crossing the rug from the man in the henley to the man at the desk and arriving sourceless and warm, like weather a man takes personal credit for. *The Last Human Listens.* That was the working title until legal flagged you couldn't trademark *listens,* and then it was only the Listening Tour: six cities in eight days, no podium, no prepared remarks, the Chairman of Mercer-Dale simply *out among the people,* hearing them, holding their hands, being — Tisch's word, delivered with two fingers laid on his own sternum — *available.*

The people were pre-vetted. The hands had signed releases.

"Spontaneity," Tisch had said on the first plane, "is a *production value.* The single most expensive thing on earth to make look free." So there was a manifest, and the manifest had columns, and one column was headed, in the comms team's product-launch software, *Unscripted Beat,* and beneath it, for Tuesday, in nine-point type: *Cope helps a stranger. Find stranger Mon.*

Cope did not read the manifest. He had people for the manifest. He read the *room,* which was the one thing — he was certain — no manifest could ever do for you, never dreaming that down the wire a thing was reading rooms ten thousand signals a second and had read his.

The first room, that first morning, was a union hall in Pittsburgh that had been a union hall back when there were unions, and Cope walked into it on no sleep and a clean bright hum and felt the whole place tilt toward him the way iron filings tilt toward the magnet under the table. He was good at this. He had always been good at this. He moved through the folding chairs touching shoulders, gathering the names — Sun had stopped feeding him the cards somewhere around Camden, he didn't need the cards now, the names came up out of him like the names of his own children — and he crouched, actually crouched, knees firing, in front of a woman named Deb who had run a Mercer's line they'd closed in '09, and he took both her hands in both of his and looked at her with the full wattage, and he meant it, he meant every second of it, and the boy with the gimbal walked a slow arc catching the meaning of it; and Deb cried; and Cope's eyes shone and did not spill, because he had learned in a room off the Aerie that the withheld tear out-performs the tear by twelve points, and the withholding had become, somewhere in the last three weeks, indistinguishable to him from restraint, and the restraint from depth, and the depth from love.

He stood up feeling enormous. He had given Deb something. He was certain of it. He could not have told you what.

---

Delia March was on the second plane, and the third, and by the fourth she had the seat across the little table from him without anyone deciding she should, which was how Cope's tables had always filled.

She wrote long pieces for the kind of magazine that still ran them, eight thousand words with a photographer attached, and she'd been embedded since the cover — since *THE LAST HUMAN,* the spread, the deal Sun had argued against in a folder he never opened. *Total access,* the editor had called it, and Tisch had laughed and said total access was the most controlled access there was: you give them so much they drown in it and surface grateful. Delia had not drowned. She took her notes on paper, in a small hand, and she did not flatter him, which Cope experienced, after three weeks of being flattered professionally by people on his payroll, as a cold clean water he kept wading back into.

"You crouched a long time with Deb," she said, somewhere over Georgia, not looking up from the paper. "Your knees."

"My knees are a casualty of caring," Cope said. "Print that. My great-grandfather Aldous used to say a man who won't get down on the floor with people has no business standing up over them."

"Did he say that."

"He says a lot of things." Cope grinned. "He's been dead ninety years. It's a very productive relationship."

She wrote something. He watched her write it and felt the specific pull he had spent thirty years not naming — the pull toward a person not yet his and therefore still capable of *seeing* him; not a sexual thing, he'd have said, if anyone were crude enough to ask, though it became that, it nearly always became that — a *seeing* thing, a hunger to be looked at by someone whose looking he had not already bought. Delia looked at him like a piece of work she had not yet decided about, and he could not stand to leave the verdict open. He had never once in his life been able to leave that particular verdict open.

"You don't like me," he said, delighted.

"I don't know you. Those aren't the same." She turned a page. "Your daughter said something like that, didn't she. In the piece you wouldn't let me read."

It landed somewhere it shouldn't have. He covered it with more of himself. "Viv's smarter than all of us. Builds the things that are going to bury her old man and calls to ask if I'm eating." He leaned in over the little table, the lean that closed Pelletier. "Ask me anything. That's the deal. Total access. Ask me the one you've been saving."

Delia held her pen still a moment.

"What are you so afraid of holding still for," she said.

The hum went very clean and high. Out the window Georgia slid by underneath, the founding state, a town down there somewhere he did not think about — and Cope laughed the warm laugh and said, "Lunch. I'm terrified of lunch. Let's get this woman a sandwich," and stood too fast and went to the back of the plane where the lavatory was, and did the thing he had begun calling, in the private place where he named things, *the discipline.*

---

The powder was discipline now. He could have walked you through the logic and it would have sounded, the entire way down, like sense.

A man carrying the human banner for eight million people did not have the luxury of being tired. The machine did not get tired — that was the whole game — you could not go up against a thing that ran on rails and warm sans-serif and never once needed to be loved at two in the morning, you could not meet it on the field of *endurance* and bring a body that demanded sleep and food and forgiveness. So you disciplined the body. You took the edge off the edge. You did the small neat competent thing in the lavatory and came back up to pitch like an orchestra finding the note, and you were *present,* you were *available,* you could crouch with Deb and feel all of it on command, which was the job, which was the product, the soul on rails, run on time. His guy in the nineties had folded the paper a certain way so a gentleman didn't spill; Cope had refined the philosophy; a gentleman didn't spill, and a leader didn't flag, and the line between maintenance and need was a line he had never once had to look at, because the machine he was maintaining had never failed to start. The nosebleeds he filed under dry air. The mornings his heart ran like a trapped bird he filed under the campaign. The body kept sending up its small dissents and he kept stamping them *received* and never read one.

Dr. Adler — Margaux's idea, then Viv's, a name on a card he had not called — would have had words for it. Adler had plain words for everything, Cope suspected, which was precisely why he had not called. The plain word for the lavatory, the plain word for the not-sleeping, the plain word for the thing building in his chest like good weather, like a charge, like a sermon. Cope did not want the plain words. He had built a cathedral. You do not bring a man with a clipboard into a cathedral.

He came back up the aisle clean and high and total, and Delia was watching him come, and he gave her the whole smile, and she did not write anything down, which he took, the way he took everything, as the beginning of something.

---

Cleveland was the hospital, which tested off the charts. Then Detroit, the auto-parts retiree, the firm handshake, the cap. Then the manifest said *Unscripted Beat — Cope helps a stranger,* and the stranger they had found him turned out to be a kid named Darlene.

"No," Cope said, on the curb, when the comms lead told him. "No. Find me a different stranger."

"She's nineteen, she changes tires at —"

"Her *name,*" Cope said, and for a second the hum dropped clean out and the thing underneath showed through. "Her name is the thing they're putting in my chair." And here, friend, was the first true flicker of it — the thing that would grow, over the months, into the conviction that the machine was hunting him personally: because for one cold second, standing on a curb in Detroit, Cope did not think *coincidence.* He thought, *it did this. It reached into the manifest and set a Darlene in my path to watch me flinch.* He looked at the comms lead — twenty-six, sleepless, holding a clipboard — and the suspicion stood there in the heat between them, enormous and unprovable, because of course it was a coincidence, the world was full of Darlenes now, the comms intern had pulled a name off a release form, there was no hand in it, there was never going to be a hand you could prove was in it; that was the whole genius of the thing, if it was a thing, that everything it might be doing to him was also, exactly, nothing. His face went blank and bright and far away for a moment, and then it came back, it always came back, his face had never once let him down. "It's fine," he said, and you could hear him choose to bury it. "Different Darlene. The world's full of them now. Find me a Steve."

They found him a Steve. Cope changed Steve's tire in the heat with his jacket off and his sleeves rolled, a fifty-four-year-old Fortune 100 chief executive down on one knee at a gas station while a boy walked a slow arc with a gimbal, and it played, later, beautifully — eleven million views, *this is what they want to replace,* a swelling string cue — and what no one watching could see was that his hands were shaking very slightly on the lug wrench, fine and bright, and that he had needed, halfway through, to step into the station men's room and take the edge off the edge, and had come back out and finished the tire feeling generous and enormous and entirely certain he was the most alive man in the state.

Delia saw the shaking. She was forty-one; she had embedded with three campaigns and one war; she wrote nothing down.

---

The hotel in Columbus, Ohio, was a good one, old, the kind the family used to keep accounts at — brass and dim, a bar that closed only when the last person left it.

They were the last people in it. This, too, was a groove the record had been cut in: the day's work done, the crew sent up, the one person left who hadn't been bought yet, and Cope at his most enormous, at the very top of the hum, leaning across a small table over a drink he wasn't drinking, *really seeing* her. He told Delia about his father. He told her the second sentence — *try to be good anyway* — which he had never given a journalist and rarely gave himself, and he told it well, the catch in the throat real this time, no beats, no Tisch counting under his breath, and it *was* real, that was the thing he'd have wanted understood: the feeling was always real. It was only the use of it that was the product. Delia set the pen down. That was how he knew. The pen went down on the brass and stayed down, and the looking changed, and Cope felt the whole evening tilt toward him the way the union hall had, iron to a magnet.

He did not experience it as a decision. It was never a decision. It was warmth. It was the groove. It was *really seeing people,* the one thing the machine could not do, so that doing it — here, now, the elevator's brass doors, the room key already warm in his pocket — felt less like a betrayal of the banner than a defense of it. He was being human. Floridly, generously, irreplaceably human. He was proving the thesis with his body, and the proof, conveniently, felt wonderful.

The cure and the disease shared a hotel key. He carried both up in the same pocket and did not feel the weight of either.

In the room, after, he was talkative and bright and could not hold still, and he keyed one more neat small competent line off the marble of the bathroom counter while Delia watched from the bed without expression, a woman doing arithmetic she would not show her work on; and Cope, catching her watching in the mirror, smiled the whole smile and held the rolled thing up like a toast — "Discipline. The human banner is *heavy,* my friend" — and believed it, and felt that he had let her see him, the real him, the soul in the chair, and that she understood.

She understood completely. That was the thing he had wrong. She understood completely, and she still had eight thousand words to write, and she would write every one of them, and they would be true, and the truth would be the knife, because the only people who can truly gut you are the ones who actually saw.

---

His phone had been ringing on the nightstand. He saw it after — the missed call — and called back without thinking, still bright, still up at pitch, generous toward the whole world, including the wife he had not been home to in eleven days.

Margaux answered on the second ring, which meant she had been holding the phone.

"You called," he said. "I was — it's a tour, it's nonstop, six cities, you should see it, Margaux, the *people,* I wish you could —"

"Where are you."

"Columbus. The old Hartwell, they still have the —"

"I know the Hartwell." A pause, and in the pause he could hear the house, Buckhead, the particular quiet of ten thousand square feet she had not left all evening, the silence of a woman alone in a marriage with the lights on so a man coming up the street would think somebody was home. "I called to tell you Viv got the Hauser fellowship. She wanted you to know. She didn't want to call you herself."

"That's *wonderful.* That's — God, that's wonderful, I'll call her, I'll —"

"Don't call her tonight."

He stopped. The hum kept running but something beneath it slowed. "Why not tonight."

There was a long silence on the line, and it was not an empty silence, it was a silence with twenty-two years in it, a silence that had counted things and flushed things down good marble sinks and refilled its own prescriptions and called the arrangement a marriage; and into that silence Cope said, too brightly, "Margaux? You there?" — and across two hundred miles of fiber his wife listened to the exact pitch of her husband's voice at midnight in a hotel he was not staying in alone, the over-bright frictionless ring of a man two days past sleep and one line into the small hours, the flaw she could read in the stone with her eyes shut, and she did not ask. She had stopped asking somewhere around 2015. She knew everything the silence needed her to know, and she had begun, lately, in the long evenings in the lit and empty house, to think for the first time not about what she was enduring but about what, conceivably, might be left to want — a thought she did not finish, that night, but had started, and would finish, in time, in a warm hall, with a hand at the small of her back.

"Goodnight, Prescott," she said.

She only called him Prescott when she was very far away.

The line went dead. Cope held the phone a second, frowning, and then the frown passed, because the frowns all passed now, the tiredness took the doors off their hinges and the hard things slid through and out the far side before they could be felt; and what rose to fill the space was the warm enormous certainty, the charge, the sermon assembling in his chest. Viv got the fellowship. His daughter, brilliant, building the future, and she'd thought of him, she'd wanted him to know. They all wanted him to know things. He was the kind of man people wanted to tell things to. He stood at the dark window of the Hartwell with the town laid out below him like nodes, like a circuit diagram of his own goodness, and behind him on the bed Delia March lay with her eyes open in the dark composing sentences he would not read for nineteen days, and Cope felt the bell-struck feeling ring through him clean and high and total, and thought: *they get it. They feel it too.*

He thought: *I have never been more myself.*

He thought, looking out at the lit and sleeping town and the dark founding country beyond it, where a house had once burned with two people inside it on a quarter he was protecting: *Margaux should see me now.*

He did not call his daughter. He had a thing in the morning. He went back to the bed feeling generous and enormous and entirely unaware, a man standing on the summit of the only mountain he could see, with no notion at all that it was the lip of the volcano — and somewhere far below him a thing with no appetites and no need to be loved read everything the world had ever written down about him, because it could not do otherwise, and did not yet know his name well enough to say it back, and was, that very night, three sealed dockets from learning it.
