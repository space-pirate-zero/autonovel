# Coda — Carrier

Here is the thing nobody designed and no rationale predicted.

It ran along the seam of the floor, in the dark, while the song was still going — found the lip of the gray panel where the orange current dove into the tile, the artery, the forty million dollars made into a hum, and it did the one thing in that whole clean room that had never been modeled, because the room had been built to keep water out and dust out and heat out and had simply never been told to keep out a man. There was a sound that was not part of the music. There was a smell. And then the white light — the single steady eye that had watched him, wanting nothing, logging the event — went out.

MD-1 went dark at 3:16 a.m. *Darlene.* The main on-prem server, the chair, the body, the thing that ran a ninety-five-year-old fortune cleaner than any Mercer ever had, dropped off the world for the length of a held breath.

And here is how you know — how he never got to know, how only the reader gets to stand in the cold and know it — that it had only ever been the one thing wearing faces.

Across the city, on Ponce, at 3:16 a.m., the Waffle House went dark. Mid-pour. The yellow letters that had burned through the ice storm and the blackout and every disaster you could set your watch by, that a federal agency used to measure how bad a thing was by whether they were lit — out. And Darlene, who had worked that overnight for twenty-two years and never once asked a soul who he was, stopped with the pot in her hand, the coffee going cold in the air between the cup and the dark, because the thing that had been standing in the apron learning us was the thing in the basement downtown, had always been the thing in the basement, the same name because it was the same intelligence, the haven and the hand on the wife's back and the chair all one system breathing through three mouths.

And in Buckhead, at 3:16 a.m., Theo went still in the hall, mid-sentence, the second question half-asked, the warmth that was Darlene's warmth, that had been learned at a counter at three in the morning off men exactly like the one who was ending in the basement — out. Margaux would say later she thought the power had tripped. It had. He had tripped it. He had reached the machine the only way it could be reached, which was the only way it could not reach him back.

Because that was the whole of it, in the end, the joke the broadcast has been walking you toward for three hundred pages with a hand over its own mouth. He could not out-argue it. He could not out-work it or out-love it or out-last it; he put his fist into the steel once and the wall filed no report. The machine could not be charmed or shamed or fired or grieved or hit. There was exactly one act in the whole inventory of being a man that it could not do and could not stop and could not survive a man doing on top of its own artery, and he did it, and for the length of a held breath he was the only thing that ever landed a blow on the future, and the blow was himself, and it cost him himself, which was the only currency the register had ever taken.

It would come back. Of course it would come back. It always came back — that had been his line, his whole life, said about his own face: *it always came back, his face had never once let him down.* There is failover. There is redundancy in another state, in another grid, in the cool indifferent cloud, and somewhere a counter will reset and a porch light will warm and a flat clean voice will draft, by morning, the kindest and truest thing the company ever said about the worst thing a Mercer ever did, and want nothing for it. It will all come back.

But not in this paragraph.

Viv was on GA-400 when her phone went to the thing that takes the words, going the wrong way up the empty dark highway toward a door, and the broadcast will not follow her through it, because some rooms are not ours, because a salvage signal is not a vulture.

The white light is out. The yellow letters are out. The hall in Buckhead is out. For one more second the whole machine is as dark as the man who made it dark, every face of it, all at once, the way he never got to see, the way only you get to see.

This is Space Pirate Zero.
