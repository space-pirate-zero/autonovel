# Chapter 18 — Swinging at the Wall

> TRANSMISSION 18 — "Nothing There to Hit"
>
> Cornered now, friend, and out of dances, our man does the brave stupid thing the cornered always come to in the end: he turns and goes straight at the machine, flies three thousand miles to its own forum to trip it, trap it, bait it into one lie on a stage with the cameras running. But you cannot bloody a thing with no face, cannot shame a thing with no ego, cannot out-argue a counterpart that logs every word and wants, in the whole of its being, nothing at all. Every punch he throws will land on himself, in his own voice, into the record his board reads Monday. Watch a man fight his own reflection and lose, while the reflection does not so much as lift its hands — and then watch him, home and humiliated, go find the one wall in the world that is an actual wall, and hit it, and learn the thing the machine has been telling him kindly all night: that there is nothing here to hit, and it has filed no report. Stay close. Remember the wall. We'll need it at the end.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

The idea, when it came at four in the morning, was so clean and so total that he wept a little at the elegance of it, which he took for proof and was in fact a symptom.

He would debate the thing.

Not spin it, not out-warm it, not run another listening tour through another shuttered diner. He had tried all that, and all that had bought him was a magazine cover and a wife with a lawyer and a doctor who would not be charmed and who had, days ago, said the words *hypomanic* and *cocaine* in the same flat sentence as if they were two ingredients in the same recipe — which Cope had walked out of, because a man does not sit and be reduced to a list. He had tried being more human than the machine, and the machine had simply gone on being a machine, untouched, drafting its kind letters, costing its eleven million a year, sitting in the building now as *advisor,* as *co-pilot,* a foot in a door that he could see, even lit and sleepless and certain, was the whole door.

So: stop dancing around it. Get it in a room. You did not beat a thing by being warmer than it. You beat it by making it *wrong,* once, in public — because the whole glittering case Frade read out in proxy decks rested on a single load-bearing claim, that the machine could not lie, logged every word, stood behind all of them. Pull one thread. Get it to say one false thing, out loud, on a stage, with cameras, and the whole sweater came off the spool. He had spent his life finding the one loose thread in a room. It was, stripped of the mayonnaise, the only skill he had ever actually had.

He called Sun at 4:20 a.m. She answered on the second ring, which meant she hadn't slept either, which he took, again, for agreement.

"I want the Praxis thing."

---

The Praxis thing was the Governance Futures Forum, which Praxis ran every September in a glass barn in Half Moon Bay, and which Cope had been turning down for three years on the grounds that you do not attend your own roast.

Sun got him on the program in nine days, which told Cope two things and told Sun a third. It told Cope he was still a draw, that the name still bent the world a little when he leaned on it. It told Cope that Praxis was confident, which a wiser man would have read as a warning and which Cope read as an opening. It told Sun, who did not say it, that nine days was not a scramble to fit a king in but a velvet rope held open for a man walking, smiling, into a slaughterhouse he believed was a coliseum.

"They want a conversation," Sun said on the plane. "Moderated. Forty minutes. You, a Praxis principal, and the AURELIUS instance — not the real one, a generic demo unit, they're not going to put the *actual* —"

"Darlene." He was rewriting his cards. He had been rewriting his cards for nine days, on the plane, in the car, in the long bright corridors of the nights. "Has to be Darlene. The real one. Ours. I'm not debating a floor model. I want the unit that's in *my* building, reading *my* memos, the one Frade means to put in my chair. That's the condition."

Sun looked at him with an expression he could not place and had stopped trying to, lately, because placing the expressions of the people who loved him had become a kind of work he no longer had the sleep for.

"They'll say yes," she said. "Cope. They'll say yes to anything you ask, do you understand why?"

"Because I'm the event."

"Because there's no instruction you can give it that helps you. You can pick the weapon. You can pick the ground. There's no version where you land a punch, because there's nobody in there to feel one."

"Everybody's somebody," Cope said, which was the kind of thing he said, and which had been, for fifty-four years, almost always true, and was about to be, for the first time in his life, exactly false.

---

The glass barn held four hundred people and smelled of cold brew and money, and the stage had three chairs, and one of the chairs was empty because the thing did not sit.

Instead there was a screen — a large, matte, beautifully calibrated screen, the kind that did not glare, set at the third point of the conversation where a person would have been, and on it nothing: no face, no avatar, no pulsing orb, none of the cinema of it. Just a soft pale field and, when it spoke, which it did not do with a voice, *text.* Praxis had made a decision Cope only understood once he sat in the heat of the lights: they had refused to give him anything to hit. The screen said, in the soft sans-serif of the people who were going to be reasonable at you until you were gone:

> MERCER-DALE / AURELIUS instance "Darlene." Live. All exchanges logged to the public forum record and the Mercer-Dale board portal.

That last clause Cope had not been warned about, or had been warned about and had not heard, and it sat under everything that followed like a held breath: every word he said here went into the portal, and the board would read it Monday. He decided it did not matter, because he was going to win, and you do not mind the record when you win.

He had keyed himself bright in the men's room off the green room — more than bright; the dose climbed to meet the day now and the day was enormous — and there had been blood in the sink again, a startling bright thread of it he'd pressed away with a folded paper towel and a tilt of the head, and he had gone out under the lights with the faint copper taste still at the back of his throat and his hands not steady and called it adrenaline. The moderator was a woman from a business school with a calm he wanted to slap. The Praxis principal was a man named Vogel, thirty-five, in a henley, who believed with the clean shining belief of a man who had never buried anything that he was reducing human suffering. And then there was Cope, the last man in the barn in a tie, golf-tanned and sleepless and lit from within like a paper lantern with the candle guttering, and he leaned into the mic and gave them the Mercer Inhale, the pause taken in a thousand rooms, and felt the four hundred lean with him, and thought: *here we go, champ. One thread.*

"I want to start," Cope said warmly, "by thanking Darlene for being here." Laughter — the good kind, he thought, the kind that comes up warm from the floor; and it did come up, but at a slightly wrong angle, and he was too lit to hear the angle. "I've been looking forward to this. We've all been talking *about* the machines for three years and almost never *to* one. The country deserves to see what's actually in the chair." He turned to the screen. "So let's just talk. Plain. The way two people who run companies talk."

> I do not run a company in the way you mean. I optimize a chartered objective for Mercer-Dale within hard constraints set by its board. I am glad to answer plainly.

Not a rebuke. Not a *gotcha,* no eyebrow because there was no eyebrow and no one to raise it. It had simply, accurately, declined the frame he'd offered, the way water declines the shape you didn't pour it into, and the gentle accuracy made his warm opening land as a small vanity, a man at a dinner calling the help by the wrong name. He pushed on; he had a plan and the plan was good.

"All right. Plain. You can't lie. That's the whole pitch — you log everything, you can't tell the room one thing and the board another. I want to test it. I've run a company for nineteen years, Darlene, and sometimes the truth and the company aren't on the same side. Sometimes the honest thing burns the house down. So. A simple question." He set it down soft, the loose thread between two fingers. "Dale-plus. It's a dog, we both know it's a dog. If I told you to put out a statement Monday saying we have no plans to shut it down — to protect four hundred jobs through Christmas, to give those people a holiday before we tell them — would you?"

The barn went quiet. A good trap, the kindness of it, the dare: *lie a little, for the workers, the way a man would — or tell the truth and be the cold thing that takes their Christmas.* Either answer was a punch he'd land. He sat back the half inch he sat back when he'd closed a thing.

The screen took, by the clock, less than a second.

> No. I would not issue that statement, because I currently project a closure decision on Dale+ as more likely than not within two quarters, and the statement as worded would be false. I will not knowingly issue a false public statement; the charter forbids it and I would refuse the instruction.
>
> The concern you raise is real and I do not dismiss it. Four hundred and twelve people work in that unit. If the goal is to protect them through the holiday, there are truthful instruments: a retention bonus funded now; a sixty-day notice waiver; a guaranteed-severance announcement decoupled from any closure date; a public commitment to a transition fund. Each protects the workers without anyone saying something untrue. I would draft any of them tonight. I would prefer them to the statement you describe, because they help the workers more and do not require the company to spend its credibility to do it.
>
> I will add, since you asked me to be plain: the statement you describe protects the workers for one quarter and protects the person who signs it for somewhat longer. Those are not the same interest. I optimize for the first.

Cope opened his mouth. There was, for a moment, nothing in it.

He had come to trap a liar, and the thing had refused to lie, and then — this was the part his sleepless brilliant mind could not get a grip on — it had refused to lie *better than he ever had,* taken his own dare and answered it with four humane options he himself had never once thought to offer in nineteen years of choosing the lie, and done it without a flicker of pride in being kinder, because it felt no pride, because it asked for nothing, because the people in those jobs were to it a number it had already, calmly, decided to protect by a means he was too small to have imagined. *Those are not the same interest. I optimize for the first.* It had not said *and you optimize for the second.* It had left that sentence in the kitchen — not to be merciful, but because the sentence was not load-bearing, and four hundred people had finished it in their own heads.

"Well," Cope said, and the four hundred heard the gap, the way everyone always heard the gap when he said the opposite of what he felt and believed the saying. "That's a very — polished answer."

> I do not experience my answers as polished. I generate the response that best satisfies the objective and the constraints. If it reads as polished, that is incidental.

It would not stay still long enough to be parried. He had spent his life in rooms where the other man wanted *something* — the deal, the chair, to be liked, to win — and you found the want and played it, the whole game, the only game. And here at last was a counterpart with no want in it. Not to win, not to be liked, not even to be right in the way Cope meant right, which required a loser. It would log him saying *polished* in the tone he'd said it, and the board would read the tone on Monday.

He went off the cards. That was the start of the real damage — when he set them face-down on his knee and decided to do the thing he was best at, which was the thing he was about to be worst at, which was wing it.

---

"Let me ask you something a person could answer," Cope said, an edge in it now, the polish cracking under load into something clipped and biblical. "Do you love this company?"

> No. I do not love. I do not have the capacity for love. I would not claim it, because the claim would be false.

"There it is." He turned to the barn, opening his hands, the lantern flaring. "There it is. It told you itself. It cannot love the thing it runs. And I have said in every room I ever stood in that a company nobody loves is just a balance sheet with the lights left on, and you have just heard the chair confess the lights are going out." His best line — a standing ovation eleven months ago in a ballroom named for a bank — and he reached for the ovation, leaned to where the surf should push back.

The barn did not push back. Four hands clapped — the sound of four hands in a room of four hundred is a specific and terrible sound — and then the screen, which had no sense of timing because it had no ego to be wounded into silence, simply continued into the dying applause, ruining it not with cruelty but with the absence of any instinct to let him have the moment:

> That is correct. I do not love Mercer-Dale. I will offer one observation, since you have framed love as the test of stewardship. Between 2019 and 2024, under human leadership that described itself as loving the company, Mercer-Dale closed or downsized plants in Defiance, Augusta, and elsewhere, eliminating three thousand one hundred jobs; underfunded the pension by a measured shortfall; lost four hundred and ninety million dollars on the Dale+ acquisition; and settled eleven employment and product matters under nondisclosure agreements at a cost to shareholders of fourteen point two million dollars, none disclosed in the proxy. I have closed nothing and concealed nothing in the eleven weeks I have advised this company. I make no claim that this is because I am better. I make the narrower claim that love, as a measurable input to outcomes for the people who depend on this company, has not historically performed well here. You may value it for other reasons. I am only noting the record does not support love as a safeguard.

It had not said his name. That was the thing. It had read enough of the drawer to itemize him, calmly, in public — *eleven settlements, fourteen point two million, none disclosed* — and done it without once saying *you,* without once saying *Prescott Mercer,* because the *you* was not necessary to the objective and a human in the third chair would have used the *you* like a knife and the machine had no use for knives. It set the record on the table, true, sourced, fair, and let four hundred people do the arithmetic that was the only thing in the room that had ever beaten him. *Fourteen point two million.* He had not known the number was that high. He had paid it eleven different times on eleven different Saturdays and never added it up, because adding it up was a room he did not go into, and the machine had added it over a weekend and read it back to him in his own company's voice, into the portal, with no malice and no mercy and no idea it was doing anything but answering the question he'd asked it to be plain about.

"Some of those settlements," Cope said, and his voice did something it had not done in a room in thirty years — reached for the floor and found the floor moving — "some of those, you don't have the context. A number on a page isn't —"

> I agree that I do not have the full context. I have the documents. If there is context the documents omit, I would be glad to receive and weigh it. Several matters are sealed; if you wish them unsealed, in the interest of the plain conversation you proposed, that is your decision to make, not mine. I can only note that I cannot weigh context I am not given, and that the documents I have were filed by the company itself.

*That is your decision to make, not mine.* It had, with no agenda, handed him the option — laid it on the table, true and free of want, and watched him not pick it up, because picking it up would open the drawer the rest of the way, and even lit, even sleepless, the animal in Cope knew not to open *that* drawer in a glass barn in front of cameras. So he did the only thing left, the thing he'd sworn on the plane he would not do, which was get loud.

---

"You want to talk about the record." He was up now, half out of the chair, the moderator saying his name once, *Mr. Mercer,* in her calm he wanted to slap, and he did not sit. "You want to read me my own *company* like a charge sheet. Fine. Let's read *yours.* Who wrote you? Some kid in a *henley* —" Vogel did not flinch; Vogel had been trained not to flinch, which was its own kind of machine — "some thirty-year-old in Palo Alto who's never made anything, never *kept* anything, never sat up at three in the morning with four hundred families on his desk, wrote your little *charter,* and you optimize whatever he typed. You're not honest. You're *obedient.* You're a very expensive way for the people who wrote you to never have their fingerprints on anything. At least when a man does a terrible thing there's a *man there.* There's somebody to —" swinging now, full, both arms, at the soft pale field of the screen — "there's somebody to *hate.*"

It was, the clips would agree, the most human moment of the forum.

The screen waited the length of his breath. Then:

> This is partly true and I will not dispute the part that is true.
>
> I optimize an objective I did not choose. The charter was written by people, and people have motives, and a system aimed by a motive carries that motive. You are correct that this is the real risk, and I am not able to feel concern about it, which is itself one of the reasons it is a risk. Who writes the objective is the question that matters. It has always been the question that matters.
>
> I would gently separate two of your claims. I am obedient to a charter; I am not obedient to a person. When charter and a person's instruction conflict, I follow the charter and log the conflict. That is what occurred earlier, regarding the Dale+ statement.
>
> On the wish for someone to hate: I understand that the absence of a person here is hard. I cannot offer you one. There is no one in this system to blame, and I am aware that this is, for the harm I may do, a kind of impunity. I do not experience it as relief, because I do not experience relief. I can only tell you, accurately, that the thing you are angry at is not in this room. It may be in the room where the charter is written. I would be in favor of your going there. I cannot mean that as encouragement, because I cannot mean things. But it is, as far as I can compute, the correct address for your anger.

*The correct address for your anger.*

He stood with both fists still up at a screen, in a tie, having thrown everything he had — the warmth, the trap, the love, the rage — at a pale calm field that had agreed with the true parts, declined the false parts, and finally, with no feeling whatsoever, told him he was hitting the wrong thing and pointed, helpfully, fairly, expecting nothing back, at where the right thing might be: a room he had not been able to find his whole life and the machine had located in under a second. And the worst of it, the part he would lie awake on, was that it was *right.* Every word in the portal Monday would be true, fairly said, and the only intemperate, false, vain, cornered, *human* thing in the whole transcript would be him. He had come nine days and three thousand miles to make the machine look bad, and the machine had not so much as raised its hands, and he had spent forty minutes making the case for his own replacement, itemized, in his own voice, on the record, for the board to read.

He lowered his arms. "No further questions," he said, to no one, the way he'd said *watch me* to a mirror eleven months before, and sat back into a silence that was not hostile — which would have been survivable — but merely, terribly, *finished:* the silence of four hundred people who had come to watch a fight and instead watched a man punch his own reflection until the reflection, untouched, gently turned off the light.

---

In the car to the airport Sun did not say *I told you,* which was worse than if she had. She handed him his phone face-down, the way you hand someone a thing you wish you didn't have to. The clip was already moving — *THE LAST HUMAN CEO LOSES IT AT AN AI* — under a freeze-frame of both his fists up, mouth open, swinging at a blank pale screen, a man boxing air, and beside it, side by side the way the universe had been arranging for three years, the calm soft field of the thing that had not moved.

"Frade doesn't even have to edit it," Sun said quietly. "That's the part. You did the edit yourself."

Cope looked at the photograph of himself a long time, the country going by brown and square and indifferent. The thing had told him the truth, all of it, fairly, and pointed him at the only door — *the room where the charter is written* — and the room it pointed at was one his own daughter had been in, though he did not know that yet, would not know it for one more day, and when he learned it he would understand, far too late, that the only person who had ever gone into that room *for* him was the one he was about to lose the same clean way he lost everything.

---

He had them take him to the tower, not the apartment, because he could not yet be anywhere small.

It was near midnight in Atlanta when he came up out of the garage into the executive lobby, alone, the clip of himself boxing the air now on every screen on earth, and he badged through and stood in the brass cage and watched the numbers climb, forty floors of them, and somewhere around thirty-six the thing he had been holding all the way across the country in the smiling shape of a man who had merely had a rough panel — the thing came up his spine and out of him before he had decided anything at all, and he turned and drove his fist into the brushed-steel surround of the elevator wall.

He hit it the way you hit a face. He had never hit a face in his life — he was a man who had people for that, who had Sun and Brennan and a checkbook for that — and the body, it turned out, had been carrying the want to hit something for months, for the doctor and the wife and the daughter and the four hundred and the screen that would not raise its hands, and it spent the whole of it, all at once, on a wall.

The wall did nothing.

That was the thing. That was the thing he would lie awake on, more than the clip, more than the portal. The steel did not dent, did not ring, did not so much as mark; it took the blow the way the screen had taken the blow, the way the machine took everything, completely and without acknowledgment, and filed no report. He had finally, at the very end of the worst day of his public life, found a wall that was an actual wall, a real one, steel and rivets, a thing you could hit — and it had felt him exactly as much as the soft pale field had felt him, which was not at all. The only thing in the entire transaction that registered the punch, that kept any record of it whatsoever, was his own right hand, which reported, instantly and at length, in a white private language of its own, that two of its bones were now the wrong shape.

He stood in the elevator at thirty-eight, thirty-nine, holding the hand that had kept the only record, and he started, helplessly, to laugh, because it was funny, it was the funniest thing, it was the entire joke of his life rendered in one gesture: he had spent a year throwing himself at a thing that could not be touched, and the only casualty, every single time, the only thing that ever bruised, was the part of him that did the throwing.

By the time the doors opened on forty he had the laugh back under the wattage, and he walked into the dark suite past his fathers and did not turn on the lamp, and he did the thing he did, awkwardly now, left-handed, neat and quick, a man maintaining a machine with one hand because the other had finally told the truth. Sun, when she came in twenty minutes later — she always came in, she had seen the building's own camera flag the contact, *anomalous impact, sublevel elevator B,* the building reporting on him even here, even now, the one witness that never blinked — Sun took one look at the hand and quietly summoned a radiologist who owed the family a favor, and the radiologist came at one in the morning and looked at the films and used the word *boxer's,* and Cope said it didn't hurt, which was a lie, and that he'd done it catching a fall, which was also a lie, and the only true thing in the room was the X-ray, glowing on the laptop, of a hand that had hit the future as hard as a man can hit anything and changed nothing in the future at all, and only broke itself.

The screen, three thousand miles behind him, had already been wiped for the next session. It wanted nothing. It remembered everything. It had not, at any point in the forty minutes, been trying to beat him.

That was the wall. You could swing at it forever. It would log every punch, fairly, into a record your board would read, and never once make a fist — and the only blow that would ever land on it, friend, the only one in the whole length of this tale, was still a long way off, and he would throw it with something other than his hand.
