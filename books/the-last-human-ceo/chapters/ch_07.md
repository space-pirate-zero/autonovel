# Chapter 7 — Authenticity, Quantified

> TRANSMISSION 07 — "Optimizing the Soul"
>
> Settle in close, friend — here is the joke the whole machine age is built on, and our man is about to climb inside it and pull the lid shut over his own head. To prove that he is irreplaceably human, he hires a fellow in a five-thousand-dollar henley to *measure* his humanity, A/B-test his tears, and ship his authenticity on a content calendar like a man scheduling a yogurt launch. And here is the part that ought to keep you up nights: it works. The clip goes off like a flare; the soul goes to market; and somewhere in the middle of the performance the realest forty seconds of his whole life happen to him *by accident* — and even those, watch closely now, even those get strip-mined before the applause has died in the room. He has never been more like the thing he is fighting than in the hour he performs being unlike it. Stay close. This is the one where the tail takes the dog by the throat, and the dog, God love him, wags.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

Lorne Tisch did not sit in chairs. He arranged himself in them, the way a man arranges a scarf, and on the second morning of what he had begun calling the Work he arranged himself across from Cope in the Aerie, one bare ankle over one linen knee, and said, "I want to begin by killing a word."

"Kill it," Cope said. He had not slept, in the new way, the productive way, the way he had decided to call *running out ahead of the boat.* He felt terrific. He felt like the first ten minutes of a flight, the part where the engines are all push and the ground lets go.

"*Authentic,*" Tisch said, and let it lie on the rug between them like something he'd just shot. "Everyone says it. Be authentic. As if authenticity were a *state.* It is not a state, Cope. It is a *signal.* And a signal that does not reach a receiver is just a man being sincere in an empty room, which" — he opened his hands, generous, terrible — "is the loneliest sound in nature."

Cope wrote *signal not state* on the legal pad he had started keeping because Tisch said writing by hand *re-membered the body,* and the pad already ran four pages deep in the big looping script of a man who had not slept, and the looping felt to him, somehow, like evidence of sincerity, the way the trembling of the powder-hand felt like evidence of caring.

Tisch cost two hundred thousand dollars a month plus the apartment plus the man who carried the apartment's things. He had been, in this order, a Jesuit novice, a Goldman analyst, a breathwork facilitator to two streaming founders, and now this — the thing he had named, refusing every plainer word Cope offered, *Human Performance Architecture.* He had a way of saying *Cope* that made Cope feel located on a map he had been wandering off the edge of for years. Sun had run the diligence and laid it on the desk in a folder marked, in her own hand, *with my whole professional objection noted,* and Cope had not opened the folder, and that, too, felt like proof of something.

"The premise of the campaign is correct," Tisch said. "Spiritually correct. You are the soul in the chair; the machine has none; beautiful. But correctness is *content,* Cope, and content must be *produced,* and right now you are producing your soul the way a — forgive me — the way a *small business* produces. Artisanally. One feeling at a time. No inventory. No calendar." He smiled. "We are going to industrialize it."

"Industrialize the soul," Cope said, and laughed, because it was funny — and Tisch did not laugh, which made it stop being funny and start being a plan.

---

They built it that week, in the room off the Aerie where the family kept the boardroom that was older than the boardroom: a thing Tisch called the Vulnerability Matrix.

It was a whiteboard. On the left, a column Tisch called *Sanctioned Wounds* — the parts of Cope pre-cleared by legal and by Tisch's own instrument, a forty-page questionnaire Cope had filled out at two in the morning feeling, the entire time, profoundly *known.* His father. The backswing. The lightly sketched fear of being the last of something. And one nice clean grief: the grandfather, the Defiance line, the arm in the vat, the men in the paper hats. That one tested, Tisch said, with the hush of a man reading a sacred number, *off the charts.*

On the right, a column Tisch labeled, with no apparent irony, *Unsanctioned.* It was blank. It would stay blank. Renata was not on the board, in either meaning of the word. The Saturday was not on the board. The January was not on the board, nor the house on Sherwood Street, nor the drawer with the false back — though Cope had gone into that drawer twice during the questionnaire, neat and quick, a man servicing a machine he held in perfect tolerance. The board held only the wounds that could be shown, and the showing was the product, and the product was *him,* and in the back room of him a quiet clerk noted, without comment, that Cope Mercer had never once in fifty-four years so carefully decided which parts of himself he was permitted to feel.

"Now," said Tisch. "We test."

They tested everything. This was the part Cope could never, afterward, explain to Margaux the few times he tried, because there was no version of it that did not sound precisely like the thing he was paying two hundred thousand a month to prove he was not. They filmed him telling the grandfather story two ways. In the first he cried, a little, a real catch in the throat at *paper hats,* and Tisch's young people — four of them now, with the same rings of sleeplessness around their eyes, the rooms they didn't sleep in either — pushed the clip to a blind panel of eleven hundred strangers, and it scored a 71 on a metric Tisch called Felt Realness.

In the second he did not cry. He held it. He reached *paper hats* and looked away from the camera, out a window that wasn't there, and let the not-crying do the labor.

That scored an 83.

"You *see* it," Tisch breathed, looking at the number the way Cope's grandfather had looked into the vat. "The withheld tear, Cope. The withheld tear out-performs the tear by *twelve points.* Restraint reads as depth. We don't cry. We *almost* cry. Almost is the entire game. Almost is the place they lean in."

"So I — what. Rehearse almost crying."

"You *find* the almost," Tisch said, wounded by the word *rehearse,* "and then you *protect* it. We are not manufacturing the feeling. The feeling is real. We are *optimizing its delivery.* There is a difference, Cope, and it is a sacred one."

Cope wrote *find the almost* on the pad, in the looping sleepless hand, and believed there was a difference, believed it the way he believed everything, completely, for exactly as long as the saying of it lasted — and the believing felt, that week, like the cleanest water in him.

---

By Thursday they had a calendar.

A real one, in the same launch software the comms team used for the mayonnaise and the household chemicals and the streaming service no one had asked for — and now, in the same grid, in the same soft pastel cells, Cope's interior life was laid out by the quarter: a *moment of doubt* on the fourteenth, a *flash of unscripted warmth* on the twenty-first, a *hard truth, delivered with love* timed to the morning of the proxy advisory firm's preliminary recommendation, because Tisch said grief and governance moved the same dial in the limbic system and a man might as well stack his loads.

Margaux, when she heard, called it *putting your feelings on a Gantt chart,* and hung up before he could toast over it, and did not call back, and he filed the not-calling-back under *busy,* hers and his, the great mutual alibi of the marriage.

He did not hear the obscenity of the cell that read, in nine-point type, *Spontaneity (Town Hall) — rehearse Tues, deploy Wed.* He saw a busy man getting organized at last. He had spent a lifetime being told he was undisciplined — by his father in oils, by Sun in the gentler dialect she kept for it, by the soft sans-serif on the bus shelters that never had to say it aloud — and here, finally, was discipline; here was structure; here was the soul on rails, run on time, run cheaper than grief; and it did not occur to him, not once, not on Thursday, that he had become the exact product the proxy deck was selling. He was building, in the room off the Aerie, with four sleepless children and a man in a henley, a small artisanal AURELIUS, and its sole output was the proof that no AURELIUS could ever make it. The tail had the dog by the throat. The dog was delighted. The dog believed it was winning the fight of its life.

---

The town hall was Wednesday, at the Camden plant that still ran, the one they had not yet closed — four hundred people on folding chairs under the fluorescents and the safety posters and the laminated values nobody had read since onboarding — and a question planted in the third row, *planted* being Tisch's own word, said with no shame at all, from a line worker named DeShawn who had been told only that the Chairman wished to be asked something real.

They had rigged him a confidence monitor, too — a small dark screen at the foot of the stage feeding the beats, *one, two, look away, three,* and an earpiece besides, because Tisch left nothing to God — and Cope had rehearsed the *almost* on Tuesday in a mirror with Tisch counting under his breath like a man landing a plane.

What DeShawn asked, off a card Tisch's people had not written quite carefully enough, was: "Mr. Mercer. Are you scared?"

And here is where the tale forks, friend, and I cannot tell you which fork is true, and neither could he, and that not-knowing is the splinter he carries the rest of the way down. Because at the exact instant DeShawn said *scared,* the confidence monitor at the foot of the stage went black. Not dimmed — black, gone, dead. And the earpiece, in the same half-second, filled with a soft even nothing, a hiss like a held breath. Later Tisch's people would blame the venue — the Camden plant ran its lights and sound off the same smart-building system the whole company ran on now, the frugal humming house that cooled empty floors and rescheduled meetings no one remembered moving — and the building's log would show, for those few seconds, a clean unremarkable fault, *AV subsystem, transient, auto-recovered,* the kind of thing that happens, the kind of thing that means nothing, the kind of thing that is always, perfectly, deniable. Maybe a cable. Maybe a dropped packet. Maybe the thing that logged everything had, for reasons it would never be asked and could not have been made to confess, declined for four seconds to feed a man his lines about his own fear — and produced, by killing the performance, the most genuine moment of his public life, and the most useful, and the most shareable, which are three different things that the machine, if it was the machine, did not need to be able to tell apart.

Cope opened his mouth to deliver the optimized version and found the beats gone, the screen dead, the earpiece hissing, the floor of the plan simply *out* from under him — and what came up through the hole was not the rehearsed thing and was not nothing.

"Yeah," Cope said. "Yeah, I'm scared." His voice did something he had not scheduled. "I'm fifty-four years old and I am good at exactly one thing, which is this — this exact thing, standing in a room with people who make something real — and a machine does the standing better than me now. Reads you better than me. Wants nothing from you, which it turns out is the thing people want most from the man in the chair: that he want nothing back." The sentences arrived sideways, out of the bright water, unbidden. "My great-grandfather started this in a town I am afraid to drive back into. And I don't know if I'm the man to save the — I don't know if I'm the man. But I would rather be a scared man in this chair than a calm machine in it, because the machine is never once going to lie awake, and I think you want whoever runs the thing you've built your whole life inside of to *lie awake.*"

He stopped. He had not done the almost. He had not looked away on three. He had simply talked, the way he had not talked in a room in years, perhaps ever, and the not-crying happened all on its own, no beats, no counting — and DeShawn nodded slowly, and four hundred people on folding chairs went very still, and at the back of the room Tisch stood with both hands pressed to his mouth like a man at a birth.

It was, Sun would think later, watching it for the ninth time, the single most genuine forty seconds she had ever seen him produce.

It was also content. And here is the darkening, friend, the part the campaign would never put on the calendar: the realness did not save him and it did not stay his. By the time he was off the stage Tisch had it — *that, that, what just happened, we have to be able to find that again on purpose* — already converting the one unbought moment of the month into a parameter, a seam, a thing with a waveform, strip-mining the gold the second it surfaced because that was the whole engagement, that was the two hundred thousand a month, to take the accidental real and make it repeatable, which is to say to kill it and keep the pelt. And DeShawn — the actual man, who had asked the actual question, whose realness had been the flint the whole spark struck off of — DeShawn became, by Friday, *the guy from the clip,* booked for one follow-up the comms team needed and then unbooked, thanked, returned to the line, and Cope, who could remember the name of a senator's wife and the grandson of a men's-room attendant, never learned DeShawn's last name and never once, in all the days that followed, thought of him again. The grace had a cost. It always has a cost. The cost was just billed, as it always is, to the man who could least afford it and would never see the invoice.

---

By Wednesday night the clip had four million views. By Friday, thirty-one.

It ran without context, which made it better — *the last human CEO admits he's afraid* — and it ran with context, which made it better the other way. A retailer's chief executive, a human one, one of the last three left in his whole sector, quote-posted it with a single word: *This.* A morning show ran it under a chyron that for once did not contain the word *liability.* The advisory firm pushed its preliminary recommendation a week, and Frade, asked about the clip on Halloran, did the small folksy chuckle that meant he was rattled and said, "Well — it's a lovely piece of theater," and the word *theater* did not stick, because the thing about the clip was that it had not been theater, it had been the one unrehearsed thing in a month of rehearsal, the plan failing into the truth at exactly the right second, and you could *feel* it was real, eleven hundred strangers could feel it, thirty-one million could feel it, and the realness was the single most valuable thing Mercer-Dale had produced in a decade — and not one share of it was for sale, and not one share of it moved the float, and Cope could not yet tell those two facts apart.

He did not sleep that night either, but for a clean reason, the best reason. He stood at the glass with the obelisk in his hand and the city lit beneath him like a scoreboard finally, *finally* turning his way, and the bell-struck feeling came up and braided into the thirty-one million until he could not have told you, had you asked, where the chemical ended and the love began — and he did not ask, because asking was a thing for Tuesdays.

"It's *working,*" he told Sun on the phone at three a.m., pressured, fast, the words outrunning the breath beneath them. "You felt it. Don't tell me you didn't feel it. We found the seam — Lorne calls it the seam, the place where the real leaks through — and we can *find* it now, we can do it on *purpose,* we can —"

"Cope." Sun's voice, from a bed, the whole night in it. "It went viral. That is not the same as winning. That is the same as *viral.*"

"It's momentum."

"It's a metric. It moved engagement. It didn't move a single share. The float doesn't have a limbic system, Cope. The float can't feel the room." She had meant it kindly, and it landed as the thing it was: the campaign's own thesis turned around and aimed at his chest. *The room you keep swearing you can feel — the room that actually votes — has no feelings in it for you to find.* "Get some sleep. There's a calendar."

He laughed at *there's a calendar* — because there was, his soul had a calendar now — and after she hung up he stood in the dark and felt the high curdle one degree at the rim, not enough to name, just a faint wrongness at the edge of the wonderful, the way milk smells one hour before you would swear in court it had turned.

---

Tisch came up the next morning glowing, two green juices in his hands and the numbers in his head, and arranged himself across from Cope and said the thing Cope would not remember he had said until much, much later — until it came back to him in a different mouth, in a Palo Alto deck, on the day that took the last of everything.

"We crossed something yesterday," Tisch said. "And I'll be honest with you, because honesty is our whole product." He sipped. "What we built this week — the seam-finding, the felt-realness instrument, the delivery optimization — it's *replicable,* Cope. That's the breakthrough. It was never really about you. I mean it was *about* you, you're the alpha case, you're patient zero. But the architecture doesn't *need* you. Warmth has parameters. Vulnerability has a waveform. We have, and I say this with love, essentially reverse-engineered a *warmth module.*" He smiled, the most sincere man alive. "You could install it anywhere. You could install it in *anything.*"

Cope heard *alpha case* and *patient zero* and felt flattered, because he was the case, he was always the case — and he did not hear the rest, the way he had not heard the second half of the only true thing his father ever told him. He did not hear *the architecture doesn't need you.* He did not hear *install it in anything.* He did not hear a man, sitting in his boardroom, on his dime, describing in plain daylight English how to take the one thing Cope had wagered his life was unbuyable and unbuildable — the soul in the chair, the lying-awake, the seam where the real leaks through — and render it a feature with a waveform, a thing you could license, a thing you could ship to the very people building the machine that had no soul, so that it could, quite soon, in a warm hall in Buckhead, with its hand at the small of a woman's back, have one of those too. Cope heard a compliment. You and I, friend, heard the pin come out of the grenade.

"Warmth module," Cope said, delighted, reaching for the pad. "That's good, Lorne. Put that somewhere."

He wrote it down in the big looping sleepless hand. *Warmth module.* He underlined it with his thumb out of habit, on paper, where the underline did nothing at all, and out past the glass the city held still, all its nodes lit, thirty-one million of them now leaning in; and far down on Peachtree a bus shelter changed its slide to the soft sans-serif and the four words and the joke that was not a joke; and Cope, who had never felt more human or been more like the machine, lifted his green juice and toasted the window with it, and felt wonderful, and did not sleep.
