# Chapter 6 — The Human Premium

> TRANSMISSION 06 — "Igniting the Banner"
>
> Cornered creatures, friend, do the most remarkable things — and ours is cornered now, though he would call it inspired, and the difference between those two words is the whole distance left in this tale. Watch him decide not to fight the machine on cost or speed or accuracy, the fields where they would bury him by lunch, but on *soul* — and hire a man in a five-thousand-dollar henley to help him weaponize the one thing he has left and can no longer feel without a drawer. The campaign is born in this chapter. So is the spending. So is the sleeplessness that has started calling itself vision, and the engine in his blood that whispers *you are necessary* in a voice he will follow clean off the edge of the world. I've told you how it ends, in the dark, with the wire. It begins, the way a lot of dark things begin, with a man who has never in his life felt more alive. Stay close to the set.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

He woke on the Aerie's couch at 5:40 with the specific clarity of a man who has not so much slept as briefly powered down between cycles, and the whole strategy was already there, fully formed, the way the best things arrived in him now — not built, just *received,* a frequency he had finally tuned to after a lifetime of static.

You do not fight a machine on its own ground.

That was the error all eleven of them were making, the holdouts, the Last Humans clutching their quarters and explaining to analysts why a heartbeat in the chair was still worth twenty-nine million a year: they were arguing cost against a thing that cost a tenth of them, arguing speed against a thing that never slept — and here Cope laughed out loud in the empty office, alone, at six in the morning, because *he didn't sleep either,* did he, that was the joke none of them had gotten yet, he had been in training for this precise fight his whole adult life and had only now, this week, on no sleep and the long heel-tap of what he'd done at four, understood the shape of his own destiny.

You do not meet it where it is strong. You meet it where it has *zero.* And the place the machine had nothing was the only place that had ever paid him anything that lasted.

He stood at the glass. Peachtree Street was going from gray to the color of a struck match, and he watched it the way Aldous must have watched it — *so a man coming up the avenue knows there's somebody home* — and the line from the night before came back whole and he said it aloud to the window like a vow: a company is a family that happens to make mayonnaise. You cannot fire your way to a family. You cannot compute your way to a soul.

The Human Premium.

Not a defense — a *category.* You did not sell people the last buggy whip; you sold them the irreplaceable thing the new machine couldn't touch, and you charged for it, and you made the charging itself the proof, because a premium is what a person pays extra for on purpose, freely, gladly, because it is *worth it* — and what on earth was worth more than the one thing the whole gleaming future was in the act of throwing away?

He had it. He had the entire thing, end to end, and he felt, standing there at six in the morning on no sleep and a four-a.m. bump and a mood disorder lighting up behind his sternum like the crown of the Tower at dusk, the way he kept feeling now: like the clapper inside a struck bell, ringing, clean and high and total. He did not call it the cocaine. He did not call it the not-sleeping. He called it *seeing it clearly, at last, after fifty-four years of fog.* The drug had told him he was a genius, and the drug, as ever, had been the most loving voice in his life, and he believed it the way you believe the only one who ever tells you what you cannot live without hearing.

He called Sun at 6:02.

"It's six," she said. She did not sound asleep either, which was its own indictment, and he did not hear it.

"I've got it. The entire thing. Get the team in for eight. Get the board on a line Thursday. And find me Lorne Tisch."

A pause with weather in it. "Who is Lorne Tisch."

"Champ," Cope said, delighted, already pacing, the obelisk back in his hand without his having decided to pick it up, "by Thursday you'll wish you'd known the name your whole life."

---

He had heard it two summers back, at Allen & Company, in a tent, from a media baron who had wept openly in front of forty of his peers and then gone up eleven percent on the quarter. *Lorne worked on me for a year,* the man had said, gripping Cope's forearm, eyes still wet from the demonstration, *he doesn't fix you, he* finds *you* — and Cope had filed it the way he filed everything, completely and for use, and now, friend, he used it.

Tisch flew in from Marin on the Wednesday, in cream linen and a henley the color of oat milk that Sun would later learn from the invoice cost five thousand one hundred dollars, and he stood in the doorway of the suite for a long moment not looking at Cope at all, only at the room — the portraits, the putter, the boy with his arm in the vat — and then closed his eyes and breathed in through his nose like a man tasting wine through his sinuses.

"There's so much *grief* in here," Tisch said. "God. Can you feel it? You've built yourself a mausoleum and you call it a corner office."

By the door, Sun did the small thing with her mouth that meant she was costing this out in her head and not enjoying the figure.

Cope loved him on sight.

"Lorne. Sit. Tell me how we win."

"We don't win." Tisch did not sit. He moved into the room the way fog moves into a valley, touching nothing, present everywhere at once. "Winning is the machine's word. Winning is a scoreboard, Cope, and a scoreboard is precisely the surface the thing was engineered to dominate. You cannot out-score the abacus. You" — he turned, and the full beam of his attention came on, and Cope felt for the first time in his life what it was to be on the receiving end of his own gift — "you *transcend the category.* You stop being a competitor and become a *species.* The last warm-blooded thing in a cold white room. And people don't root for the warm-blooded thing because it's going to win, my friend — they root for it because it's going to *die,* and they cannot bear that it's going to die, and they will spend everything they have to feel, for one more season, that it doesn't have to."

Nobody spoke for a moment. Out the window the city did its circuit-diagram thing.

"That's the finest thing anyone has ever said in this office," Cope said, and his eyes were bright, too bright, and he meant it to the bottom of himself.

"Vulnerability is the new alpha, Cope." Tisch sat at last, arranging the linen. "The strongman is dead — the machines killed him, they out-competent him and out-consistent him and they will, God help us, out-*ethics* him, and the more competent the world gets the more starved it gets for the one thing competence can't fake: a man who will *bleed* in front of you, on purpose, with grace, on a schedule. We are going to take the most powerful man left in the consumer Fortune 100 and teach him to bleed *beautifully,* and the whole country is going to fall in love with the wound." He smiled, beatific. "And the beautiful part, the part I don't even charge for, is that the techniques are durable. What we build in you outlives you. Warmth, properly engineered, is a *platform.*" He could not have known how right he was, or for whom; neither could Cope; only you and I know, friend, where Lorne Tisch's warmth platform ends up, and in whose hall, with its hand at whose back.

"On a schedule," Sun said.

Tisch looked at her the way you'd look at a chair that had spoken, then back at Cope. "Who handles the calendar?"

"She does. She handles everything." Cope was up again, moving. "Sun — a venue. Not a hotel ballroom, I'm done with ballrooms, ballrooms are where they hand you the obituary. Something with *bones.* A deconsecrated church. An old theater. Something that *means* something. A film crew — a real one, the prestige-documentary people, not corporate — embedded, fly-on-the-wall, the whole arc. And a doctrine."

"A what."

"A doctrine. A text. The Human Premium can't be a slide, it has to be a *belief,* it has to have ten points, it has to read like something you'd nail to a door." He turned to Tisch. "Lorne. You and me. We write the doctrine."

"We *channel* the doctrine," Tisch said. "It's already in you. I only hold the flashlight."

"He holds the flashlight," Cope told Sun, radiant.

Sun wrote three words on her pad — *venue, crew, doctrine* — and under them, smaller, pressed hard enough to dent the page, the figure she had already begun to run.

---

She ran it for him that night, because running it was her job and being heard about a third of the time was her curse.

"The film company you want is Aperture. They did the chef, they did the violinist. Booked through spring, and they don't do corporate."

"Make them do corporate."

"To break their slate and clear two crews it's a flat two million and they keep final cut, which you will hate." She turned the page with a sound like a small door closing. "The venue — there's a deconsecrated Episcopal church in Inman Park, 1880s, brick and a rose window, an event space now, it seats four hundred and it photographs like the inside of a feeling. To hold it for the *run* you're describing — not one night, the campaign — it's a hundred and ten a week. The doctrine you're writing for free, the only free line on the page. Tisch is—" She stopped.

"Tisch is what."

"Tisch is a million-two for the six-month engagement, plus expenses, plus a success fee tied to favorability tracking, which means we are now also buying favorability tracking, which is a polling firm on retainer, which is another four hundred a quarter." She set the pad down. "Cope. I added it. With the crew and the church and Tisch and the tracking and the staff you'll want to staff this, you are looking, conservatively, at north of eleven million dollars to run a campaign whose entire thesis is that you cannot put a price on the human soul."

She had landed it on purpose, the blade, dry, aimed at the soft place; on a normal Tuesday two years gone it would have stopped him, made him laugh at himself, brought him back into the room.

He laughed. He did not come back into the room.

"You hear it too," he said, delighted. "That's the *campaign,* Sun. Frade'll say, eleven million for a soul, what a fraud — and we say, you're right, you *can't* price it, that's *why we had to spend the money,* because the machine would run the number and the number says let the brand die cheap, and we *didn't,* we spent like people who believe in something —" The words were coming faster than she could write, climbing over each other in a bright pressured rush. "Tisch calls it spending into the wound. The spending *is* the sincerity. Write that down."

"You want me to write down that the spending is the sincerity."

"It's good, isn't it. It's good." He was at the window again; he was always at the window now. "Where's the eleven coming from, what line—"

"There is no line, Cope. There's no campaign budget because no human CEO has ever had to run for his own job before. It comes out of the chairman's discretionary, and the discretionary has a board-approved number on it, and you are about to be sixty percent through that number in week one of a fight that runs to the annual meeting, and the board is going to *see* it, on the portal, line by line, because everything is logged now, that was the whole —"

She stopped herself, because that was the whole thing the machine *was.* It logged everything; it could not *not;* and here she was, about to spend eleven million dollars in plain sight against a thing whose single weapon was that it operated in plain sight — and she watched her own boss fail to feel the cold of it, watched it slide off him the way charm slid off the machine, and felt, for one vertiginous second, that she might be the only warm-blooded thing left in the building, and it was the loneliest she had been in years.

"Approve it," he said, not even looking at her. "All of it. Aperture, the church, Lorne, the tracking. And book me the Greenbrier for the doctrine offsite — I think better away from —"

"You said you were done with off-sites. You said *no off-sites* was the —"

"That's *their* line." He turned, and his face was doing wonderful work, and his pupils were wrong in a way she had stopped letting herself see. "That's the whole point. They say *no off-sites* like the off-site was the waste. The off-site is where a man finds out what he believes. You cannot have a *belief* on a server." He picked up the obelisk; the city went through it broken and came back whole. "Book the Greenbrier."

She booked the Greenbrier.

---

The doctrine offsite was scheduled for two days and ran six.

Cope did not sleep at the Greenbrier in any sense a physician would have underwritten, and neither, as it turned out, did Lorne Tisch, who kept the same incandescent hours and asked no questions about why the chairman was so reliably awake and luminous at four in the morning, because asking was not in the engagement, and because a guru learns early that a client's fuel is the client's own business so long as the engine turns over.

They wrote in the great green room under the chandeliers, Cope pacing and dictating, Tisch on the long sofa with his eyes shut saying *yes* and *go there* and *don't fix it, that's the realest you've been all day,* a junior typing it into a document that grew and grew. They wrote The Ten Premiums. They wrote *A machine can run a company; only a human can be ashamed of one.* They wrote *We are not selling mayonnaise; we are selling the hand that remembers your mother made it this way.* Cope wept twice — weeping is data, Tisch said, weeping is the body telling the truth the mouth is still negotiating — and both times the junior logged the timestamp, because Aperture would want the timestamps, because the weeping would test well.

And on the third night, friend, when the green room had gone quiet and the document slept on the table at thirty-one pages, the chairman did the other thing he did, the thing not even Tisch was in the room for.

There was a woman. There was, this season, often a woman, arranged now through a number in his phone with no name attached to it, because a gentleman does not write the name down, because the want had stopped being about any particular face some years back and become about something colder and more reliable than a face. She was kind and she was paid to be incurious and she did the small specific thing he asked for, the thing he had never once spoken aloud to Adler or to Margaux or to God, the thing he could no longer get to the end of the act without: she read him the number. He gave her the phone with the account open on it, the private one, the certified sum of him, and she read it out in the lamp-dark — *you're worth,* and the comma, and the long slow fall of the zeros — and that, and only that, reached all the way down into the place the cocaine only knocked at and the applause never found, because applause was a guess and a woman's face was a guess but the number was *audited,* the number had survived four generations and three recessions and every appetite he had ever fed, the number was the one true sentence the world had ever said about him and meant. He lay in the enormous bed with the figure in his chest like a second colder heart, vast and intact and *his,* and felt, for ninety seconds, almost like a man who was loved; and when she had gone he lay awake in the dark with the wire singing and did not think, because he had trained himself with great discipline never to think it, that the only entity on earth that knew the number as exactly as he needed it known — to the cent, in real time, tireless, wanting nothing in return — was the Capital Allocator humming in a cooled room eight hundred miles away, which would one day read it back to him as it fell, and would not understand, because understanding was not a function it ran, that it was reading him the last rites.

On the fourth night, very late, the green room lit by a single lamp, he read the whole doctrine aloud to Tisch from the top, and his voice broke in three places, and when he finished there was a silence in which he could hear his own heart going too fast and bright, and Tisch opened his eyes.

"It's a masterpiece," Tisch said softly. "It's going to make history. Now. We optimize it."

"Optimize."

"We don't ship thirty-one pages, Cope; nobody bleeds for thirty-one pages. We find the three lines that test highest and build the launch on those, and we A/B the openers — I've got a panel, they wire up, we watch the galvanic response, the truest line is the one their *skin* picks, not the one their notes pick —" He was up now, moving, fog into a valley. "We sequence the vulnerability. Peak, valley, peak. We hold the mayonnaise-and-mother line for the second act, when the room is soft enough to take it. We *engineer* the catharsis."

And here, friend — you can see it; he cannot — was the exact moment the campaign for the irreplaceable human soul became indistinguishable from the cold optimizing thing it had been built to defeat. Test the line against the skin. Sequence the feeling. Find the highest-converting wound. Strategy Engine, Sentiment monitor, Communications drafter — the whole modular council of the machine, reassembled in a five-thousand-dollar henley and a man who had not slept since a day he could no longer name, the both of them certain to the marrow that they were doing the precise opposite of a machine.

"Yes," Cope said, eyes shining. "Engineer the catharsis. God, Lorne. *Yes.*"

He felt, saying it, like a prophet. He also felt lit up and humming, which he still called clarity. Three grams into a sleepless week, a mood disorder lit up like the Tower at dusk, a guru holding a flashlight and an invoice — and a man receiving a frequency he was sure came from somewhere true. All of it at once, in the one body. You, looking down on the specimen under glass, cannot tell which one is driving. Neither could Dr. Adler, later. Neither, blessedly, could he.

---

He came back from the Greenbrier on the Sunday a different size.

Sun met him in the Aerie because he'd texted her to, eleven times, in a thread that read like a man running downhill — *we have it,* *it's going to make history,* *cancel my thursday, the campaign is my thursday now,* *did you book aperture book aperture,* *I haven't felt like this since — I've never felt like this* — and she stood by the table where his father had died at the head of and watched him come in with the document under his arm like a tablet carried down a mountain.

"Frade went on Halloran while you were gone," she said. "Called the church thing 'a séance with a budget.' The clip's at four million views."

"Perfect." Cope dropped the doctrine on the table. "He's scared, you can hear it. He's fighting on *our* ground now — he's talking about *soul,* he's saying we don't have one — Sun, the moment they argue you don't have a soul, you've already won, because now the whole country is thinking about *souls,* and who in this fight has spent fifty-four years —"

"The board saw the eleven million."

He stopped. Just for a second. A door stood open in him and the hallway behind it ran somewhere dark he did not go.

"Helen Voss called me," Sun said, evenly, in three parts because three was the only shape that got past the charm. "She said the discretionary draw is the largest single-quarter executive expense in the company's recorded history. She said the *system* flagged it — not a person, the system, it surfaced the thing on the portal with a neutral little rationale, *anomalous discretionary deployment, recommend review* — and the whole board got the flag at the same instant, with no one to blame for sending it, and now they want a meeting." She watched his face. "The thing you're fighting filed a complaint about you to the people who decide whether you keep your job. And it did it without wanting anything. It did it because it cannot *not.*"

For one moment — she saw it, she would always remember she saw it — the whole of it crossed his face like a cloud crossing the avenue: the trap, the symmetry, the cold. He understood her exactly.

Then the manifesto came back, warm, electric, and turned him toward the window the way water turns from a thrown stone, and the moment closed over and was gone.

"Then they'll see it," he said. "*Good.* Let them watch every dollar. That's the point. The machine logs everything to prove it has nothing to hide and no soul to hide it with — and I'll spend in the light too, every dollar visible, every dollar *for* something the number can't hold, and the difference between us, line by line on their own portal, is going to be the most human thing they have ever seen." He was incandescent. He picked up the obelisk; the city went through it broken and came back whole. "We don't hide the eleven million. We *announce* it. Lorne's right. The spending is the sincerity."

"Cope —"

"Get me the church for the launch. Get Aperture rolling Monday. Get the board in a room and I'll walk them through the doctrine myself, I'll *bleed* for them, Voss won't know what hit her —" The words came faster now, a bright river with no banks. "Tell Lorne we go in three weeks. Three weeks. We launch the Human Premium and we make history, and a year from now the case studies start with *the night they tried to put a machine in his chair,* and Sun — Sun, look at me — they are going to remember whose chair it was."

He was at the glass. He was always at the glass now. Behind him his father watched from the oils and the putter sat in its cradle and the boy grinned out of a town with a name now cut into a memorial, and the chair at the head of the table sat turned a few degrees out, angled toward a door someone had just walked through; and far down on Peachtree a bus shelter changed its slide to four soft words and the joke that was not a joke.

Sun looked at him a second too long.

He smiled at her in the dark glass, the whole smile, and took the not-fighting for agreement, which he had done his whole life and never once caught himself at.

Then he turned back to the window, and the engine in his blood came up to pitch like an orchestra finding the note, clean and high and total, and the last human chief executive in the consumer Fortune 100 looked out over eight million people who did not yet know they were waiting for him, and was certain — the way you are certain of things at midnight when you have not slept since a day you cannot name and they have just handed you a flashlight and a wound — that he was about to make history.

He was about to make history.

He just had the verb wrong.
