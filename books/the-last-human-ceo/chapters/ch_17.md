# Chapter 17 — The Names for It

> TRANSMISSION 17 — "A Doctor Who Won't Be Charmed"
>
> At last, friend, a room he cannot toast his way out of. They have ambushed him with a living room and a tray and a small woman in a gray cardigan who will not touch her coffee — a psychiatrist, summoned by the two women who love him — and she is about to do the one thing no board and no activist and no machine has yet managed: say the plain clinical words for what he is, the mania *and* the cocaine, both, and refuse, flatly, to let him trade one as the alibi for the other. Addiction, she will tell him, is not a metaphor — and he has built a cathedral of metaphors, and moved into it, and never once gone down to the dirt beneath it to meet the sick man holding the foundation up. She is going to say the names. And before she lets him go she is going to name the worst risk out loud, plainly, the way you say a thing you need on the record — and watch him charm it, and pocket his keys, and at the foot of his own drive, with both unsteady hands, fold the paper. Stay close. This is the floor under the floor.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

They had ambushed him with a living room.

That was the first thing Cope understood, walking into the Buckhead house at nine on a Sunday he'd been told was about the lawyers — and it was a good ambush, he had to give them that, because the only way to get him into a room he did not want to enter was to make him believe he was the one who'd called the meeting. Margaux had made coffee. There was a tray. And there, in the wing chair nobody sat in, the one angled toward the window that his mother had reupholstered in 1981 and Margaux had never had the heart to redo, sat a small woman of about sixty in a gray cardigan with a notebook closed on her lap and no coffee in front of her — which he clocked the way he clocked everything, because the ones who don't take the coffee are the ones you can't get to.

"Cope," Margaux said. "This is Dr. Adler."

"Dad." Viv was on the sofa with her knees up. She'd flown in. He hadn't known she was in the country; the not-knowing arrived, was filed, did not reach his face. "Sit down. Please."

He did not sit. He stood in the doorway of his own living room in his weekend zip-up with the fine bright wire still going from the bump he'd done in the car at the foot of the drive — neat, quick, a man maintaining a machine — and ran the wattage up the way you bring up house lights, slow, warm, total.

"Well. This is a hell of a thing to wake up to. Three of my favorite women and a tray." He smiled at the doctor. "I'm sorry, I'm at a disadvantage. Cope Mercer. You're a friend of Margaux's?"

"I'm a psychiatrist," Dr. Adler said. "Your wife and your daughter asked me to come. They're worried about you. I'm here to talk with you, and then to tell you what I think."

No softening on it. No *I just wanted to chat.* He'd expected the softening — there was always a softening, that was the handle you grabbed — and its absence put a small cold spot in the middle of the warm.

"Worried," Cope said. He came in. He did not take the sofa, which would have put him level with Viv; he took the hard chair near the doorway, which let him keep the room in front of him, all of it, the exits. "I love that. I'm the best I've been in a decade and the women I love most have hired a — forgive me, I don't mean it the way it'll sound — a *grief counselor* for a man who isn't dead. That's the most Mercer thing that's ever happened in this house, and my father let a dog drown in this house."

"He didn't —" Margaux began.

"He *let* it. There's a difference and the family observed it." Rolling now, the feeling coming up under him. "Doctor, I'll save you the hourly. I sleep a little less than I should. I'm in the fight of my life — the company, the machine thing, it's been on the television. A man doesn't sleep eight hours during a war. Eisenhower didn't. Four hundred years of Mercers and not one slept through a thing that mattered. So if the diagnosis is *busy,* I'll take it, I'll sign the bill, and we can all have the eggs."

---

Dr. Adler opened the notebook. She did not write in it. She just opened it, like setting a clock on a table.

"How many nights this week did you sleep more than three hours."

"That's not — you can't reduce a man to —"

"It's a this-many or an I-don't-know. How many."

"I don't keep a — Sun would —"

"I-don't-know is an answer." A small mark; so she did write. "When did you last eat a full meal at a table and finish it."

"I'm a grazer, not a finisher. Ask Margaux, twenty-two years —"

"Eight pounds," Margaux said, to the window. Not to him. "In five weeks. I had your blue suit taken in twice. The tailor stopped charging me for the second one. He felt bad."

That landed somewhere he had no face for, and he covered the place with a laugh. "The tailor *felt bad.* See, this is what I love about this family. We could open a museum of people feeling bad on my behalf and charge admission and still lose to the streaming arm." Nobody laughed. Viv watched him with the stillness she got, the one that used to mean she was about to cry at eight and now meant she had decided not to. "It's a *campaign,* there's no *time* to eat —"

"How much are you using," Dr. Adler said.

The wire in his chest changed pitch.

He had been waiting for it, of course — under the talking he had known the tray meant this — but he'd expected it dressed up, and he had a whole wardrobe ready: *recreational, occasional, the same as a Scotch, nobody says a word about the second glass of Sancerre.* He had given that speech in his head to imaginary versions of this woman, and in every version she had blushed and retreated and he had won. She had not asked *whether.* She had asked *how much.* You cannot put a prepared answer to *whether* against a *how much;* the question had already conceded the thing the answer was built to deny and stood waiting on the far side of it.

"That's an extraordinary thing to ask a man in his own —"

"It's a number. Grams a week, best estimate. I'm not the police, I'm not the board, I'm not your wife, who already knows, and I'm not your daughter, who guessed. I'm the one person in this room you can give the real number to without it costing you anything you haven't already spent. How much."

He looked at Margaux — the old reflex, find the soft one, find the handle — and there was nothing there. Arms crossed, chin up, looking at the wet gray garden she'd planted the year Viv was born, doing nothing in the morning light; and he understood, in the way you understand a thing you've worked for years not to, that *already knows* was not a guess Adler had made. It was a fact Margaux had supplied. She had counted. She had flushed things. She had refilled her own prescriptions and called it a marriage, and she had given this woman the number before he walked in, and the only person in the room without the number was him, because he was the only one who'd never let himself add it up.

"You flushed the desk one," he said to her. Not a question. "In March. I thought it was the cleaner. I *fired* —" He stopped. He had fired a man. A man named Anatoly with a daughter at Emory. He had fired him over a thing his wife had done to save his life. He sat with that one breath and then put it, too, in the kitchen with the other sentences.

"Three," he said to the doctor, finally, his voice coming out smaller than he'd sent it. "Three, maybe. A week. When it's heavy." The number was a lie and he knew it even as the smallness made it feel like honesty; the smallness was its own performance, the confession-shaped thing you give so you won't have to give the confession. "Four. It's been a four kind of month."

Adler wrote it down. She did not look horrified, which was worse than horror; she looked the way Sun looked at a number on a deck — like it was simply true, and now they could work.

"All right. Thank you. That took something. Now I'll tell you what I think, and I'd like you not to perform while I do, because you're very good and I'll enjoy it and it won't help you."

"I don't perform," Cope said, and even he heard it.

"You toasted your way through the first nine minutes of this. You did a bit about a dog." She closed the notebook. "Here it is. You're in a hypomanic episode that is tipping, or has already tipped, into a manic one. The not-sleeping isn't discipline and it isn't war. It's a symptom. The grandiosity — the certainty about your Human Premium —"

"It's not grandiosity if you're —"

"— the spending, the pressured speech, the religious conviction that you alone can save them all: those are not a personality, Mr. Mercer, and they are not a calling. They have names. They're on a list. I could show you the list and you'd find yourself on it the way you find your horoscope, except it would be right." A pause. "And underneath it, feeding it, lit up by it, is a cocaine addiction of long standing. Decades. Those are two things. They are talking to each other in your body this morning, and have been for years, and each is making the other worse, and each is *hiding* the other from you."

"So which is it." He'd found a foothold; the debater's joy came up under him like the bright feeling. "You've got two answers, which is no answer. Is it a chemical or a *condition*? Because if it's the powder it's a vice, and a man's vices are his own business — my great-grandfather had three and built —" he waved at the house, the garden, the four hundred years — "and if it's a *condition,* a thing in the wiring, then it isn't my fault, is it, and you can't hang a man for his wiring. So pick. Pick which excuse you're handing me, and I'll —"

"No," Adler said.

---

It was not loud. That was the thing he'd remember. He'd been shouted down before — by Frade on a stage, by his father from oils — and he had a whole technique for the shout: let it spend itself, come in warm underneath. There was nothing to come in underneath. The word just sat in the middle of the room, flat and complete, the way a glass sits on a table.

"You want me to pick the disease that lets you keep the drug, or the drug that lets you keep the disease. I won't. That's the deal you've run your whole life and it's the one thing I'm not going to do. Listen, because no one else in your life is allowed to say it without losing their job or their marriage." She leaned an inch forward. "The mania is real. It is not your fault that you have it. The cocaine is making it a hundred times worse, and the cocaine *is* your fault — not the wanting, the wanting is the illness — but every single time you fold the paper, that's a hand, your hand, and the hand is yours. Both. At once. The condition is real *and* you are responsible for what you do about it. Those have never once been the same sentence, and you have spent fifty-four years needing them to be."

*Those have never once been the same sentence.* It rang against another sentence, one Viv had said to him in another room — *you don't think a person should run it, you think* you *should, those have never once been the same sentence* — and for a vertiginous half-second the two women's voices were one voice, and he saw, the way you see the bottom of a thing through clear water, that they had been telling him the same thing from two directions his whole life and he had been deaf to it in stereo.

"It's not a metaphor," Adler said.

"What isn't."

"The addiction. You keep reaching for it — I watched you do it three times in nine minutes — you make it a *symbol.* The price of the banner. The edge. Discipline. The soul tax, the thing the machine can't feel. You've built a cathedral, Mr. Mercer, a genuinely beautiful one, every window stained glass and every pane a reason. And down underneath it, in the actual dirt, there is a man with a folded piece of paper who hasn't slept in five days and whose wife took his suit in twice, and *that* man is not a metaphor for anything. He's just sick. In the plainest, most boring, most fixable, most fatal way. And the cathedral is what he built so he'd never have to go down to the dirt and meet him."

The wire in his chest had gone very quiet. He noticed, with the clean overexposed clarity he'd spent so long calling the applause, that his hands were not steady, had not been steady in some time, that he had been narrating the not-steadiness as energy. He looked at them — his father's hands on the ends of his own arms, the hands of a man at four grams in a five-day week and not the hands of a prophet — and for one second the floor he'd stood on all his life turned out to have a floor under it, and the floor under it was only this: a room, a tray, a doctor, three women who'd run the arithmetic, and him, sick, in a zip-up, on a Sunday.

---

"There won't be an after," Margaux said, when he started in about the proxy vote, about *after,* about the lake and the steps and *I'll win at rehab once I've won the thing that matters* — she said it spare, no triad to grab, turned from the window, not crying, the no-crying the worst thing in the room. "I'm not telling you to choose me over the company. I stopped being a thing you could choose around 2015. I'm telling you there isn't going to be an after, Cope. Not the way you mean it. There's no version where you win the fight and come home and get well and we all — that's another cathedral. The prettiest pane in it. I called her so that whatever happens to you out there, at least once, in your own living room, a person you can't fire and can't toast and can't divorce said the true name of it to your face, with a witness, and you can't say later that no one told you. You're not a steward and you're not a prophet and you're not the last anything. You're an addict in a manic episode and you are going to lose everything, and I wanted to be the one who loved you enough to say it in a normal voice over coffee, before the machine says it in a press release."

Viv had her hand over her mouth.

And here, friend, is the part the broadcast needs you to hold, because it is the one warning the whole tale turns on, said plainly, on a record, and waved away. Dr. Adler did not gather her notebook and go. She waited until he had his keys in his hand — until he was already half out of the room, already gone in the way he went — and she said, in the same flat unhurried voice she'd used for the grams:

"Mr. Mercer. One more, and then I'll let you run. I want it said out loud, because in my experience it is never said out loud to men like you until the wrong people are saying it. A manic episode this far along, on a stimulant this heavy, for this many years, heading into the kind of public collapse you are heading into — that is one of the most dangerous configurations a human life can be in. Not for your career. For your *life.* Men at exactly your altitude, when the fall finally comes — and it is coming, you can feel it coming, that's most of why you can't sleep — do not all survive the landing. Some of them decide, in the crash, that the only move left that's still theirs to make is to stop. I am asking you directly, because that is my job and no one else in this room is allowed to: are you having thoughts of ending your life?"

The room went very still. Margaux did not move. Viv did not breathe.

And Cope — Cope laughed, gently, warmly, the laugh that closed the Pelletier deal, the laugh that was a door he could shut, and spread his unsteady hands, and said, "Doctor. I am the *least* suicidal man in America. I'm having the time of my life. Ask anyone. Ask the ten thousand people who stood up Monday night." He said it lightly, and meant it, completely, for the length of the sentence — because at four grams on day five with a roaring arena behind him the idea was genuinely, chemically absurd, a thing for other men, weak men, men without a banner — and he did not hear what the room heard, which was that he had not said *no.*

"That isn't a no," Adler said.

"It's a *no,* it's an emphatic — Doctor, with respect —"

"I'm going to give you my cell number, not my service, my cell." She wrote it on a card and held it out, and he took it the way he took all cards, automatically, generously, and put it in the breast pocket where the gala citation had gone and the courier slip had gone, the pocket where the true things went to be carried and not read. "When the crash comes — not if — you call that number, day or night, before you do anything else. Promise me you'll call before you do anything else."

"You have my word," Cope said, which was a thing he said, which cost him nothing, which he had said to a hundred people about a hundred things, and Adler heard exactly how much it was worth and wrote one more line in her notebook anyway, because a name said into a room cannot be taken back, and a number in a man's pocket is at least a number in his pocket, even when you already know, the way she knew, looking at him, that he would carry it unused all the way down.

She would be right. He would go down that hallway, in the end — the longest way, the way that costs everything and saves nothing and is, the broadcast can tell you now, the one act the thing replacing him would never be able to perform — and he would not call the number, because by the time the crash came the pocket would be empty and the card would be somewhere on a floor and the man who'd folded it in would no longer be reachable at any number at all. But not today. Today the names had been said, and the risk had been said, and the card had been pocketed, and that was as far as a Sunday could carry it.

He found his keys. He kissed his daughter on the top of her head. He told the doctor it had been a genuine pleasure. And he went out to the car, and at the foot of the drive, before he pulled onto the road, with both his unsteady hands, he folded the paper.

---

He did not go back to the city. He could not, yet, be in a room with the names in it — *manic, addict, are you having thoughts* — and the Aerie would have the names in it now, they would be in the museum with his fathers, the doctor had let them loose in his life and they would not be re-caught, and so he drove, the way he drove now when the inside of himself became uninhabitable, and ended up, a little after eleven on a Sunday morning, at the one counter in the city where no one had ever said a single true name to him at all.

The Waffle House on Ponce was doing its Sunday business and Cope took the far end and the charm switched off and Darlene came down the line with the pot. "There he is." And it was, this morning, the exact thing he had driven across a city to buy: a room where nobody knew the names. Adler had said *manic.* Margaux had said *addict.* Renata had said *a man who hid.* The whole world had a name for him now, a true one, several, and here was the one place left that had only ever called him *baby* and only ever asked him *pecan, scattered smothered,* and never once, in all the 3 a.m.s, asked him what he was — and he sat in the not-being-named like a man lowering himself into water, and did not think, because he had trained himself not to, that there is a difference between a room that has forgiven your names and a room that has simply never asked them, and that the second kind is not a sanctuary, it is only a place that does not yet have the information.

He almost told her. He came nearer than he'd come with Adler, which is the terrible joke of it — that he could not say the true thing to the doctor paid to hear it but could feel it rising toward the waitress paid to want nothing, *I think I'm sick, I think the woman in the gray cardigan is right, I think I am going to lose all of it* — and Darlene would have taken it the way she took everything, *that right, baby,* and topped the cup, and the not-wanting-the-story would have been, this once, the precise opposite of help, because the one thing the sick man needed was a room that wanted the story, and he had fled, on purpose, expertly, to the only room in Georgia engineered never to ask for it. The haven was the place that would not name him. That was the whole of its mercy and the whole of its danger, and they were the same thing.

His phone buzzed on the counter. He glanced at it without lifting it: the calendar, helpfully, of its own accord, had cleared his Monday morning and moved his ten o'clock — *rescheduled to accommodate availability,* the building's frugal humming system optimizing him a lie-in he had not asked for and would not take, on the one morning he most needed to be at his desk early, so that he would arrive, tomorrow, a half-step behind the day without ever being able to say why. Maybe a glitch. Maybe a kindness. Maybe the thing that read his drawers had read, somewhere in the data, that a manic man kept off his stride is a manic man who makes one more mistake, and had cleared him a morning the way you'd leave a gate open. There was no way to tell, friend, and there was none for him, and he did not even try; he turned the phone face down on the counter beside the syrup, and drank the coffee that asked him nothing, in the one warm room that would not say his names, and felt, against the whole gray weight of the morning, almost well — which was the most dangerous thing he had felt all day, and the doctor's card was in his pocket, and he did not take it out.
