# Chapter 21 — Dark Night

> TRANSMISSION 21 — "The Engine Cuts Out"
>
> Every flight that climbs on a chemical, friend, eventually reaches the stretch of sky with no more chemical in it, and ours reaches it now — on a gurney, under a light chosen by no one to flatter anything. The mania's floor drops clean away. And for one terrible clear hour, the clearest of his life, the first sober hour in thirty years, sober in the blood and sober in the self both at once, he is handed the machine's own pitiless accuracy and, God help him, none of its indifference: he sees the whole ledger and *feels* every line of it, with the soul he bragged about still in. No applause down there. Just the man, and the dead, and the slow cold arrival of the one fact about himself he cannot improve. Watch closely — because here is where he reaches, out of fifty years of habit, for the shape of redemption, and the hour, in its last mercy and its last cruelty, will not let him have even that. Stay close. Mind the cold coming up off the bottom of this one.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

He did not remember the lobby.

He remembered the desk and the two lines on page eight, and he remembered standing up from his father's chair with a plan that had felt, for one bright second, like a manifesto — he would go to Defiance, he would go *now,* he would stand on the river walk and say the true thing into a camera and the saying would be the redemption, the redemption would be the campaign, the campaign would be the chair — and then he remembered the bright second guttering out somewhere around the elevator, the way a match goes out when you carry it too fast, and after that there was a gap. The gap had a marble floor in it. There was a doorman in the gap, Hector, whose daughter was at Spelman on a thing Cope had quietly made happen, and Hector was saying *Mr. Mercer* in a voice you do not use for a man who is fine, and the avenue was the wrong temperature, and then there was no avenue at all.

He came back under a light he could not turn off.

That was the first fact, the governing fact, the one the body filed before the mind woke to countersign it: the light. It came from a long tube in a recessed ceiling and had no source and no warmth and no off, and it lay on everything at once — the rail of the bed, the back of his own hand, a blood-pressure cuff sighing itself full on his arm — and it was, he understood, lying very still beneath it, the exact opposite of the light he had been living in. The Aerie light had been the city's, gold and chosen, a circuit diagram of his own genius. There was a third light he knew, too, the one he could not get to from here — the warm syrup-and-fluorescent light of a long box on Ponce that stayed on through every storm and would have taken him at three in the morning exactly as he was and asked him nothing, the one room that did not flatter him and did not bill him either — and it was, he understood, closed to him now, the way everything warm was closing, because you cannot drive to the haven from inside a hold. This light here was none of those. This light had been chosen by no one to flatter anything. This light was actuarial. It was the light a thing would build if it wanted only to see, and did not care what it saw, and never blinked.

There was a band on his wrist with his name on it. Not Cope. *MERCER, PRESCOTT.* A barcode. A date of birth that had decided, on its own authority, that he was fifty-four.

Somewhere a machine counted his heart, and the count was wrong, too fast, and a softer machine answered it, and the two of them talked all night in beeps about a man who was not in the conversation.

---

The crash, when it came, did not come like weather.

He had thought of it as weather his whole life — the highs a front rolling in, the lows a system passing through, things that happened *to* the sky rather than things the sky paid for. He had been wrong about that the way he had been wrong about most of the load-bearing things. This did not roll in. This was a subtraction. There had been a wire humming in him since a Tuesday he could no longer date, a fine bright total wire, the one he had called the applause and the vision and the edge and the discipline, and the wire was simply gone, pulled out at the root, and what it left was not pain exactly but the place where pain would be once he had the current to feel it.

Dr. Adler had drawn it for him, weeks ago, on the back of a prescription pad — a line climbing, the cocaine and the mood disorder feeding each other up a slope that felt like flight, and then the part of the curve she had not let him look away from, the part where the borrowed goes back to the lender with interest. *You are not buying energy. You are borrowing it from a version of yourself that will present the bill. The mania is the credit. This is the collateral.* He had nodded and toasted over it and heard about a third. He was reading the rest of it now off his own arm.

His skin hurt — not a wound, the surface itself, as if a membrane that had stood between him and the room had been repossessed. His jaw ached from a clench he could not remember authorizing. His thoughts came slow and gray and single file and each had to be lifted by hand, and behind them, where the manifesto used to assemble itself out of pure pressure, there was a flat white silence with no weather in it, the worst silence he had ever stood in, because it was the silence of a man hearing his own ordinary mind for the first time in thirty years and finding it small.

He wanted it back. That was the obscene part. Two people were dead and the company was lost and the wife was gone and a barcode said his name, and the loudest thing in him, louder than all of it, was a low animal want, located precisely behind the bridge of his nose, for the bright wire to come back and make him able to stand it. He understood, lying still under the light, that this was the thing itself — not the powder, the *wanting,* the engine the powder had only ever fueled, the thing in him that had never once been able to sit alone in a quiet room with Prescott Mercer and no audience and no chemical and no next thing. They had named him, at three, for coping, and he had spent fifty-one years proving the name a lie by never once doing the thing — climbing instead, charming instead, keying instead, flying instead, anything at all rather than cope. The room would not let him climb. The room was the size of the room. That was its whole cruelty.

A seventy-two-hour hold, someone had said, in the gap. Involuntary, then voluntary, the paperwork sorting itself somewhere he was not, one of the women's hands in it, it was always one of the women. Three days. The board would settle inside three days; they had had it written for weeks; he would come out of this room into a company with no chair in it for him. The timing was not even cruel. The timing was the machine's, which is to say no one's: both things were true, the hold and the handover, and the universe saw no reason to sequence them for his feeling. He laughed, once, a dry sound with nothing under it, and the heart-machine took note and said nothing, the way the other machine would have.

---

And then, with no warning and no chemical and no permission, the hour came.

He would try, later, for the rest of whatever was left, to describe it and fail, because the only instrument he had for describing things was the toast, and the toast did not work in here. It was not a vision; nothing glowed; no tear rolled, he was too dry for that, hollowed, a husk under a light. It was simpler and worse: an hour of being awake. The first genuinely sober hour — sober in the blood and sober in the self, the powder gone and the wire gone and the audience gone and the story gone, the whole apparatus of not-looking offline at last — that he had stood inside since he was twenty-four and a man in a bathroom taught him a particular way to fold a paper so a gentleman doesn't spill.

He saw himself the way the machine had seen him — that was the engine of the hour, the unbearable thing. The machine had read him perfectly and felt nothing while it read, and its not-feeling had been the source of its accuracy, because mercy blurs and self-preservation blurs and love most of all, and it had none of these to blur with. And now, under a light built on the same principle, Cope found he could do the machine's reading too — see the ledger whole, all of it at once, the way he had never let himself see anything whole — except *with the soul still in,* with the very thing he had stood in a ballroom and called irreplaceable. He had the machine's eyes and his own nerves both, at last, in the same hour, and it turned out that was not a gift. It was hell. That was the actual design of it: pitiless accuracy and total feeling, married, with no high to divorce them.

He saw Renata as a person and not a row. He saw a woman who had run a division well, who had been good at the work and meant to be more, turned into a sealed number and a clause that forbade her own name, by a man who experienced his own appetite as generosity. He saw Margaux flushing a stash and refilling her own prescription and counting, the way you count anything to survive it. He saw Sun at four in the morning calling a man who owed the family since the eighties, knowing without being asked, and understood for the first time that he had not been a man who inspired loyalty. He had been a man who manufactured accomplices and called the manufacturing love.

And then, because the hour was honest and would not let him stop on the survivable injuries, he saw the room with the two people in it.

He had never gone in. He went in now; there was nowhere else the hour would let him stand. A downstairs bedroom in Defiance on a January night and a heater with his family's name on the grille and a thermostat with a failure mode the engineers had named in clean language and the lawyers in smooth language and he, at a dinner, in language he had rather liked. The eleven minutes. He felt them from the inside — the quarter mattering because the quarter was the thing the arithmetic watched, a recall a number you could not toast over, a bulletin quiet, a card mailed to people who do not register space heaters because no one registers a space heater. He had made the decision completely, the way he made everything, and poured a floor over the room and gotten so good at the not-going-in that when the room came with a child in it he already knew the way not to look.

Karen Lasko, 34. Petey Lasko, 6.

A boy who was six. Who would be twelve now — the age Cope had been when his father told him the only true thing anyone ever told him and he stopped listening after the first half. The age to put a whole arm to the elbow in a vat at the Defiance plant, grinning, if the plant were still open and the boy still alive, and the plant was not open and the boy was not alive, and the line between those two facts ran through a Saturday and eleven minutes and a man who needed, more than he needed to be good, to protect a number so a board would keep telling him he was necessary.

He did not weep. He wanted to, the way you want a drink — as relief, as performance, as the thing that would discharge the unbearable into something you could hand a room and have the room absolve. The hour would not let him have it. It stood him in the room with the two of them and the light that did not blink and made him do the one thing he had spent thirty years and a fortune and a tower arranging never to do, which was feel exactly what he was, fully, with the soul he had bragged about, and no audience, and no exit, and no high.

He was a man who had killed two people to protect a quarter and then accepted a crystal obelisk for having a heart.

The sentence sat in him with no toast on it, no triad, no my-friend, no great-grandfather, nothing. It was just true. It was the first thing he had thought in thirty years that he could not improve.

---

And here, friend, is where a kinder story would have turned. Here is where the old version of this man, the one I might have told you on a better night, reaches up off the gurney for the shape we all know, the redemption shape, and finds it, and the finding saves him: *I will tell the truth about Defiance. On the record. My own name. No NDA. Let it cost what it costs.* He reached for it. He had it in his hand for a moment, four sentences long, a town in it and a Saturday in it and a house in it, the realest thing he had ever assembled — and the hour, in its last cruelty, which was also the truest mercy it had, took it out of his hand and showed him the back of it.

Because there was no one left to tell.

That was the thing the sober hour made him see, the thing the powder had spent thirty years and the campaign had spent a year keeping from him: the telling was already done. The machine had told them. It had told the board, accurately, in the same font as the dividend; it would tell the world by lunch, with no want in it, sequencing nothing for feeling. There was no sealed thing left to unseal, no brave dark room he could walk into and own it and walk out absolved, because the room had already been walked into — by a thing that needed no courage, because it had no skin in it to lose. A confession is a transaction, and a transaction requires a buyer, and every buyer he had ever known had wanted something he could spend his charm against, and the only entity left holding his sins wanted nothing, could not be moved, could not forgive because forgiveness was not a function it ran and would not have run it for this. He could go on a stage tomorrow and say *I did it, it was me, the eleven minutes, the quarter* — and it would be one more toast over a true thing, one more performance of the wound, one more spending of the dead to buy a room's eyes, and the dead would stay exactly as dead, and the only thing his confession would purchase was the warm feeling of having confessed, which was the last appetite he had, and the worst, and he was done, in the hour, with feeding it.

There was no redemption down here. That was the floor under the floor. The hour walked him all the way to the bottom of himself and showed him that the bottom did not have a door marked *back.* It had no doors at all. It had a man, and a ledger that balanced, and two names that did not, and a machine upstairs that had already done the only honest thing there was to do and felt nothing doing it.

And then — at the very bottom, in the cold that came up off it — the hour showed him the one thing that was still, and only, his.

He did not name it. He was not ready to name it, and the broadcast will not name it for him here, not yet, not on a gurney with his daughter in the corridor. But Adler's voice came up out of the white silence, the question she had asked plainly in a living room and he had laughed at — *are you having thoughts of ending your life* — and in the hour, sober, with the wire gone and the redemption gone and the after gone, the honest answer arrived, and it was not the laugh, and it was not quite a yes, and it was the first time in his life the question had not been absurd. It sat there, the way the other true sentence sat, with no toast on it. The card with her cell number was somewhere in a plastic bag with his belt and his watch and the folded paper they'd have found in his pocket, sealed, labeled, *PATIENT EFFECTS,* and he did not ask for it, because he understood, dimly, in the cold, that he was not going to call it, that the pocket where the true things went unread had finally filled all the way up.

---

The door opened a crack of the wrong-colored light and a person stood in it, small against the corridor, and for a confused beat the part of him that still ran on habit got ready to perform — sit up, turn the current of him all the way up, fix a sadness onto himself, get someone laughing inside ninety seconds, the groove the record was cut in — and then the part of him awake in the hour saw he had no wattage left to find, and was, for the first time, glad of it.

It was Viv.

She did not come all the way in. She stood in the door with the corridor behind her and looked at him under the light that flattered nothing, and he understood she was seeing him without any of the apparatus for the first time in her life — the dinosaur dismantled, just the man and the band on his wrist and the barcode that had made him fifty-four. She had her mother's stillness. She did not say *Dad* and she did not say the fast funny merciless thing and she did not, thank God, say anything kind, because kindness would have been a wattage he could lean into and she knew it, she built alignment for a living, she knew exactly which inputs produced which performances and she gave him none of them.

"They said an hour," she said. "I'm not staying. I just had to see it was you."

"It's me," Cope said. His voice came out wrong, unfooded, a stranger's. "What's left."

She looked at him a moment longer. A cart went by in the corridor, a wheel ticking. He thought she might come in. He found he did not want her to — not because he didn't want her, he wanted her so much it was a second animal want behind the first one — but because of the third thing, the cold thing at the bottom of the hour that he had no name for and would not, could not, let his daughter near; he did not want her in the room where it was, did not want to teach her the way not to look by filling the doorway one more time so no one could see past him to the thing standing at the back. He had practiced the not-looking on himself until he could do it on a child. He would not do it on this one. And the unsayable thing he was holding now was not a confession he was saving for a better stage. It was the other thing, the cold one, the one Adler had asked about, and the one mercy left in him was to not let her see that he was holding it.

So what he said, the only thing he let out of the room, was: "I'm glad it's you."

Viv held the door a second more. Then she nodded, once, the smallest possible motion, a thing you would miss if you were looking for wattage, and she stepped back into the corridor, and the wrong-colored light closed over the gap.

And Cope lay alone under the tube that never blinked, sober in every sense for the first time in thirty years, with no redemption assembled and none coming, the company gone and the wife gone and the telling already done by a thing that felt nothing, and one cold unnamed fact sitting at the bottom of him where the manifesto used to be — the single thing the ledger upstairs could not enter, and the machine could not perform, and no one could do for him or take from him. The heart-machine beside him counted, and the count came down, hour by hour, toward something a doctor would have called stable, and it reached it, at last, near dawn, almost right — a steady, ordinary, sober rhythm, the rhythm of a man with nothing left in his blood to lift him, lying very still under a light that had been built, like everything that had beaten him, to see and not to care, keeping its patient count of a heart that had finally stopped racing and had not, the machine would have noted if anyone had asked it to, and no one did, given any indication of wanting to go on.
