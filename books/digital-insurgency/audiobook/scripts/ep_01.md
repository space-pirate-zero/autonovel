# EP 01 — Orientation: The Mirror Test

*Digital Insurgency, 2nd Edition — audio field course. Module 0.*
*Source: Prologue ("The Portrait in the Server Room") + Five Claims.*
*Runtime target ~18 min. STATUS: ✅ reference script (locked template).*

Cast this episode: NARRATOR/SPZ, ZERO. (No boss yet — the boss is the listener.)

---

## [SIGNAL IN — TRANSMISSION 01]

> ♪ **SCORE — SIGNAL IN** · shortwave tune-in sweep + static burst → DI theme motif (~4s) · theme tail ducks under the first line · hard cut to the Broadcast.

SPZ: [softly] Pull your chair in close to the set, friend, and mind the static —
out here on the salvage decks the static is most of what's left. I'm Space Pirate
Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9, and this is *Digital Insurgency* — a
field course for smuggling the future past the thing that kills it on contact.

SPZ: Every company you'll ever try to change has an immune system. It doesn't hate
you. It doesn't know you exist. It just kills anything new the way a body kills a
splinter — with forms, committees, sunk cost, and a fear reflex older than the org
chart. Fourteen episodes, I'm going to teach you to get past it. Not by burning it
down. [pause] Four percent at a time.

SPZ: But before the physics, before the protocol, before any of the clever stuff —
one test. The one that decides whether you're an insurgent or just a decorator.
Let me show you where I learned it. Not from me. From her. Watch.

---

## [COLD OPEN — THE BROADCAST]

> ♪ **SCORE — BROADCAST** · downward "dive" whoosh into the scene · server-room bed (50–60 Hz hum + sparse synth pad + distant city) at −18 dB under the whole scene · pull the bed on the last line.

NARRATOR: Three-fourteen in the morning. Floor B-2. The server room hums at a pitch
that lives in your back teeth.

NARRATOR: A woman stands in front of the dashboard the whole company glows up to
the lobby. Forty-seven million users. Engagement up three hundred forty percent. A
green line climbing out of frame. Someone built the gradient to feel like a
sunrise, and it works — her eyes track it the way they're meant to, up and to the
right, the body answering before the brain gets a vote.

NARRATOR: Her name is Zara Okonkwo-Reyes. In about three minutes she stops using
it. [pause] Her thumb finds the implant scar at the base of her skull — a habit she
stopped noticing two years ago.

NARRATOR: She came down to pull one log. Latency spike on the match queue, ticket
in her name, fix it before standup. She drops into the warm chair, cracks her
knuckles, opens the service that throttles match notifications. The one she
half-wrote herself. Clean enough to frame.

ZERO: [matter-of-factly] I remember shipping this. Pizza box on the desk. Landed
green on the first deploy. Little hit of it in my chest, like winning a round.

NARRATOR: There's a flag in the config she doesn't recognize.

ZERO: [flatly] `MATCH_DELAY_ENGINE: ENABLED.`

NARRATOR: She reads the comment above it. [pause] Reads it again. Her jaw stops
working. The delay isn't a bug. Premium accounts match fast. Free accounts wait —
and the wait is *tuned.* Not load-balanced. Not random. Tuned. She follows the
import. The import opens a second dashboard. This one doesn't go up to the lobby.

ZERO: [quietly] `Loneliness exploitation index — zero point eight four.`
`Addiction retention coefficient — rising.`

NARRATOR: She says them out loud, low, like the sound of them in the room might
bend them into something else. They don't bend. There's a git blame on the delay
engine, and she reads the names down the side of the screen, expecting strangers.

NARRATOR: Her own handle is fourth from the bottom. A commit from eighteen months
back.

ZERO: [softly] I built the pipe. Somebody turned it the wrong direction, and the
pipe didn't care — because I built it not to care. [exhales] That was the elegant
part. That was the part I was proud of.

NARRATOR: Forty-seven million people, and the engine under them is a machine for
keeping each one a little bit alone. That's the load-bearing wall. And here's the
thing that should have warned her years ago and instead kept her comfortable: the
prettier the app got — and it got gorgeous, there's a Webby upstairs in a glass
case, lit from below like a relic — the uglier this had to run underneath to hold
the weight up.

NARRATOR: The face on the wall. [pause] The painting in the basement.

NARRATOR: She lifts the badge off her lanyard and sets it on the keyboard, square
over the function keys, so the next person can't pretend they didn't find it. She
leaves both dashboards open. Then she walks — through the lobby, past the Webby in
its case, into the Atlanta night where the air comes off Peachtree wet and warm.

NARRATOR: She never badges back in. From here, she's ZERO.

## [THE LESSON — THE MIRROR TEST]

> ♪ **SCORE — LESSON** · hard cut, bed drops out, physics pulse starts (dry, close, no reverb) · a rising data-stinger blip on each equation named · pulse stops cold at the section end.

SPZ: That's the Mirror Test. Before you smuggle anything into a company, look at
what you're smuggling.

SPZ: A cooler interface bolted on top of an exploitation engine doesn't make you an
insurgent. It makes you a decorator. You touch up the portrait. The portrait keeps
rotting under your brush — and the check still clears. So look at the engine. [pause]
Not the gradient. The engine.

SPZ: This is the one idea the whole course hangs on, so I'm going to name it plain.
It's the oldest trick in the building, and it's got a name from a book. The Dorian
Gray move. The *product* stays beautiful — clean UI, high NPS, a shelf of awards.
The *portrait* in the server room rots — the manipulation loops, the addiction
math, the exploitation metrics. And it's not a coincidence. The beauty is a
*prerequisite* for the ugliness. The nicer the face, the worse the basement has to
run to pay for it. Hold that picture. It runs from that server room all the way to
the last episode — where, if we do this right, the portrait starts to heal.

SPZ: Here's your map. Five claims. Everything in this course is one of these five,
wearing a lab coat.

SPZ: One. [pause] Every line of software on earth is about to be rewritten. The
rewrite is coming whether you're ready or not.

SPZ: Two. Authenticity is the real currency of the future. When anyone can generate
competent, the only thing with value left is the human fingerprint.

SPZ: Three. The enterprise is a physics problem, not a sales problem. Inertia,
fear, entropy, friction — these aren't moods. They're forces, and forces have
equations. That's what most of this course is: the equations.

SPZ: Four. The corporate immune system can be bypassed — but not defeated. You do
not win a fight with a body's immune response. You get past it. Camouflage, not
combat.

SPZ: Five. Good trouble is the only kind of trouble worth making. John Lewis's
words. We're going to make a lot of it — quietly, on purpose, inside the rules.

SPZ: Two pillars hold those five up. The Physics — the equations, twenty-four of
them before we're done. And the Broadcast — her story, ZERO's, running underneath
every lesson, because the physics only means something when you can see it cost
somebody something.

## [THE SPZ CLOSER]

> ♪ **SCORE — CLOSER** · everything drops; a warm chord blooms (the "sit-down" cue) · confessional bed: vinyl crackle + lone Rhodes + Waffle-House room tone · crackle holds under the last word.

SPZ: [softly] I'll tell you where I found my own portrait. Waffle House on Ponce,
two in the morning, back when I was very good at my job. I had a number on a slide,
and the number went up, and I made the slide prettier every week. Never once asked
what it cost the people on the wrong side of that arrow.

SPZ: The picture was in the basement the whole time. I had the badge. I had the
key. I just liked the lobby better. Lighting's nicer up there. And you can't hear
the servers.

SPZ: Here's the part nobody tells you. Walking out clean is the *easy* version.
ZERO did it in one night, because she's faster than me — always has been, you'll
see. Me? It took three more years. Three years of refreshing that green line and
pretending the basement was somebody else's floor.

SPZ: You want to know what finally moved me? A waitress refilled my coffee without
being asked. Looked me dead in the eye. Treated me like a human being for two
seconds — no formula, no score, just a person seeing a person at the worst table in
Atlanta. [pause] That's the whole book, if you want it cheap. The system optimizes
everyone into a number. The work is learning to see the human under it. Even when
the human is you. Even at two A.M. Even at the worst table.

## [FIELD ASSIGNMENT]

> ♪ **SCORE — ASSIGNMENT** · one decisive percussive hit + a rising two-note "go" figure · short punchy CTA motif (~10–15s) · button, then silence.

SPZ: One assignment before the next drop. Find your second dashboard. The one that
doesn't go up to the lobby. Whatever you ship this week — open it up and name the
engine under the gradient. Say it out loud, low, in an empty room if you have to.
You don't have to quit like she did. You just have to *look.* That's the whole
test. Almost nobody passes it, because almost nobody looks.

SPZ: [dryly] Go look at your engine. [pause] Next time: the Graveyard — every line
of code on earth, and the expiration date stamped on it you can't read yet. This
has been *Digital Insurgency.* Signal finds signal.
