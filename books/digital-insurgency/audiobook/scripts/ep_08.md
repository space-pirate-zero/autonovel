## [SIGNAL IN — TRANSMISSION 08]

> ♪ **SCORE — SIGNAL IN** · shortwave tune-in sweep + static burst → DI theme motif (~4s) · theme tail ducks under the first line · hard cut to the Broadcast.

SPZ: [softly] Pull in close, friend. Space Pirate Zero, Spaceship Alpha 9. *Digital
Insurgency.*

SPZ: John Lewis got his skull fractured on a bridge in Selma and spent the rest of
his life telling people to go make good trouble. Necessary trouble. He did not mean
chaos. He did not mean a manifesto in the all-hands. He meant surgery — the
disciplined, almost surgical act of breaking a wrong rule in a way that forces the
system to either change or show its true face.

SPZ: Tonight I'm going to show you the most dangerous man in this whole course. He
never raises his voice. His name appears on nothing. And his badge hangs crooked. I
want you to watch how he got that way — because it starts with a kid getting walked
to an elevator at four-forty in the afternoon, and it starts three years before
anything else in this story. [pause] Watch.

## [COLD OPEN — THE BROADCAST]

> ♪ **SCORE — BROADCAST** · downward "dive" whoosh into the scene · server-room bed (50–60 Hz hum + sparse synth pad + distant city) at −18 dB under the whole scene · pull the bed on the last line.

NARRATOR: Three years before the story. MegaCorp, third floor. The light is the
color of old paper. Marcus Bishop stands in the doorway of a conference room and
watches a kid lose her job.

NARRATOR: Keisha Williams is twenty-four. Natural hair, glasses with a smudge on the
left lens she keeps forgetting to clean, a MegaCorp hoodie two sizes too big because
she ordered it that way on purpose. She is not crying. She is explaining. Her hands
move when she explains.

KEISHA: [plain, young, hurrying] It saves two million a year. I — okay, I ran it on
last quarter's data. The compliance reports that take Sarah's team nine days? It
does them in an afternoon. I tested it three times. The numbers match the manual
ones to the dollar.

NARRATOR: Across the table, a man Bishop has known for eleven years folds his hands.
The badge on his lanyard hangs perfectly straight.

NARRATOR: [flatly, as the manager] "That's not the issue, Keisha."

KEISHA: Then what's the issue.

NARRATOR: [as the manager] "You didn't take it through the review board."

NARRATOR: A pause. The fluorescent light hums at sixty hertz. Down the hall a
printer wakes up and starts grinding.

KEISHA: [quieter now] The board takes eight months. The savings start the day it
ships. I did the math. Eight months of waiting is one-point-three million dollars. I
thought — I thought that was the whole point. Saving money.

NARRATOR: The man does not look up from his hands. He says they have a process. The
process is the point. She built a system that touches production data without
review — a security finding, a compliance violation. And then, softer, which is
worse: it's a beautiful tool. He's not saying it isn't. But he can't have people
deciding on their own what gets built. She understands that. If everyone did what she
did —

KEISHA: [not loud, just certain] Everyone doesn't do what I did. Nobody does what I
did. That's the problem you should be worried about.

NARRATOR: The light hums. They walk her out at four-forty P.M. Bishop watches from
the doorway. She doesn't take the laptop — it's theirs — but she takes the smudged
glasses off and cleans them on the hem of the hoodie, finally, on her way to the
elevator. Like she's got time now. Like the one thing she's got left is time. The
doors close on her.

NARRATOR: That night Bishop does not go home at five. He sits at his desk under a
single lamp and pulls up the policy library. The whole thing. Acceptable Use.
Change Management. Architecture Governance. Data Handling. Procurement Authority.
Innovation Sandbox Guidelines — Draft. Two hundred and forty-seven pages.

NARRATOR: He reads like a man reading the dictionary in a prison cell — because
there's nothing else, and the nothing else is a door. He reads the way Malcolm read,
copying words he didn't know until he knew all of them. Page eighty-one: a clause
that exempts operational efficiency tooling under fifty thousand dollars of annual
impact from full review, if it doesn't touch customer data. Page one-nineteen: a
sandbox provision nobody's invoked in four years, because nobody read past the
title. Page one-forty-two, paragraph three: a line that lets a senior director
authorize a time-boxed proof of value on his own signature.

NARRATOR: By two A.M. he has a yellow legal pad. By four A.M. there are twenty-three
numbers on it. Twenty-three gaps between what the policy says and what its authors
meant. He caps the pen — a Parker Jotter, black ink — and clicks it once.

NARRATOR: Then he reaches up and loosens his lanyard. Just a little. Just enough. He
lets the badge tilt. Fifteen degrees. Maybe twenty. He looks at it crooked against
his chest, and he does not fix it. [pause] He will never fix it again.

## [THE LESSON — THE GOOD-TROUBLE COEFFICIENT & THE INSURGENT INDEX]

> ♪ **SCORE — LESSON** · hard cut, bed drops out, physics pulse starts (dry, close, no reverb) · a rising data-stinger blip on each equation named · pulse stops cold at the section end.

SPZ: Keisha's mistake was not building the tool. The tool was correct. Her mistake
was building it *visibly,* *unilaterally,* and *without a cover policy.* Right thing,
wrong way — and the immune system ate her in a conference room at four-forty in the
afternoon. Bishop learned the lesson she paid for. You build the right thing the
*survivable* way. You find the clause. You cite the page. And when they ask who
authorized this, the answer is never "I did." The answer is "page one-forty-two,
paragraph three did." Bureaucracy can't argue with itself.

SPZ: Two equations run this. The first one keeps you from spending your courage on
the wrong fights — because courage is finite, and you want to spend it where it
converts. Call it the Good-Trouble Coefficient.

SPZ: Here's the math, spoken. You take the real, measurable change the action
produces — dollars saved, hours returned, harm reduced — and you multiply it by how
true the action is to what you actually believe. Then you divide all of that by the
risk plus the friction. Impact times belief, over risk plus friction. Score each one
to ten. Risk is the realistic personal cost if it goes wrong — the firing, the
freeze-out, the bad review. Friction is the institutional drag — the committees, the
reviews, the time it takes to move.

SPZ: The benchmark, in words. Green is a coefficient at or above four —
high-conversion trouble. Big change, you believe in it, the cost is survivable. Make
this trouble. Yellow is between two and four — marginal; only worth it if you can
shrink the bottom of the fraction, so find a cover policy, cut the blast radius, drop
the friction before you commit. And red is below two — bad trouble. Either the impact
is theater, or you don't really believe in it, or the risk will end you. Walk away.
Save the courage.

SPZ: Three dispatches, and they're the whole craft.

NARRATOR: Field Report A. Keisha's tool, the way she shipped it. Impact: a nine —
two million a year, real. Belief: a ten — she believed in it completely. Risk: an
eight — unilateral, touched production, no cover. Friction: a seven — the board would
have killed it or delayed it to death. Run it: nine times ten is ninety, divided by
eight plus seven — fifteen — is six. Green on the top line. But read it again. The
*idea* scored green. The *execution* drove the risk to eight with no mitigation. The
math says the trouble was worth making. It does not say she made it well.

NARRATOR: Field Report B. Bishop's tool. Three years later. Same idea, different
method. He rebuilt Keisha's compliance tool under the fifty-thousand-dollar
efficiency exemption — page eighty-one. Ran it in the dormant sandbox — page
one-nineteen. Authorized the pilot on his own signature as a time-boxed proof of
value — page one-forty-two. Impact: still a nine. Belief: still a ten. But risk drops
to a two — every step cited a real policy, the cover airtight. And friction drops to
a three — he routed around the board legally, not by hiding. Run it: ninety, divided
by two plus three — five — is eighteen. Triple green. Same impact. Same belief. He
*tripled the coefficient by gutting the denominator.* That is the entire lesson. The
coefficient tells you whether to fight. It does not tell you how. Lower the bottom
of the fraction before you swing.

NARRATOR: Field Report C. The all-hands manifesto. You stand up in the quarterly
meeting and tell three hundred people the company has lost its soul. Impact: a two —
you changed nothing, you vented. Belief: a nine — you meant every word. Risk: a nine
— you are now The Problem. Friction: a five — nothing moves; you just made yourself
radioactive. Run it: two times nine is eighteen, over nine plus five — fourteen — is
one-point-three. Red. It *felt* like courage. It was expensive noise. Save it.

SPZ: The second equation is the one that keeps you alive long enough to do this
twenty-three times. You give the credit away. Call it the Insurgent Index. Spoken:
the number of problems you solve quietly, divided by the number of times you stand
in the light and say *I did this.* Quiet fixes, over public credit.

SPZ: Benchmark, in words. Green is an index at or above five — ghost mode. You solve
five times more than you claim. The immune system can't see you because there's no
profile to target. Maximum survivability, maximum reach. Yellow is between two and
five — a visible insurgent; effective but exposed, building a reputation, which is a
target. And red is below two — you're a self-promoter with a cause. You claim as much
as you solve, or more, and the system sees you coming. Bishop calls that getting
fired for being right out loud.

NARRATOR: Field Report A. Bishop, the three-year tally. Problems solved quietly:
twenty-three — the loopholes, each one a shipped fix. Credit claimed publicly: near
zero. Score it as one — the single time he let himself be named, to protect Sarah's
team — and the index is twenty-three. Triple green. He is invisible to REAPER because
there is nothing to flag. His profile says Senior Director, Digital Operations. It
does not say *fixed twenty-three things the company doesn't know were broken.*

NARRATOR: Field Report B. The Growth Hacker from an earlier drop. Problems solved
quietly: three — he did ship some real things. Credit claimed publicly: forty — the
talks, the threads, the "how I ten-x'd retention" posts. Three over forty is
zero-point-zero-seven-five. Red. He's a billboard. The system loves him exactly as
long as the numbers hold — and erases him the day they don't, because his entire
existence is a claim.

SPZ: Bernie Sanders has run the same campaign for forty years on three words: not
me, us. He gives the movement the credit and keeps the power — which is competence
and trust. Bishop runs the same play. He hands the win to Sarah's team. He lets the
VP take it upstairs. He becomes, on purpose, the most forgettable man in any meeting.
And the most necessary one.

## [THE SPZ CLOSER]

> ♪ **SCORE — CLOSER** · everything drops; a warm chord blooms (the "sit-down" cue) · confessional bed: vinyl crackle + lone Rhodes + Waffle-House room tone · crackle holds under the last word.

SPZ: I had a manager once I genuinely loved. That's the part nobody tells you. He
drove forty minutes to my apartment when my dad died. He fought for my title bump in
a room I wasn't allowed in. And he killed every good idea I ever had — slow, with
kindness, by sending it to "the right people." And I let him. Because how do you
fight a guy who shows up at your door with a casserole?

SPZ: Took me years to understand he wasn't protecting the company from my ideas. He
was protecting *himself* from a feeling. He got blamed for something once, before my
time, and it rewired him. Every new thing I brought him lit up the same old fear —
and the casserole and the title bump and the forty-minute drive were all real *and*
they were all the velvet on the cage.

SPZ: Here's what I wish I'd done. I wish I'd read the rulebook. The actual rulebook.
I treated policy like weather — something that just happens to you. Bishop treats it
like a lock he gets to pick. Same two hundred and forty-seven pages. One of us was a
victim of them, and one of us read them more carefully than the people who wrote
them — and guess which one still works there, quietly fixing twenty-three broken
things, while the rest of us were busy being right out loud and getting walked to
the elevator at four-forty.

SPZ: [softly] John Lewis got his skull broken and called it good trouble.
*Necessary* trouble. The necessary part is the part everybody skips. He didn't break
it for fun. He broke it because the math worked — big change, deep belief, and a cost
the movement could survive. So read the rules. Find the doors. Give the credit away.
And tilt your badge fifteen degrees, every single day, so you never forget you're in
there for a reason. Deface the currency. Just cite the page while you do it.

## [FIELD ASSIGNMENT]

> ♪ **SCORE — ASSIGNMENT** · one decisive percussive hit + a rising two-note "go" figure · short punchy CTA motif (~10–15s) · button, then silence.

SPZ: One task. Pick the single policy document that most often blocks you, and read
it end to end tonight, with a pad. Mark every dollar threshold, every exemption,
every signature authority, every "draft" provision nobody invokes. You're hunting
the gap between the letter and the intent. Count them. If you find fewer than five in
your most-used policy, you read it too fast — go again. Bishop found roughly one door
every eleven pages. They're there. The authors built them and forgot.

SPZ: And one more, smaller thing. Choose one permanent daily refusal — something the
system can't fire you for, but that reminds you which side you're on. Bishop tilts a
badge. Yours might be smaller. Carry one act of refusal on your body every day, so
that on the morning you have to choose between your comfort and your conscience, the
muscle is already warm.

SPZ: [dryly] Go find your first loophole. [pause] Next time: Calculus for Cowards.
A burned prophet dumps a twenty-two-slide deck at slide three, hands a CFO a single
sheet of paper — and then learns to spot the deal that smiles at you while it bleeds
you dry. This has been *Digital Insurgency.* Signal finds signal.
