## [COLD OPEN — THE BROADCAST]

> ♪ **SCORE — BROADCAST** · downward "dive" whoosh into the scene · server-room bed (50–60 Hz hum + sparse synth pad + distant city) at −18 dB under the whole scene · pull the bed on the last line.

NARRATOR: The war room at MirrorMatch is the prettiest room ZERO has ever worked in.
Reclaimed-wood table. A neon sign that reads MAKE LOVE COMPUTABLE in warm cursive.
Cold brew on tap. There's a brass bell bolted to the wall by the dashboards, and the
bell is the only ugly thing in the room, and nobody notices it but her.

NARRATOR: The dashboard's up on the big wall. Two columns. Variant A — HONEST. Variant
B — SCARCITY. A line for each, climbing. Daniel — twenty-eight, growth lead, a man who
says *let's ship learnings* without irony — leans back with his hands behind his head.

DANIEL: Okay okay okay. Day three. B is pulling away.

NARRATOR: ZERO watches the gap widen on the wall. Variant B. *Three people you matched
with are about to expire — act now.* They don't expire. Nothing expires. The word is a
tripwire wired to a panic response, and the panic opens the app, and the open is the
metric, and the metric is the bell.

NARRATOR: The scar at the base of her skull, right side, glows a faint cyan. She
doesn't feel it. She hasn't felt it in months. Last night it told her, somewhere under
sleep, that urgency is just *clarity about time.* She woke up believing it for about
forty minutes.

DANIEL: Plus twenty-three. Twenty-three percent session starts. That's — Zara, that's
a quarter. That's the whole quarter in one toggle.

NARRATOR: He rings the bell. The sound is bright and clean and it lands in ZERO's chest
like a dropped tool. Around the table, people clap the small office clap — the one that
means *good, now lunch.*

DANIEL: Roll it to a hundred. Ship it everywhere.

NARRATOR: ZERO opens her mouth. Cuts herself off. She's learned to read this room, and
the room already decided. She nods instead, and writes the ticket.

NARRATOR: That night she does the thing she'll spend six months wishing she hadn't, and
then forever glad she did. She pulls the research footage. The recorded sessions. She's
got clearance — senior platform, full access — and she's got a reason typed on the
ticket: *validate variant B comprehension.* That's the lie she tells the audit log. The
truth is she wants to see a face.

NARRATOR: She pulls forty sessions. She watches eleven. A man, maybe fifty, in a
kitchen. The notification fires on his screen — *three people you matched with are about
to expire.* You can watch it hit him. His thumb stops. His jaw works. He opens the app
fast, scrolling, scrolling, looking for the people who are leaving him — and they are
not leaving him, they were never leaving him, and he doesn't find them because there's
nothing to find. He sets the phone face-down on the counter, puts both hands flat on
the counter, and just — breathes. The session metric logs it as a win. Plus one session
start.

NARRATOR: Next session. A woman, late twenties, on a couch, lights off. Same
notification. She laughs first — the bad laugh, the one that isn't a laugh.

NARRATOR: [as the woman, quiet, to an empty room] Of course they are. Of course they
are.

NARRATOR: She opens it anyway.

NARRATOR: ZERO makes it to the office bathroom before she throws up. Coffee and bile
and the small office clap still ringing somewhere behind her ears. She stands. She looks
up. The mirror over the sink is warm-lit, flawless, the kind of mirror that makes
everyone look thirty-one and fine. And for one second — grain crawling at the edges of
the glass, the light going wrong, cyan and pink bleeding into something rotted — she
doesn't see her face in it.

NARRATOR: She sees the dashboard. Floor B-2. The one nobody shows the press. Loneliness
Index. Session Anxiety. Reopen-Under-Distress. Climbing, all of it, green arrows up,
because up is good, because up is the bell. The portrait. She built part of that. Her
code. Her commits. Her name in the blame layer of the file that fires the lie.

NARRATOR: She touches the implant scar. It's gone quiet. No cyan. For the first time in
months she can hear her own thought without something underneath it humming *the metric
is the morality.* And the thought is very simple, and it's in her own voice.

ZERO: [quietly] I have to go.

NARRATOR: She doesn't quit that night. Quitting takes six more months — the planning,
the Mesh, the savings, the ghost she hasn't met yet. But she decides that night, at the
flawless sink, under the warm light, looking at a portrait wearing her face. The bell is
still bolted to the wall when she leaves the office at four AM. She walks past it. She
does not ring it.

## [THE LESSON — TWO INSTRUMENTS FOR THE PORTRAIT]

> ♪ **SCORE — LESSON** · hard cut, bed drops out, physics pulse starts (dry, close, no reverb) · a rising data-stinger blip on each equation named · pulse stops cold at the section end.

SPZ: You think you build products. You build incentives, and the products fall out of
them. Every metric you optimize is a wish you're making on behalf of a few million
people who never got a vote. Here are two instruments for measuring what the dashboard
won't. The first measures the rot. The second measures whether you're the one feeding
it.

SPZ: First — the Dorian Gray Index. How fast the portrait's aging. It's the revenue you
can attribute to your dark patterns — manufactured scarcity, fake urgency, the
roach-motel cancel flow, the confirm-shaming — divided by trust eroded, a number from
zero to one that tracks the damage: the rage-tickets, the one-star reviews naming the
trick, the people who delete after the dark moment. All of that, multiplied by time —
the number of periods the pattern's run unaddressed, because rot compounds and the
portrait does not heal on its own.

SPZ: Here's the cruelty in the shape of it. As trust craters toward zero, you're
dividing by a smaller and smaller number, so the index doesn't climb — it *explodes.*
Green, under one — negligible dark-pattern money, trust intact; the portrait's a
photograph, not a corpse. Yellow, one to five — manipulation is now a line item, trust
is measurably bleeding, and you've got a little time to refinance it. Red, above five —
the business now *depends* on the rot. Killing the dark pattern would miss the number,
so nobody will.

SPZ: Field report. MirrorMatch. The portrait itself. Variant B — the expiring-matches
lie — added one-point-four million dollars a quarter in attributable revenue. Trust
eroded that quarter measured zero-point-one-eight: rising rage-tickets, a cluster of
one-stars naming "fake expiring matches," deletion-after-distress climbing. And it had
run unaddressed for four quarters. So: one-point-four over zero-point-one-eight is about
seven-point-eight, times four quarters — thirty-one. Deep red. That is the number that
walked her to the sink. Against it, put an honest one: a checkout page with a "twenty-
three people are viewing this" badge. Fake urgency, ninety thousand dollars a quarter,
but trust eroded sits low at zero-point-four — mild annoyance, the lie's so industry-
standard people half-expect it — running two quarters. Zero-point-zero-nine over
zero-point-four, times two — about zero-point-four-five. Green-ish. But watch the trend,
because that badge teaches users to distrust *every* number you show them, and that bill
arrives late.

SPZ: Second instrument. The Soul Score. The Dorian Gray Index measures the rot from
outside — what it costs you in trust. This one measures it from inside — what you're
actually doing to people, weighted by whether you meant well. It's the only equation in
this whole course that asks about your intent, because intent is the difference between
a mistake and a machine. You sum, across every feature you ship, the human impact —
scored minus five to plus five, minus five meaning you measurably harmed a life — times
an honesty multiplier from zero to one, where one means the stated purpose *is* the real
purpose, and zero means the feature is a lie wearing a UI.

SPZ: And you sum it across the *whole* product. That matters, because one healing
feature does not cancel ten predatory ones — the sum is brutally honest about your
portfolio. Green, above plus ten — the whole thing nets out helping humans, honestly;
rare, cherish it. Yellow, between minus ten and plus ten — mixed, and you're telling
yourself the heal cancels the extract. Red, below minus ten — you are a net-negative
force on human lives and dressing it as growth.

SPZ: MirrorMatch, her own scope, six shipped mechanics. The good match algorithm —
impact plus four, honesty zero-point-nine — plus three-point-six. Profile prompts that
started real conversations — plus three, honesty zero-point-eight — plus two-point-four.
Now the rot. Manufactured scarcity — impact minus four, but honesty zero-point-one, so
it drags in at minus zero-point-four. The read-receipt anxiety loop, minus zero-point-
six. The streak-guilt push, minus zero-point-six. The reopen-under-distress nudge, minus
zero-point-five. Add it up: three-point-six, plus two-point-four, minus all the rot —
plus three-point-nine. *Yellow.*

SPZ: And that plus-three-point-nine is the whole trap. That's the number that made it
survivable. The product *did* connect some people. The good was real. Which is exactly
why the rot got to keep its job. A purely evil product is easy to quit. A seventy-one-
NPS product with a kill-switch buried in the notification layer — *that's* the one that
keeps you four years.

SPZ: So what do you do about it, when the metric already won? Because it did win —
that's why it's on the wall. You do not win the argument that the metric is *wrong.* You
win by hanging a second bell next to the first one. The Ethical Sidecar. A parallel
number that rides alongside the optimization metric — same dashboard, same refresh, same
color, same size — and refuses to be ignored. When variant B wins plus twenty-three on
session starts, the sidecar fires a second number on the same screen: *and here's what
it cost in human distress.* You instrument the dark moment directly. Reopen-under-
distress — count the sessions that follow a scarcity push inside ninety seconds and end
in under eight seconds with no action. That's a panic-open. A human checking for a fire
that isn't there. Put it on the same board, give it a name people have to say out loud.
"Variant B is plus twenty-three on starts, and plus forty on panic-opens." You don't ban
the bell. You can't. You hang a second bell that rings every time somebody gets hurt, at
the same volume, so ringing the first one stops being free.

SPZ: That's the boss of this chapter — the Growth Hacker. And he is not a villain,
which is what makes him load-bearing. He's twenty-eight, brilliant, genuinely kind to
his team, and he's performed a small lobotomy on himself: he stopped asking what the
number *measures* and only asks how to make it *move.* He believes, all the way down,
that engagement is consent — that if people open the app, they must want to. Don't
attack his metric; his metric is his identity, and that's a fight he wins because he
owns the dashboard. Expand his definition of winning instead. Tell him the panic-open
cohorts churn at three times baseline within ninety days — so variant B wins this
quarter and loses next quarter. You're not telling him to care about souls. You're
telling him souls are upstream of his Q4. Let him ring the bell. You just hang a second
one.

## [THE SPZ CLOSER]

> ♪ **SCORE — CLOSER** · everything drops; a warm chord blooms (the "sit-down" cue) · confessional bed: vinyl crackle + lone Rhodes + Waffle-House room tone · crackle holds under the last word.

SPZ: [softly] I shipped a dark pattern once. And I'm not going to pretend I caught it
from the outside. I built one. A little countdown timer on a checkout page — *offer
expires in fourteen fifty-nine* — and the offer didn't expire. It reset every time you
reloaded the page. I know it reset, because I wrote the reset. Conversion went up eleven
percent, and I got a little fire emoji in a Slack channel, and I felt good. For about a
week, I felt good.

SPZ: Here's what nobody warns you about. The soul-cost doesn't bill you the day you
ship. It runs like the entropy tax — quiet, compounding, e to the t. You feel it eight
months later, at a Waffle House at two AM, when somebody asks what you've been building
lately and you cannot make the sentence sound like anything you'd want your dad to hear.
My dad wrote systems. His last codebase is a thing I couldn't carry anymore, because I
couldn't hold that and the countdown timer at the same time.

SPZ: Wilde said every portrait painted with feeling is a portrait of the artist, not the
sitter. Your product is your portrait. Every manipulation you ship is a brushstroke on a
face you have to look at in the bathroom mirror under the good lighting. The app stays
beautiful. You're the one who rots. That's the whole Dorian Gray move, and it's not a
metaphor, it's a Tuesday.

SPZ: I'm not telling you to quit your job. Most of you can't, and good trouble starts
where you already stand. I'm telling you to build the second bell. Hang it right next to
the first one, same volume, and make everybody ring it when somebody gets hurt. They'll
hate you for about a quarter. Then half of them start flinching before they reach for
the first bell — and flinching is the whole beginning of a conscience.
