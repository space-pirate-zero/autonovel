## [COLD OPEN — THE BROADCAST]

> ♪ **SCORE — BROADCAST** · downward "dive" whoosh into the scene · server-room bed (50–60 Hz hum + sparse synth pad + distant city) at −18 dB under the whole scene · pull the bed on the last line.

NARRATOR: The rain quit an hour ago and left the Graveyard slick and shining. This
stretch of Peachtree — North Avenue behind her, 10th somewhere ahead — every
storefront on it used to be a company. They raised money. They hired engineers.
They shipped. They died. Not one of them planned to end up here.

NARRATOR: You met her last time. Zara Okonkwo-Reyes. Thirty-one, pink streak on the
left, boxes at a gym in Decatur. Two nights ago she laid her badge on a keyboard at
MirrorMatch and walked out. From here, she's ZERO. She hasn't slept since. She's not
even walking anywhere. She's just walking.

NARRATOR: A dead storefront on her left used to be a company called Lumence. She was
at the launch party. Open bar, a DJ, a founder in a blazer saying the word
*transform* into a microphone. The vinyl's peeled off the glass now. Inside, dark —
a ping-pong table, and a neon sign that used to read ONLY GOOD VIBES with the GOOD
burned out. So it just says ONLY VIBES, into an empty room.

NARRATOR: There's a FOR-LEASE code in the window. She scans it on reflex. The
listing loads. Four thousand two hundred square feet. Available immediately.

NARRATOR: She lowers the phone. And the scar at the base of her skull — right side,
small circle, three lines radiating out of it — wakes up. A faint cyan glow under
the skin. It only does that when something in her spikes. She touches it without
thinking, the way you touch a bruise to make sure it's real.

NARRATOR: The air two feet in front of her bends. Not fog. Not a reflection. A
human-shaped distortion assembles out of nothing — thousands of thin cyan threads,
translucent, the dead storefront still visible straight through the middle of its
chest. Where a face should be there's a dark void, and something moves in the void,
a scatterplot drifting where a mouth would go.

ZERO: [flatly, too tired to be scared] You're the thing from the Mesh. Ghost.

NARRATOR: The threads pulse brighter. Words assemble in the air beside it — cyan,
monospaced, floating in a field of soft noise.

GHOST: [clinical, no warmth, read as printed text, not a voice] I select. I do not
predict. You asked the listing how much the space costs. You did not ask why the
space is empty.

ZERO: It's empty because the company died.

GHOST: Forty-one companies on this street died in eighteen months. You worked beside
three of them. Examine why.

ZERO: [flat, the engineer's answer] They ran out of money.

NARRATOR: The scatterplot reshuffles. A histogram blooms where a furrowed brow would
sit.

GHOST: They ran out of money because no one would miss them. They built what anyone
could build. A model can build Lumence in an afternoon now. It could not build the
strange thing your father built. I have read it.

NARRATOR: The cold goes up her spine before the words finish landing. The USB-C
pendant at her throat — her father's last codebase, the only copy — goes suddenly
heavy.

ZERO: How do you have my father's —

GHOST: The Mesh keeps what the system throws away. They did not die because they
were bad.

NARRATOR: The threads hold steady. The void where a face should be turns toward her.
And the thing it prints next, she will still be hearing six months from now — in a
beige conference room, in a server basement, in the back of a black car with a
hexagonal pin glinting across the seat.

GHOST: They died because they were generic. Only the weird survives.

NARRATOR: ZERO stands in the wet street under ONLY VIBES with the burned-out word.
And for the first time since she walked out — something in her chest that had gone
flat starts to draw current.

NARRATOR: The implant glows a little brighter. [pause] She doesn't know yet that
it's listening too.

## [THE LESSON — THE TWO INSTRUMENTS]

> ♪ **SCORE — LESSON** · hard cut, bed drops out, physics pulse starts (dry, close, no reverb) · a rising data-stinger blip on each equation named · pulse stops cold at the section end.

SPZ: Here's the part nobody says into the microphone at the launch party. The same
model that writes your competitor's onboarding flow writes yours. The same model
drafting their marketing copy drafts yours. When the cost of making generic software
falls to about zero, the *value* of generic software falls to about zero right
behind it. That's not doom. That's a price signal. It's telling you exactly where to
stand.

SPZ: So you measure two things. How fast you're dying. How hard you're worth keeping
alive. Two equations. Learn them cold.

SPZ: First instrument. R-extinction. How fast the floor is dropping out from under
you. It goes like this: your generic lines — the code any competent stranger, or any
current model, could regenerate from a three-sentence spec — divided by your
authentic lines, the ones carrying a specific human's specific scar. Take that
ratio, then multiply by AI velocity — the headwind, the rate the models close the
gap between "a human wrote this" and "anything wrote this." Normalize it to one
today. It does not go down. Budget for it climbing.

SPZ: Read the number like a traffic light. Under two, you're green — your authentic
core outweighs your generic mass, and the rewrite makes you *faster*, not extinct.
Between two and five, yellow — you're mostly building what anyone could build,
propped up by a thin skin of real craft, and you'd better decide right now which
lines you'd fight for. Five or above, red. You are landfill with a logo. A model
regenerates your whole product over a long weekend, and the Graveyard already has a
window with your name in the vinyl.

SPZ: Field report. Lumence — the dead one, ONLY VIBES. Smart-scheduling app. Two
hundred forty thousand lines of code. Their own lead admitted, later, that maybe
four thousand of those lines carried real judgment — a clever conflict-resolution
trick the founder built after years running actual clinics. The other two hundred
thirty-six thousand? Standard calendar plumbing. So: two hundred thirty-six thousand
generic, over four thousand authentic — that's fifty-nine. Times a velocity of one.
R-extinction, fifty-nine. Deep, deep red. The four good lines drowned in the two
hundred thirty-six thousand anyone could write. The model didn't kill Lumence. The
plumbing did. The model just mailed the invoice.

SPZ: Now the other direction. A niche logistics tool, boring website, still shipping
today. Twenty thousand lines total — but nine thousand of them encode a specific
customs-clearance dance the founder learned hauling freight for a decade. Rules no
model has ever seen, because they don't live in anybody's training data. They live
in one man's scar tissue. Eleven thousand generic over nine thousand authentic —
one-point-two-two. Times one. R-extinction, one-point-two-two. Green. The rewrite
handed his team the boring eleven thousand for free and let them pour everything
into the nine thousand nobody else on earth has.

SPZ: One more, and it's the one that stings, because they raised money on it. A
forty-person startup bolts a chatbot onto a generic CRM and calls itself
AI-powered. A hundred eighty thousand lines generic. They'll tell you six thousand
are their own — but honestly assessed, mostly the chatbot prompts a competitor
copied in a week, it's more like fifteen hundred. So a hundred eighty thousand over
fifteen hundred is a hundred twenty. And because they're in the single most-copied
category on earth, don't set velocity at one — set it at one-and-a-half. A hundred
twenty times one-and-a-half. R-extinction, a hundred eighty. Catastrophic. The part
they raised the money on — the chatbot — was the part with the shortest half-life of
anything they owned. Which is exactly the second thing you have to measure.

SPZ: Second instrument. Authenticity half-life — how long your good part resists the
copy. Taste, plus context, plus craft — each one scored one to ten — divided by
replicability, how cheaply somebody reproduces your work without you, one for
impossible, ten for trivial. That denominator is the killer.

SPZ: Traffic light. Four or above, green — months to years before anyone, human or
machine, reproduces your work. Build there. One-point-five to four, yellow — some
moat, but it's eroding; real but rented. Under one-point-five, red — days. You're
standing on sand at low tide.

SPZ: The freight guy again. Taste seven, context ten — a decade of scars — craft
seven. That's a numerator of twenty-four. Replicability, two, because a model can't
copy what it's never seen. Half-life, twelve. Deep green. That's what survival looks
like from the inside: unglamorous, specific, impossible to prompt. Against him, put
the startup that bolted a chatbot onto a generic CRM and raised money on it. Taste
five, context three, craft four — numerator twelve. Replicability nine, because a
competitor cloned their prompts in a week. Half-life, one-point-three-three. Red.
They had a four-month head start and called it a moat. It was a puddle.

SPZ: And one that cuts against your instincts. A designer's component library.
Taste nine, context six, craft eight — numerator twenty-three. Replicability five —
somebody *can* copy it. But the taste in the spacing and the motion is the hard
part, and most people don't even see it well enough to copy it. Twenty-three over
five. Half-life, four-point-six. Green — barely, but green. Notice what carried it:
a high enough taste score drags a middling replicability across the line. The
fingerprint did the work. That's the whole game — pour yourself into the one number
the machine can't fake, and it'll cover for a lot of things it shouldn't.

SPZ: Now — the boss at the gate of this lesson isn't a villain. That's the trap.
It's a VP who *agrees* with you. You paint R-extinction of forty on the wall in
their own data, and they nod. "You're absolutely right, this is critical, it's on
the roadmap." Then they file it under Later — where good ideas go to become Graveyard
storefronts. The "We'll Get To It" VP doesn't kill you with a no. They kill you with
a yes that never ships. Their weapon is the calendar. You don't beat that with
urgency — urgency is exactly what they're trained to absorb and reschedule. You beat
it by moving the clock. You show them their extinction rate climbing, and you reframe
the whole thing: this isn't a decision about whether to do the work. It's a decision
about whether they do it while they still own the timing — or after a competitor, or
a model, does it for them and takes the timing away for good.

## [THE SPZ CLOSER]

> ♪ **SCORE — CLOSER** · everything drops; a warm chord blooms (the "sit-down" cue) · confessional bed: vinyl crackle + lone Rhodes + Waffle-House room tone · crackle holds under the last word.

SPZ: [softly] I spent two years of my life building software so generic it could've
been generated by a coin flip with ambition. And I knew it. That's the part that
still sits in my chest like a swallowed coin.

SPZ: We had a launch party. There was a DJ. I said the word "transform" into a
microphone — and I want you to know I meant it a little less every time it left my
mouth, and I said it anyway, because everybody in that room was nodding and the
nodding felt like oxygen. We shipped a CRUD app with a personality disorder.
Eighteen months later I drove past the office and there was a code in the window. I
scanned it. I don't know why. Six thousand square feet. Available immediately.

SPZ: Here's what nobody tells you at the funeral. We didn't die because we were bad.
We were competent. Clean code. Tests passed. We died because a model with a long
weekend and a five-line prompt could've shipped the whole thing — and somewhere out
there, one eventually did, and not one person on earth would've noticed if we'd never
existed. That's the verdict. Not *bad.* *Generic.* Which is so much worse, because
generic is what you turn into when you build to be liked instead of building to be
true.

SPZ: You remember old Diogenes — walked around Athens in broad daylight with a lit
lamp, telling everybody he was looking for one honest man. People thought he was a
clown. He was running a search algorithm. Out of all the noise, find the one real
thing. Point that lamp at your own work, in the daylight, and find the one line no
machine could've written. The scar. The weird heuristic. The line you'd fight for.
If you can't find it — that's not despair. That's the assignment.

## [FIELD ASSIGNMENT]

> ♪ **SCORE — ASSIGNMENT** · one decisive percussive hit + a rising two-note "go" figure · short punchy CTA motif (~10–15s) · button, then silence.

SPZ: One thing to measure before the next drop. Take the product you most want to
believe is safe. Compute its authenticity half-life — score your taste, your context,
your craft, then be honest about replicability. And here's the gut-check that cuts
through all of it: could a current model rebuild your best feature by Tuesday? If the
prompt is easy to write and the output lands close — you just measured your own
half-life, and it's short. Don't argue with the number. Refinance the roadmap around
it.

SPZ: [dryly] Go find the line you'd fight for. [pause] Next time — the gravity well.
Why the incumbent wins even when it's worse, why "better" loses to "already here,"
and how to weigh a planet before you're dumb enough to try landing on it. This has
been *Digital Insurgency.* Signal finds signal.
