# Day 12/30 — The Risk Tax
*A fable from the field manual · Chapter 4*

Conference Room 14-B. Beige carpet, fluorescent buzz, a speakerphone shaped like a stingray. The CIO sat down like a man bracing for a car accident.

ZERO opened her mouth to say *transform* — and watched his amygdala fire in real time. His jaw set. His shoulders climbed a quarter inch toward his ears. He didn't hear another word; the threat-brain had already run the math the slide deck couldn't see — probability of failure times the cost of downtime, plus the time-to-panic times the one number that actually ruled the room: what it would do to *his* career when it broke.

She stopped. Closed the laptop.

"What's the thing keeping you up at night?" she asked. "Not the board-deck version. The real one."

He blinked. Nobody asks that. "SARs," he said, before he could stop himself. "We're drowning. My best people are quitting."

"Then I'm not here to transform anything," ZERO said. "You've got a process that works. Nobody's getting fired on my watch. I'm here to refinance the part of it that makes your people quit."

His shoulders came down. A centimeter. That was the whole game — she had stopped being a threat and started being relief.

Later she told BISHOP: the pitch never mattered. The product was identical in both rooms. The only variable that moved was the number running silently behind the CIO's eyes — his private cost of being wrong.

---

**What it means.** The math running in your prospect's head, whether they know it or not. They aren't evaluating your features — they're evaluating their own career risk. If the panic-and-career term dominates, no upside will move them. Your job is to shrink that number, not inflate the benefit.

## The equation

$$ RT = (P_f \times C_d) + (T_{panic} \times C_{career}) $$

🟢 < 100 · 🟡 100–1000 · 🔴 > 1000 (career risk > any upside; walk).

**The moral of the story is:** You aren't selling to a company; you're selling to one frightened person doing risk math behind their eyes. Shrink their cost of being wrong before you ever mention the upside.
