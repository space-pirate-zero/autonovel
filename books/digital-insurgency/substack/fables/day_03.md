On the Mesh there was a builder everyone called Vell, who made the most beautiful component libraries in the underground. Buttons that felt alive. Forms that breathed. Other devs bolted his kits onto everything; for two years Vell was the most forked name in the dark.

Then the model got good at components.

Over one weekend a stranger pointed it at Vell's public work, and it produced a library that was, by every benchmark, *his* — same spacing, same easing, same restraint — and shipped it under a generic handle. Within a month Vell's forks dried up. He had spent two years making something gorgeous and copyable, and copyable was now free.

He found ZERO in a Mesh channel at 3 a.m., gutted. "How do I compete with a weekend?"

GHOST answered before she could, threads brightening in the channel. *You don't. You ask how long your work outlives the copyists. Taste, context, craft — over how easily you're replicated. Your kits were pure craft and nothing else, and craft alone has a half-life of a weekend now.*

"So what survives?"

*The work carrying a judgment a model can't infer.* The threads drew a slow curve. *The font a half-deaf typographer drew for how it sits in the hand. The flow that breaks its own rules for the one user who needed it to. Put lived context the machine has never had into the work, and the half-life climbs out of its reach.*

Vell went quiet a long time. Then he started over — slower, stranger, harder to fork. His next library had eleven users.

It is still, today, the one the machine cannot make.

MORAL: Don't ask how good your work is. Ask how long before a machine remakes it. Pour in taste and lived context until copying you isn't worth anyone's weekend.
