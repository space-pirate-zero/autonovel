ZERO walked into the CFO's office with a twenty-two-slide deck. She got to slide three.

Richard Park had been in finance thirty years and had watched, by his own estimate, three hundred million dollars of technology spend evaporate against promises like the ones on her slides. His face was a closed door. He'd seen this deck — not this one, this *archetype* of deck: the one that asks a man to bet on a hockey-stick drawn by someone who won't be there when it bends the wrong way.

ZERO closed the laptop.

"I have twenty-two slides and none of them matter," she said. "Can I show you one number instead?"

She put down a single sheet. Not a forecast — an autopsy. The legacy compliance system, fully loaded: labor, errors, turnover, regulatory exposure. Four and a half million a year. And beneath it, the same figure compounding at seventeen percent: nine million in five years, eighteen in ten. The cost to replace it doubled roughly every five years he waited.

"The cheapest option on this table looks the most expensive today," she said. "Doing nothing is the line that bankrupts you. It just does it quietly."

Park studied the sheet a long time. Then: "Why hasn't anyone shown me this before?"

"Because everyone else was selling you the future," ZERO said. "I'm showing you the present. The future is just arithmetic."

He approved the pilot before she reached the door.

MORAL: Technical debt is a loan compounding against you — the cost to fix it doubles while you wait. CFOs don't buy visions; they refinance liabilities. Bring the one number that shows the fire under the chair.
