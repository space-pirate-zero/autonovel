REAPER caught ZERO's scent first. PRISM's algorithm flagged her access as "anomalous vendor behavior," and the internal audit team began to circle — three auditors, a forty-seven-point security questionnaire, and the particular smile of people paid to find something wrong.

Most vendors panic here, or lawyer up. Both trip the immune response and get you ejected. ZERO did the opposite. She walked *toward* the audit.

She set a three-inch binder on the table. Color-coded tabs. SOC 2. Penetration test. Data-flow diagram. Incident-response plan. A full audit trail of every query her system had ever run, timestamped and hashed. And then the page that mattered: a comparison matrix — her controls against the incumbent vendor's. She passed thirty-six of thirty-seven. The legacy vendor passed twenty-eight. She flagged the one she failed *herself*, in writing, because nothing disarms an auditor like a vendor who hands over their own weakness.

The audit was scheduled for three hours. It took forty-five minutes.

"You didn't beat it by being secure," BISHOP told her after, adjusting his crooked badge. "You beat it by making the legacy vendor the risky choice. The CISO's job was never security — it's having someone to blame. You handed him a stamp and a clean conscience."

REAPER's red flag downgraded from *anomalous* to *reviewed and cleared.* The fortress's own paperwork, turned into a key. Michael Collins beat an empire by reading its intelligence better than it read itself.

MORAL: The CISO sells the absence of blame, not security. Don't fight the audit — bring the artifacts, the comparison matrix, and your own flagged weakness, and make keeping the old vendor the dangerous choice.
