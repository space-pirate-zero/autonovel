The pilot needed funding, and ZERO had learned never to ask a CFO to believe in anything.

So she didn't. She brought a trade so lopsided that saying no would have been the irrational act.

The math was plain. The human cost of the work her tool replaced: 2.8 million a year. The cost of her tool: a hundred and eighty thousand. The pilot itself: forty-five thousand. Divide the savings by the pilot and you got a number — fifty-eight — meaning the experiment paid for itself fifty-eight times over before the first invoice came due.

"This isn't a cost," she told Park. "It's a reallocation. You're moving money out of a line item that sets it on fire and into one that doesn't. I'll even structure it so you don't pay until the savings clear the price. Net zero risk."

Park, who had killed a hundred pitches that asked him to gamble, had never been handed a trade he'd be *negligent* to refuse. Branson built an empire out of a record shop, not a war chest — by finding the deal so obviously good the only question left was why you hadn't done it yesterday.

That was the exact question on Park's face. Not *should we?* — *why didn't we?*

He signed.

MORAL: Don't ask the CFO to believe in your vision — hand him a trade he'd be negligent to refuse. When the pilot pays for itself many times over, "should we?" becomes "why didn't we do this yesterday?"
