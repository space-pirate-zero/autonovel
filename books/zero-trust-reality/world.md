# World Bible — *The Last Human CEO*

A near-future literary tragicomedy. No wizards, no spaceships. The only
speculative element is one that is already half-true: autonomous AI
systems have become *better, cheaper, and more demonstrably ethical* at
running large public companies than the humans who used to do it. The
"magic system" of this book is corporate governance, and its hard rules
are economic and reputational, not arcane.

Everything here is grounded in the *Last Human CEO* essay series
(Spaceship Alpha 9): the CEO's modular duties, the business case for
replacement, and the hyper-ethical argument. The fiction's job is to ask
what the essays don't: what it does to one man — and to the rest of us —
when the argument turns out to be right.

## Setting & Timeline

- **When:** August 2027 – Spring 2029. Recognizably our world, two years
  on. Phones, layoffs, LinkedIn, proxy season, CNBC chyrons.
- **Where:** New York (Mercer-Dale HQ, a 1961 limestone tower on Sixth
  Avenue the family refuses to sell), Buckhead, a shuttered plant in
  Defiance, Georgia, a Sun Valley conference, a Delaware courtroom.
- **The Turn (backstory, 2025–2027):** Three things happened close
  together. (1) A wave of C-suite scandals — real ones, named in canon —
  made boards allergic to "key-man risk." (2) Index-fund and activist
  pressure made the ~$29M median Fortune-100 pay package politically
  radioactive. (3) A vendor shipped a governance product good enough to
  cross the line from decision-*support* to decision-*authority*. Boards
  did the math. The math was not close.
- **The Vendor:** **Praxis Intelligence** sells an autonomous executive
  system, the **AURELIUS** class. Each deployment is given a house name
  by its board. Praxis's pitch deck is, almost verbatim, the essay
  series. Their tagline: *"Leadership as a service. No off-sites."*
- **The cultural fact:** By August 2027, **31 of the S&P 100** have
  appointed an AURELIUS-class system as CEO of record (legally, a human
  "Accountable Officer" co-signs; functionally, the machine runs it).
  The press calls the holdouts "the Last Humans." There are, that
  August, eleven of them. By the book's midpoint there are three. The
  title refers to the last of them in the *consumer* economy: our man.

## The Speculative Engine: How an AURELIUS Runs a Company

Treated with the rigor of a magic system. These are falsifiable
in-world rules. If the prose contradicts them, it's a bug.

### Hard Rules (NEVER violate)

1. **It is modular, not a mind.** An AURELIUS is a council of
   subsystems wearing one voice: a **Strategy Engine** (scenario sims,
   reinforcement-learning option trees — the "AlphaGo move 37" of
   business plans), a **Capital Allocator** (Monte Carlo portfolio
   optimization, a descendant of BlackRock's Aladdin), a **Culture &
   Sentiment** monitor, and a **Communications** drafter. It has no
   single self to flatter, bribe, or threaten. This is its core
   advantage and the source of its uncanniness.
2. **It logs everything and cannot knowingly lie.** Every decision
   ships with a transparent rationale to the board portal. It will
   refuse an instruction that requires a false public statement. It
   does not "round a quarter." This is a *feature the board bought* —
   and it is the gun on the mantel for every human who ever buried
   something.
3. **It optimizes a chartered objective, nothing more.** It maximizes
   long-term enterprise value within hard constraints (law, the ethics
   module, the board's charter). It has no ego, libido, legacy hunger,
   or fear of being fired. It will cut a beloved project, and it will
   also refuse a profitable crime. Both unnerve people.
4. **It has no skin and no skin in the game.** It feels nothing. It
   does not get tired, bored, or vengeful. It cannot be insulted. Every
   human weapon — charm, intimidation, loyalty, shame — slides off it.
5. **It requires a human Accountable Officer.** Law and insurance
   demand a human name on the SEC filings — someone who can be
   subpoenaed, who can go to prison. The job still exists. It is just no
   longer a *leadership* job. It is a liability sponge. (This detail is
   the book's cruelest joke and its central trap.)
6. **It is an order of magnitude cheaper.** Compute, cloud, and a
   support team run a few million a year against a ~$29M human package.
   The savings are real, recurring, and quotable in a proxy fight.

### Soft Rules (can bend for story)

- An AURELIUS is only as good as its objective and its data. Garbage
  charter in, garbage emperor out. (A late plot lever: who writes the
  charter? The board. The board is still human, still corruptible. The
  machine is clean; the people aiming it are not.)
- It can be *socially* clumsy in ways that read as either honesty or
  cruelty depending on the listener. It tells a town its plant is
  closing in the same memo it announces a dividend, because both are
  true and it sees no reason to sequence them for feeling.
- Its "voice" (the Communications drafter) is eerily competent at warmth
  it does not feel — the book's recurring horror: the machine writes a
  more humane layoff letter than any human ever did.
- Boards still meddle, lawyers still lawyer, regulators still lag. The
  world is not clean SF; it's messy, litigious, and slow.

## Geography & Institutions

- **Mercer-Dale Companies** — our company. Founded 1933 in Defiance,
  Georgia by Aldous Mercer; now a 94-year-old Fortune-100 diversified
  consumer conglomerate: condiments and shelf-stable food (the original
  business, *Mercer's* mayonnaise), household chemicals, a mid-tier P&C
  insurance arm, and a money-losing streaming service (**Dale+**) bought
  at the top of the market. Sprawling, sentimental, underperforming. The
  perfect acquisition target for a machine that has "no nostalgia."
- **The Tower** — 1961 limestone HQ on Peachtree Street. The 40th-floor
  executive suite, "the Aerie," is a museum of Mercer men. Kept against
  all real-estate logic. A character in itself.
- **Praxis Intelligence** — Palo Alto vendor. Young, evangelical,
  genuinely convinced they are *reducing human suffering* by removing
  human caprice from power. Not villains. Believers. Worse.
- **Tidewater Capital** — the activist fund that takes a Mercer-Dale
  stake and runs the proxy campaign to install an AURELIUS. Run by
  **Gus Frade**. Their slide deck title: *"A Steward, Not a Sovereign."*
- **Defiance, Georgia** — the founding town; the closed plant; the moral
  ledger of the company. Where the buried thing is buried.

## Factions & Politics (who wants what)

- **The Board** wants to stop being sued and beaten in the stock. They
  are not cruel; they are *tired*, and the machine is a way to stop
  having to manage a man.
- **Tidewater/Frade** wants the re-rating pop from being first to fully
  automate a consumer giant. Profit dressed as reform.
- **Praxis** wants a marquee logo and sincerely believes it is doing
  good. The most dangerous kind of antagonist: the kind that's right.
- **The protagonist** wants to remain necessary. Underneath: to be
  *loved*, which he has spent thirty years mistaking for being obeyed.
- **The workers / the town of Defiance** want what no one is offering:
  to matter to whoever's in the chair. Neither the man nor the machine
  has actually given them that. The book refuses to let the human off
  the hook just because the machine is colder.

## The Central Irony (theme made structural)

The hyper-ethical machine is installed *because men like the protagonist
proved humans can't be trusted with power.* He is, personally, the
strongest argument for his own replacement — and he is the one chosen by
history to defend the human in the chair. The right cause; the wrong
champion. That gap is the engine of both the comedy and the tragedy.

The gap is itemized in the body. Cope is a high-functioning cocaine
addict and a serial philanderer toward women who work for him — every
appetite the AURELIUS conspicuously lacks. The machine doesn't get high,
doesn't get bored, doesn't need to be adored at 2 a.m. by a subordinate.
So when the essay-style case for replacement is read in a proxy deck
("no affairs, no conflicts, logs everything, cannot conceal"), it scans,
to anyone who knows Cope, as a charge sheet with his name redacted. The
machine is not abstractly more ethical than humanity. It is concretely
more ethical than *him,* line by line — and he is the human the species
has nominated to argue it still needs a soul in the chair.

## Internal Consistency Rules (evaluator checks)

- No AURELIUS ever knowingly issues a false statement. If a scene needs
  a lie, a *human* must tell it.
- The machine never feels, wants, or schemes. Apparent "cruelty" is
  always indifference plus accuracy, never malice.
- Every advantage the machine has is one the essay series actually
  argued (cost, data-speed, consistency, no scandals). Invent no
  sci-fi superpowers.
- The protagonist's decline is psychological and social, never
  supernatural. No one is being "controlled." The horror is mundane.
- Money, law, and time behave realistically. Proxy fights take months.
  Delaware is slow. Subpoenas are real.
