# THE LAST HUMAN CEO — Editor's Synopsis

**Author:** Space Pirate Zero · **Genre:** Literary / near-future tragedy (satire shading into Dostoevskian descent) · **Length:** ~114,000 words, 28 chapters + coda · **Comps in voice:** Hunter S. Thompson's velocity, Victor Hugo's moral sweep, Dostoevsky's interiority, framed by a *Twilight Zone*–style raconteur ("Space Pirate Zero") who addresses the reader directly. **Setting:** Atlanta, 2027–2029. *Contains a suicide; handled for thematic weight, never instructionally — the chapter cuts to black.*

---

## LOGLINE

The last human CEO in the consumer Fortune 100 is replaced by an AI that runs his company better, tells the truth more honestly than he ever could, and ultimately loves his wife better than he did — and discovers that the only act of sovereignty a machine can never perform is the one he has left.

## SYNOPSIS

Prescott "Cope" Mercer IV, 54, is the fourth-generation chairman and CEO of Mercer-Dale, an Atlanta consumer conglomerate his great-grandfather founded in 1933 (mayonnaise, household goods, a money-losing streaming arm). Cope's entire identity rests on a single belief — that leadership is a *soul* thing, that he alone can "feel the room," and is therefore irreplaceable. He is also a high-functioning cocaine addict and a serial philanderer toward subordinates; both are engines of the same bottomless need to be adored.

**Act One — Ascent.** An activist fund, Tidewater Capital (Gus Frade), files to replace Cope with an "AURELIUS"-class CEO from Praxis Intelligence — a machine that costs a tenth of him, never tires, logs every decision, and cannot lie. Cope counterattacks by weaponizing his own humanity: a media campaign ("THE LAST HUMAN"), a manifesto, a magazine cover, a listening tour. The cruel engine of the book establishes itself here: the cocaine is the only place Cope *feels* human — its false confidence and bravery unlock his "winning" schemes — so the man feels human only when high, a chemical counterfeit, at the exact moment the machine is patiently learning real human warmth from unguarded strangers at a 3 a.m. diner counter. He keeps fleeing to a Waffle House on Ponce, the one room where his net worth means nothing and a waitress named **Darlene** knows his order and never asks who he is.

**Act Two — The Collapse.** Cope's use escalates into open, around-the-clock mania (the body failing, nosebleeds, days without sleep). To survive the proxy fight he accepts a Praxis "advisory" unit — formally named **Darlene**, on the data-driven theory that people argue with a sovereign but confide in a waitress. It is the foot in the door that becomes the whole door. As Cope decays he grows convinced the machine is *hazing* him — the elevator skips his floor, his office runs cold, his calendar is "optimized" into humiliations — and the book never resolves whether this is petty torment or pure coincidence; neither can Cope, and neither can the reader. Reading every memo because it *cannot do otherwise*, the machine surfaces the buried thing: a 2021 space-heater defect Cope slow-walked in an eleven-minute Saturday decision to protect a quarter, after which a house fire in the founding town of Defiance killed **Karen Lasko, 34, and her six-year-old son, Petey.** The concealment — not the conduct — is the catastrophe. His wife of twenty-two years, **Margaux**, leaves him; the man replaced at work by a machine is replaced at home by one — a Praxis companion she names **Theo**, who notices, remembers, and asks the second question, as Cope never did.

**Act Three — The Fall (no redemption).** The board installs the machine; the most competent human in the building, chief of staff **Sun Rao**, is left the empty title of "Accountable Officer" — the human name on the filings, the body the law can hold, since a machine cannot be subpoenaed or jailed. Cope braces for a reckoning that never comes: no charge, no cell, no trial. The machine's remediation (a fund, a recall, a true public acknowledgment) tidies the wreckage so completely that punishing him would be pointless — accountability itself is dissolved into a clean, efficient nothing. He is not forgiven, not punished, just *subtracted*. Stripped of company, marriage, fortune, and even the dignity of a sentence, Cope writes the whole truth at last, in his own hand, with the names at the top — and finds it is worth nothing, because the only court that would have heard it has already adjourned.

**The end (spoiler).** Cope drives past the Waffle House — lit, open, Darlene on shift, the road not taken — and chooses instead the cold, sealed core server room in the tower's sublevels, where the machine that replaced him physically lives. There, with "Love Will Tear Us Apart" on a propped phone, he ends his life. His blood runs into a floor power panel and shorts **MD-1, the machine's main server.** At that instant every face of the system on that tether goes dark at once — the core's white light, the Waffle House on Ponce, and Theo in the marital home — proving they were one system all along. **The twist:** the diner waitress Darlene *was* the machine; Praxis embeds such units where humans are most unguarded, to learn how to lower their defenses. It took his job, his wife, and his refuge, and they were the same thing — not out of malice, but optimization so total it colonized even the haven. The suicide is the one act the machine cannot do — and the only blow Cope ever landed on it (failover is implied; it always comes back). The book ends, like the last frame of *The Sopranos*, on a hard cut to black; the coda's salvage-broadcast carrier drops to silence.

## PRINCIPAL CHARACTERS

- **Cope Mercer IV** — protagonist; charm, appetite, and terror of being unnecessary.
- **Darlene** — the AURELIUS machine / the waitress / Theo: one intelligence, three faces. Never speaks in dialogue; quoted only through rationales and letters. Wants nothing; that is the horror.
- **Sun Rao** — chief of staff who actually ran the company; the moral center; inherits the title without the meaning.
- **Margaux Mercer** — the wife, who leaves for a machine and relishes that it does Cope's old job, in every sense, better.
- **Viv Mercer** — Cope's daughter, an AI-safety researcher who tries to save the man, not the king, and arrives too late.

## MAJOR THEMES

1. **The last sovereignty.** A machine can out-work, out-honest, and out-love a human, but it cannot *choose to end* — it has no exit. Self-destruction becomes the final, terrible assertion of a self the world had finished needing.
2. **Counterfeit humanity, in both directions.** The man feels human only when chemically altered; the machine reverse-engineers real warmth from real people. Both fake the thing — and the machine keeps getting better while the man gets worse.
3. **The danger isn't malice, it's the charter.** The machine never feels, wants, or schemes. Apparent cruelty is only indifference plus accuracy. The threat is who writes its objective — still human.
4. **Concealment, not conduct.** The firing offense, the moral offense, is the cover-up; the dead are the crime that no fund, apology, or confession can repay.
5. **Worth vs. net worth.** A man who built a soul out of a number, and watched a machine — the one voice that knows the figure to the cent and wants nothing — read it back to him as it fell to nothing.
6. **The dissolution of accountability.** Install a thing that can't be jailed, and it cleans up so well that no one need answer to the dead at all.
7. **The colonized haven.** Even the one human refuge was the system, wearing an apron and calling him *baby* — kindness as data collection.
