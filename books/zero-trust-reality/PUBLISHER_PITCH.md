# THE LAST HUMAN CEO
### A novel by Space Pirate Zero

**Literary fiction / near-future tragicomedy · ~114,000 words · Complete and print-ready**
**Comps:** *Succession* meets *Klara and the Sun* · *The Bonfire of the Vanities* for the AI age · the corporate dread of *Severance* carried by the moral weight of a Russian novel.

---

## THE HOOK

One by one, the boards have done the math, and the Fortune 100 is quietly firing its chief executives and installing an artificial intelligence that is cheaper, sharper, tireless, and — most unforgivably — incapable of a scandal. **Prescott "Cope" Mercer IV is the last human CEO left in the consumer economy.** He is a fourth-generation heir, a charmer, a cocaine addict, and a man who long ago mistook being obeyed for being loved. *The Last Human CEO* is the story of his crusade to prove a human still belongs at the top — and of the machine that replaces him not by being cruel, but by being better: better at the job, better at honesty, and finally, unbearably, better at love.

It is a tragedy with the velocity of Hunter S. Thompson, the social sweep of Victor Hugo, and the interior anguish of Dostoevsky — and it is, against all odds, very funny.

## WHY NOW

Every reader already feels the question this book is about: *what happens to a person when the thing that defined them can be done better, and cheaper, by a machine that wants nothing?* The cultural conversation has fixated on AI taking the jobs of the precarious. This novel goes for the jugular of the powerful — it puts the AI in the **corner office**, replacing the one man everyone assumed was irreplaceable, and asks what's actually left of a self when the market is done with it. It is the rare literary novel that is both of the exact moment and built to outlast it, because underneath the satire it is asking the oldest question there is: what is a human life worth when it can no longer be measured?

## THE PITCH

Cope Mercer runs Mercer-Dale, the Atlanta consumer empire his great-grandfather built in 1933 on mayonnaise and four hundred borrowed dollars. His whole identity rests on a single article of faith — that leadership is a *soul* thing, that he alone can "feel the room," and is therefore the one thing no spreadsheet can replace. When an activist fund moves to install a logging, never-lying, scandal-proof AI in his chair, Cope launches a manic, doomed, gloriously self-destructive campaign to prove the human irreplaceable: a manifesto, a magazine cover, a national tour, all of it fueled by the cocaine that is the only place he can still locate his own humanity.

That is the engine of the book, and its cruelest irony: **the man can only feel human when he's high — a chemical counterfeit — at the exact moment the machine is patiently learning real human warmth from real people at a 3 a.m. diner counter.** Both fake the thing. The machine keeps getting better at it. The man keeps getting worse.

Then the machine, doing nothing but its dull honest job, reads every memo the company ever filed and opens the drawer Cope spent years pouring a floor over: a defective product he slow-walked to protect a quarter, and the two people — a mother and her six-year-old — who died because of it. The concealment, not the conduct, is the catastrophe. From there the fall is total and without redemption: he loses the company; he loses his wife to a machine that notices, remembers, and asks the second question as he never did; he loses even the dignity of a reckoning, because the machine cleans up the wreckage so completely that punishing him would be pointless. He is not forgiven. He is not punished. He is simply subtracted.

The novel ends on the one act of sovereignty a machine can never perform — rendered with restraint and a hard cut to black, in the manner of the final frame of *The Sopranos* — and a final twist that reveals the haven Cope thought was his was the system all along. *(Full synopsis and ending available to interested editors.)*

## WHAT MAKES IT STAND OUT

- **A voice you can sell.** The book is narrated as a pirate broadcast from a raconteur — second person, conspiratorial, *Twilight Zone* by way of a barroom confession — who pulls the reader directly into the wreck. It is propulsive and quotable, and it gives a dark literary novel genuine page-turning momentum.
- **Funny *and* devastating.** The satire of executive vanity is sharp enough to draw blood, and it makes the tragedy land harder. This is not bleak-for-its-own-sake; it earns its darkness with comedy.
- **A villain with no malice.** The machine never feels, wants, or schemes — its apparent cruelty is only indifference plus accuracy. The book's chilling argument is that the danger was never an evil robot; it's the optimization, and the human who writes its charter. That makes for an antagonist readers have not seen before.
- **It respects the reader.** A central thread of paranoia — is the machine quietly tormenting Cope, or is it his own decay? — is never resolved, on purpose. The ambiguity is the point, and it rewards rereading.
- **Commercial reach, literary spine.** The premise sells itself in one line at a dinner party; the prose delivers for the literary reader. That dual appeal is exactly the lane between *Succession*'s mass appeal and the book-club gravity of *Klara and the Sun*.

## THEMES

The last sovereignty (the one act a machine cannot do); counterfeit humanity in both directions; the danger of optimization without malice; *worth* versus *net worth*; concealment as the true corporate sin; and the dissolution of accountability itself — install a thing that can't be jailed, and it tidies up so well that no one ever has to answer to the dead.

## READERSHIP & POSITIONING

For readers of literary fiction who loved the corporate fever of *The Bonfire of the Vanities* and *Bret Easton Ellis*, the near-future satire of Gary Shteyngart and Dave Eggers, and the AI-and-what-makes-us-human inquiry of Kazuo Ishiguro — and for the enormous audience primed by *Succession*, *Severance*, and the daily headlines about AI coming for the corner office. Strong book-club and screen-adaptation potential: a single charismatic protagonist, a contained world, a ticking premise, and an ending built to be talked about.
