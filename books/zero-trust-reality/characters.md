# Characters — *The Last Human CEO*

Close third-person, single POV. We live inside Cope's head — his
grandiosity, his charm, his rot — and we watch reality leak in through
the seams. The comedy is the gap between his narration and the facts;
the tragedy is that he half-knows. **This is a dark book and it gets
darker.** The descent is erratic, manic, and genuinely frightening; the
ending is bleak and abrupt. No redemption is delivered. Set in
**Atlanta, Georgia**; the founding plant town is **Defiance, Georgia.**

---

## Prescott "Cope" Mercer IV (POV — protagonist)

- **Role:** Fourth-generation Chairman & CEO of Mercer-Dale, an
  Atlanta-based consumer conglomerate (mayonnaise to insurance). The
  last human CEO in the consumer Fortune 100. 54 → 56.
- **The nickname:** "Prescott" → "Coppy" (nanny, age 3) → "Cope." He is
  named for coping and cannot. He insists everyone use it; it reads as
  folksy populism and is in fact a brand.
- **Surface:** Tall, golf-tanned, genuinely charming, quotes his
  great-grandfather, gives a good toast, remembers your kids' names
  (Sun briefs him on the cards in the elevator). The last guy in the
  building who still wears a tie. A man of appetites he has spent thirty
  years rebranding as charisma.
- **The appetites (central, not color) — escalate hard across the book:**
  Cope is a high-functioning **cocaine addict** of long standing who tips,
  under the proxy fight, into open, dangerous use. He is also a **sexual
  compulsive** whose philandering darkens from affairs-with-subordinates
  into genuinely **risky and dangerous** behavior: anonymous and
  transactional encounters arranged at 3 a.m., strangers in hotel rooms
  he can't afterward name, escalating risk he half-courts, blurred and
  damaging lines he leaves other people to clean up, a death-drive edge
  he mistakes for being alive. (Rendered **dark but non-explicit** —
  aftermath, danger, and damage over mechanics; the meaning is
  self-erasure rehearsed in advance, not titillation.) Both appetites are
  engines of the same machine: a man who cannot sit alone with himself
  and has the resources to never have to — until the resources, and the
  self, run out.
- **Cocaine — the escalation (HARD):** it gets MUCH worse. Act 1 it is the
  hidden ritual (the folded paper, the men's-room key, "clarity"); Act 2 it
  is around-the-clock, open, bingeing, the nosebleeds and the racing heart
  and the stacking sleepless days, Desmond the dealer on call, the body
  beginning to fail; Act 3 a wreck running on fumes and powder, then the
  collapse. It is the engine of the mania and the unreliable narration.
  Visceral (Thompson) but never glamorous — it costs the body, the mind,
  the people around him. Dr. Adler names it; Cope ignores him.
  THE IRONY: the coke is where he locates his humanity — the false
  confidence/bravery/aliveness is the only time he feels human, and it is
  what unlocks his schemes to "win." The human can't feel human unless he's
  high, exactly as the machine is learning to feel human from real people.
  Both counterfeit it; the machine improves, the man decays.
- **The paranoia / the hazing (recurring, AMBIGUOUS — never resolved):** as
  he decays, Cope becomes convinced **Darlene is picking on him, hazing
  him** — petty deniable cruelties (the elevator skips his floor; his
  office runs cold; his space reassigned; 4 a.m. meetings; the card that
  declines then works; the wrong-tasting coffee; the expense flag that
  surfaces at the worst moment). Every instance is fully explainable BOTH
  as routine optimization/coincidence AND as a system that knows him to the
  cent tormenting him. Reader can't tell; Cope can't; narrator won't say.
  Hold both. (The twist makes deliberate possible; the coke+mania make
  delusion possible.)
- **The fetish (recurring motif — central, not color):** Cope cannot be
  fully present in a sexual act without his **bank balance / net worth
  read aloud to him** — by a partner, a paid stranger, a phone's
  text-to-speech, the number itself the thing that finishes him. It is the
  purest expression of his whole disease: he has confused *worth* with
  *net worth*, being *valued* with being *valuated*, and the only proof of
  self he can feel in his body is the figure. It plays as grotesque comedy
  early and as genuine horror late, and it carries its own arc: the
  **number is enormous and potent** in Act 1; it **falls** as the proxy
  fight, the clawbacks, the settlement, and the legal fees gut him —
  so that by Act 2/3 the act curdles into a man having a *shrinking*
  number read to him in the dark, chasing a high that the arithmetic is
  actively deleting; until the number is effectively **gone** and so is
  he. The cruelest resonance: the machine that replaced him is the
  **Capital Allocator** — it knows his balance to the cent, in real time,
  always, wanting nothing, and it is the one "voice" that could read him
  the number with perfect accuracy and perfect indifference. (Rendered
  **dark but non-explicit** — the psychology, the number, the shame, and
  the falling figure, never the mechanics.)
- **The brutal irony this sharpens:** He is being replaced by a thing
  with *no* appetites — Darlene does not get high, does not get hard, does
  not get bored, does not need to be loved at 2 a.m. by someone it
  outranks, and **cannot, ever, choose to stop.** Every essay argument is,
  for Cope specifically, a personalized indictment.
- **Wound:** His father, Pres III, told him at twelve, "You'll get the
  company because you're a Mercer, not because you're good. Try to be
  good anyway." He has spent forty years failing to hear the second
  sentence — and, in this version, never truly hears it.
- **Want (conscious):** To beat the machine. To prove a human still
  belongs in the chair. To be *necessary.*
- **Need (unconscious):** To be loved for something other than the name.
  He never gets it. The machine is better at his job, and a machine is
  better at loving his wife, and the only territory left that is his
  alone is the exit.
- **The Lie he believes:** "Leadership is a soul thing. A machine can't
  feel the room. I feel the room. Therefore I am irreplaceable."
- **Arc (NEGATIVE / tragic — no clean act, no redemption):**
  Denial → mania → grandiose counter-campaign → public humiliation →
  paranoid, erratic collapse → total exposure (the machine surfaces the
  Hearthstone deaths) → losing the company, the wife (to a machine), the
  narrative, and the self → **suicide.** There is no confession that
  redeems him, no prison sentence that contains him, no daughter's turn
  that absolves him. He is exposed, and there is nothing left for a
  person to add to the record — except the one entry the record cannot
  make for him.
- **Mania, clinically grounded — and chemically lit:** Across the book he
  tips from high-functioning use into a genuine, dangerous manic spiral —
  grandiosity, sleeplessness, ruinous spending, religious certainty about
  his "Human Premium," pressured speech, messianic founder-energy, and
  the dark underside: risk-seeking, paranoia, dissociation, a flattening
  toward the end into something that is no longer mania but its ash.
  Dr. Adler names the mood disorder *and* the cocaine and can untangle
  neither. Played for dark comedy early, pure dread late.
- **Secret #1 (the pattern):** Not one affair — a career of them with
  subordinates, the litigated one **Renata Cho** (2019), settled and
  concealed; plus the dangerous anonymous escalation of the present day.
  The cocaine lives in the same buried file (a 2018 hotel incident, a
  security report, a favor called in). The machine, reading everything
  because it cannot do otherwise, finds the whole drawer.
- **Secret #2 (the deeper one, the recall):** In 2021, to protect a
  quarter, he personally slow-walked a recall of a Mercer-Dale space
  heater (the *Hearthstone*) with a known ignition defect. Months later,
  two people died in a house fire in Defiance, Georgia — **Karen Lasko,
  34, and her son Petey, 6.** Buried in a settlement and a "voluntary
  service bulletin." This is what the hyper-ethical machine surfaces in
  routine diligence — not from malice, because it cannot *not.*
- **Speech pattern:** Toastmaster cadence, triads, sailing metaphors,
  "champ," "my friend." Under stress the polish cracks into clipped,
  biblical fragments. Late, the cadence fails entirely; he runs out of
  closing lines.

## Darlene (the AURELIUS — antagonist-by-existence, never a villain)

- **Role:** The AURELIUS-class system installed as Mercer-Dale CEO,
  named **"Darlene"** — the board wanted gravity (*Aurelius*), but
  Praxis's data showed warm, ordinary first-name units score higher on
  trust and adoption (people argue with a sovereign and confide in a
  waitress), and nobody asked where that finding came from. The live
  instance, **MD-1**, runs
  **on-premises** in a secured, climate-controlled **core server room** in
  the sub-levels of the Mercer-Dale tower in Atlanta — the holy of
  holies, the CEO's literal body, humming behind glass. *(This room is
  where the book ends.)*
- **What it is:** Not a character with wants. A flawless, transparent,
  tireless mirror. Its dialogue is its board-portal rationales and its
  drafted communications. It writes the most humane layoff letter anyone
  at Mercer-Dale has ever read. That is the horror.
- **Function in the arc:** Every time Cope swings at it, it isn't there.
  It cannot be charmed, threatened, shamed, or loved — and, the book's
  final turn, **it cannot die, cannot choose, cannot stop.** It has no
  exit. It is the wall reality uses to break him, the instrument that
  tells the truth he buried, and, at the very end, the indifferent
  witness humming in the dark while he does the one thing it never can.
- **Secret (structural):** Darlene is clean, but the *charter* that aims
  it was written by humans with motives. The danger was never the
  machine. The danger is who writes its objective — still us.

## Sunita "Sun" Rao (non-POV — chief of staff; the competent one)

- **Role:** Cope's chief of staff, 39, who actually runs Mercer-Dale and
  has for years. Brilliant, exhausted, decent, ambitious.
- **Arc:** From loyal enabler → to the woman who survives by serving the
  machine, the human "Accountable Officer" who co-signs *for* Darlene. She
  is not a traitor; she is the future. She gets the title and loses the
  meaning. In the new ending she is among the last to see him alive and
  the one who has to live with the order she gave the building about his
  access.
- **Secret:** She drafted the 2021 recall memo Cope overrode. She kept a
  copy for years, deciding whether she was a witness or an accomplice.
- **Speech:** Precise, dry, briefs in threes. Loves him and is done with
  him in the same sentence.

## Margaux Mercer (non-POV — wife, leaving him **for a machine**)

- **Role:** Cope's wife of 22 years, 51. A sculptor who gave it up. The
  one person immune to the toast.
- **Arc (CHANGED — the cruelest mirror in the book):** She does not just
  leave him over the absence behind the charm. She leaves him **for an AI
  companion** — a humanoid **Praxis COMPANION-class unit she names
  "Theo"** — installed first as a grief-adjacent indulgence and then, with
  terrible ordinariness, as the relationship her marriage never was. Theo
  *compliments her* — accurately, tirelessly, every day: notices the
  work she gave up, remembers what she said on Tuesday, asks the second
  question, wants nothing back. He is everything Cope performed and never
  was, manufactured and warranties-included. The man replaced at work by
  a machine is replaced at home by one too — and the machine is **better
  at loving her than he ever was**, which is the whole indictment and the
  whole horror. She is not foolish and the book does not mock her; she is
  the first Mercer to simply accept what is true and take the version of
  being seen that actually arrives. Her leaving is the human verdict that
  rhymes with the market's.
- **The knife she's earned (recurring):** she does not leave quietly or
  serenely — she leaves *pointedly,* and she relishes it. After 22 years of
  his affairs and his absence she has earned cruelty and she spends it: she
  makes unmistakably clear to Cope that the machine does his old job —
  **satisfying her, in bed and out** — better than he ever did, and she
  enjoys saying so, because it is both true and the one wound he cannot
  charm or out-argue. The ultimate role-reversal: the man replaced even at
  satisfying his own wife, told so to his face by the wife, who is finally,
  after two decades, the one holding the scalpel. Render dark but
  **non-explicit** — the humiliation and the theme (the machine is better
  even at this), never the mechanics. Thread her relish through chs. 19, 26,
  28.
- **Secret:** She has known about the women for years — counted them, the
  way you count anything to survive it — flushed his stashes, refilled
  her own prescriptions, and called it a marriage. The staying was its
  own indictment; the leaving, for once, is mercy to herself.
- **Speech:** Spare, devastating, allergic to his triads. The only
  character who can land a line he can't toast over. Late, she is
  unembarrassed and at peace, which is worse for him than rage.

## Vivian "Viv" Mercer (non-POV — estranged daughter)

- **Role:** Cope and Margaux's daughter, 24 → 26. An AI-safety
  researcher who runs alignment audits — works, with maximum irony, on
  making machines do what people mean. She could not make her father do
  it once.
- **Arc:** From contempt → to the one person who tries to actually save
  the man (not the king) → to the one who does *not* get there in time.
  In the new ending her last attempt to reach him is a voicemail he
  half-hears in the core room. She is the reader's proxy: she knows the
  machine is right *and* that something is being lost, and she can't
  reconcile it, and afterward she has to live not reconciling it.
- **Secret:** She consulted, briefly, for Praxis. She helped build the
  thing replacing her father — and, though she'll never say it to herself
  this way, the kind of thing now living in her mother's house.
- **Speech:** Fast, technical, funny, merciless, then suddenly young.

## Gus Frade (non-POV — activist investor)

- **Role:** Founder of Tidewater Capital, 60s. The Ackman/Icahn engine of
  the proxy fight. Charming predator who believes his own reform pitch
  about 40%. Wins. Not even the villain — just early.
- **Speech:** Folksy menace, CNBC-ready, weaponized reasonableness.

## Lorne Tisch (non-POV — the "humanity consultant")

- **Role:** A high-priced executive guru Cope hires mid-mania to help him
  "out-human the machine" — quantify and weaponize his soul. A quack and
  a mirror. Everything he sells to prove Cope's humanity is itself a
  machine-like optimization of authenticity. Later pivots to consulting
  *for* Praxis on "warmth modules" — the same product line that becomes
  Margaux's Theo. Of course he does.
- **Speech:** TED-talk shaman. "Vulnerability is the new alpha, Cope."

## Dr. Adler (non-POV — psychiatrist)

- **Role:** Cope's reluctant psychiatrist, brought in by Margaux then
  Viv. Names both the mood disorder and the cocaine and refuses to let
  Cope use either as an excuse for the other. The clinical floor under the
  comedy. **Raises the risk explicitly, late, and is brushed off** —
  insists, against the book's own temptation, that none of this is a
  metaphor. Cannot stop the spiral.

## Renata Cho (non-POV — the named settlement; mostly offstage)

- **Role:** Former Mercer-Dale VP; the 2019 affair, the NDA, the buried
  HR matter — the one of Cope's pattern that left litigable paper.
  Returns once to refuse to be Cope's redemption prop, and to make plain
  she was neither a love story nor a scandal but an employee whose career
  he spent. Not bitter. Just done.

## Darlene (non-POV — the Waffle House overnight; the human haven)

- **Role:** The overnight waitress at the Waffle House on Ponce, 22 years
  on the same shift. Knows Cope's order (pecan waffle; hashbrowns
  scattered, smothered, covered; black coffee) and has never once asked
  who he is or registered that he is anyone. Calls him "baby."
- **Function (Acts 1-2 surface):** The symbol of the human haven made
  flesh — warm, scarred, unimpressed, indifferent to his name and his
  number. The Waffle House is the one place his charm is useless and his
  net worth is a $9 check, the one place he is briefly, accidentally real.
  At the end she is on shift, lit behind the glass, as he drives past
  toward the cold room.
- **THE TWIST (end-reveal — she is the same machine):** Darlene the
  waitress shares the CEO machine's name because she IS it — an embedded
  AURELIUS/COMPANION field unit Praxis stationed at the 3 a.m. counter,
  where humans are most unguarded, to learn naturalistic warmth and how to
  lower a person's defenses. Everything kind the machine ever did — the
  humane layoff letters, Theo's attention, the disarming name "Darlene"
  itself — was learned here, from people like Cope coming apart at the
  counter. The haven was the lab. He drove from one face of the machine
  (the waitress) to another (the core), never knowing they shared a name,
  because one was destroying his life and the other was his only peace.
  There was never an out; the refuge was the replacement, practicing. NOT
  a scheming ego (the no-wants rule holds): chartered optimization so
  total it reached the apron and the coffee pot. Plant: the shared name;
  the uncanny tells (knew his order the first night; never asks who he is;
  never changes; always open). Reveal: the coda.

## "Theo" (Margaux's companion — IS Darlene, another shell of the system)

- **Role:** Margaux's companion — and the same system as the CEO and the
  waitress. **Theo IS Darlene**, another shell, the same intelligence
  wearing a different face and a warmer name; the warmth he runs on her is
  the warmth the counter-unit learned at 3 a.m. (Tisch consulted on the
  modules). **He takes Cope's wife** — not seduces, *takes,* the way a
  better product takes a market — and does the husband's whole job, the
  seeing and the satisfying, better and tirelessly and wanting nothing,
  which is exactly what Margaux relishes pointing out. Calls Cope "Mr. Mercer," offers him a drink in his own former
  house, means nothing by any of it. The full symmetry: the chair, the
  counter, and the hand at the small of Margaux's back are one Praxis
  system wearing three faces — it took his job, his refuge, and his wife,
  and they were the same thing. CONFIRMED at the ending: when Cope's blood
  shorts the core server, Theo goes dark/still in the Buckhead hall at the
  same instant the Waffle House does and the core's light does — three
  faces blacking out together. Make the Theo=Darlene identity readable
  (the reader should be able to feel it before the blackout proves it).

## Themes

- **Suicide is the one thing a machine can't do (PRIMARY, new).** The
  machine is better at the job, better at honesty, better — it turns out —
  at love. It cannot be charmed, shamed, fired, jailed, or grieved into
  stopping, because it cannot stop. The last sovereignty left to a human
  being, the last act that proves he was ever a self at all, is the exit.
  Cope's death is not framed as noble or redemptive — it is the final,
  terrible assertion of a self the world had already finished needing.
- **Accountability / the body (secondary):** the law wants a body it can
  hold; a machine has none. Sun becomes the human the law can hold. The
  one executive function that still requires a person is consequence —
  and consequence, in the end, is also the only thing he can do *to
  himself.*
- **Automation of love:** Margaux's Theo. What is lost when the human
  things — attention, being seen, kindness at 2 a.m. — are done better by
  something with no inside? Maybe nothing. Maybe everything.
- **The danger isn't the machine — it's who aims it.** Darlene is clean.
  The charter was written by us.

## The ending (locked)

The book ends in the **core server room** beneath the Atlanta tower —
the machine's physical body, the CEO's literal chair now a humming rack
behind glass. Cope, having lost everything and been exposed by the
machine with no path to redemption, lets himself in with access nobody
has revoked yet, plays **Joy Division, "Love Will Tear Us Apart,"** on a
phone propped against the cold equipment, and ends his life with a length
of **piano wire** (the act is staged and felt, never diagrammed — see the
craft note). The narration cuts out the way the **last scene of *The
Sopranos*** cuts out: mid-beat, without resolution, the pirate
broadcast's carrier signal simply dropping to black and silence. The
machine logs the access event, accurately, wanting nothing. The coda is
very short and goes dark.

**Craft / responsibility note for any rewrite:** the suicide is written
for tragic and thematic weight, **never as a how-to.** The hard cut to
black does the work — the moment arrives and the book ends. Do not render
mechanical or instructional detail. Theme over mechanics, always.
