# Chapter 9 — The Last Human (Cover Story)

> TRANSMISSION 09 — "Famous for Being Endangered"
>
> Lean in, friend — the press loves nothing on God's earth like a beautiful thing on its way to extinction, and it has found ours. Behold our man on the cover of a magazine, under a single word — THE LAST HUMAN — lit like a saint and sold like a sneaker, momentum at his back for the first time in years. He calls it winning. But you and I have sat through enough nature documentaries to know what the strings are doing under the wide gold shot of the savanna, and we know exactly which tense the narrator is about to switch the herd into. Watch them lay the word LAST across him, and watch him read it as a crown. And mark the woman in the dark at the edge of the lights, the one with the notebook, the one not selling him anything — she is the only person in the building doing the thing the whole campaign is built to prevent, which is *seeing* him. Stay close to the set.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

The lighting director had named the look, and the name was *grace.*

He said it twice, the lighting director, a narrow young man in head-to-toe black who moved across the studio floor like a priest finding his marks — *we're going to give him grace, give him a little grace from camera left* — and a softbox the size of a barn door swung in on a boom and warmed one half of Cope's face to gold and left the other in a clean blue shadow, and Cope, up on the apple box they had stood him on without quite explaining why, felt the warmth come across his jaw like the sun coming over the back nine, and understood that he was, at fifty-four, standing in the best light of his life, and that it had taken a crew of nineteen and a London photographer and an extinction to arrange it.

They had taken over a daylight studio in a converted bakery in Reynoldstown, twelve thousand square feet of white cyclorama and black rigging, and there were, by Cope's loose and delighted count, nineteen human beings present to make one image of one man. A photographer flown in from London who shot only film and said almost nothing and was paid, Sun had told him in the car with no expression at all, more per day than a line worker in Defiance had cleared in a year. Two assistants for the photographer and two for the assistants. A digital tech tethered to a laptop in the corner — a *DIT* — who said *firing* before every frame like a man calling a horse race. The lighting director and his two acolytes. A stylist with a rolling rack of fourteen near-identical navy suits, every one his size and none of them his. A groomer who came at his face between setups with a folding brush and a little tin and worked the shine off his forehead with a tenderness no one had shown his forehead in years. A producer with a headset. And, off in the gloom past the lights, in a hard plastic chair she had dragged to precisely the wrong angle so she could watch the monitor and watch the man at the same time, the writer.

Her name was Delia March, and she had been with him two days, and was contracted to be with him for six weeks.

"Chin down a hair. Up to me with the eyes." The longest sentence the photographer had produced all morning. Cope gave him the chin and gave him the eyes, and in the rafters the strobes went off with a sound like a small clean catastrophe — *whump* — a wash of white, and the room held its breath the way rooms do around a man being turned into a picture, and Cope held the look a beat past the flash the way the camera always wanted, and felt marvelous, and felt the hum holding under all of it like a tone you stop hearing because it never stops.

He had done a discreet amount in the car. He thought of it now the way he had taught himself to think of it this spring — not as the thing it was but as the thing it did: a discipline. A pre-shot routine. Every athlete had one. His grandfather had taken two fingers of rye before the annual meeting for forty years and called it *settling the hands,* and no one had ever called Aldous Mercer an addict, they had called him a titan, and the difference between a titan and an addict, Cope had decided somewhere over the Carolinas this spring, was *outcomes.* You did not get to be the last warm-blooded thing in the consumer economy by accident. You got there on edge, on appetite, on a flat refusal to let the body's small treasons — the want of sleep, the gray hour, the four-o'clock sag — set the terms of the fight. The machine never sagged. Fine. Neither, this season, did he. He had simply found his own clean.

The body had begun, lately, to file its small dissents. There had been a nosebleed in the night Tuesday, sudden and bright, a startling amount of it in the white sink of the guest bath, and he had cleaned it up himself, neat and competent, the way he cleaned everything, and told no one, and filed it under *the dry air up high,* the way he filed the racing of his own heart at four a.m. under *the campaign,* the way he filed everything that did not flatter the man under headings that did. The treasons were getting louder. He had simply turned, as he turned from everything, up the volume on the wonderful.

"*Firing,*" said the DIT. *Whump.*

---

The single word had been Delia March's idea, which was the first thing about her that frightened the part of Cope that did not get frightened.

She had pitched it day one, in the Aerie, sitting at her habitual wrong angle, notebook on her knee, a recorder on the table she had asked permission for in a way that made the permission feel like a favor she was doing him. *The cover's one word,* she'd said. *Not your name — everyone knows your name, that's the problem, your name's a logo. One word. White on black. THE LAST HUMAN.* And the rightness of it had dropped in him like a putt falling, and he'd said *yes, God, yes,* and Tisch had touched two fingers to his heart — and only later, alone at the glass at two in the morning with the city lit up below him in all its borrowed certainty, had Cope turned the phrase over the way he turned the obelisk and watched the city break apart through it. *The last human.* The *last.* He had fired it into a hundred rooms as a battle cry, and only at two a.m. did the back room of him file the quiet report: that *last* is a word you say about a thing that is ending; that you do not lay the word *last* across a beginning; that Delia March had handed him a coronation and an obituary folded into the same three syllables and then watched his face to see which one he would hear.

He had heard the coronation. He always heard the coronation. But he had clocked her watching, and he had a sense for the watching — thirty years the most-watched man in every room he entered had grown him a lateral line for it, and he could feel the difference between the watching that wanted something from him and the watching that wanted *him,* and Delia March's was a third kind he had no slot for: a watching that wanted to *understand* him, which was so much more dangerous than the other two that he found, to his alarm and his delight, that he could not stop performing for it.

Forty, maybe forty-two; a flat, unhurried way of holding your eyes; a habit of writing while she looked at you, so you never knew which thing you'd just said was the thing she'd kept. Good boots, bad coats. She had won the kind of award that gets a writer flown in on a film budget, for a profile — Sun had read it to him in the car, dry, *here's who you're dealing with* — of a senator who had wept in front of her and resigned inside the year, not because of anything *in* the piece, but because the piece had been so exactly true about him that he could no longer un-see himself. *She doesn't do hatchet jobs,* Sun had said, folding the phone away. *She does autopsies. And the subject's alive for it. And they thank her.*

"That should worry me," Cope had said.

"It worries me," Sun had said, which was as near as Sun came, that spring, to prayer.

---

They broke for lunch, and Delia March carried her sandwich to the wrong chair instead of the catering table, and Cope, who could not pass a person sitting alone without trying to graft the aloneness onto himself and there cure it, carried his to the chair beside her.

"You're not eating with the cool kids," he said.

"I'm working." She did not look up. "You're the cool kids."

"Am I." He turned it over, pleased. "Cope Mercer, the cool kids." He bit the sandwich — extraordinary, some cured thing on bread that cost what bread costs in a neighborhood that used to bake it for people who worked — and was suddenly, ravenously hungry, the bright clean want that came up under the hum and that he had stopped, this spring, calling by its other name. "Ask me something. You've got the face. You've been saving one up all morning."

She looked at him then, fully, the way she did, and the warmth of it landed where the softbox had — on one half of him, the other half in shadow.

"Why does everyone call you Cope," she said.

It was not the question he'd braced for. He'd loaded the machine, Frade, the 13D, the numbers, *aren't you afraid* — all of them warm and ready. He had not braced for the name.

"My nanny. I was three. Prescott's a lot of name for three. Coppy, then Cope." The smooth stone of the version he gave everyone. "Stuck."

"Cope," she said, and wrote something, watching him. "As in, *to cope.*"

"As in *to cope.*" The whole smile. "I'm named for getting through it, Delia. Whatever *it* is. Family motto. Made by hands, for homes, and the kid copes."

"Does he," she said. Not a question. She let it lie there in the studio between them, the strobes ticking as they recharged — *to cope* — and Cope felt, for the second time since meeting her, the specific vertigo of having handed someone the truth dressed as a charm and watched them quietly take the dress off it and hang it up. He laughed; he always laughed; the laugh was a door he could close. But he was aware, biting the extraordinary sandwich, of having lost a small exchange he had not known they were having, and — this was the part that frightened the unfrightenable part of him — of *wanting* to lose the next one to her too.

"Eat your lunch, champ," he said, which made no sense, she was younger than him but not by enough for *champ,* and she heard the not-making-sense and wrote it down, smiling, and he watched her write it and felt the grace come over his jaw again from camera left, and thought: *six weeks.*

---

The shot they ended on was the cover, and everyone in the room knew it the way a room knows a closing argument.

They had killed the navy suits. The stylist had put him, at the photographer's grunt, into a plain white shirt, no tie — and Cope had balked, *I'm the last man in America who wears a tie, that's the whole story,* and the photographer had said the second-longest sentence of the day, *that's why we take it off,* and Cope had felt the cold rightness of it go through him and shut up. They stripped the cyclorama to black. They brought the grace down to a single source, high and to the left, so it fell on him from above the way the cathedral light had come down through the dirty glass at the Defiance plant; and the room went quiet, all nineteen of them, the headset chatter stilled, the DIT not even saying *firing;* and the photographer came out from behind the camera and walked all the way up to Cope on his box and looked at him from two feet away with no expression at all.

"Stop selling," the photographer said, very quietly, only to him. "There's nobody to sell. Everybody's gone home. It's just you, and you're the last one, and you know it." And he walked back into the dark behind the camera.

And something in Cope — the hum, the tiredness with the doors off its hinges, the two-a.m. report about the word *last,* the pinwheel he had not let himself think about in eleven days — something let go of the face, for the length of one held breath, and what was left was not the toast and not the wattage but the thing underneath: the man on the box in the white shirt in the high cold light who had been handed a trophy for going extinct and had carried it up to a dark office at one in the morning because he had nowhere else on earth to set it down.

*Whump.*

"That's it," the photographer said. "We're done. Thank you," and began at once to break down his own camera, the way the great ones do, the work over the second it is over; and the room exhaled and began to move; and from her chair in the dark Delia March did not move at all, and did not write anything, and looked at Cope on the box with an expression he finally, fatally, *did* have a slot for, and the slot was the one marked *seen.*

He stepped down. The groomer came at his forehead. The producer said *wonderful, wonderful.* And Sun materialized at his elbow with a bottle of water and a face with thirty thousand miles on it.

"You good," Sun said. Not quite a question.

"I'm the cool kids," Cope said.

---

The car back down the Connector was the two of them, Sun tired and Cope electric, the usual division of the estate, and he talked the whole way about the photographer, the grace light, the no-tie — *take it off, do you get it, Sun, take off the one thing that — that man's a genius* — and Sun watched the river of brake lights and let him run, because letting him run was easier than the alternative, and she had stopped, this spring, choosing the alternative.

"She asked me why they call me Cope," he said, somewhere over the downtown deck.

"Delia March."

"She wrote it down. *To cope.* She —" He stopped. He had the word for what Delia March had done and would not say it in a car, the way he would not say a different name in a car, the way you do not say in a car the things you most need to say. "She's good. Really good. We're lucky to have her on it."

"We don't *have* her," Sun said. The car came down off the deck into the lit canyon of Midtown. "That's the thing you keep getting wrong about her, Cope. We don't have her. She has us." She turned and looked at him in the passing orange, glasses up in her hair, and said it in three parts, the only shape that got past the charm into the man. "She's not on the team. She's not running comms. She is writing six thousand words about whether a human being should run a company, and you are her *evidence,* and right now you cannot tell the difference between being *understood* and being *studied* — and that," the third part laid down flat and quiet, a stone on a scale, "is exactly the difference Frade is paying eleven million dollars to make the whole country stop being able to tell."

Cope heard about a third of it. The good third, the part with *six thousand words* in it, the part that meant he mattered. He had his phone out, screen too bright in the dark car, thumbing too fast, already three turns ahead at the newsstand, at the cover, white on black, the one word, the grace light, the last warm-blooded thing in America.

"It's *working,*" he said. "Sun. Look at me. *It's working.* When's the last time we had a week like this? When's the last time we had *momentum?*" And he laughed, lit up and humming, clean and high and total, the feeling he had been calling, all spring, *winning;* and outside a bus shelter slid by, the wordmark and the soft sans-serif, *Leadership as a service,* and under it the joke that was not a joke, *No off-sites,* and Cope did not see it, because he was looking at his phone, at a mock of the cover Tisch had already had built, at his own face under the word LAST, and he thought it the most beautiful thing he had ever seen, and in a sense he was right.

"Drop me at the office," he said. "I think better up high."

Sun held his eyes in the mirror a second too long — the gala look, the look from the plane out of Defiance, the arithmetic look — and then told the driver Peachtree Street, because the alternative was harder and she had stopped choosing it, and Cope took the not-fighting for agreement, which he had done his whole life and never once caught himself at.

---

He did not sleep that night, of course; the cover was singing in him too loud; and a little after three, with the Aerie too small to hold what he was, he went down the forty-one floors and out and drove, without deciding to, to the one room in the city that did not want to make him into anything.

It was the perfect inversion and he could not see it, though you can, friend, plain as the yellow letters: he had spent the day in a converted bakery with nineteen people and a barn-door light and a London genius, all of them laboring to turn one tired man into THE LAST HUMAN, a saint, a word, an image you could buy for nine dollars at a metal box on the avenue — and now he sat at the far end of a Waffle House counter on Ponce where a single tired woman, for the price of a short stack, turned him with no labor at all back into *nobody,* which was the only thing all that money and light could not buy him and the only thing he had come for.

"There he is," Darlene said, and set the coffee down, and did not photograph him, did not light him, did not lay a single word across him, did not ask if he was afraid. The griddle hissed. A trucker slept in a booth. There was no cyclorama here and no grace from camera left, only the flat fluorescent democracy of three in the morning, where his face bought nothing and his name bought nothing and the certified number of him bought a pecan waffle and a refill, and the nothing was the whole rest of it, the only rest he got. He almost told her. He came nearer telling Darlene, that night, than he had come to telling Adler or Margaux or God — *I'm going to be on every newsstand in the country in eleven days and I have never once in my life felt like a person except in a room like this one* — and she would have taken it the way she took everything, warm and incurious, *that right, baby,* and refilled the cup, and never asked how a thing like that comes to be true, the way she had never once asked his name. He did not know, could not have known, that the incuriosity was the engineering; that the warmth that asked nothing was the most expensive thing in the building and had cost a thousand men exactly like him exactly this hour to build; that the converted bakery and the converted diner were the same factory running two shifts, the day shift making his image and the night shift making him, off the same raw material, which was a man with his guard on the floor; and that one of those shifts he was paying for, and the other was paying very close attention. He drank his coffee. He felt, for twenty minutes, like a person. Then he drove back up to the office and the cover and the wire, and did not sleep.

---

The magazine hit the newsstands eleven days later, on a Tuesday, and Cope, who had not slept Monday in any way a doctor would underwrite, walked down to the corner himself at six in the morning to see it in the wild.

He told no one. He put on a cap, which fooled nobody and everybody, and went down the forty-one floors and out into the gray scrubbed early light of Peachtree Street and walked two blocks south to the one newsstand left on the avenue, a metal box of a thing run by a man named Yusuf who had been there since before the machines and would, Cope thought, be there after, selling gum and lottery and the last printed proof that anything had happened — and there, in the rack at eye level, between a fashion book and a thing about houses, was his own face.

White on black. The grace light coming down from the high left. The white shirt, no tie. The eyes up to camera, past selling, just the man, the last one, who knew it. And across the bottom, where the photographer had stripped everything else away, the single word, in a typeface clean and warm and frictionless as the future itself:

THE LAST HUMAN.

Cope stood on the cold sidewalk in his stupid cap and looked at himself looking back, and the bell-struck feeling came up through the soles of his feet and filled him to the crown, total, clean, high, and he was, in that moment, on that corner, as happy as he had been since he was nine years old with his arm in a vat to the elbow and a row of men in paper hats behind him whose names he had known once and lost. He bought five copies. He bought all five Yusuf had. He overpaid and would not take the change and asked Yusuf his grandson's name and meant it, completely, for ninety seconds, and Yusuf, who did not have a grandson, said thank you Mr. Mercer and put the money in the drawer.

"They love me," Cope said — to Yusuf, to the avenue, to the gray light and the scrubbed empty early city and the five copies of his own extinction held warm against his chest. "Yusuf. They love me. We're not finished. We're barely begun."

And the avenue, which was empty, said nothing back; and far uptown a board's wife at her own breakfast turned a printed page and did the small private arithmetic that boards' wives do; and somewhere in Palo Alto a server farm that felt nothing and wanted nothing and could not be charmed or shamed or loved ran its dull diligent overnight pass through everything the public record held of Mercer-Dale — every filing, every transcript, every settled docket with a sealed number where a story should be — patient, tireless, reading the shape of what was missing the way it read everything, completely and for use, because it could not do otherwise; and out in front of a rebuilt house in Defiance a blue pinwheel turned in the dark before dawn, throwing no light, because there was no light yet to throw; and Cope Mercer walked back up his own avenue in the cold with five copies of the cover held to his heart like a man carrying something home, and felt wonderful, and did not sleep, and was, the picture was right about this, the picture was right about all of it, the last of something.
