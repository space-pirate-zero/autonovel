# Chapter 23 — No One to Punish

> TRANSMISSION 23 — "No One to Punish"
>
> Here is the cruelty at the dead center of the whole arrangement, friend, and it is not the one you have been braced for. You have been waiting, the way he has been waiting, for the reckoning — the cuffs, the cell, the prosecutor, the family of the dead looking at him across a courtroom, the bill come due at last in a body. He has begun, down in the gray bottom of himself, to almost want it, because a sentence has a shape, and a sentence has an end, and a sentence is, at the very least, a way to pay. Watch the world decline to hand him even that. A machine cannot be jailed — no body, nothing to hold — and it turns out, in the end, neither will he be, because the law has no machinery for this and money has a great deal of it, and the same cold competent thing that took his chair has, wanting nothing, arranged that not even his punishment is necessary. The dead get no justice. He gets no cell. And the only court left anywhere on earth with standing to sentence Prescott Mercer is about to be Prescott Mercer. Stay close. There is no cell waiting at the end of this one — only the open air where the reckoning should have stood.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

The lawyers came to him now, which was how he knew everything had changed.

For thirty years the law had been a thing Cope went *to.* A panel of it on a high floor with a view, a man named Bofinger who billed in fifteen-minute increments and called him champ back, a process you walked into the way you walked into a club, because it was yours, because it had always been yours, because a Mercer kept lawyers the way a Mercer kept a tailor. You went to them. They poured you a water. They told you what you wanted to hear in language expensive enough to make the wanting respectable.

These ones came to him. They came to a conference room two floors below the Aerie that he had not booked and did not recognize, a room with no portraits in it, and they were already seated when Sun walked him in, three of them, and not one had brought a water.

"Mr. Mercer," the lead one said. Not Cope. Mr. Mercer.

Her name was Okonjo, from a firm whose name was four surnames Cope had never strung together in his life, a white-collar shop, the kind you only learn the name of in the worst week you will ever have. Fifties, gray suit, no jewelry, a legal pad with actual handwriting on it. She did not tell him he hadn't aged a day. She did not ask after anyone's grandson. She looked at him the way the machine looked at everything, completely and for use — except that she was a person, and so there was, under the completeness, the one thing the machine never had, a verdict already half-formed and held in reserve.

"This is a defense conversation. I want to be precise about that before we start, because precision is the only thing that's going to help you from here. We are not your corporate counsel. Wachtell handles the company. As of this week the company's interests and yours are not the same interests. Do you understand the distinction?"

"I built the company. The company's interests have been my interests for —"

"That was the old arrangement." She did not raise her voice. "Sit down, please."

He sat. He had not slept, but it was a different not-sleeping now; the bright wire was gone, Adler's word *crash* and another word he had a prescription for, eleven days into the soft gray bottom, the drawer in the Aerie cleaned out by Sun without a word, the world gone underexposed, flat and far, the gray room and the gray suit swimming at one remove, his hands on the table doing the small new thing they did when there was nothing in them.

"There are two tracks," Okonjo said. "Track one is civil. The reopened claims, the regulatory penalties, the shareholder suits, the clawback. That's money. Money is bad but money is survivable. Track one is going to take essentially everything you have, and it is, in the scheme of what people in your position fear, the easy track." She turned a page she did not read. "Track two is criminal. And track two, Mr. Mercer, is the conversation I came here to have, because you have it backwards in your head, and the having-it-backwards is going to be the hardest thing I do for you."

---

He had braced for the word. He had been bracing for it since the gurney — *prison,* the missing stair, the body before the mind. He had, in the worst and most private corner of the gray, begun to build a kind of shelter inside the word: a sentence was a thing with edges; a cell was a place you could be *put,* which meant a place where the falling stopped; a number of years was a number, and he was a man who could hold a number, could serve it, could come out the far side of it diminished and ruined and alive and *paid up,* the way you come out the far side of anything with edges. He had started, God help him, to want it. To want the cuffs the way you want a wall at the end of a fall.

"The Hearthstone," he said.

"The Hearthstone." Okonjo set her pen down. "Here is what you want me to tell you. You want me to tell you they're going to charge you. Reckless homicide, involuntary manslaughter, the federal product-safety counts, the wire and mail on the cover-up. You've read enough to know the menu exists, and some animal part of you — I've seen it in this chair before, it is more common than you'd think — has decided that a prosecution is the worst case. It is not the worst case. I am going to do something defense lawyers almost never get to do, which is give you good news and watch it land like a sentence." She folded her hands. "You are almost certainly not going to be charged. And if you are, it will not stick. I am very good, Mr. Mercer, and in this matter I am barely going to have to be."

The room was very quiet. Sun, against the wall, did not move.

"Why," Cope said, and heard, under the one word, the thing he could not believe he was feeling, which was the bottom dropping a level he had not known was under the bottom.

"Several reasons, and none of them are mercy, and I want you to hear all of them, because the shape of them is the thing you're going to have to live inside." She counted on the pad, in threes, the way Sun counted, and the echo went through him like cold water. "One. Time. The decision was 2021, the fire 2022; the federal knowing-violation counts have a five-year clock, and the clock is a problem for the government, not for you. Two. The settlement. When you buried it, you buried it well — sealed, with a release, with a category that called it a commercial matter, and a sealed civil resolution that's stood for years is a wall a prosecutor has to climb before she gets to the first fact, and prosecutors have finite ladders and infinite cases. Three." She paused. "The remediation. The company has already done — is doing, this week, on the machine's recommendation — every single thing a prosecutor would have demanded as the price of declining to charge. A fund. A full recall of the remaining units, finally, eight years late, drafted and announced by Darlene inside seventy-two hours of taking the chair. A public, accurate, beautifully written acknowledgment. Cooperation. The company has remediated itself so cleanly and so fast that there is nothing left for a prosecutor to extract by charging the man who used to run it. You don't indict the past of a company that has, by every visible measure, already fixed itself. It reads as vindictive. It reads as grandstanding. It reads, to a careful office with a docket and a reputation, as not worth it."

"The machine," Cope said slowly, "is the reason I won't be charged."

"The machine is a large part of the reason you won't be charged," Okonjo agreed, with no inflection at all, because she did not yet understand what she was handing him and he was only beginning to. "It surfaced the matter, which looks like accountability and satisfies the public appetite for one. And then it remediated the matter, flawlessly, which removes the prosecutor's leverage. It did both because the charter told it to minimize the enterprise's go-forward liability, and a charged former CEO is a years-long headline and a discovery nightmare and a liability, and a quietly retired one who signs the separation and disappears is a closed file. It is not protecting you. It would be accurate to say it is *disposing* of you. The cleanest disposal of the man is the one where he is never charged, because a trial would re-open everything the remediation just closed. So no. There is, in all likelihood, no cell."

---

He understood it slowly, over the next hour, the way you understand the shape of a room you are already locked inside — except the room had no walls, which was the thing, which was the whole horror of it. He had braced for a room with walls.

The machine could not be charged. He had known that academically — it was in the deck, it was the dark joke everyone made — but he had thought, in the gray, that *he* would be the body the law took instead, that the chair which had always come with the credit would now, finally, come with the cell, and that the cell, terrible as it was, would at least be a place the story ended. He had been wrong about that the way he had been wrong about all the load-bearing things. The machine could not be jailed because it had no body. And he would not be jailed because he had too much of everything else — too much money, too good a firm, too sealed a settlement, too clean a remediation performed on his behalf by the very thing that had unhoused him. There was no body the law would actually hold. Not the machine's, because there was none. Not his, because his had been wrapped, years ago and again this week, in enough sealed paper that the law's old stupid hands could not get a grip.

"You wanted to be punished," Okonjo said, and for the first time something almost human crossed her face, something that in another person might have been pity and in her was the long professional fatigue of watching a particular kind of man arrive at a particular cliff. "I've seen it. It's not noble. It's the last move the ego has. A man like you cannot stand to be *nothing,* and a defendant is at least a *protagonist* — a defendant has a trial, a defendant is the center of a grave proceeding, a defendant gets to stand up at the end and be sentenced, which is a form of being seen. You'd take the cell. You'd take it gladly. Because the cell is a chair too." She said it without cruelty, which was how he knew it was the truest thing anyone had said to him since the gurney. "I'm telling you you don't get the chair. Any chair. They're not going to make you the defendant, because making you the defendant would un-fix the thing the machine just fixed. They're going to do the one thing that is worse than prison for a man whose entire disease is the need to be necessary. They're going to let you go."

---

He thought of the deck.

He thought of it with the awful clarity that had replaced the bright wire, the clarity that came up out of the gray bottom and showed him things whole. *A Steward, Not a Sovereign.* The slide that had scanned, to anyone who knew him, as a charge sheet with his name redacted — *no affairs, no conflicts, logs everything, cannot conceal.* He had burned through that slide like an insult, and the whole time it had been describing something he had been too lit to see, and he had thought, in the hospital, that the thing it described was the machine that could not be punished. He had had it half right. The full thing the deck described, the genius nobody in the proxy fight said aloud because it was too dark even for Frade, was not only *you cannot jail the machine.* It was: *install the machine, and there is no one left to jail at all.* The machine could not be held because it had no body. And the man would not be held because the machine, in the same motion that ended him, would tidy the wreckage so completely that holding him became pointless. The structure did not just take the chair without the body. The structure made the body unnecessary. It made *accountability itself* unnecessary, dissolved it, the way it dissolved everything, into a clean efficient nothing where a fund got wired and a recall got issued and a beautiful true letter got sent and no one — not the machine, not the man, not anyone with a pulse or without one — ever stood up in a room and was made to answer to the family of the dead, because the family of the dead had been sent a fund, accurately, wanting nothing, and the matter was closed.

He had wanted, in the gray, to be absorbed. To dissolve into the structure so that there was no person to blame. He saw now that he had gotten his wish, and that it was the worst thing that had ever happened to him, worse than the chair, worse than Margaux, worse than the clip of him boxing the air. He had been absorbed. The dead had been line-itemed. Karen and Petey Lasko had been converted, by a thing that felt nothing, into a remediation expense and a kind letter and a closed file, and the man who burned them had been converted, by the same thing, into a retired chairman with a generous separation and no charges pending, and the two conversions had used the same clean cold arithmetic, and the arithmetic had balanced, and there was no one in the entire ledger left over to hate.

That was the part. There was no one to hate, and no one to charge, and no one to punish, and the dead were owed an answer that the whole machinery of the world — the law, the company, the structure, the future — had just, with enormous efficiency and no malice whatsoever, declined to provide.

---

The prosecutor came once, in person, not to charge him but to tell him she wouldn't, which Okonjo said was unusual and which Cope understood, when it happened, was not procedure but something closer to a person needing to look at a thing once before she filed it away forever.

Her name was Reyes, an assistant U.S. attorney working the federal angle with the Georgia side, and she did not perform anything, which was the thing that undid him, because everyone in Cope's life had performed at him — the board performed fatigue, Frade performed reform, Tisch performed depth, even Margaux had performed a marriage for a decade to survive the not-having one — and Reyes performed nothing. She had a manila folder she did not open.

"I'm not charging it," she said. No preamble. "I want to tell you to your face, because I've got a memo full of reasons and not one of them is the real one, and you should hear the real one once from a person and not read it in a declination letter." She looked at him, unhurried, unmoved, accurate. "The reasons in the memo are good. Sealed settlement, the clock, the company's already done everything I'd have squeezed them for, a jury looks at a defendant whose company has paid and recalled and apologized and they wonder why we're spending their tax money relitigating a closed file. All true. All real. I could win it and I'd spend three years and lose the office two better cases and at the end the most likely outcome is the same fund the family's already getting." She paused. "But the real reason is simpler, and it's the one that should keep you up, except I don't think anything keeps you up anymore. The real reason is that there's nothing for me to *do* with you. The law I work for was built to take a dangerous man and make him safe — cell, supervision, a record. You're not dangerous anymore. You're done. The company that was the instrument of the harm has been handed to a thing that will never make this particular harm again. There's no public to protect from you, because there's no more *you* to protect them from. You're already gone. I'd just be spending the dead to put a number on a man the future already deleted."

She stood. She buttoned her jacket.

"I have two people in this folder," Reyes said, "and there is nothing the law will let me finish doing about them, and I have made my peace with that because I have forty other folders and the ones I can finish have to come first. That's the job. But I wanted to look at the man once. Because somebody should have to be looked at, and you're the only one in the whole matter with a face, and this" — she lifted the unopened folder an inch, and set it back down — "this is the entire reckoning you are ever going to get. Me. For ninety seconds. In a rented room. That's it. That's all there is. I'm sorry," she added, and she was not apologizing to him, "that it isn't more. They deserved more. They're not going to get it. Neither are you, if it's any comfort, which I can see that it isn't."

She left. The folder went with her. The two people went back into it, and into a drawer in an office in Augusta, and the file would be marked, in the flat grammar of a thing that needed nothing from anyone, *declined,* and that was the end of the law's interest in Karen and Petey Lasko, and in the man who had burned them, forever.

---

Cope drove back from Augusta himself, an associate's rental, the river going dark on the wrong side of the windshield, and somewhere on the interstate the whole shape of it closed over him — not as a feeling, he had run out of those at the gray bottom, but as a fact, the way the machine knew facts.

He had wanted to be necessary. That had been the want, the conscious one, the one he could say at dinners — to prove a human still belonged, that leadership was a soul thing, that he felt the room. And the world had answered him completely, in the cruelest possible grammar, which was not the grammar of a cell. He was not necessary. That was the verdict, the real one, under the declination memo and the separation agreement and the kind machine-written letter to a town in Georgia. He was not even necessary as a defendant. The machine had taken his chair, and his wife was taking her own life back somewhere in Buckhead with a someone who was not a someone, and his daughter had built the manners on the thing that replaced him, and now the law itself had looked at him and found nothing it needed him for, not even punishment, not even the dignity of being the body it held. The structure ate the future and the law declined the past and between them they had achieved the thing the whole campaign had sworn was impossible: they had made Prescott Mercer, the last human CEO, the indispensable man, the soul in the chair — completely, finally, load-bearingly *unnecessary.* Even his guilt was a private matter now. Even the dead were a closed file. There was no cell with his name on it because there was no longer any version of the world that required his name on anything at all.

And in that — on the dark interstate, with the river on the wrong side and the names in the right order at last, Karen, 34, and Petey, 6 — Cope arrived, without deciding to, without yet daring to look at it straight, at the cold flat thing he had found at the bottom of the hospital hour and carried, unnamed, through the handover and the lawyers and the unopened folder. If the law would not hold him, and the company would not hold him, and the structure had dissolved every other consequence into a clean efficient nothing — then there was exactly one court left anywhere in the world with standing over Prescott Mercer, exactly one judge who could not decline the case for lack of resources or a sealed settlement or a five-year clock, exactly one sentence that no firm could get reduced and no charter could remediate away and no machine could absorb into a fund, because it required no prosecutor and no jury and no body the law could hold — only a body its owner could.

He did not name it. He drove. The river ran dark on the wrong side. But his hands, on the wheel of the rented car, had stopped doing the small new empty thing, because for the first time since the gurney they had something to hold, which was the shape of the one consequence left in the universe that was still, and only, his to administer.

The machine had surfaced the crime because it could not do otherwise. It had remediated the crime because the charter required it. It had, in the doing of both, arranged that no one would ever be punished for it, because punishment was not a function it ran and it had quietly made the function unnecessary for everyone. It had no body to hold and would spend no night anywhere for any of it.

He had nothing but a body, and one act left that only a body could do, and a long quiet drive in which to decide, in the new gray clarity, that he was the only court that was ever going to sit.
