# Chapter 25 — Nothing to Sign

> TRANSMISSION 25 — "Nothing to Sign"
>
> Watch a man who talked and bought and charmed his way out of every room he was ever in walk into the last one and find that nothing here works. Nothing to sign that matters, nothing to refuse that matters, nothing to confess that a machine has not already confessed for him, weeks ago, cleaner than he ever could. The finest lawyers money can buy sit around a long table with, for the first time in their gilded careers, nothing to do. Then watch him go home and do the one thing he spent a lifetime avoiding — write the whole truth, in his own hand, with the names at the top — and discover it is worth nothing, in any market on earth, because the only court that would have heard it has already adjourned for want of a body it was willing to hold. Stay close. Watch where a man goes when the truth turns out to be the one thing nobody will buy.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

The settlement was sixty-one pages and it loved him almost as much as the machine did, which was to say accurately, for the company's convenience, and not at all.

That was the thing Cope kept arriving at, in the conference room on the fourteenth floor of Kessler Brandt — 14-C, the good coffee, four people across the long table billed by the hour at more than Desmond made in a season, all four wanting, with a unity that was almost moving, for him to go quietly. The document was a kindness built like a kindness: read it right and a great many smart, expensive people had spent a weekend finding the version of the world where Prescott Mercer IV walked out with a number and his name. There was just the one thing wrong with it, and it was not the clause. It was that none of it mattered, and he was the only one in the room sober enough to see it.

"It's a mutual non-disparagement and confidentiality provision," Levin said, for the third time, in the voice you use for the third time — senior of the four, white hair kept long enough to suggest he had once been interesting, a man who had sat at Pres III's funeral. "Standard. The company doesn't characterize the events, you don't characterize the events, and you keep, after the clawback, a number most people would —"

"The events are already characterized, Bob." He had read the pages; for once in his life he had read the actual pages. "The machine put it all out a month ago — the memo, the Saturday, the eleven minutes, the names. You're asking me to sign a confidentiality agreement about a thing that is already, completely, public. What exactly am I being paid to not say."

A small silence with money in it.

"The framing," Okonjo said, from Levin's right, because Levin's face had started doing a thing. Fifties, no jewelry, the only one of them who had never once told him a comfortable thing. "Not the facts — the machine took the facts out of everyone's hands the morning it posted them. What's left is whether you're a sympathetic figure or an unrepentant one. Sign, go quietly, and the company's statement makes you a flawed, tired man overtaken by a changing market, who stepped down with dignity. Decline, and they let the framing find its own level, which is lower. That's the entire transaction. It's a settlement about adjectives."

He had braced, for weeks, for this to be the room where he was sentenced — had built, in the gray, a kind of shelter inside the idea of a sentence, a thing with edges, a place the falling stopped, a way to pay. There was no sentence in it, because there was no case; the most expensive defense lawyers in the country had assembled to negotiate an adjective, and Okonjo, who had gone to law school to keep men out of the room he'd been ready to walk into, had nothing to do in this one, and he could see her knowing it, the specific stillness of a surgeon told the patient does not, after all, require surgery, only a kind word and the door.

And here it was — the place where, a month ago, he would have stood up, found the room, taken the Mercer Inhale, said something with a triad in it and three generations of brass behind it, made the catastrophe feel like a backswing. He could feel the toast come up like reflux even now; the machinery did not stop when the man got sober, that was the thing nobody warned you about. *Bob. Ms. Okonjo. My great-grandfather Aldous used to say —*

He shut his mouth on it, and sat in the silence where the toast would have gone, and the four of them watched him not perform and did not understand what they were seeing, because they had only ever seen the other thing.

"Aldous never said anything," Cope said. "I made him up. Forty years of him and he never said a word; I needed a dead man to agree with me, so I built one and put a recipe in his mouth. I'm very good at putting words in the mouths of people who can't argue back." He looked at the document. "The decedents, for instance."

He did not sign it — not as a stand, not as a refusal that bought anything; Okonjo had shown him there was nothing to buy. He simply did not pick up the pen, because picking it up meant agreeing to be a sympathetic figure, agreeing to the dignity-obituary, agreeing to be disposed of nicely and to thank them for the linens. The truth was gone, owned by a machine. But the adjective was still his, the last thing in the room that was, and it was worth nothing, and he declined it.

"I don't want the nice version," he said, standing. "I've had the nice version my whole life. I'd like, at the very end, to not be handed one more."

---

Sun was in the lobby. She was not his anymore in the way she had been — she was the Accountable Officer now, the title sitting on her like a coat one size too heavy, and one of the cruelties of the new arrangement was that the person who had spent twelve years catching the second card out of his hand could no longer afford to stand next to the hand.

"You didn't sign," she said. Not a question. Glasses up in her hair, the gray expensive lobby sliding across the empty lenses.

"There was nothing to sign, Sun. You knew that before I walked in. It published everything a month ago. Including your memo."

"Including my memo." Her voice did not change. The integrity review had shown the world the recall memo she'd drafted in 2021 and he'd overridden — had shown, in the same flat font, that she had been right and early and ignored, the closest thing to exoneration she was ever going to get, arrived without her asking and without anyone's mercy, the machine clearing her in the same breath it buried him because both were simply true. "I spent seven years deciding whether I was a witness or an accomplice," she said. "And the thing that replaced you decided it for me, in an afternoon, in a footnote. I'm a witness. It's in the record. I didn't get to choose it and I didn't get to earn it. It was just *accurate.*" She looked at him. "Do you understand that I will never know, now, which one I'd have chosen, because it chose before I could? That's what it took from me. Not the chair. The chance to find out what I was."

He had thought, walking down, that his going down loud might somehow shield her. There was nothing to shield. She was already exposed, already cleared, already living in the after of a verdict a machine had handed down on her soul without consulting it.

"I'm sorry," he said. It was not a toast. It had no triad. "For all of it, but for that especially. That you ran it for twelve years and the reward was a footnote that decided who you were before you could."

Sun pulled her glasses down and looked at him a second too long, the way she had in the car the night they gave him the obelisk, except the man she was looking at was different now, smaller, finally here. "Go home, Cope. There's nothing here to do." And then, because she was decent, and done, both in one breath: "And don't do the thing your daughter called me about. The calm thing. I've known you twelve years. I know what your noise sounds like and I know what your quiet sounds like, and I have never once heard the quiet you've got now. That's not peace, whatever it feels like from in there. Call somebody. Not me. Somebody whose job it is."

"You have my word," Cope said, the thing that cost nothing, and Sun heard exactly what it was worth, and her face did the thing Viv's had done at the door, and she did not say anything else, because there was nothing left that she had the standing to say, and she went back up to a building that was hers to sign for and not to run, and he went out into the city that no longer had a use for him.

---

That night there was no high left to go up to — the Aerie was the machine's now, a museum of Mercer men kept on as a period room, and he had no stomach for being an exhibit in it. He went back to the borrowed loft in Cabbagetown, the brick and the high windows and the kettle, and sat on the end of the bed in the lamp light with a legal pad on his knee, and did the thing he had avoided his entire life.

He went into the room where it was kept.

He had built a cathedral of metaphors so he would never have to — *thermal event profile, got out ahead of it, protect the brand* — and Adler had taken the doors off one by one over eleven flat sober days, uncharmable, saying *the word is recall, say the word,* saying *they have names, do you know their names,* and he hadn't, that was the obscene part: he had killed two people to protect a quarter and never learned their names, had filed them under *the decedents* the way the lawyers did, because the lawyers had learned it from men like him.

He knew them now. He wrote them at the top of the pad, first, before anything else, in his own hand — the hand that had underlined a word in the dark the night of the gala — and under the names he wrote what he had done. Not the version. The Saturday, the memo Sun sent, the eleven minutes, the window of time he had decided was worth the heaters catching fire, which was the phrase he wrote where *thermal event profile* used to live, because that was what it had meant. He wrote that the concealment, not the heater, was the thing — that a machine doing nothing but reading every memo it was given had found in an afternoon what he had spent seven years building rooms inside himself not to find, and that this was not cruelty, the machine could not be cruel; it was only the thing they had bought it to be, a thing that could not look away, which was the thing he had never once been. At the bottom he wrote: *I am the strongest argument for my own replacement.*

And then he read it back, the way he read everything back, and understood the last thing. It was worth nothing. Not figuratively — exactly nothing, in every market that existed. It would not buy his liberty, which was not in danger, or the dentist-money the not-signing had already cost, or forgiveness, because the only two people with the standing to give it were at the top of the page and exactly as dead as he had decided they could afford to be. It was not even a confession, because a confession requires a confessor, and there was no one on the other side of this — only a man and a legal pad and a lamp, the truth finally and completely faced, alone, for no audience and no absolution and no record that didn't already exist. The truest sentences of his life, and the most worthless.

Around one he found the phone in his hand and a number on the screen he had not consciously summoned — no name on it, you do not put a name on that name — and his thumb hung over it a long time. That was the new horror the rooms with the bad coffee had not advertised: you got to watch yourself reach. The wanting did not arrive with trumpets; it arrived as logistics, a practical thought about a practical man who could be across town inside forty minutes, and his heart was going wrong in his chest like a bird in a bag. He pressed the side button until the screen went black and sat holding the dead phone like a railing. It did not feel like a victory. It felt like standing in a doorway, counting — and he understood that he would be doing this exact thing, in some room or another, every night for the rest of whatever was left, and that *the rest of whatever was left* had become, somewhere in the worthless writing of the truest pages of his life, a quantity he was no longer sure he was agreeing to.

He left the legal pad on the desk, the names face up — not as a confession; he was done confessing to rooms that wouldn't hear it — but the way you leave a true thing where someone might one day find it and know that once, at the end, with no one watching and nothing to gain, the man had at least gone into the room.

Then he turned off the lamp, and sat in the dark of the borrowed loft, and did not sleep, and out the high window there was no lit map of his own genius now, only the ordinary orange dark of a city that had finished needing him, and somewhere across it, forty floors down beneath a tower his great-grandfather had lit at the crown so a man coming up the avenue would know there was somebody home, the one room he had never visited hummed in the cold, and kept, with no one to keep it for, the only appointment he had left.
