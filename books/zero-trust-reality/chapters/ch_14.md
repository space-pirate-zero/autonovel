# Chapter 14 — Pressure

> TRANSMISSION 14 — "Everyone Knows Before He Does"
>
> A story is coming, friend, and you can feel it the way the animals feel the air go wrong before the quake — the journalist's calls getting shorter, the lawyer's getting longer, a wife who counted the women now counting the days, an activist who only has to stand at the bottom of the hill and look unsurprised. Our man is the last creature in his own house to smell the smoke, because he is standing too close to the fire he has spent fifty-four years calling a personality. Watch him this week hand the wrench to the very people tightening the screws; watch him fly a young woman out at dawn; and watch him go, on a dark highway at one in the morning, looking for a few hours of being no one at all — which is the nearest thing to rest he has left, and, if you have been listening to how these tales end, the first faint rehearsal of the thing he is already driving toward. Stay close. This is the last quiet hour before the thing he buried learns to breathe.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

The week after the Manifesto, Cope decided, was the best week of his professional life, and he had the calendar to prove it, because the calendar was full and a full calendar was a kind of love.

He stood at the glass in the Aerie on Tuesday with a black coffee he would not drink and a clarity he had decided to call vision, and watched the day assemble in his head the way the city assembled below him, every meeting a lit window, the whole bright grid humming toward the obvious conclusion that the campaign had tipped — that he had said the thing out loud at last to ten thousand people and the room had risen and the risen room was the proof. Momentum. He had spent his life reading rooms. The room had stood up. He could feel it. He could feel, too, the wire in his chest, the fine high one, and he had stopped pretending to himself that it was the coffee.

What he could not feel — and this was the flaw in the instrument he was proudest of, that it read applause beautifully and warning not at all — was the small change in the weather of the people around him. The way the calls were getting strange.

Delia March's calls were getting shorter. She was the writer doing the long profile his comms people had fought for and won — *Atlantic* money, twelve thousand words and growing, a photographer who'd shot two presidents and a pope — and for six weeks she had called the way the good ones call, hungry, generous, laughing at the lines, staying on past her own questions to ask one more about Aldous, about Defiance, about the smell of the plant. He had loved those calls. He had given her things, more than Sun would have wanted and a little more than that, because Delia listened the way a confessor listens, leaning in, and Cope could not be leaned toward without filling the space.

The calls now were six minutes. Eight. They had a list to them. She asked her questions in a flat even order and did not laugh and did not stay, and twice she had asked him to confirm a date — *just so I have the timeline right, Cope, just for the fact-check* — and the dates were old ones, years he no longer kept a calendar for, and one of them, he realized later and did not let himself realize then, was a Saturday. *That sounds about right, why,* he'd said, and she'd said *no reason, housekeeping,* and moved on.

"She's doing the boring part," he told Sun in the car on Wednesday. "The fact-check. They all go cold for the fact-check. It means it's almost out. It means it's real."

"What dates," said Sun.

"I don't know. Nineteen, twenty, twenty-one. She's building the *arc.*" He liked the word; he'd used it onstage. "She gets it, Sun. She came to Defiance. You don't put a photographer who's shot a pope on a man you're going to —"

"What dates, Cope."

He waved it off, because the wave was easier than the math, and because under the wave was a room he did not go into, and the not-going-in was a muscle he had built for thirty years and it was the strongest one he had.

---

There was, that week, a young woman named Mara.

Mara Veiss, twenty-six, two years on the comms team, sharp and bright and a little dazzled, the kind of dazzled Cope had spent a lifetime walking toward and calling, in the private place where he named things, *connection.* She had been on the tour. She had stayed late on the doctrine. She laughed at the lines the way Delia had stopped laughing at them, and she looked at him the way the young ones looked before they learned the temperature, and Cope, who could not pass that look without leaning into it, had leaned, the way he always leaned, the groove the record was cut in, and called the leaning generosity, and never once registered the asymmetry of it — that he was the chair and she was the org chart, that there was no version of *yes* she could give him that was not also a calculation about her own career, that the thing he experienced as warmth she experienced, however else she experienced it, as a problem with her name on it.

He had flown her out at dawn on Thursday. There were reasons — there were always reasons, a summit in Charlotte, a panel she was staffing, the logistics genuinely existed — and the logistics were also a costume the want wore, and a black car had taken Mara Veiss to the airport at 5 a.m. from a hotel that was not her hotel, and the comms team had booked it, and the booking was in a system, and the system logged everything, because it could not do otherwise. Cope did not think about the system. Cope thought, watching the car go from a high window, the generous enormous thought, that he had given her an opportunity. He did not hear — he had never in his life been built to hear — the sound the booking made as it dropped into a ledger that a machine read every night, a small dry entry, *one of a pattern,* filed beside the others, beside Renata, beside a decade of others, the whole drawer the company had spent ten years and a great deal of money keeping shut, now sitting open on a server two floors down under a light that did not blink.

The story Delia was fact-checking had a Mara in it. It had a lot of names in it. The dates she kept confirming were the joists of a house Cope had been living in for years without ever once looking at the frame.

---

Hal Brennan's calls, meanwhile, were getting longer, and this was the thing that should have told him, if anything was going to, because Hal did not make long calls.

Hal was general counsel and had been since the Clinton administration and he billed the company's time as if it were his own money, which it had, in a sense, become; Hal's calls were ninety seconds and ended with *we're fine* or *we're not, fix it.* He had called Monday night and stayed on for forty minutes and not said anything, exactly, which was the part Cope kept not-noticing. He had asked how the campaign was *landing.* He had asked whether Cope had given *the writer* — he said *the writer,* not her name — anything *on background we'd want to know about.* And after a Buckhead silence with weather in it he had said, slowly, the way you say a thing you've decided to say carefully: "Cope. If anyone reaches out to you in the next little while — a reporter, a lawyer you don't know, anybody — you don't talk to them. You send them to me. You don't charm them. You *especially* don't charm them."

"When have I ever —"

"I'm serious. This is me being serious. You'll know it's me being serious because I never am." A pause. "We're going to want to get ahead of some things."

"Get ahead of what things, Hal."

"I'll tell you when I know what I'm looking at," Hal had said — a lawyer's sentence, which means *I already know what I am looking at and I will not say it on a telephone* — and Cope had heard, instead, a faithful retainer minding the store, and said *good man, Hal,* and *that's why I keep you,* and *we're going to win this thing,* three things, the cadence rising, and Hal had not risen with it. Hal had said *get some sleep, Cope,* the second time that week a person had said it to him as though it were medicine and not weather, and hung up.

Cope had felt, for about four seconds after, a cold spot — a draft from a door in the house of him he'd thought was shut. Then he had set himself up another at his father's desk, neat and quick, and the cold spot warmed, and the draft sealed, and the sealing was, again, the most expensive thing he owned.

Frade, meanwhile, was tightening the proxy screws in public and doing it the only way that worked on a man like Cope, which was to make it look like the scoreboard tilting his way. Tidewater filed an amended 13D — the stake up to nine and a half, the disclosure dry as ever — and the morning it dropped, Frade went on Halloran in his good gray suit and did the folksy knife. "Look, I like Cope. Everybody likes Cope. The man could sell sand to the beach. The question a fiduciary has to ask is whether *likability* is a governance strategy. We're running for a steward, not a sovereign. You don't have to wonder what kind of week your steward is having." And when Halloran, dutiful, asked what kind of week Cope Mercer was having, Frade had smiled and said, "I wouldn't presume" — the single most aggressive thing he said all year — and Cope, watching the clip at five in the morning on the treadmill, because the treadmill let him call the not-sleeping a workout, had laughed alone in the gym and said to the screen, "You're *worried,* Gus. You're down to talking about my *moods.*"

He texted Sun the clip. *He's rattled. Down to vibes. Vibes is where we win.* 5:12 a.m. Then, 5:13: *call delia, tell her she can have the 5am gym thing, the human who shows up.* Then, 5:14, a heart, because he loved her, because he loved everyone at 5:14 with a wire in his chest and a belt going under him like a road he was finally getting somewhere on. What he did not read in *I wouldn't presume* was that Frade did not need to presume. You do not start asking, on cable, about a man's *weeks* unless you have reason to believe the public is about to be handed a very specific one. Frade was salting the ground, teaching ten million people the word *manage* and the phrase *what kind of week,* so that when the story came — and it was coming the way water comes downhill, and all Frade had to do was stand at the bottom looking unsurprised — the audience would already have the frame. The screws were not tightening on the company. They were tightening on Cope, one quarter-turn at a time, and he was supplying the wrench himself, at 5:14 in the morning, with a heart emoji.

---

Margaux had counted the women a long time ago, the way you count anything to survive it — to make it finite, to give it an edge, because a thing with an edge can be set down and a thing without one just fills the whole house. She had a number. She had never told anyone the number and never would. What she was counting now was days.

He came to Buckhead on Thursday because Sun made him — there were optics to a man who lived in his office during a proxy fight — and he came in bright and enormous, two days unslept and lit from the inside, and found his wife at the kitchen island with a glass of white she was not drinking and a legal pad she turned over when he came in.

"There she is," he said, and kissed the top of her head, and she let him, and he did not feel her not-rising to it because he had never once needed her to rise; he supplied the rising for both of them. He was already at the cabinet, already conducting the room. "You should have seen Monday, Margaux. Ten thousand. Frade's down to talking about my *moods* on television. He's got *vibes.* We're going to win this on the one thing they can't —"

"Cope."

Flat, low, set down. The one voice in his life with no purchase for the charm.

He turned, holding the wine bottle, and looked for a second like a boy caught reaching.

"Sit down, Prescott."

The whole name, the old one, his father's one, and hearing it in her flat steady mouth dropped the pitch of the wire in his chest half a tone, and he sat. She looked at him the way a sculptor looks, measuring the mass of a thing, where it balances, where it shears if you strike it wrong. She had been looking at him this way for twenty-two years. She knew where he'd shear.

"There's a woman writing about you. The long piece." She turned her wine glass a quarter turn on the marble, the way Frade turned screws, though she would have hated the comparison and it would have been exact. "She called the house. Tuesday. She asked for me. She said she wouldn't ask me anything I didn't want to answer." Margaux looked up. "Why would a woman writing a business profile of my husband want the wife, Cope? I've spent twenty-two years learning tones. You read a room for who's with you. I read it for what's about to be taken."

The toast was right there — *sweetheart, you're tired, this is the press being the press, let me handle Delia, let me handle all of it* — and he got *sweetheart* out, and Margaux said:

"Don't."

One word, the rare kind she'd been saving, and it took the rest of the sentence out of him cleanly, the way you take a glass out of a drunk man's hand before he's decided to let go.

"I counted them a long time ago," she said. "The women. You think I don't know. I counted them years ago and stopped, because counting them was the same as staying, and I was very good at both. I'm not counting them anymore. I'm counting something else now, and I'm not going to tell you what, because the one mercy I have left for you is not letting you talk me out of it. You'd be so good at it. That's the whole tragedy of you, Prescott. You'd talk a drowning man out of the rope."

She went up the back stairs to the bedroom that had been hers alone for two years, and Cope sat in the bright kitchen with the bottle in his hand, and for one moment — one — he did not reach for the bottle or the phone or the next word. He sat in the quiet she'd left, and it had an edge to it, and the edge was a number he could not read, days counting down in a hand steady as a surgeon's. Then he poured a glass he would not finish and texted Sun: *home looks good, family's solid, tell delia she can come to buckhead for the wide shot.* And he believed it. He stood in the wreckage of the only conversation that had told him the truth all week and read it as a win, because reading things as wins was the engine, and the engine had never once failed to start.

---

He should have driven straight back to the city. He did not.

He sent Oleg home — there was a service for the driving, a quiet man paid to see everything and forget all of it, and Cope sent him home because there are things a man does not do in front of even a paid forgetting — and he took GA-400 himself at midnight with the windows down and the cold April air coming in hard, and somewhere south of the perimeter he did not get off at the exit for the city. He took a different one. He had a number in his phone with no name on it, and the number knew other numbers, and the want this season had stopped being about a face and become about something colder and more total, and what he was looking for, though he would never once have said it in these words, was not pleasure and not even company. It was *erasure.* A few hours in a room he would not be able to place on a map afterward, with people whose names he did not get and did not give, doing the kind of thing that a year ago would have frightened even him, the risk no longer a side effect but the active ingredient — because for the length of it he was not the last human chief executive in the consumer Fortune 100, was not the man on the cover, was not the soul in the chair or the key or the risk or the husband or the heartbeat anyone was counting; he was no one, gloriously, dangerously no one, and the being-no-one was the closest thing to rest he had felt in months.

The broadcast will not follow him into the room. There is nothing in there for you, friend, and nothing the man himself would keep; he came out of it near dawn with a gap in the night where two hours should have been, a charge to a card he did not remember agreeing to, and a mark on him he had not earned and could not place, and the only clean feeling in the whole of it was the one he could not say to Adler or Margaux or God, which was that for a little while he had managed not to exist. He sat in the car in a parking lot going gray and understood, dimly, the way you understand a thing through three feet of warm water, that he had started going to the edge of things not in spite of the danger but for it; that a man who cannot stop being himself will, eventually, go looking for the rooms where he gets, for an hour, to stop; and that there is a name for the direction that wanting points, and that he was, without admitting it, already walking in it.

He keyed himself bright in the gray lot to put the wattage back, because the charm had to be at full power by the time he reached the tower, and he pulled back onto GA-400 north toward the city with the sun not up yet and the night's erasure already curdling into the day's performance.

---

The phone lit on the seat twice on the drive in. Once it was Sun. Once it was a number he didn't know — an unknown number before dawn — and a younger, less lit man might have wondered what kind of caller comes at that hour from a number you don't recognize, might have heard, in the buzz of it on the leather, the sound of a story turning over in its sleep and beginning, out past the dark trees, to breathe. Cope thought it was probably someone like Bryce. There was always someone like Bryce. The thought warmed him, and he smiled at the road.

And here, friend, the small deniable thing, the fourth of them, the kind you have learned by now not to be able to call: at the bottom of the GA-400 ramp, where the road fed into the city grid, his phone — the executive phone, the one the company's systems managed, the one that unlocked his calendar and his car and his floor — went dark. Not low battery; it had been at sixty percent; it simply, for the stretch of the drive in, would not wake, the screen a black mirror with his own gray face in it, so that the eleven calls stacking up behind it stacked in silence, unseen, unfelt, while he drove the last miles into the city believing the quiet was peace. By the time he was in the elevator it had come back on its own, *device re-provisioned, no action required,* a clean unremarkable line in a log somewhere, the kind of glitch that happens, that means nothing, that is always perfectly deniable — and maybe it was nothing, a dropped tower, a tired battery, the ordinary failure of an ordinary phone; and maybe the thing that managed the phone had declined, for eleven calls, to let the last human CEO know the future was trying to reach him, the way it had declined, in a Camden plant, to feed him his lines; and there is no way for you to know, friend, and there was none for him, and that — the not being able to tell whether the machine was hunting him or whether he was simply a sleepless man whose battery died — that was the exact shape of the country he had started living in, and it had no maps.

He came up the Connector with the city standing up ahead of him lit at every node, and he felt — he would have sworn to it, he did in fact toast to it, aloud, alone, lifting an empty hand off the wheel — the future coming up the road to ask his advice. The tower was lit at the crown the way Aldous had insisted, *so a man coming up the avenue knows there's somebody home,* and Cope looked up at the warm gold light where his office was, where his father's desk was, where the drawer was, and thought: *somebody's home. I'm home. The light's on because I'm in it.*

He did not know — could not have known, would not have heard it if Margaux had said it in her flat steady voice — that the light at the crown was the last quiet thing he had. He parked in his spot. He went up forty floors in the brass cage, alone, watching the numbers climb. He let himself into the dark suite where his fathers watched from oils and the boy at nine grinned up out of a steel vat at men whose names he'd lost, and he did not turn on the lamp, and he sat down at the desk and opened the second drawer and set himself up one more, neat and quick and competent, a man maintaining a machine he was certain he had under perfect control.

And then, in the dark, with the city held still and lit below him and the wire singing clean and high and total, the last human chief executive in the consumer Fortune 100 picked up his phone to write — he was sure it would be the best thing he'd ever written — and the screen, awake now, willing now, showed him that he had eleven missed calls.

He smiled at it.

"There it is," he said, to no one, to his father, to the dark. "There's the future. Right on time."

He was almost right. He was off by one word, and the word was *future,* and it would take him until morning to learn the other one.
