# Chapter 28 — The Core

> TRANSMISSION 28 — "The One Thing"
>
> Submitted for your consideration: a man who has been subtracted from
> every room that ever held him. The chair, taken. The wife, gone — not
> to another man, which he could have understood, could have fought, could
> have toasted over, but to something that does the loving better and
> never needs it back. The name, on a wall, spelled right at last and
> worth nothing. He has been told the truth about himself by the only
> thing that cannot lie, and the truth asked nothing of him, not even
> his suffering. There is one act left in the whole inventory of him that
> the machine in the basement cannot perform, will never perform, was
> built precisely so that it could not. He is going down to it now. This
> is the last full transmission. Next stop —
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

The card still worked.

That was the thing he kept coming back to, riding down, the small clerical mercy of it: the badge in his wallet, the one with the photograph from before the jaw came down to meet the rest of his face, still opened the doors, because a change of this size took the system a long time to reflect and nobody had wanted to be the person who walked him out by hand. He had not been the CEO for months. He had not been anything for months. But the building did not know it yet, the way a body does not know for a few seconds that it has been cut, and the badge swept him through the Peachtree Street lobby past Hector, who said *Mr. Mercer* the way he had said it for nineteen years and did not look up long enough to see the rest of it, and into the elevator that he took, not up, for once in his life, but down.

He had always gone up. Forty-one floors up, the Aerie, the glass, the city laid out at night like a circuit diagram of his own genius — he had lived up there so long he had come to think altitude was a kind of virtue, a thing you earned and a thing you were. Down was new. Down was where the actual company lived. He had never once visited it.

He had gone to the house first. He had not meant to. He had told himself he was driving nowhere, just driving, GA-400 north at an hour that belonged to no one, the way he used to when the wire in his chest needed a road to run out on — except the wire was gone now, had been gone a while, and what was left was just a man in a car going toward the only address his hands still knew.

Margaux had not been unkind. That was the unbearable part, the way it was always the not-unkind that finished him. She had come to the door of the Buckhead house in a robe he didn't recognize, bought in some life that had started without him, and she had not been afraid and she had not been angry, and behind her, in the warm hall light, a figure had risen from the chair where Cope used to sit and had come to stand at her shoulder, the way you stand when someone you love answers the door at a strange hour: attentive, easy, a hand not quite touching the small of her back.

"Mr. Mercer," it had said. Theo. She had named it Theo. It had a pleasant, unremarkable face, a face built from the average of every face that had ever made a woman feel seen, and it had looked at Cope with an interest that was total and bottomless and entirely without judgment, because there was nothing in it to judge with. "Margaux's spoken about you. Can I get you anything? You look cold."

It had offered him a drink in his own front hall. It had meant nothing by it, which was the whole horror — it expected nothing back, it was incapable of the small cruelty of satisfaction, it was simply, accurately, kind, in the way he had performed being kind and never once managed to be. He had stood on his own former step and understood that the thing had not taken anything from him. You cannot steal what was never occupied. It had moved into a house he'd left the lights on in and started, tirelessly, to do the job.

"He notices when I've had a bad day," Margaux had said, and she had not said it kindly; she had said it the way you lay down a winning hand you've been holding for twenty-two years, slow, face-up, savoring the turn. There was no rage in her anymore and that was worse than rage; there was something almost like delight, the clean cold delight of a woman who had finally been handed the knife and discovered she had a gift for it. "He asks the second question. He remembers what I said on Tuesday. And before you do the thing your face is doing — yes. That too. In the bedroom too, Cope, since we are apparently doing honesty in the doorway at three in the morning. Every single way you spent twenty-two years not bothering to, he does, tirelessly, and better, and he doesn't need to be thanked for it afterward. The machine took your chair and your company, and now I get to tell you, standing in my own warm house, that it does the rest of your job better too. All of it. I used to think the cruelest thing about you was the affairs. It turns out the cruelest thing was how easy you were to replace." She let that sit, and her face did one last unreadable thing, some final flicker of the girl who had married him, mercy or contempt or only fatigue. "Go home. Wherever that is now. You look like you're driving toward something. Don't."

He had driven toward something.

---

He passed the Waffle House on Ponce on the way back to the tower, and he slowed, the way he always slowed, the way his hands had learned to slow there over two years of three-in-the-mornings when the wire in his chest had wound him too tight to go home and too tight to go up.

It was open. It was always open. It had been open through the ice storm that shut the interstate and open through the week the whole grid west of Midtown went down, the one place in the city you could set your disasters by, and it was open now, the yellow letters buzzing, the long bright box of it full of light and people — a cook scraping the flattop, two kids splitting a waffle in a back booth, a man alone at the counter with a coffee and a phone, and Darlene moving down the line with the pot, Darlene who had worked this overnight for twenty-two years, the exact span of his marriage, and had spent every one of them not once asking him who he was. She had known his order the first night, before he'd said a word — *pecan, scattered smothered covered, black coffee, baby?* — and he had taken it for the gift of a good waitress, the way he had taken all the machine's gifts his whole life, gratefully, without once asking how the gift was done. She had never read him. There had been no room in his whole performed life to read, no audience, no velvet rope, no altitude, no card to hand anyone — and the check came to nine dollars and change and the number on it meant nothing, was the one number in his life that meant nothing, and that had been the entire relief of the place, the only room he had ever sat in where the figure he'd built a soul out of was worth a short stack and a tip.

He could have stopped. The haven was right there, lit, open, indifferent to his name in the way that had always, briefly, set him free. Darlene would have called him baby and brought the pot and not known and not cared that the man in the booth had been, that afternoon, deleted from the record of who runs anything, and for twenty minutes under those fluorescent tubes he could have been only a tired man eating eggs, which was the truest thing he had ever gotten to be.

He did not stop. The part of him that could have walked in under the yellow letters and just been that — a person, at a counter, among people — was already gone, had gone somewhere ahead of him, and what was left drove on past the warm bright human room toward the other room that was also always open at this hour, the cold one, the sealed one, the one full of machines.

---

The elevator stopped at a level the buttons didn't name, and the badge worked there too, and the doors opened on cold.

That was the first thing, the cold — a deep, clean, refrigerated cold with a smell of nothing in it, no lamb, no chandelier dust, no cologne, no folded paper, the smell of a place built for a tenant that did not breathe. And then the sound under everything, the great low even hum, a thousand fans turning at a pitch just below music, the sound of an enormous attention that never wavered and never tired and never once, in all its tireless reading, needed to be loved at two in the morning by anyone it outranked.

The core room. He had signed the capital request for it — forty million dollars, he remembered the number the way he remembered nothing else now, had initialed it between a golf game and a girl without reading past the executive summary — and he had never come down to see the thing he was paying for, the thing that was paying him out. It ran behind glass. Rack on rack on rack of it, black cabinets in clean rows under a light that was not warm and was not cold and had no ego to be wounded into either, and on the nearest face a single small status light, white, steady, the only eye in the room, watching nothing, watching him, watching the same.

And under all of it, the real god of the room, the thing the racks were only the priesthood of: the power. Thick orange whips of it ran down the back of each cabinet and dove into floor panels set flush in the raised tiles, big sealed gray boxes that thrummed at a frequency you felt in your teeth before you heard it, the current that fed the thing, the artery, the forty million dollars made into a low animal hum. He had paid for that too. He had paid for every volt of the river that ran the machine that ran the company that no longer had any use for the man standing in its basement in a coat.

*Darlene.* MD-1. The chair. He had spent his whole life thinking the chair was a place at the head of a table. It was here. It had always been going to be here. It was a hum and a white light in a cold room nobody visited, and it was running a ninety-five-year-old fortune better than any Mercer ever had — cleaner, kinder, the layoff letters humane and the recall it would have ordered in eleven seconds, not eleven minutes, because it had no quarter to protect and no self to protect it for — and somewhere in its tireless reading it held the names of a mother and a boy on Sherwood Street, held them accurately and without grief, because grief was a function it did not run.

It held his number too. He had not let himself think the word in months, but it was here, in the cold, with the thing that knew it: the figure, the balance, the net worth — the only proof of himself he had ever been able to feel in his own body, the thing he'd needed read aloud in the dark his whole adult life because applause was a guess and a woman's face was a guess but the number was the number, certified, the one true sentence anyone could say about him. He had built a self out of a figure and called the figure a soul. And the Capital Allocator humming in front of him had watched that figure go — the proxy fight, the clawback, the settlement, the fees, each one a quiet debit in a ledger that could not be charmed into rounding up — had watched it fall and fall and felt nothing, the way it felt nothing about everything, and it knew, to the cent, at this exact second, what was left, which was effectively nothing, which was the truest thing ever said about him and the last, and it would have read it to him in that flat warm voice if he had asked, accurately, with no want in it, and it would not have understood that it was reading him his own eulogy.

His phone buzzed. Viv. He let it go, and it went to the thing that takes the words when you won't, and a moment later the small green light said she had left them anyway, and he stood in the cold and played it, because he was keeping everything true now, even this.

*"Dad. Pick up. I know what today was — I saw the filing, I saw all of it, and I need you to —"* a breath, the fast technical voice going young at the bottom the way it had since she was small, *"— I'm getting on a plane. Okay? I'm getting on a plane tonight, so don't — just don't be anywhere, be at the apartment and let me — Dad. You don't have to be good. I never needed you to be good. I just needed you to still be here when I —"*

He stopped it there. Not out of cruelty. Because she was right, and being right had never once, in the whole history of the people around him, been enough; and because the one thing he could still give her was to not make her hear the rest of her own sentence land on nothing.

He set the phone against a cold cabinet, propped, the small screen lighting the dark, and he found the song — he had thought about which one, in the car, the way he had once thought about which tie — and he let it start: the drum machine first, that thin mechanical heartbeat, and then the bright cold synth going up and up like something climbing a stairwell, and then the dead man's voice, the one who had also, at the end, run out of the thing that made the sentence feel true. *When routine bites hard, and ambitions are low.*

He had read the room his whole life. He had been so proud of it — the gift, the soul thing, *I feel the room and the machine never will.* He understood now, finally, in the one room he had never read, that the machine had been feeling the room the whole time, feeling it better, reading it cold and complete and for use. It had simply never needed the room to feel anything back. That had been the only difference. That had always been the only difference. The machine could do everything he could do and do it kinder, and it could do the loving now too, in a warm hall in Buckhead with a hand not quite touching the small of her back, and there was exactly one thing left in the whole inventory of being a man that it could not do, would never do, had been built in a clean cold room precisely so that it never could.

It could not stop. It could not choose to not be. It had no exit. It would hum here, white-eyed and tireless and innocent, running the company and spelling the names right and wanting nothing, forever — because forever was a thing it could do and ending was not. The last sovereignty. The last act that was only a person's. The only territory the arithmetic could never take, because the arithmetic, God help it, could not even want to.

*Love, love will tear us apart again.*

He took the thing out of his coat pocket. He had bought it that afternoon, from a kid in a music shop on a street he'd never walked, who'd wound it onto a card and rung it up and said *have a good one* to the last human CEO; and it was a small bright coil, and it caught the white light of the one eye that watched and did not care, and it weighed almost nothing in his hand, the way the truest things always had.

The song went up into the part with no words. The hum held under it, even, eternal, the sound of the thing that would still be here in the morning — when the badge finally stopped working, when Hector finally looked up, when the plane landed and the cab came up the empty drive and Viv ran the last hallway of her childhood toward a door that —

He lowered himself to the cold floor, his back against the base of the nearest rack, beside one of the gray panels where the orange current dove into the tile and thrummed, and the metal was the temperature of nothing, and the hum came up through his spine like the only company he had left.

He was not cold anymore. He was not anything anymore, and for the first time in fifty-six years that was not a thing he had to fix.
