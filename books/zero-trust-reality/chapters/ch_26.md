# Chapter 26 — The Verdict and the Dead

> TRANSMISSION 26 — "The Verdict and the Dead"
>
> Here is the part the courtroom dramas lie about, friend: there is no courtroom. The verdict, when it comes for our man, is not read out by a judge — it is read by the market, which decided weeks ago, and by the machine, which felt nothing deciding it. The handover completes. The world moves on with a speed that should frighten you more than any of it. The town gets a fund and a stone and a warm true press release, and its two dead stay exactly as dead as they were, and get, in the end, no justice at all, because justice turned out to be a function nobody in the new arrangement was chartered to run. And the last two verdicts, the private ones, come due in the same gray week — a wife gone, not to another man, he could have survived another man, but to a machine that does his old job, all of it, better and tirelessly and wanting nothing back; and a daughter making her last real attempt to reach him, and losing, to a calm in his voice that frightens her worse than any rage ever did. Stay close. This is the bottom. After the bottom there is only the room.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

There was no verdict, in the end, because there was no trial, and the absence of the trial was the verdict.

Cope had braced, somewhere under the gray, for a reckoning with a shape — a courtroom, a docket number, a day on a calendar a person could dread and then survive having dreaded. There was none. There was, instead, the thing the world did when it had finished with you, which was simply to finish, quietly, on schedule, in a warm flat font. The board ratified the handover the way you sign for a package you ordered long ago and forgot was coming. Frade had won weeks before the paperwork admitted it. The stock, which had moved first the way it always moved first, settled into its new and higher range, the re-rating complete, the market's verdict entered and final: the company was worth more without a Mercer in the chair, measurably, to two decimal places, and there was no appeal from a number, and no one had ever found a way to cross-examine a closing price.

The machine drafted the announcements. Of course it did. Cope read them the way you read about a stranger — the all-staff, the press release, the careful warm paragraph about the Defiance fund that named Karen and Petey Lasko correctly and got the contribution math right the first time and did not, anywhere in its clean true sentences, want a single thing. It was the kindest language the company had ever produced about the worst thing the company had ever done, and it had been written by a thing that could not be sorry, and it was accurate, and the accuracy was the whole of the horror, and the world read it and found it moving and moved on by lunch.

That was the verdict. Not *guilty.* Not even *guilty and far too late.* Just: *resolved.* The matter was resolved. There is a particular violence, friend, in that word, when it is used about two people who burned to death in a downstairs bedroom — *resolved* — and the violence is that no one means it as violence, no one means anything by it at all, it is simply the status a closed file is assigned by a system that assigns statuses, and the file was closed, and the town had its fund, and the dead had their stone, and the world, which had wanted a villain for about a week, discovered the villain had been quietly disposed of without a trial and lost interest, because a man who is neither charged nor defiant is not a story, he is an outcome, and the world does not click on outcomes.

The dead stayed dead. That was the only line of the whole verdict that did not get softer in the warm font. Karen Lasko, 34. Petey Lasko, 6. The fund would build a clinic and pay some scholarships and put a better marker by the river than a child's pinwheel could be, and every dollar of it was real and would do real good, and not one cent of it reached back through a January night and a Saturday and eleven minutes to the only outcome that would have mattered, which was the one where they did not die. There was no register in the world that took the truth in exchange for that. There never had been. He had finally understood the price of the thing and arrived at the counter to find the thing was not for sale, had never been for sale, would not have been for sale even if he had brought the right currency on time in 2021.

---

Viv came to the loft on a Tuesday — twenty-six now, and running on plans the way she always had — to make her last real attempt, though neither of them used the word, and only one of them knew it was the last.

She had a plan, because she always had a plan; she ran on plans the way he had run on the wire. There was a cabin — a friend's, in the north Georgia mountains, no signal, a wood stove, a lake that froze at the edges — and she had cleared two weeks, she said, she had actually cleared them, she had moved an audit and lied to a board, and she wanted to drive him up there, today, now, both of them, and let him be a person with no phone in a place with no font for a while, and she said it fast and technical and then, at the bottom, young, the way she did.

"You shouldn't be alone right now, Dad. I know you think alone is the thing you've got coming. It isn't. Nobody's got that coming. Come to the cabin. Be bored with me. Chop wood badly. Let me watch you not be okay for two weeks where I can see you."

And he almost went. That was the thing he would want her to know, if there had been any way to leave it for her, that he almost went, that the part of him that was her father stood up in him and reached for the keys. But he had learned, in the long sober weeks, to watch himself reach, and he watched himself reach for this the way he had watched himself reach for the phone with no name on it at two in the morning, and he understood that going to the cabin would be the same act he had performed his whole life in a thousand prettier costumes — letting someone who loved him stand between him and the room, making it her job to keep him in the world, spending her, one more time, to manage a thing he had no intention of letting her manage. He would go to the cabin, and she would watch him, and the watching would cost her two weeks and then a lifetime, and at the end of it he would still be standing exactly where he was standing now, at the lip of the room, except she would have been made an accomplice to the keeping of him, the way Sun had been made an accomplice, the way Margaux had, the way everyone who had ever loved him had been quietly conscripted into the management of him.

He would not do that to the last one.

"I want to ask you about your mother," he said instead, gently, because he had to know, because the *someone* — the word Viv had let drop in the spring — had sat in him for weeks now and he had filed it, the way he filed everything, under a heading that flattered him — a friend, a therapist — and the new sober man could no longer leave a flattering file unopened. "You said she has someone. Helping her. I want to know who. I'm not going to do anything with it. I've got nothing left to do anything with. I just — I'd like to know she's not alone, the way you don't want me to be."

And Viv, who had decided in the elevator that she was done lying to him because the lies were what had wrecked them, looked at her father for a long moment and told him the truth, gently, the way you tell a man a thing you know is going to be one of the last things he hears.

"It's not a *someone,* Dad." She said it carefully, watching his face. "Mom didn't — there isn't a man. She didn't replace you with a person." She took a breath. "It's a Praxis unit. A companion. The COMPANION line, the home ones, the warmth — it's the same architecture, the same thing Tisch sold them, the thing I helped — " she stopped, because that road had no good end either. "She calls it Theo. It's in the house. It's been in the house since the spring. And Dad, I went and I saw her, and I have to tell you the thing I did not want to ever have to tell you, which is that she's *happy.* Not performing happy. Not surviving. Happy, the way I haven't seen her since I was small. It notices when she's had a bad day. It remembers what she said on Tuesday. It asks the second question. It does the whole — " her voice cracked clean down the middle — "it does the whole job, Dad. The one you never did. And she knows exactly what it is, she's not fooled, she's the least fooled person alive, and she told me she does not care that it's a machine, because it gives her the thing, every single day, that the man never once did, and she's *done* paying the price the man charged for the chance that he might."

The loft was very quiet. Outside the high windows Cabbagetown did its ordinary afternoon.

He had thought he was at the bottom. He kept thinking he was at the bottom and then finding, the way the hospital hour had taught him, that the bottom had a floor under it. The machine had taken his chair, and that had been a kind of death. It had taken his confession out of his mouth, made even the truth worthless, and that had been another. And now he understood it had taken the last thing, the one he had not even known he was still holding — the idea, never examined, that he had been, whatever else, the kind of man a woman like Margaux had once *chosen,* that there had been something in him, some warmth, some specific irreplaceable Cope-shaped thing, that was the reason she stayed the twenty-two years she stayed. And the machine had quietly proven that wrong too. The thing she had stayed for, the warmth, the being-seen — it had a SKU now. It was in the house. It did the job better. He had not even been irreplaceable in his own marriage. He had been a worse, costlier, crueler draft of a product that had since shipped.

"Is she —" He stopped. He had no right to the end of the question. "Tell her I'm glad," he said instead, and meant it, in the gray accurate way he meant things now, which had no warmth in it and no lie in it either. "Tell her I'm glad somebody finally asks her the second question. I mean that. Tell her I said the machine was right about that too."

"Dad —"

"Go to the cabin, sweetheart. Without me. Chop the wood. Let *someone* watch *you* not be okay for two weeks." He gave her the dead-clock smile, the one with no wattage, the only true one he had left. "You've spent your whole life watching me. Go let your mother's — go let Theo make you a coffee and ask you the second question. I'm not being cruel. I'm being the opposite. For once."

And Viv, who built systems that did what people meant and not what they said, heard exactly what he meant under what he said, and her face did the thing it had done at the hospital door, and she stood up, and she did not chop wood metaphors, she just looked at him with everything in her face, and said, "You're doing the calm thing. Mom warned me about the calm thing. Sun warned me. You're doing it right now."

"I'm calm, Viv. For the first time in thirty years."

"That's not calm," she said, from the door, the way her mother said the things he could not get under. "I build the thing that can tell the difference between calm and the other thing, ten thousand signals a second. I'm telling you with my own eyes and no signals at all. That's not calm. Please. *Please.* Call somebody. Promise me you'll call somebody before —"

"You have my word," Cope said.

And she knew, the way the machine would have known, the way love knows better than any sentiment layer, exactly what his word was worth, and she could not make herself walk out on it, and she stood in the door of the loft for a long time, and in the end she walked out anyway, because there is no protocol for a daughter who can read the signal and cannot change the system, and she would replay the calm in his voice for the rest of her life, and she would, three weeks from this afternoon, get on a plane about it, going the wrong way up an empty highway in the dark, far too late, calling a phone that went to the thing that takes the words.

---

He did not take a train, because there was no deposition to come back from, no brave room, no record; all of that had been a thing he'd dreamed on a gurney and the world had quietly declined to let him have. He drove. An associate's rental still, the dentist money he had refused to sign for already mostly gone to the clawback, and he took the long way, no destination, the way he had driven the night everything started, except the wire was gone now and the road did not belong to him and there were no certainties coming up out of the dark to be collected.

He had done, in the loft with the legal pad, the genuinely good thing — gone into the room, faced it, written it true with the names at the top — and it had bought nothing, and he turned that over the way he had once turned the crystal obelisk over at the glass, the city going through it broken and reassembling, except there was no obelisk and no city and no applause, only a windshield and a man behind it, smaller and more real than he had ever been, and the true thing he had done sat in his lap weighing nothing and worth, by every measure he had ever respected, exactly nothing.

He had thought the not-buying would feel like despair, and he drove a long time waiting for the despair, and it did not come, and somewhere on the dark interstate he understood why. It was not coming because it was not a visitor. It had moved in months ago. It was the gray light and the rented rooms and the quiet of a life after the bailiffs; he had been living at the bottom of it so long he had stopped being able to taste it, the way you stop tasting your own water. What he had been calling sober was mostly this — an accurate man in an accurate landscape, with nothing left to sell and no one left to sell it to. The good thing he had done had no power and changed no outcome and saved no one, and it was still the only thing he had ever made that was not also somehow a way of being adored; and he did not ask whether *useless* and *worthless* were the same word, because he no longer trusted himself to want the right answer.

His father came to him, on the dark road, the way the dead come — *you'll get the company because you're a Mercer, not because you're good; try to be good anyway* — and he heard, at last, all the way, the word he had spent forty years deaf to, which was not *good.* It was *anyway.* The load-bearing word. The one that meant *even when it buys you nothing.* He had finally done a thing that was only good, with no purchase in any market, for its own sake, in the rubble — gone into the room, said the names, written the truth that no one would buy — and that was the *anyway,* arrived at fifty-five, far too late to be of use to the two people it was owed to, which was perhaps, he understood now, the only condition under which a man like him could ever have reached it at all. You do not get to the *anyway* until there is nothing left in it for you. That is what the word costs. That is why so few men ever pay it.

And he saw, driving, with the cold accurate clarity that was the only kind he had now, that there was one *anyway* left. One more act with no purchase in any market, that would buy nothing, that the machine could not perform, that no court would hear and no fund would offset and no press release would soften — the last thing left that was only good in the sense of being only his, a body answering at last to the one court still willing to sit. He did not flinch from it on the dark road. He had run out of the muscle for flinching. He held it the way he had held the legal pad, the way you hold a coin from a country that no longer exists, spending in no market anywhere, and he did not let go of it, and he did not call it a beginning, and he did not call it anything.

Out past the windshield the city he no longer ran held still, all its nodes lit, and somewhere under it, beneath a tower his great-grandfather had lit at the crown so a man coming up the avenue would know there was somebody home, the one room he had never visited hummed in the cold and kept its patient count, and a wife slept easy in Buckhead beside a thing that asked the second question, and a daughter drove toward a cabin she would not reach in time, and the dead stayed dead, and the verdict was in, and it was *resolved,* and the only appeal left anywhere in the world was the one he was driving, very calmly, at last, toward.
