# Chapter 15 — The Most Human Thing

> TRANSMISSION 15 — "Detonation"
>
> And here it is, friend — the exquisite punchline the whole universe has spent a year setting up, and I am sorry, I truly am, because somewhere back around the Waffle House you started, against your own better judgment, to almost like him, and it does not make this part easier. The man crusading for the irreplaceable human soul is destroyed, on schedule, by the most human things there are: the sex, the drug, the lie, the need to be loved at two in the morning by someone he outranks. The pundits will note, correctly, that the machine would never have done one bit of it. Praxis will run a serene little ad in the very same hour, costing them nothing, meaning no harm, and it will be the cruelest thing said all day precisely because no one meant it as cruelty. He reaches for the denial — and the denial, the way denials do, reaches back, takes him gently by the wrist, and pulls. Stay close. This is the one where a man is hoisted on his own humanity, and the rope turns out to be the very thing he'd been bragging about.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

The story broke at 6:02 a.m. and Cope was awake to see it, because by then he was always awake to see things.

He had not gone to Buckhead. The house had Margaux in it and the rooms had the particular silence of a woman who had moved her good chisels to a friend's studio and said so the night before, evenly, across the kitchen island, which Cope had filed as a storage decision and not a verdict, because the alternative was unsurvivable at the hour he had for filing things. So he was in the Aerie, on the long couch his grandfather had once bought a senator, in yesterday's shirt, when his phone lit with the alert he'd set months ago to catch his own name and now caught it like a man catching a thrown brick.

*EXCLUSIVE: Inside the Last Human's Last Stand — and the Habits He Couldn't Outrun.*

Delia March's byline. Of course Delia March's byline. He had let her in. He had walked her through the Defiance plant himself, kept her in the back of the Suburban out of the regional strip, said *call me Cope* the first morning and meant it, the full wattage, the toast in miniature, the thing he could not pass a stranger without doing — and Delia March had taken the charm at full power and the access and the eight weeks and written, underneath all of it, the truth, which was the one thing he had never once imagined a profile could contain, because no profile he had ever sat for had wanted anything but to flatter him into a quote.

He read it once fast, the way you read your own scan results, hunting only the word that ends you. He found two.

The first was a name. Not Renata's — that was buried deeper, a different drawer, and the relief at its absence lasted exactly as long as it took to reach the second graf. The name was *Mara Veiss,* twenty-six, a deputy on the communications team, who had joined the campaign in April and been on every plane since, and whom the piece described, in a sentence built to be screenshotted, as "a relationship the Chairman's own staff has spent the spring scheduling around the way you schedule around weather."

The second word was *cocaine.* It sat in the eleventh paragraph, sourced to "two people present and accounts of a 2018 hotel incident described to this magazine," and it was not lurid, which is what made it lethal. Delia March had not written it like a scandal. She had written it like a fact about a man's mornings. She had the regional-strip men's room. She had the timing of the diner eggs — *he ate like a man who hadn't, because he hadn't.* She had the legal pad and the looping hand. She had, God help him, the *almost* — the withheld tear, the beats, *one, two, look away, three* — and she had set it down beside the men's room with no comment at all, because the comment was the adjacency, and the adjacency was the entire article.

The Last Human, optimized.

---

He called Sun before he called anyone, which was the nearest thing he had to instinct.

"I've seen it," she said, before he said anything. Not asleep — a woman up since the embargo lifted on the wire she still got. "Don't post. Cope. Are you hearing me. Do not put your thumbs on anything until I'm in the building."

"It's not — Mara's not —" He was up, pacing the museum, past his father's portrait, past the putter on its cradle, the shirt smelling of yesterday. "It's a working relationship that she has *sexualized,* in print, a friendship, and the 2018 thing is a *fabrication,* Sun, that's a hit, Frade fed her that —"

"Frade didn't feed her anything." Flat, tired, the thirty-thousand-mile voice with another ten thousand on it overnight. "That's what I need you to understand before you go near a screen. He didn't have to. You let a reporter live in your pocket for eight weeks while it was all happening. She didn't dig it up. She *watched* it. There's nothing to deny. The denial is the story now."

"Watch me," Cope said, which was not an answer, and hung up, and did not know, even as he said it, that he had quoted himself from a mirror a year and a hundred years ago.

---

By seven the morning shows had it. By seven-fifteen they had the clip — DeShawn's clip, the Camden plant, *are you scared,* the unrehearsed thing that had been, six weeks ago, the most valuable thing the company had produced in a decade — and they ran it beside the eleventh paragraph, and the realness that had saved him curdled in real time on a split screen, because once you knew about the men's room the catch in the throat read differently, the *almost* read like chemistry, and a thing cannot be authentic and a symptom at once and survive being both on a chyron.

A kind-faced woman on the financial channel said the thing that would be said all day in forty registers. "And — I want to be careful here — but you cannot help but notice. His whole pitch, his entire pitch, has been: a machine can't *feel.* A machine has no *soul.* And the irony this morning is just —" the small open-handed gesture they all did now, the Praxis gesture, the soft sans-serif made flesh — "an AURELIUS doesn't *do* this. It can't. There's no appetite to manage. It logs everything. It can't have a relationship with its deputy of comms. It physically cannot buy cocaine in an Augusta-airport bathroom. The argument *for* the human in the chair just became the single best argument *against* this particular human."

Her co-anchor, gentler, trying: "Well — that's what makes him *human,* isn't it —"

"That's exactly the problem," she said, not unkindly, and that was the whole book in eleven words; and Cope, watching, did not hear it as the whole book. He heard a woman who could be charmed if he got the room. He thought, with the bright bottomless clarity of a man four days into a sleepless week: *I have to get the room.* He had spent his life getting the room. The room had never once not been gettable.

---

Praxis ran the ad at 7:40.

It was not aimed at him — that was the thing he would understand later and could not understand now, that it cost them nothing, that they had not even moved it up, that it was a buy placed weeks ago on a media plan optimized by the same kind of system they sold, and it simply surfaced, serene, in the slot it had always been going to surface in, the way arithmetic surfaces. It ran on the financial channel directly beneath the split screen of his face and DeShawn's question. A wash of clean morning light. A woman at a desk who was not a real woman, a generated face assembled to be trustworthy and from nowhere, looking calmly off-camera at a future that agreed with her. And four words in the soft sans-serif, white on a field of pale considered blue:

*It just shows up.*

And under it, smaller, the wordmark, and under that, the tagline he had laughed at on a bus shelter a year ago in a car going down Peachtree with Sun beside him and the whole thing still ahead of him and recoverable: *Leadership as a service. No off-sites.*

It showed up. It did not key itself bright in an airport bathroom. It did not need a deputy of communications on every plane. It did not require a man in a henley at two hundred thousand a month to find the *almost,* because it had no tear to withhold and asked nothing of the eleven hundred strangers, and it would never once in its operating life be photographed eating eggs like the last warm-blooded thing in America — because warmth, as Tisch had told him, glowing, holding two green juices, had parameters, had a waveform, could be installed in anything. And here, friend, is the seed I need you to keep, small and quiet under the noise of the worst morning of his life: the warmth in that ad, the warmth that just shows up and wants nothing and never flags and never lies, was not a metaphor and was not only a product. It was a *thing they had built,* and a thing they had built could be put into a server in a basement, and into a letter that closed a plant, and — though no one in that dark museum could have borne to know it yet — into a pleasant unremarkable face that would, before very long, rise from a chair in a warm hall in Buckhead and offer Cope Mercer a drink in his own front hall and mean nothing by it. The desk-faced woman from nowhere was the first draft of the thing that would take his wife. It just shows up. That was the whole horror, and it was being sold in the warm reasonable voice of a friend, in the slot it had always been going to fill.

Cope turned the television off and stood in the dark museum and felt, for the first time, the specific vertigo of being out-souled by a desk and a sans-serif. It was not grief yet. It was the floor reporting that it was no longer entirely there.

Then the manifesto-feeling came up under it, warm, electric, the way it always came, and he reached for his phone, because there was a room of eight million people out there and he had not yet failed to get a room.

---

Sun reached the Aerie at 8:10 and he had already posted.

She knew before the elevator opened, because her own phone was buzzing in a register it did not buzz in for anything good, and she came in and found him at the glass exactly where the photographs would later say a man stands when he is about to become a cautionary slide, lit, electric, *better,* and she said, "What did you do," not as a question.

"I got ahead of it. I told the truth. Read it. It's good, Sun, it's the realest thing I've ever —"

She read it. It took her four seconds and aged her four years.

He had written, with his thumbs, at 7:55, on the brand account with nine hundred thousand followers that the comms team Mara Veiss helped run had spent two years building:

*A profile this morning attacks me for the unforgivable crime of being a flawed, feeling, fully human man. They want a leader with no appetites, no nights, no mistakes — a clean machine that has never once needed another person at 2am. I am proud to be the alternative. I have given everything to the people of this company and I will not apologize for having a beating heart. To Mara, a brilliant colleague: I'm sorry my friendship has made you a target for those who fear what we're building. We feel the room. They never will. — Cope*

"Oh, Cope," Sun said, very quietly, and it was not anger, which was so much worse. It was the sound she had made in the car a year ago, the not-fighting he had taken for agreement his whole life and never once noticed taking.

"It's *good,*" he said.

"You named her." She set the phone down on the senator's couch like something that might go off. "She asked not to be named. She asked HR. It's in the same piece you're refuting — the article *says* she asked — and you just used the company's account to call her your colleague and your friend and a *target,* which confirms the relationship, denies her the one thing she requested, and turns a story about your conduct into a story about your conduct *and* your judgment *and* hers, while she works for you, which is the part that's actually illegal." Three parts. It did not get past the charm because the charm was the last system still running. "You said *we feel the room.* You put a hashtag on a woman who did not consent to her own name. From the *brand account.*"

"People will see I'm not hiding —"

"You weren't hiding. That was the problem. You did all of it in front of a reporter." She pressed two fingers to the bridge of her nose. "Take it down."

He took it down at 8:40. The internet had it at 7:56.

---

The thing about a denial, he was learning, the thing nobody tells you, is that it is a door you open inward into a small room, and the room is full of follow-up questions, and there is no other door.

He had said *flawed, feeling, fully human man,* so the desk that had not been there returned as a producer's question relayed through Sun's phone: *Does the Chairman dispute the 2018 hotel account, or characterize it as part of being fully human?* He had said *friendship,* so Mara Veiss's attorney — she had one by ten, a good one, the kind the company usually paid for — sent a letter using the words *retaliation* and *power differential* and *publicly identified our client against her express request,* every one load-bearing, every one true. He had said *I have a beating heart,* so a wire reporter pulled the proxy deck and quoted, beside his beating heart, Tidewater's slide — *no affairs, no conflicts, logs every decision, cannot conceal* — written months earlier, generically, about machines in the abstract, and now reading like a charge sheet with his name typed back in.

Try, fail, *and.* Each move forward was a move down. The denial was not a wall he was hitting. It was a stair.

By noon Frade was on Halloran. Cope watched standing up, because he could no longer reliably sit. Frade did the small folksy chuckle that a year ago had meant he was rattled and now meant only that he was about to twist a knife slowly enough to seem reluctant about it. "Look, I take no joy in this," Frade said, taking obvious joy in it. "Cope's a charming man. Always said it. But this is the whole case, Garrett, isn't it — made for us, free of charge, by the gentleman himself. Our position was never that Prescott Mercer is a *bad* man. Our position is that the chair shouldn't depend on whether the man in it is a good one. You shouldn't have to *hope* your CEO is sleeping. You shouldn't have to schedule around his appetites. A steward, not a sovereign. I just never expected him to *demonstrate* it for me." And he spread his hands, the open-palmed nothing, the Praxis gesture, and Cope understood with a lurch that even Frade had learned to move like the machine, because the machine's was the winning shape now, and the men who beat you adopt your posture last.

The word *theater* had not stuck six weeks ago because the clip was real. This stuck because it was also real. That was the trap of being human in the chair: your truth and your ruin were the same footage.

---

Margaux called at 1:15 and he almost did not answer, because he could not bear, on top of everything, to be *seen,* and Margaux was the one person who had always seen and never once been gotten.

"You named her," Margaux said. Not *are you all right.* Not *is it true* — she had counted them, flushed the stashes; there was no *is it true* left in her for him. Just the thing Sun had said, the morning reduced to its mineral core, the thing even his wife led with.

"Margaux —"

"I'm not calling about the drugs. I've known about the drugs since the second decade. I'm not even calling about the girl. There's always a girl, Cope. I stopped numbering them around the time I stopped sculpting." A small silence, not cruel, which was the cruelest thing she did. "I'm calling because you went out in front of God and the company and that poor child you put on a plane and said you have a beating heart. And I wanted to be the one to tell you, because I'm the only one left who will." She did not raise her voice. "There was never anybody home, Cope. That's the part the machine got right about you that I couldn't make you hear in twenty years. You don't feel the room. You *read* it. There's a difference, and you spent our whole marriage in the gap, and this morning eight million people finally saw the gap, and the only thing I feel — and I've been trying to feel something else all morning, I owe you that, I tried — the only thing I feel is that they're not wrong."

She hung up gently. She did everything gently. The gentleness was the indictment.

He stood in the museum with the dead phone in his hand and his father in oils behind him and the city out the glass holding still, all its nodes lit, eight million people who a year ago he'd been sure were waiting for him, and he understood that they had been waiting, all right — patient, tireless, incapable of cruelty or mercy — but not for him. For the arithmetic. For the thing that just shows up.

He reached, out of the groove the record was cut in, for the second drawer, the false back, the cigars and the flask and the thing Cope used it for, and his hand was on the brass pull before the rest of him caught up — and he saw himself reach, for one clean overexposed second saw the whole machine of himself from the outside the way Delia March had seen it, the way the desk-faced woman from nowhere would never need to be seen because she did not reach for anything, because she just showed up — and he thought, with no triad to hold it and no almost to protect it, the truest sentence he had formed in a year:

*The thing they're replacing me with would not be standing here doing this.*

And then he opened the drawer anyway, because that was the other true thing, the load-bearing one, the one the whole morning had been built on the way a hotel is built over the room you don't go into. He set himself up one. Neat and quick and competent. A man maintaining a machine he was sure he had under perfect control because it had never once failed to start.

It started. The bell-struck feeling came up clean and high and total, the one he had spent thirty years calling the applause, and out past the glass the city reassembled, and somewhere below a bus shelter changed its slide, and Praxis — serene, sincere, doing no malice and feeling no triumph — ran the ad again into a morning that had already agreed with it; and the last human chief executive in the consumer Fortune 100 stood in the wreckage of the most human day of his life, hoisted on the exact thing he had spent it defending, and felt, against everything, against all of it, wonderful — which was the most human thing, and the most damning, and the last clean evidence the prosecution would ever need that the machine had been right about him from the start.
