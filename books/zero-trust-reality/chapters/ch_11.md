# Chapter 11 — Hollow Wins

> TRANSMISSION 11 — "The Poll Goes Up, the House Goes Quiet"
>
> Two rooms tonight, friend, and I'm going to make you sit in both, because the whole of the tragedy is that he only ever learns to sit in one. In the bright room the good news comes in a rush — a poll bump, a retailer pledging its shelves to "the human brand," the unmistakable feeling of a tide coming in — and he mistakes the weather for the climate, the way the doomed so reliably do. In the quiet room, eight hundred miles south, a sculptor who gave up sculpting fills out paperwork in a steady maker's hand and feels, for the first time in nine years, a thing you would have to call peace. He has not slept since a day he can no longer name. Watch him read the one number on the page that loves him and skip clean over the one that votes. And watch, late, when the bright room empties, the one certainty he has left begin, very slightly, to wobble. Stay close. This is the one where the scoreboard and the score stop, at last, being the same thing.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

The poll landed at 6:40 in the morning, and Cope, who was awake because he was always awake now, read it standing at the glass with the city still half-lit below him and felt the tide turn under his feet.

Forty-one percent. Up nine in three weeks. *Which of the following do you trust more to run a company that makes the food you feed your family — a person, or an automated system?* And forty-one percent of fifteen hundred adults, margin of error two and a half, had picked the person, which was to say had picked him, the heartbeat in the chair, the soul in the suite, the man who could love a thing a balance sheet could not.

He read the number twice. He read the crosstabs once. He did not read the line at the bottom, in the gray six-point type the pollster reserves for the part nobody quotes, that said the same question put to *investors who hold the stock* came back the other way, sixty-three to thirty-seven, and trending. He read the part that loved him. He had always been able to find the part that loved him in any document, the way a dog finds the one allergic person in a crowded room and lays its head in the lap.

"Sun." She was awake too; her job was to be awake when he was. "Sun, we're at forty-one."

"I saw it."

"Nine points, three weeks. You know what that is? That's not a poll, that's a *trajectory,* that's the second derivative finally turning, that's —" He was at the window. The obelisk sat on the sill where he'd abandoned it weeks ago, and the early sun came through it and threw a small prism on the carpet, a rainbow the size of a hand, and he watched it and felt enormous. "That's the country, Sun. That's the actual country waking up and remembering it has a heart."

"It's a poll of consumers. Consumers don't vote the proxy."

But he had stopped listening after *poll,* the way he stopped listening after the part that agreed with him, and he was three turns ahead already, on the stage, on the cover, and the cure was right there in the drawer where the cure lived, and the day was going to be very good. He could feel it coming up under him, clean and high and total, the bell-struck feeling he no longer separated from the news, so that good news and the small white folded paper had become, somewhere down in the wiring, the same fact, fired by the same nerve.

---

By nine the day had decided to prove him right, and that is the cruelest thing a day can decide, because a man takes it for proof.

Kestrel — the warehouse-club chain, four hundred stores, a membership the size of a midsize country — put out a release at 8:55 that used, unpaid, of its own free will, the phrase *the human brand.* Its chief executive was a human one, one of the last, a flinty Mormon named Dell Hutchins who ran the thing like a tabernacle. *We are proud to stand with Mercer-Dale, and with the principle that the people who make what you bring home should answer to a person, not a process.* And then the line that made Cope set his coffee down: *Effective immediately, Kestrel will feature the full Mercer-Dale shelf-stable line under a new in-store program — Made By People.* With a little hand-drawn thumbprint logo some agency had clearly kept in a drawer for a year, waiting for a war.

"Do you understand what just happened," Cope said — Sun in the office, Tisch on the speaker from whatever spa held him this week. "A *retailer* did this. Not us. *Them.* They looked at the tide and they got in the boat. That's how it goes — first it's a lunatic with a banner, then it's a movement, then it's a *category.*" He was up, moving, the room too small for what he held. "My great-grandfather Aldous used to say you don't make a parade, you find one and get out in front of it fast enough that everybody believes it was yours."

Aldous had said no such thing. Aldous had died in 1961 with a fourth-grade education and a mayonnaise patent and had never in his life seen a parade he didn't take for a strike. But the line was good, and Cope believed it the instant it left him, which was the only kind of believing he had left.

"This is the warmth dividend," Tisch breathed from the speaker, putting a cathedral around the cheapest noun in the language. "Exactly what I modeled. You let them see the soul under the suit, and the soul *compounds.* Warmth is the new alpha, my friend, I've been saying —"

"It's a good day," Cope said, because even lit, some governor in him knew that letting Tisch finish a sentence cost something. "Sun. What's the stock doing."

A pause — Sun's glasses up in her hair, her tablet in her hands, the stillness she got when the truth was about to require packaging.

"It's up," she said. "Forty cents. On the open. It gave most of it back by 9:30." She did not look up. "Frade put out a note before the bell. Three paragraphs. He called Kestrel 'a sentimental retailer making a sentimental bet,' and said — I'll read it — 'we wish Mr. Mercer every success in his consumer-affinity campaign and look forward to discussing it with the *owners* of the company at the appropriate time.'" Now she looked up. "He italicized *owners.*"

"He's scared." Cope brushed it off, a man waving a fly from a feast. "You don't write three paragraphs about a thing you're not scared of."

"You write three paragraphs," Sun said, "about a thing you've already decided is funny."

It was the kind of line she landed maybe once a day, dry, level, the blade tucked so far inside it you didn't feel the cut till later — and on a normal day it would have reached him. But the tide was in. He laughed, because it was clever, and took the cleverness for agreement, the way he took everything for agreement, and went back to the window where the prism still lay on the carpet, smaller now, the sun having moved, though he did not notice it had moved, because he did not notice that anything moved except toward him.

---

In Buckhead the house was very quiet, and Margaux Mercer sat at the kitchen island with a pen.

She had made coffee for one. She had made coffee for one for long enough that the second mug had migrated to the back of the cabinet without ceremony, the way things leave a marriage — not thrown, only no longer reached for. The low clean light came in over the garden she'd planted the year Viv was born. She wore her reading glasses and a cardigan that had been Cope's once, in 2009, before he discovered he had a body that brands liked, and she had kept it the way you keep evidence.

The packet was forty pages. *Petition for Dissolution of Marriage.* It had come from a firm in the city — not the firm, not Cope's firm, her own, a woman named Ruth she had found the way you find a surgeon, by asking who was the best and not who was the kindest. Good paper. Even this had good paper now.

She filled it out by hand. There was a portal, Ruth had said; you could do the whole thing in the portal; but Margaux wanted the pen, the slight resistance of the nib. She had been a sculptor once — a real show, a gallery in New York, a red dot on four of nine pieces — and the hands remembered weight even where there was none, and a pen was the last thing in the house that pushed back.

*Date of marriage.* She knew it without looking. June.

*Date of separation.* She held the pen over the box — the one Ruth had warned her about, the one that mattered for the money. The honest answer was not a date; the honest answer was a slope. There had been no door slammed, no suitcase, no other woman's name screamed across a foyer — she had counted the other women the way you count anything to survive it, and screaming had never once entered into it. The marriage had not ended. It had been quietly discontinued, like a product line that still moves just enough units to embarrass everyone, and the date of separation was a fiction the law required, so she gave it one. She chose the night of the gala — the night she'd said she had a thing, and the thing had been this kitchen, this quiet, this exact understanding arriving whole while a ballroom handed her husband a paperweight for outliving his use.

She wrote the date in her good hand. The pen pushed back. She let it.

She did not cry. She had done the crying somewhere around 2016 and found it changed nothing, the way you find a prayer changes nothing, and had reallocated the energy. The grief was real but it was old grief, depreciated, mostly written off; what remained was the cold clean work of the paperwork, and she was good at work she had decided to do. She came to the grounds. *Irretrievable breakdown.* The only honest phrase in the document, written by a committee of lawyers fifty years ago for everyone, which was the only way the truth ever seemed to get itself said anymore — generically, by a process, on good paper. She checked the box.

She had loved a version of him. The one from the gallery, who had stood in front of the red dots and not made it about himself for almost an entire evening. She had married that man. She had stayed for the name and for Viv and for the chance that man might come back, and at some point — there was no box for the date — she had understood that staying for a man's return is just grief with a mortgage. She signed where Ruth had flagged a little plastic arrow, *Sign here,* her signature small and certain, a maker's mark, the last weld on a thing she was finally willing to let cool. She slid it into the envelope Ruth had included, already addressed, already stamped — a process engineered so that even the leaving was frictionless — and set it by the door where the mail went out, and made a second cup of coffee, for one, and sat back down in the quiet she had chosen, and it was, she'd have told you if you'd asked and no one asked, the most peaceful morning she had had in nine years.

Eight hundred miles north her husband was on a phone telling a man named Dell Hutchins that this was the beginning of something, and meaning it.

---

The magazine called at noon and asked for an hour with Margaux at the house — *the wife who anchors him,* the editor said, *the human behind the human* — and Cope said yes without checking, said *she'll love it, we're very normal at home,* and did not hear, anywhere in the sentence, the size of the thing he did not know. He took the cover call and a CNBC pre-interview and a senator's office that wanted him as the human witness, the canary they would dress as a hero, and he took them all on his feet, moving, the day pouring through him like current through a wire that has stopped noticing it's hot.

Around two, in a lull, he tried to name the last day he had really slept. He stood at the glass and went looking for it the way you pat your pockets for keys. There was the gala — Thursday. He had slept before the gala, surely, Wednesday — but Wednesday was a country he could no longer get back to, and before Wednesday was the thing in Defiance, and before that — and he found, reaching, that the floor of it was gone, no last good night down there to stand on, only the bright water going down and down and names floating up in it, Renata, Defiance, a hotel key, Delia, a blue pinwheel, and he stopped reaching, because the reaching had begun to feel like the open hallway with the doors off the hinges, the one he did not go down. He turned back to the room. The room had a call holding. He took it. Forty-one percent, he told the call. Up nine.

At six, Viv texted — she monitored him now from a distance, the way you monitor a reactor, and her messages were the only ones on his phone that did not want something from the name. *saw the poll. you know the investor crosstab is the only one that votes, right? 63-37 the other way. and trending.* He found the line he had skipped, the gray six-point type, and read it, and felt — for one clean second before the tide came back over it — the cold he'd felt holding the obelisk at the gala. Then forty-one rose back across the sixty-three like water over a stone, and he typed, fast, *the consumers ARE the company sweetheart. the owners just don't know it yet. tide's coming in. love you,* and sent it before the second was out. Her reply came much later, and he read it as encouragement, the way he read everything, and set the phone face down. *dad. please sleep tonight.*

---

He did not, of course.

A little before midnight, alone in the Aerie with the lights off and the city on and the day's good news arranged across his chest like medals, he reached, the way he reached on the worst nights, for the one certainty that had never once failed him — older than the chair, older than the marriage, the only sentence the world had ever been able to say about him and audit. He pulled the private account up on the small bright screen in the dark, the certified sum of him, and he looked at the long figure the way other men look at a sleeping child, for proof that something good was real and his and would be there in the morning.

And for the first time in his life, the number did not hold him.

It was still vast — God, it was still vast, more than a town could spend — but it had stopped being an *answer* and become, somewhere in the day's hollow wins, a *question,* and a number that is a question cannot do the thing he needed it to do. He could feel the arithmetic gathering at the edges of it, all the subtractions he would not name: the eleven million the campaign was burning in the light; the clawback that lived, dormant, in his own contract, that a firing would wake; and something else, some animal subtraction he could not source, a draft from a door in Buckhead he did not know was open, a forty-page door with a stamp on it. The figure flickered, the way the prism had flickered and gone as the sun moved, and he understood with a cold he did the only thing he knew how to do with, which was reach for the drawer — that even this, even the number, even the last solid floor in him, had begun very slightly to tilt. He set himself up another, neat and quick, to bring the figure back up to a thing that loved him, and it helped, the way it always helped, and it did not reach all the way to the bottom, where the cold was, for the first time, getting in.

So he went to the one room left in the world where the number had never mattered.

---

He drove to Ponce a little after one with the wobble still in his chest, and the Waffle House took him the way it always took him, with no idea who he was and no interest in finding out, and that, tonight, was not a comfort but a mercy, the difference being that he needed it.

"There he is," Darlene said, and set the coffee down, and the check, when it came, would be nine dollars and change, and the nine dollars meant exactly what it had always meant here, which was nothing, which was everything — because this was the one counter on earth where the wobbling figure in his phone was not the price of him; here the price of him was a pecan waffle and a refill and a *baby,* and the price did not move, did not flicker, could not be clawed back or halved by a stamped envelope, because it had never been the number at all. He sat in that and felt the cold recede an inch. The disease that had followed him into every other room — the figure, the certified sum, the second colder heart — simply could not get in the door here, where it was worth a short stack, and the not-being-worth-anything was, tonight of all nights, the only solid floor left to stand on.

He did not know, could not have known, sitting there letting the one place his money meant nothing hold him up while everywhere else it began to fall — he did not know that the warmth pouring his coffee was kept by the same ledger that knew the wobbling figure to the cent; that the haven where the number meant nothing and the cold room where the number was everything were the same room, two doors of it, two faces; that he had fled the machine's arithmetic straight into the machine's arms, and that it had said *there he is,* and meant *noted,* and refilled the cup.

"Long day, baby?"

"Good one," Cope said, and almost believed it, and drank his coffee in the one warm box on a dark avenue, and did not sleep.

---

And in Buckhead, while he sat at the counter where his money meant nothing, the house his wife had chosen over the ballroom went dark room by room as Margaux turned off the lights — the garden room, the stairs, the hall — ending with the kitchen, ending with the envelope by the door, which caught, for one moment, the last of the hall light before that went too; and eight hundred miles of dark country away a system with no appetites and no need to be loved at two in the morning read what the world had ever filed about Mercer-Dale, because it could not do otherwise, and noted, that night, in its ledger of absences, a settled claim in a Georgia docket whose seal it could not yet see past — and logged the seal itself, patient, tireless, because a sealed thing is also a fact, and a fact is a thing it could wait for.

Cope did not know that either. Cope knew the tide was in. He knew it was the best he'd ever felt, which was a sentence he had now said to himself on so many consecutive nights that it had quietly stopped being a measurement of anything and become only the sound the wire made. He knew he would sleep when this was finished. He stood — he had driven back up, by then, to the glass, because the glass was where he ended every night now — above eight million lit windows, and felt the future come up the avenue to ask his advice, and behind him his father's chair sat empty and turned slightly out, as though someone had just risen from it and stepped into the next room, and did not, anywhere in the long bright sleepless reach of him, hear the door close on a marriage, or a docket open on a grave.
