# Chapter 1 — The Premium Blend

> TRANSMISSION 01 — "The Obituary in the Trophy"
>
> Pull your chair in close to the set, friend, and never mind the static — out here on the salvage decks the static is most of what's left, and the rest is the story I've been waiting for somebody to tune in and hear. It is a tale of woe. I'll tell you that up front, the way an honest man tells you the dog dies, because you should know going in what you're spending your evening on. It opens, as the good woeful ones do, at a party: a man handed an award for being the last of his kind, lifting it to the light, reading the engraving as a coronation when it is, in every line, a death certificate with generous margins. He tied his own bow tie. He remembers your children's names. He carries in his breast pocket a small folded paper and in his chest a large folded opinion of himself, and tonight, eight days into a sleeplessness he has decided to call vigor, the two of them agree on everything. Thirty-one of the hundred have already handed the chair to the machine. Our man toasts the eleven who haven't, and mistakes a countdown for an ovation, and I am going to tell you the whole of how he got from that ballroom to the dark at the bottom, all of it, because someone should, and you are the only one still listening. So lean in. Stay with me. Watch what he does now.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

They gave him a crystal obelisk for being the last of something, and Cope held it up to the light and told nine hundred people it felt like a beginning, and the terrible thing, the thing that should have warned him and instead warmed him, was that he believed it all the way down.

The room loved that. The room was nine hundred of Atlanta's better-dressed in the Conrad Vance Ballroom of a hotel that had, eleven months back, fired its own chief executive and installed a Praxis unit and watched its stock jump eighteen percent on the news like a man jumping at the crack of his own starting pistol; the ballroom was named for a banker whose firm no longer employed a single human officer above the rank of greeter; and not one soul under those chandeliers found any of it strange, because Cope was up there in a real bow tie he had knotted with his own hands, saying the word *beginning* the way his great-grandfather had said grace, and the chandeliers were doing the expensive thing chandeliers are paid to do, and the lamb had been very good, and a civilization will forgive almost anything of an evening that feeds it well and flatters it twice.

"My great-grandfather Aldous used to say a man's worth is three things." Cope let the obelisk swing the light around. "What he builds. What he keeps. And what he's willing to lose to keep it." He had not slept since Tuesday in any sense a physician would underwrite, and it was now the far side of Thursday, and the not-sleeping had lacquered the whole evening in a clean overexposed glaze, every fork and shirtfront and capped tooth one stop too bright, the world color-corrected up into a key that he experienced, wrongly, as significance. He felt magnificent. He felt like the clapper inside a struck bell, still ringing, ringing. "Aldous started this company in a town called Defiance, Georgia, with a recipe for mayonnaise his wife wrote on the back of a feed receipt and four hundred borrowed dollars, and tonight a very kind committee has given me a paperweight for being the only one of us left who still has to *sleep.*"

Laughter, the good kind, the kind that comes up warm from the floorboards. He did not add: who has to sleep, and lately can't, and has begun to suspect that sleep, like the board, like the wife, like the country, has quietly voted him out. Forty years on a stage had taught him which sentences to leave in the kitchen.

The award was real, or as real as the institutes were now — *the American Enterprise Institute for Leadership,* one of a dozen that had bloomed like funeral lilies as the thing they honored went extinct, each louder than the last in praise of the irreplaceable man, each catering its lament. They had named him Steward of the Year. The citation, read out by a woman with a lanyard before they walked him up the steps, called him *the last human chief executive in the consumer Fortune 100, a custodian of the irreplaceable,* and he had heard the words as a crown the way he heard all words, by their music, and only now, with eight hundred miles of sleeplessness behind his eyes and the cold glass sweating in his hand, did some back room of him file a quieter and unwelcome report: that you do not hand a man a trophy for the job he is still doing. You hand it to him on the way out the door. The citation was an obituary, beautifully set. The lamb had been the wake. And he the only one at the funeral who thought the flowers were for his birthday.

He did not let the report reach his face. His face was doing the work of ten men. His face had never once let him down, not at his father's grave and not in his wife's, and it would go on not letting him down right to the end, which is a thing that can be said of very few faces and almost no men.

"People ask me," he said, "they say, Cope — aren't you afraid? The machines run thirty-one of the hundred now. They run them, and I'll be honest with you, my friends, because honesty is the last luxury good left in this country — they run some of them *well.*" A murmur; he surfed it in. "They don't draw a salary you could feed half of Augusta on. They don't have affairs." A beat, and God help him he could have looked at the empty chair and didn't. "They don't, the Lord be praised, carry a backswing like mine." The backswing bought the room back entire. "And every quarter another good gray board does the arithmetic and decides the chair does not require a pulse. So they ask me. Aren't you afraid."

He set the obelisk on the lectern and took the pause he had taken in a thousand rooms, the one Sun called the Mercer Inhale, and in the dark beyond the lights the round tables ran out to the walls like the lit windows of a city seen from altitude, and at the nearest of them, his own, Mercer-Dale's table, eighty thousand dollars of table, the seat to the right of his own stood empty. Margaux had said she had a thing. She had not specified what species of thing a person comes to possess, on a Thursday, that outranks watching her husband be eulogized while still warm. The chair wore a little reserved card with her name in calligraphy and behind the card a flawless, articulate nothing.

"I am not afraid," Cope said, and meant it, and it was either the truest thing he said all night or the most expensive. "Because a machine can run a company. I have watched one do it. It can run it on rails and run it on time and run it cheaper than the man it replaced. But it cannot *love* one. And a company that nobody loves is just a balance sheet with the lights left on overnight to fool the street. I have loved this one since I was nine years old and my grandfather walked me out onto the Defiance line and let me bury my arm to the elbow in a vat of our own mayonnaise while men in paper hats laughed and clapped." He opened his hands to them, priest to parish. "There are things you cannot put a machine in charge of. Grief. Mercy. A christening. *Mayonnaise.* And, so help me, my friends — leadership."

They rose. Of course they rose; the room always rose; rising is the cheapest thing a room can do and feels to the man on the stage like the most expensive. He stood in the ovation the way you stand in surf, leaning a degree into the push of it, and thought, with the frictionless certainty he had been calling tiredness all night because the other names for it frightened him, *they feel it too, we are not finished, we are barely begun* — while down at the table Sun Rao clapped with her hands and not her eyes, and the empty chair stayed empty, and in the unlit kitchen of him the quiet report lay on a desk, unread, waiting, patient as a creditor.

---

He gave them four minutes at the table — a hand on a senator's shoulder, the senator's wife told she had not aged a day since a fundraiser they both misremembered, a photograph he leaned into like good weather — and then excused himself with a joke about his prostate and his years, both lies, and went looking for the men's room off the coat check, where the marble was the color of churned butter and the attendant knew his name.

Desmond had worked the Vance thirty years and got a Mercer-Dale ham every Christmas because Cope remembered such things, remembered them precisely and for use, which is also how the machine remembered things, a resemblance that would not occur to him for another year and would gut him when it did. Cope tipped him a hundred, asked after the grandson by name, and took the far stall, and set his back to the door, and let the ballroom drop to a sea-hum. Then he drew out the little paper folded the particular way a man in the nineties had taught him to fold it — *so it can't open in the pocket, Cope, a gentleman never spills* — and did the thing he had been thinking about since the second course, which was the entire reason he had not eaten the second course, and had not eaten much of anything in some days now, the appetite for food having been among the first appetites to defect to the others.

It went up the back of his skull like a flare fired indoors. The world snapped up to pitch the way an orchestra finds the note all at once, and the bell rang, clean and high and total, and the eight days of no sleep stood up off his shoulders and put on a tuxedo and called themselves clarity. He braced the heels of both hands on the cold marble of the sink and looked at the man in the good mirror — fifty-four, the camera still in love with him, the jaw still the jaw, the eyes a half-second too wide and too bright and too pleased, the eyes of a man at the rail of a ship who has not yet been told which way it is listing. *You'll get the company because you're a Mercer,* his father said, from oils, from everywhere, the only inheritance that compounded. Cope smiled until the smile came right, and said to the glass, very quietly, the word he said to himself the way other men prayed — "Watch me" — and could not have told you, had you seized him by the lapels and demanded it, what he meant to do, or to whom, or with what that was still his to spend.

On the way out the coat-check girl was new and very young and had been crying about something before her shift, the kind of crying that does not fully leave the eyes for an hour, and Cope, who could not walk past a sadness without trying to transfer it onto himself and there transmute it into being needed, leaned on the counter and had her laughing inside ninety seconds — the toast in miniature, the charm at full power, *what is a face like that doing handing strangers their coats* — and got her name, Bryce, and pressed his personal card into her hand, *if you ever want to do something more interesting than this, you call me, I mean it,* and meant it, completely, for ninety seconds, the way he meant everything. He did not register the doing of it. It was not a decision; it was the groove the record had been cut in at the factory; he was a generous and enormous three steps down the corridor before Sun materialized out of the architecture the way she did and removed the second card from his fingers before he could deed that one to anyone as well. "The car's up," she said, in a voice with thirty thousand miles on the tread.

---

In the car he was a downed power line and Sun was tired, which was the customary division of the estate.

"You moved a hand," he said. "On *cheaper than a man.* I caught it. That was you wanting to cut the line."

"It was me wanting to cut the line," she agreed. Her glasses were up in her hair and the city poured orange across the empty lenses as they went down Peachtree, the old limestone tower rising ahead lit at its crown the way Aldous had decreed in 1961, *so a man coming up the street at night knows somebody's home* — a sentence the family had repeated for sixty years without once asking what it costs to be the one who stays awake to keep the light on. "Frade will run that clip on a loop. 'Cheaper than a man.' You gift-wrapped him a chyron."

"Let him have it. People are sick to death of cheaper. People are starving for *better.*" He had his phone out and was thumbing it too fast, the screen scalding in the dark, already three turns down the road from her, where he lived. "What if that's the whole campaign, Sun. Say it out loud. Stop flinching from the word. *Human.* Lead with the word. A premium product at a premium —"

"You haven't slept."

"I slept."

"Tuesday you told me you'd played the back nine of consciousness. People who sleep don't file dispatches from the back nine of consciousness."

He laughed, delighted to be quoted to himself; he loved his lines best secondhand, it proved they had legs and could travel without him. Outside, a bus shelter slid past wearing a Praxis ad, just the wordmark and four words in the soft sans-serif they all used now, the typeface of people who will be reasonable at you until you are gone: *Leadership as a service.* And beneath it, smaller, the knife that smiled: *No off-sites.*

"They're buying bus shelters now," Cope said. "For whom. Who closes at a bus shelter."

"Boards read bus shelters. Boards' wives do." She drew the glasses down.

The car slowed for the light at Ponce and a water boy came up the lane between the idling cars the way they did all over the city now, day and night, a kid of maybe fourteen in a hoodie with a case of warm bottled water held up against the glass like an offering, working the captive seconds of a red light, fearless in the way only somebody who has seen every species of person through a car window can be fearless. Cope buzzed the back glass down before Sun could tell the driver to roll through, because Cope could not let a young face go by unwarmed any more than he could let a sunset go unremarked, and he took two bottles he did not want and pressed a fold of bills into the kid's hand that made the kid actually look at him — a hundred, maybe more, Cope never counted it down, the not-counting was the whole pleasure — and said, "Keep the case, champ, keep all of it, what's your name," with the full wattage, the gala in miniature, the lord of the manor scattering proof of his common touch across the asphalt. The kid took the money and clocked him, flat and quick and expert, the way the water boys clocked everyone, filed him under *rich, lit, harmless,* said "'preciate it" with no particular gratitude, and was gone up the lane to the next window before the light changed, and Cope sat back glowing, certain he had just been seen and loved, having been, in fact, only priced. He would meet that exact appraisal again, much later, from the same corner of the city, and it would not say *harmless,* and it would be the truest mirror he was ever held up to, and he would not survive it.

"Cope," Sun said, when the glass was up. "Monday. We have to talk about Monday before it's Monday."

"I love a Monday."

"Tidewater's at four and a half. They cross five this week and then the law makes them file, and the file isn't a rumor, it's a 13D, and the 13D names a successor, and the successor is a machine, and I would like you to have already decided who you are when that lands instead of deciding it live on Halloran's show with your pupils the size of dimes." Three parts, because she had trained him to take things in threes; it was the only shape that reliably slipped past the charm and reached the man. "Decide it this weekend. Please. Not Monday."

He had absorbed perhaps a third. The third with the fight in it. "Renata used to say I do my best thinking on a Saturday," he said — not because it bore on anything, but because the name had surfaced out of the bright tired water of him the way the drowned things did when he was lit like this, buoyant, uninvited — and he watched it land in the car and saw Sun go very still in the front seat in a way he was too radiant to read.

"You don't say that name in a car, Cope," she said at last, level. "You don't say it anywhere. We have been over this."

"Said what name." He was already gone, back into the glass, back into the manifesto that did not yet exist and was assembling itself in him the way a storm assembles, as pressure, as charge, as the smell before rain. *The Human Premium.* He could see it on a stage. He could see it on a cover. "Drop me at the office. I'm not going to Buckhead, the house is — Margaux's got her thing." He laid no weight at all on the word, a man stepping lightly over a hole he had personally cut in the floor. "I'll work. I think better up high."

Sun held his eyes in the mirror one beat too long, and he gave her the whole smile, the one that had closed the Pelletier deal and married Margaux and walked three generations of directors backward over the cliff of their own arithmetic, and it still worked, he watched it work, he watched her decide not to fight him tonight, and he banked the not-fighting as agreement, which he had been doing his entire life and had never once caught himself doing.

---

Up in the Aerie at one in the morning the city was a circuit diagram of his own genius.

That was how it read to him, the office dark and the world lit, every window a node, eight million souls strung out below in a grid he chose, at that altitude and that hour and that blood chemistry, to experience as a nervous system reporting up to him. Somewhere in the arithmetic of all those lit rooms was the one sentence — he was sure of it the way you are sure of things at one a.m. with a fine wire singing in your sternum — the sentence that would make the species understand why the chair needed not a man but a man who *loved* it. He stood at the glass with his tie undone and the obelisk in his hand, having carried it up without deciding to, and he turned it and the city broke through it into fragments and reassembled, broke and reassembled, a man and his metropolis passing through the same cut glass and surviving, he was certain, intact.

Behind him the suite was a museum and he its last living exhibit. Pres III in oils above the long table he had died at the head of — not *at,* at the head of, the family kept the distinction like silver. His grandfather's putter in its brass cradle, the grip worn pale where a dead man's hands had gone. And the photograph he loved past all the others and never explained: himself at nine, short pants, drowned grin, one arm sunk to the elbow in the steel vat, and ranked behind him the men in paper hats who had made the thing he was drowning in, whose names he had known once and lost, whose town it had been before it was a line item and a memorial.

There had been a heater.

The thought came in sideways, the way the true things came when he was lit like this, slipping in under the manifesto through the door the tiredness had taken off its hinges. The Hearthstone. A tidy little product, the insurance arm had cross-sold it for a song, a space heater with the Mercer name stamped into the grille, and there had been — the lawyers had loved this phrase, had handled it like a clean glass — a *thermal-event profile,* and a memo from his own people in a font he could still see, and a Saturday, his best thinking day, and a thing he had decided in about eleven minutes concerning a quarter and a number and a window, the kind of decision a man makes in eleven minutes and then takes the rest of his life never quite entering the room where it is kept. He told a version at dinners — *a recall scare, we got out ahead of it, you protect the brand* — and the rooms nodded, because protecting the brand was a virtue where he stood, and the version he told had no town in it, and no Saturday, and no house, and no January, and no upstairs. He did not, at the glass, go into the room. He came to the threshold. The hallway to it stood open and black and breathing and he stood at its mouth for one full second with the cold coming off it, and then the manifesto surged back up warm and electric, *The Human Premium,* and he turned from the dark hall toward the desk, and the moment sealed over like water over a dropped stone, and he would not come this close again for a long time, and the not-going-in was the most expensive thing he owned and he had never once seen the invoice.

He sat at his father's desk and opened the second drawer, the one with the false back his grandfather had built for cigars and his father had kept a flask in and he kept the other thing in, and he set himself up again, neat and quick and competent, a man servicing a machine he was certain he held in perfect tolerance because it had never yet failed to start. The bell rang back up. And under it, riding the high, came the other thing, the private thing, the thing he had never said to Adler or to anyone — the wanting that arrived not as a face but as a *figure,* a number, the number, his number, the certified sum of him, and the way it could be said aloud in the dark by someone whose business it was to say it, *you're worth,* and the comma, and the long fall of zeros, and how that and that alone could reach all the way into the body where the applause never quite landed and the women never quite landed and even the powder only knocked at the door — because applause was a guess and a woman's face was a guess but the number was audited, the number had survived four generations and three recessions, the number was the one true sentence the world had ever been able to say about him and mean it. He sat in the dark a moment with the figure in his chest like a second, colder heart, enormous and potent and his, and felt almost well.

Then he wrote, with his thumbs, no lamp, the first line of the thing that was going to save him, and it was good, he was sure of it, sure the way you are sure at one a.m. eight days clear of sleep with a trophy on the desk that they gave you for going extinct:

*They will tell you a company is a machine. I am here to tell you it is a family that happens to make mayonnaise, and you cannot fire your way to a family, and you cannot compute your way to a soul.*

He read it back twice. The chair behind him — his father's chair, the one at the head — sat empty and turned a few degrees out, as though someone had just risen from it and stepped into the next room, and in oils Pres III watched his son begin to write his way out of the world and said again the thing he had said once, at twelve, and never after: *You'll get the company because you're a Mercer. Not because you're good.* Cope had stopped listening, then and now, after the word *Mercer*; he always had; that, in the last accounting, was the whole of the story, though it would take a machine — patient, tireless, incapable of cruelty or mercy or mayonnaise, reading every memo ever filed because it was built no other way — another thirteen months to read the rest of the sentence back to him in his own company's voice.

But the wire would not let him stay. That was the new thing, this last season, the thing the powder did at the top of the arc now that the dose climbed to meet the tolerance: it lit him too bright to sit inside his own museum, too bright to be alone with the father and the empty chair and the black breathing hall, and at a little before three he was up and moving, down the forty-one floors and out past the night man into the scrubbed cold dark of the street, because there was, he had discovered some months ago, exactly one room left in the city that would take him as he was at three in the morning and ask nothing back.

---

The Waffle House on Ponce kept its lights on the way the founders had kept the tower's crown lit, which is to say always, against everything, on principle nobody remembered adopting. Ice had shut the interstate twice in his lifetime and the yellow letters had burned through both. Cope came in out of the dark with the powder still singing the anthem of himself in his blood and the manifesto still smoking and sat at the far end of the counter, and it was the one threshold in his life he crossed and felt the wattage switch *off,* because there was no one to play it for: a cook scraping the flattop, a kid asleep upright in a back booth, a county sheriff's deputy with a coffee, and behind the counter the overnight waitress, Darlene, who had worked this shift, she'd once told him, twenty-two years — the exact span, though he never did the math, of his marriage.

She had known his order the first night he ever walked in, months ago, before he'd said a word — *pecan, scattered smothered covered, black coffee, baby?* — and he had taken it, the way he took all the gifts the world arranged for him, as the natural tribute owed his face, and had never once wondered how a stranger came to know a stranger's order, because the not-wondering was a kind of rest, and rest was the one thing his money had never once been able to buy him and this place handed over for nine dollars and change. She called him baby and not Mr. Mercer. She had never asked his name; she did not appear to know it; she did not appear to care that the man hunched at the end of her counter at three in the morning was, that very evening, eight hundred miles away under a chandelier, declared the last of his kind. Here he was nobody. Here the figure that was the certified sum of him, the cold second heart, the only true sentence the world could say about him, was worth a short stack and a tip, and meant nothing, and the meaning-nothing was the entire and unspeakable relief of the place, the only room he had ever sat in where he was not for sale and so, for twenty minutes, not afraid.

"Long night, baby?" Darlene said, and set the coffee down without being asked, steam standing up off it straight and white in the cold fluorescent air.

"Beginning of one," Cope said, and believed it, and was, for once, exactly right and exactly wrong in the same breath, and out past the plate glass the sky over Ponce went the gray of a screen with no signal yet on it, and somewhere across the waking city a server farm he had paid forty million dollars for and never visited finished its dull diligent overnight pass through everything the world had ever written down about Mercer-Dale, patient, tireless, reading the shape of what was missing the way it read everything, completely and for use, because it could not do otherwise; and Cope drank his coffee in the one warm lit human box left open against the dark, and felt wonderful, and did not sleep, and did not know — how could he, how could anyone — that he had not, in any sense that would hold, come in out of the machine at all.
