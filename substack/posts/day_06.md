# Day 6/30 — The Entropy Tax
*A fable from the field manual · Chapter 2*

On the third floor of MegaCorp, Sarah Chen's compliance system had been built in 2011 and never touched since — because touching it was nobody's job and breaking it was everybody's nightmare.

Every year it cost a little more. A few more contractors to babysit it. A few more overtime weekends when the filing window got tight. A few more good analysts who quit rather than spend another quarter as a human bridge between two systems that refused to speak.

Nobody saw the curve, because nobody added it up. The line item just said MAINTENANCE — flat, boring, the same word every year. And a flat word hid an exponential bill.

ZERO ran the real number one night with GHOST: maintenance, errors, turnover, regulatory exposure, compounding at seventeen percent a year while everyone upstairs admired the green dashboard.

"They think the status quo is free," she said.

*The status quo is a loan,* GHOST answered, *and nobody is making payments. The interest accrues in the dark. Software rots like a bridge, not like fruit — slowly, slowly, then all at once, on a Tuesday, in a headline.*

The next morning ZERO didn't pitch the future. She put a single sheet in front of the CIO: here is what doing nothing costs you today, and here is the same number in three years, and in five.

He stared at it a long time. "Why has nobody shown me this?"

"Because everyone else was selling you tomorrow," she said. "I'm showing you the fire under your chair."

---

**What it means.** The compounding cost of doing nothing. Software rots like a bridge, not like fruit — slowly, then catastrophically. The biggest lie in corporate finance is that the status quo is free. It isn't; it's a loan accruing interest in the dark while everyone admires the green dashboard.

## The equation

$$ E_{tax} = Maintenance \times e^{\lambda t} $$

Read the curve, not a point: 🟢 flat · 🟡 bending · 🔴 hockey stick.

**The moral of the story is:** Doing nothing is never free — it's a debt compounding behind a word like "maintenance." Open the bill, draw the curve, and the status quo stops looking like the safe choice.
