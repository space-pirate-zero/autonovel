# Day 17/30 — Wedge Velocity
*A fable from the field manual · Chapter 7*

ZERO didn't storm MegaCorp with a platform. She slipped in with a splinter of a thing, aimed at one bleeding neck.

The neck was Sarah's. Her team was missing the thirty-day filing window — three analysts gone, a hiring freeze, sixty-hour weeks. ZERO sat and watched the work and found that two and a half of every three and a half hours per report was pure copy-paste: a human being used as a USB cable.

So she built the ugliest tool of her life. A toolbar button. Default icon. No design. It did the two and a half hours and left the human the one hour that was actually human — the judgment. It ran on Sarah's own machine. No procurement. No architecture board. Nothing for the immune system to even see.

Friday, 9 a.m., she set it up in eleven minutes. *Point it. Paste the ID. Hit go. Check the red fields.*

Sarah ran one report in nineteen minutes instead of three and a half hours. She ran a second and timed it on her phone, like she was catching the universe in a lie.

At 5:15 that afternoon, Sarah Chen stood up, put on her coat, and walked out into the daylight for the first time in two years. She called her daughter from the parking deck.

That was the wedge. Not the platform. Not the roadmap. One exhausted person going home on time — and telling everyone who'd listen what gave her the evenings back.

---

**What it means.** You don't storm the fortress with a platform. You slip in with a shim that ends one specific person's misery today. If adoption needs procurement, legal, or IT permission, your wedge is blunt. Find the bleeding neck — the problem with a 90-day deadline that someone is losing sleep over.

## The equation

$$ V_w = \frac{Pain_{acute} \times Trust_{increment}}{Friction_{adopt}} $$

🟢 > 50 (viral) · 🟡 10–50 · 🔴 < 1 (it's a platform pretending to be a wedge).

**The moral of the story is:** Don't storm the fortress with a platform. Slip in a tiny tool that ends one person's specific misery today — acute pain times trust, over friction. A wedge that sends someone home at five spreads itself.
