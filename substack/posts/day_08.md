# Day 8/30 — Technical Debt Compound
*A fable from the field manual · Chapter 2*

Sarah Chen's team filed Suspicious Activity Reports for a living, and they were losing.

Last quarter the monitoring system threw 47,000 alerts. The team of twelve — now nine, after three quit — found problems faster than they could ever fix them. Every patch surfaced two new cracks. The backlog didn't shrink; it bred. They had passed the line where the water comes in faster than you can bail, and they all knew it, and none of them said it out loud.

ZERO sat beside an analyst for two hours and watched her drown in real time: copy, paste, retype the date because the format wouldn't take, alt-tab to a system built in 2009. The woman hadn't seen her kid's swim meet since March.

That night ZERO told GHOST she wanted to pitch the team a roadmap.

*Don't,* GHOST said. *You do not hand a drowning person a blueprint for a better pool. Hand them a bucket. They have stopped believing the water can go down. Give them one inch of proof, today, before you give them a vision.*

So she didn't pitch anything. She built one ugly tool that killed the worst two hours of the worst task, set it up at a single desk, and watched the analyst run a report in nineteen minutes instead of three and a half hours.

The woman didn't say thank you. She said, very quietly, "Where was this."

That was the inch. And once the water drops an inch, people will follow you all the way to the sea.

---

**What it means.** When this crosses 1.0, the team is underwater — finding faster than fixing. Don't pitch efficiency to a drowning person. Be the bucket, not the blueprint. The drowning don't want a vision; they want air, today.

## The equation

$$ D_{comp} = \frac{Bugs\_Found}{Bugs\_Fixed} $$

🟢 < 0.8 · 🟡 0.8–1.0 · 🔴 > 1.0 (underwater).

**The moral of the story is:** A team that finds problems faster than it fixes them has stopped believing things can get better. Don't sell the drowning a vision — hand them one bucket and prove the water can fall.
