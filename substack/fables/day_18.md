Three years before ZERO, there was Keisha.

Twenty-four years old, eight months at MegaCorp, brilliant in the way that sees a problem and builds the fix before the requirements doc is written. She found the compliance team burning six thousand hours a quarter on something a tool could do in an afternoon. So she built the tool, on her own time. It worked. It saved two million dollars a year.

They fired her for it. Not because it failed — because she'd shipped it without the Architecture Review Board, and the VP whose three-million-dollar vendor she'd just made obsolete filed the complaint. Security escort. A cardboard box. Badge killed before she reached her car.

BISHOP watched it through the conference-room glass and did nothing, and the doing-nothing was the worst thing he ever swallowed.

That night he didn't go home. He read the policy — all two hundred and forty-seven pages. By 4 a.m. he had twenty-three loopholes on a yellow pad: twenty-three places the rules said one thing and meant another. Then he reached up, loosened his badge until it hung crooked, fifteen degrees off true, and never straightened it again.

Keisha's mistake was never the tool. The tool was right. She built the right thing the *visible, unilateral, unprotected* way, and the immune system ate her at 4:40 on a Tuesday. BISHOP learned the lesson she paid for: don't break the rules — read them better than the people who wrote them, and build a case so airtight that punishing you costs more than promoting you.

Good trouble, John Lewis called it. Necessary trouble. But only the kind with receipts.

MORAL: Make good trouble when the impact is real and your intent is clean — but only the survivable kind. Don't break the rule; read it better than its authors, and keep the receipts.
