Two compliance vendors pitched MegaCorp in the same week.

The first had a glossy deck, a war chest, and a demo of an "AI-powered, fully autonomous" platform that, looked at closely, was a thin coat over the same model anyone could rent by the hour. Impressive. Substitutable. The kind of thing a competitor could clone by Friday.

The second was Sarah, standing up in a meeting she wasn't supposed to be in, holding a tool ZERO had built — ugly, narrow, and carrying a decade of Sarah's own hard-won judgment about which alerts were nothing and which were a federal case. You could feel the human in it. You could not rent it by the hour.

The first vendor had more money. The second had more trust — and trust, divided by how easily a machine can replace the judgment behind it, is the only currency that holds its value when everything else goes free.

ZERO thought of the mayoral race the whole Mesh still talked about — the candidate who didn't outspend the machine, he out-*realed* it. Clear message, relentless ground game, words that felt like a person instead of a focus group. Trust to the ceiling, substitutability on the floor. The machine had more money. He had the higher number. He won.

*You will not out-generate the generators,* GHOST said, watching the second vendor leave with the deal. *You out-real them. The fingerprint is the moat.*

MORAL: Trust issued by a human, divided by how easily a machine can fake that human's judgment, is the currency of the future. You don't beat the machine on volume — you beat it by being unmistakably, un-fakeably real.
