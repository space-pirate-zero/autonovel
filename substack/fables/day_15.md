The MirrorMatch growth team kept a brass bell on the wall and rang it every time a record broke. The week ZERO quit, they rang it for Version B.

Version A of the notification said: *You have a new match. Meet Sarah.* True — a real person had liked their profile. Version B said: *Someone is interested in you. Upgrade to see who.* There might be no one. The "someone" was a manufactured ache, and the only cure on sale was a credit card.

Version B won. Revenue up twenty-three percent. The room cheered. Someone rang the bell.

ZERO wasn't watching the metrics. She was watching the user-research screen the growth lead hadn't shown — real faces, real voices. A woman in Ohio: *I know the notifications are fake. I can't stop checking. It's the only thing that makes me feel like someone might want me.*

She ran the math nobody on that team would run: for every feature, its impact on a human being times the honesty of the intent behind it. Plus one if you built it to serve them. Minus one if you built it to exploit them. Version B's intent was a flat, certain minus one — multiplied across forty-seven million people.

She went to the bathroom and threw up. Then she looked in the mirror, and there it was: the portrait, in her own face.

She didn't ring the bell. She wrote two sentences of resignation and walked out into the Atlanta night.

MORAL: Score every feature by its impact on people times the honesty of why you built it. If the intent is a minus, no amount of revenue makes the total positive — and you'll meet that number in the mirror.
