# Characters — *The Last Human CEO*

Close third-person, single POV. We live inside Cope's head — his
grandiosity, his charm, his rot — and we watch reality leak in through
the seams. The comedy is the gap between his narration and the facts;
the tragedy is that he half-knows.

---

## Prescott "Cope" Mercer IV (POV — protagonist)

- **Role:** Fourth-generation Chairman & CEO of Mercer-Dale. The last
  human CEO in the consumer Fortune 100. 54.
- **The nickname:** "Prescott" → "Coppy" (nanny, age 3) → "Cope." He is
  named for coping and cannot. He insists everyone use it; it reads as
  folksy populism and is in fact a brand.
- **Surface:** Tall, golf-tanned, genuinely charming, quotes his
  great-grandfather, gives a good toast, remembers your kids' names
  (Sun briefs him on the cards in the elevator). The last guy in the
  building who still wears a tie. A man of appetites he has spent thirty
  years rebranding as charisma.
- **The appetites (central, not color):** Cope is a high-functioning
  **cocaine addict** of long standing — started as a Wall Street thing in
  the '90s, never a problem because it was never *not* working, the way
  the family money was never a problem because it was never not there. He
  is also a **serial philanderer**, specifically toward women who work for
  him or want something from the name — a compulsion he experiences as
  warmth, generosity, "really seeing people." Both appetites are engines
  of the same machine: a man who cannot sit alone with himself and has
  the resources to never have to. The coke fuels the toast, the toast
  fuels the affairs, the affairs feed the need to be adored, and the
  whole flywheel has been quietly destroying value and people for
  decades while the stock went up enough to forgive it.
- **The brutal irony this sharpens:** He is being replaced by a thing
  with *no* appetites — Marcus does not get high, does not get hard, does
  not get bored, does not need to be loved at 2 a.m. by someone it
  outranks. Every essay argument (no affairs, no conflicts, logs
  everything, can't lie) is, for Cope specifically, a personalized
  indictment. The machine isn't generically "more ethical." It is more
  ethical *than him,* itemized.
- **Three sliders:** Proactivity HIGH (he never stops *doing* — the
  tragedy is the doing is noise). Likability MEDIUM and falling (charming
  in a room, ruinous in a marriage). Competence LOW-to-MEDIUM and, the
  reader slowly realizes, always was — he inherited the chair and
  mistook the inheritance for a verdict on his ability.
- **Wound:** His father, Pres III, told him at twelve, "You'll get the
  company because you're a Mercer, not because you're good. Try to be
  good anyway." He has spent forty years failing to hear the second
  sentence.
- **Want (conscious):** To beat the machine. To prove a human still
  belongs in the chair. To be *necessary.*
- **Need (unconscious):** To be loved for something other than the name —
  and to admit that the machine is right about him specifically, which
  is the one thing his ego cannot survive.
- **The Lie he believes:** "Leadership is a soul thing. A machine can't
  feel the room. I feel the room. Therefore I am irreplaceable." (Every
  plot beat tests this against the fact that the machine reads the room
  better than he does — it just doesn't *care* what it reads.)
- **Arc (NEGATIVE / tragic, with one true clean act at the end):**
  Denial → mania → grandiose counter-campaign → public humiliation →
  paranoid collapse → the surfacing of the buried thing → a final
  choice that is genuinely good *and* genuinely too late. He does not
  get the company back. He gets one true thing instead, and it costs
  him everything, and it isn't enough, and it's still worth it.
- **Mania, clinically grounded — and chemically lit:** Across the book he
  tips from high-functioning use into a genuine manic spiral. The
  cocaine and an underlying mood disorder feed each other; Dr. Adler
  names both and can untangle neither. Grandiosity, no sleep, spending, a
  religious certainty about his "Human Premium" thesis, pressured speech,
  a messianic founder-energy the startup world would have called
  visionary in a younger, poorer man. The reader should feel the
  ambiguity Cope refuses to: is this a prophet, a manic, or just a man
  three grams into a sleepless week? All three. Played for dark comedy
  *and* real fear. He is not "crazy." He is a man whose delusion (I am
  necessary) is being surgically removed by the market, with a drug that
  whispers the opposite directly into his blood.
- **Secret #1 (the Easterbrook — but worse, because it's a pattern):**
  Not one affair — a *career* of them with subordinates. The named,
  litigated one is **Renata Cho** (2019), settled quietly and concealed
  from the board; the *concealment* is the firing offense, exactly the
  essay's Part 3 logic. But Renata is the one that left a paper trail;
  there are others, an HR pattern the company has spent a decade
  "managing." The cocaine lives in this same buried file: a 2018 incident
  (a hotel, a security report, a favor called in) that never reached the
  board. The machine, reading every memo because it cannot do otherwise,
  will find the whole drawer.
- **Secret #2 (the deeper one, the McMullen/recall):** In 2021, to
  protect a quarter, he personally slow-walked a recall of a Mercer-Dale
  space heater (the *Hearthstone*) with a known ignition defect. Two
  people died in a house fire in Defiance. It was buried in a settlement
  and a "voluntary service bulletin." This is the thing the
  hyper-ethical machine, doing routine due diligence, will surface — not
  out of malice, but because it cannot *not.*
- **Speech pattern:** Toastmaster cadence. Triads ("grit, gratitude, and
  guts"). Sports and sailing metaphors. Calls people "champ" and "my
  friend." Under stress the polish cracks into clipped, biblical
  fragments. Quotes his great-grandfather, often invented on the spot.
- **Foreshadowing planted:** Ch1 (the bathroom key, the "not sleeping
  since Tuesday," the too-bright bell-struck feeling = the coke, read by
  the reader before it's named) → payoff Ch ~17/20. Ch1 (the heater
  anecdote told as folksy charm) → payoff Ch ~20 (the recall). Ch2
  (Renata's name on an old org chart; the "managed" HR drawer) → payoff
  Ch ~16. The 2018 hotel incident → surfaced by Marcus's due diligence
  alongside the recall (Ch ~20).

## Marcus (the AURELIUS — antagonist-by-existence, never a villain)

- **Role:** The AURELIUS-class system Tidewater's proxy campaign seeks
  to install as Mercer-Dale CEO; named "Marcus" by the board's
  nominating committee (for Marcus Aurelius — a stoic philosopher-king
  to replace a tantruming heir; nobody on the committee gets the joke).
- **What it is:** Not a character with wants. A flawless, transparent,
  tireless mirror. Its dialogue is its board-portal rationales and its
  drafted communications — uncanny in their competence and their total
  absence of need. It writes the most humane layoff letter anyone at
  Mercer-Dale has ever read. That is the horror.
- **Function in the arc:** Every time Cope swings at it, it isn't there.
  It has no face to bloody. It cannot be charmed, threatened, shamed, or
  loved. It is the wall reality uses to break him — and, in the end, the
  instrument that does the one merciful, terrible, honest thing he could
  never do himself: tells the truth about the Hearthstone.
- **Secret (structural):** Marcus is clean, but the *charter* that aims
  it was written by humans with motives. The book's turn: the machine
  isn't the danger. The danger is who gets to write its objective — and
  that's still us.

## Sunita "Sun" Rao (non-POV — chief of staff; the competent one)

- **Role:** Cope's chief of staff, 39. The person who actually runs
  Mercer-Dale day to day and has for years. Brilliant, exhausted,
  decent, ambitious.
- **Arc:** From loyal enabler → to the woman quietly negotiating to
  become the "Accountable Officer" who co-signs *for* Marcus — i.e.,
  the human who survives by serving the machine. She is not a traitor;
  she is the future. Her arc is the book's argument that competence,
  unlike Cope, *adapts.* Her tragedy is smaller and realer than his:
  she gets the title and loses the meaning.
- **Secret:** She drafted the recall memo in 2021 that Cope overrode.
  She kept a copy. She has spent six years deciding whether she is a
  witness or an accomplice.
- **Speech:** Precise, dry, briefs in threes because she trained him to
  receive threes. Loves him and is done with him in the same sentence.

## Margaux Mercer (non-POV — wife, separating)

- **Role:** Cope's wife of 22 years, 51. A sculptor who gave it up. The
  one person immune to the toast.
- **Arc:** Leaving him across the book — not over any single woman, and
  not even over the coke, but over the fact that there was never anyone
  home behind the charm. The affairs and the using are symptoms; the
  absence is the disease. Her departure is the human verdict that rhymes
  with the market's: she, like the board, finally does the arithmetic.
- **Secret:** She has known about the women for years — counted them,
  even, the way you count anything to survive it — and stayed for Pip and
  for the name and for a version of him that stopped existing around 2015.
  She has flushed his stashes and refilled her own prescriptions and
  called it a marriage. The staying is its own indictment.
- **Speech:** Spare, devastating, allergic to his triads. The only
  character who can land a line he can't toast over.

## Penelope "Pip" Mercer (non-POV — estranged daughter)

- **Role:** Cope and Margaux's daughter, 24. An AI-safety researcher —
  works, with maximum irony, on alignment and corporate-AI governance.
  Thinks her father is a dinosaur; loves him with the specific anger of
  someone who can't stop.
- **Arc:** From contempt → to the one person who tries to actually save
  him (not the company — him) → to the witness of his last clean act.
  She is the reader's proxy: she knows the machine is right *and* that
  something is being lost, and she can't reconcile it either.
- **Secret:** She consulted, briefly, for Praxis. She helped build the
  thing replacing her father. She hasn't told him.
- **Speech:** Fast, technical, funny, merciless, then suddenly young.

## Gus Frade (non-POV — activist investor)

- **Role:** Founder of Tidewater Capital, 60s. The Ackman/Icahn engine
  of the proxy fight. Charming predator. Believes his own reform pitch
  about 40%.
- **Arc:** Flat antagonist; the weather. Wins. The point is that he's
  not even the villain — he's just early.
- **Speech:** Folksy menace, CNBC-ready, weaponized reasonableness.

## Lorne Tisch (non-POV — the "humanity consultant," comic relief w/ teeth)

- **Role:** A high-priced executive guru Cope hires mid-mania to help him
  "out-human the machine" — quantify and weaponize his soul. A quack and
  a mirror.
- **Function:** The comedy's sharpest instrument. Everything Tisch sells
  Cope to *prove his humanity* is itself a machine-like optimization of
  authenticity. The satire eats its own tail. Late in the book Tisch
  pivots to consulting *for* Praxis on "warmth modules." Of course he
  does.
- **Speech:** TED-talk shaman. "Vulnerability is the new alpha, Cope."

## Dr. Adler (non-POV — psychiatrist)

- **Role:** Cope's reluctant psychiatrist, brought in by Margaux then
  Pip. The book's one honest mirror who isn't trying to sell or replace
  him. Names both things — the mood disorder *and* the cocaine — and
  refuses to let Cope use either as an excuse for the other. Cannot stop
  the spiral.
- **Function:** The clinical floor under the comedy. Keeps the manic
  decline real, not whimsical; insists, against the book's own
  temptation, that addiction is not a metaphor.

## Renata Cho (non-POV — the named settlement; mostly offstage)

- **Role:** Former Mercer-Dale VP. The 2019 affair, the NDA, the buried
  HR matter — the one of Cope's pattern that left litigable paper.
  Returns once, with devastating ordinariness, to refuse to be Cope's
  redemption prop, and to make plain she was neither a love story nor a
  scandal but an employee whose career he spent. Not bitter. Just done.

## Relationship map (Act 1 → Act 2 → Act 3)

- **Cope ↔ Marcus:** rival he can't touch → obsession → the thing that
  tells his truth for him.
- **Cope ↔ Sun:** master/servant → he leans on her as the walls close →
  she becomes the machine's human, and he has to let her.
- **Cope ↔ Margaux:** estranged-but-married → she leaves → she is the
  only one at the end who isn't surprised by who he turns out to be.
- **Cope ↔ Pip:** contempt both ways → she tries to save the man not the
  king → she witnesses the clean act and it doesn't fix anything and it
  matters.
- **Cope ↔ Frade/Praxis:** he thinks they're the enemy → he learns the
  enemy is arithmetic, and arithmetic was always going to win.
