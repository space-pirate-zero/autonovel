# Outline — *The Last Human CEO*

## Structure

- **Form:** Literary tragic dark comedy. Corporate satire with a real
  heart. Think *DeLillo's* dread + *Shteyngart's* comedy + the moral
  weight of a guy who actually did something unforgivable.
- **Length target:** ~88,000 words, **28 chapters**, three acts +
  coda.
- **POV/Tense:** Close third on Cope Mercer, past tense, free indirect
  discourse. We are trapped in his self-justification; the facts arrive
  sideways.
- **Time span:** Aug 2027 → Spring 2029. Marked by proxy season, earnings
  calls, a Sun Valley conference, a Delaware trial.
- **Save the Cat spine** (percent marks approximate):
  - Opening Image (1%): Cope toasting himself at a gala for "the Last
    Human CEO," not getting that the award is an obituary.
  - Theme Stated (5%): Pip — "Dad, you don't think a person should run
    it. You think *you* should run it. Those aren't the same."
  - Catalyst (10%): Tidewater files its 13D; the proxy campaign to
    install Marcus begins.
  - Debate (10–20%): fight it or spin it? Cope chooses to *out-human*
    the machine.
  - Break into Two (20%): hires Lorne Tisch; launches the "Human
    Premium" campaign. Mania ignites.
  - B-Story (Pip & Margaux; the human verdict tracking the market's).
  - Fun & Games (20–50%): the campaign, the absurdity, early "wins,"
    escalating sleeplessness — the promise of the premise.
  - Midpoint (50%): a false victory (a shareholder vote delay / a viral
    moment) that is actually the beginning of the end; Marcus is
    installed as "co-CEO/advisor" anyway. Stakes go personal.
  - Bad Guys Close In (50–75%): Renata surfaces; Margaux leaves; Sun
    starts negotiating with the other side; the mania peaks.
  - All Is Lost (75%): the Hearthstone recall — Marcus's routine due
    diligence surfaces the 2021 cover-up. The two dead in Defiance.
  - Dark Night of the Soul (75–85%): collapse, hospital, the truth of
    what he is.
  - Break into Three (85%): the one clean choice left.
  - Finale (85–99%): he does the genuinely good, genuinely too-late
    thing — and loses everything anyway.
  - Final Image (100%): mirror of the opening; the chair, empty or
    filled, and who's standing where.

## Themes (genuine questions, no easy answers)

1. If the machine is cheaper, better, *and* more ethical — what is the
   human *for*? Is there a value to human leadership that survives the
   loss of every measurable reason for it?
2. When we mourn "the human touch," are we mourning something real — or
   mourning men like Cope mourning their thrones?
3. The machine is clean. The people who aim it are not. Did we automate
   away corruption, or just relocate it to whoever writes the charter?
4. Can a bad man do one good thing, too late, and have it count?

## Act 1: The Last Human (Ch 1–8) — Denial & the Catalyst

### Ch 1: "The Premium Blend" (Cope, ~3,200)
- BEATS: Opening image — the "Last Human CEO" tribute gala. Cope toasts,
  charms, keys a bump in the men's room, doesn't sleep, doesn't see the
  obituary inside the award. We meet the voice, the chemically-lit
  "mania," Mercer-Dale's decline, and the appetites — without ever
  naming them as a problem, because Cope doesn't.
- PLANTS: the bathroom key / the bell-struck too-bright feeling (= the
  coke, read by the reader before it's named); a coat-check flirtation
  that's clearly a worn groove, not a one-off; the Hearthstone told as a
  folksy anecdote; the insomnia; great-grandfather "quotes"; the empty
  seat at his table (Margaux).
- EMOTIONAL ARC: triumphant → first hairline crack.
- STATUS: drafted (v2 — appetites seeded)

### Ch 2: "Key-Man Risk" (Cope, ~3,000)
- BEATS: Monday in the Tower; Sun runs everything; board chair signals
  the 13D is coming; Cope's bravado vs. the numbers. Renata's name
  glimpsed on an archived org chart. The literal phrase "key-man risk" —
  the board's polite term for a man who could blow up the company by
  being himself — lands on Cope as a compliment; the reader hears the
  diagnosis. Sun manages a small live version of the problem (a young
  comms hire Cope is too warm with) with weary professionalism.
- PLANTS: Renata Cho; the "managed" HR drawer; the 2018 hotel favor
  (alluded to via a security-firm invoice Sun reroutes); Sun's old recall
  memo (mentioned, not opened).
- STATUS: unwritten

### Ch 3: "Thirteen-D" (Cope, ~3,000)
- BEATS: CATALYST — Tidewater files; Frade on CNBC calling Cope "a
  beloved liability." The word *liability* lands like a diagnosis.
- STATUS: unwritten

### Ch 4: "A Steward, Not a Sovereign" (Cope, ~3,000)
- BEATS: the Praxis/Tidewater pitch to the board; Marcus demonstrated;
  Marcus drafts a layoff letter kinder than any Cope ever signed.
- THEME beat: the humane-machine horror.
- STATUS: unwritten

### Ch 5: "Theme Stated" (Cope, ~2,800)
- BEATS: Pip confronts him; the line. Margaux's separate bedroom made
  literal. DEBATE: fight or spin.
- STATUS: unwritten

### Ch 6: "The Human Premium" (Cope, ~3,000)
- BEATS: BREAK INTO TWO. Cope decides to out-human the machine. Hires
  Lorne Tisch. The campaign is born. Mania ignites (no sleep, big spend).
- STATUS: unwritten

### Ch 7: "Authenticity, Quantified" (Cope, ~2,800)
- BEATS: Tisch's methods — optimizing Cope's soul; the satire's thesis
  scene. First "win": a town-hall clip goes viral.
- STATUS: unwritten

### Ch 8: "Defiance" (Cope, ~3,200)
- BEATS: a campaign stop at the founding town/closed plant. The reader
  sees the Hearthstone ground he's standing on without knowing it yet.
- PLANTS: the burned house; a name on a memorial.
- STATUS: unwritten

## Act 2: The Human Premium (Ch 9–20) — Mania, False Victory, the Walls

### Ch 9–11: Fun & Games
- The campaign rises. Magazine cover ("THE LAST HUMAN"). Cope is using
  hard now — reframing it to himself as discipline, as edge, as the cost
  of carrying the human banner — sleeps in the Aerie, spends, and the
  affairs accelerate with the adrenaline (a campaign staffer, a
  journalist embedded on the profile). Comic set-pieces with a cold
  undertow: the more he performs "the soul in the chair," the more
  appetite-driven and machine-like-in-his-compulsions he actually is.
  Small real wins the reader can feel are hollow. Margaux starts the
  paperwork.

### Ch 12: "Midpoint — The Delay" (~3,200)
- BEATS: FALSE VICTORY. He wins a vote delay / a viral redemption
  moment. Crows. But the board's term sheet installs Marcus as
  "co-pilot/advisor" anyway. The machine is *in the building.* Stakes go
  personal: from "save the company" to "save myself."

### Ch 13–15: Bad Guys Close In (I)
- Sun begins quietly negotiating to be Marcus's Accountable Officer.
  Margaux's leaving becomes real. Mania peaks: a grandiose all-hands
  "Human Premium Manifesto." Then THE SCANDAL BREAKS: during the proxy
  fight, a story lands — an embedded journalist's piece, or a leak Frade
  doesn't even have to pay for — alleging a current affair with a
  subordinate and, separately, the drugs. The Last Human's whole campaign
  about the irreplaceable human soul is detonated by the most human thing
  imaginable. The dark-comic peak: pundits note that the *machine* would
  never have done this; Praxis's stock-photo serenity vs. Cope's
  TMZ-grade humanity. Try-fail: *no, and* — his denial makes it worse.

### Ch 16: "Renata" (~3,000)
- BEATS: with the press already circling, the 2019 settlement gets pulled
  into the light — and Renata herself surfaces, not via the machine, via
  a human, ordinary and merciless. She refuses to be a love story, a
  scandal, or his redemption prop. The *concealment* from the board, not
  the affair, is the governance wound — exactly the essay's Part 3 point.
  Cope tries to spin it as a witch hunt against "a flawed human man" and
  it curdles in his mouth.

### Ch 17–19: Bad Guys Close In (II)
- Dr. Adler names the mania (Ch 17). Cope's counter-attacks all slide off
  Marcus — he keeps swinging at a wall (Ch 18). Pip reveals she
  consulted for Praxis; the betrayal that's actually love (Ch 19).

### Ch 20: "All Is Lost — The Hearthstone" (~3,400)
- BEATS: Marcus, in routine pre-handover due diligence, opens the whole
  drawer at once — the 2018 hotel security report, the chain of "managed"
  HR settlements, and, underneath them, the 2021 recall cover-up: two
  dead in Defiance. It cannot *not* report it; it has no instinct for
  mercy or self-preservation to make it look away. The sex and drugs are
  the scandal; the dead are the crime. The machine destroys him not with
  malice but with honesty — the exact ethic the board paid for. PAYOFF of
  Ch 1, Ch 2, Ch 8 plants. The reader feels the awful symmetry: every
  appetite he hid was a rehearsal for the bigger thing he hid.

## Act 3: Accountable Officer (Ch 21–28 + Coda) — Collapse & the Clean Act

### Ch 21: "Dark Night" (~2,800)
- Collapse. Hospital / 72 hours. The mania's floor drops out. He sees,
  for one clear hour, exactly what he is.

### Ch 22–23: the reckoning
- The board moves to settle and install Marcus fully; Sun gets the
  Accountable Officer title (her small true tragedy); the legal exposure
  for the Hearthstone lands on a human — and a machine cannot go to
  prison. Only a person can.

### Ch 24: "Break into Three" (~2,600)
- The one clean choice: Cope can let the settlement bury Defiance again
  (and possibly save himself), or he can do the thing he never did —
  tell the whole truth, take the accountability personally, on the
  record, no NDA.

### Ch 25–26: Finale
- He does it. A deposition / public statement / Delaware testimony where,
  for once, he does not toast over the truth. It is genuinely good. It is
  far too late. It does not save the company, the marriage, or the chair.
  Frade wins. Marcus is installed. The town gets an apology and a
  fund — and its two dead stay dead.

### Ch 27: "Accountable Officer" (~2,600)
- The handover. Cope, briefly, is offered the Accountable-Officer
  liability-sponge role and refuses it; Sun takes it. The machine writes
  him a farewell letter kinder than he deserves and he reads it alone in
  the Aerie.

### Ch 28: "Final Image" (~2,400)
- Mirror of Ch 1. A room, a chair, a toast — but now Cope is in the
  audience, sober in every sense, watching the future be introduced.
  Pip is the only one who looks at him. One small, true, unresolved
  human beat. No redemption arc closed clean. The Stability Trap
  refused: he ends *truly different*, *truly diminished*, and the loss
  stays lost.

### Coda (~1,500)
- Defiance, a year on. The memorial, the fund, an ordinary afternoon.
  The machine runs Mercer-Dale well. Better, even. And something the
  book has refused to name as either sentimental or worthless is just…
  absent. The final question handed to the reader, not answered.

## MICE threads (close in reverse order)

- **(M) Milieu:** the wave of automation (opens Ch1, closes Coda).
- **(I) Inquiry:** what is a human leader *for*? (opens Ch5 theme, the
  reader's question, closes unanswered in Coda).
- **(C) Character:** Cope's arc denial→truth (opens Ch1, closes Ch28).
- **(E) Event:** the proxy fight / who runs Mercer-Dale (opens Ch3,
  resolves Ch26 — Frade/Marcus win).
- Close order: E (26) → C (28) → I/M (Coda). Innermost-opened closes
  last; the human question outlives the plot.

## Try-Fail cycle plan (sample)

- Ch 6–7: out-human the machine — *yes, but* the win is hollow/viral-only.
- Ch 12: win the delay — *yes, but* Marcus gets installed anyway.
- Ch 18: attack Marcus directly — *no, and* there's nothing to hit; he
  only damages himself.
- Ch 24–26: tell the truth — *yes, and* it's right — *but* it saves
  nothing. The only un-hollow "yes" in the book is also the most costly.
