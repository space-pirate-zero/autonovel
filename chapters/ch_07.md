# Chapter 7 — Authenticity, Quantified

> TRANSMISSION 07 — "Optimizing the Soul"
>
> Here is the joke the whole machine age is built on, and our man is about to live inside it: to prove he is irreplaceably human, he hires a consultant to *measure* his humanity, A/B-test his vulnerability, and ship his authenticity on a content calendar. The clip goes viral. The soul goes to market. He has never been more like the thing he's fighting than in the moment he performs being unlike it. Next stop: the tail eats the dog.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

Lorne Tisch did not sit in chairs. He arranged himself in them, the way a man arranges a scarf, and on the second morning of what he was calling the Work he arranged himself across from Cope in the Aerie with one bare ankle over one linen knee and said, "I want to start by killing a word."

"Kill it," Cope said. He had not slept, in the new way, the productive way, the way he had decided to call *running ahead of the boat.* He felt terrific. He felt like the first ten minutes of a flight.

"*Authentic,*" Tisch said, and let it lie there on the rug between them like something he'd shot. "Everyone says be authentic. Be authentic. As if authenticity is a *state.* It's not a state, Cope. It's a *signal.* And a signal that doesn't reach the receiver is just a man being sincere in an empty room, which —" he opened his hands, generous, awful — "is the saddest sound in nature."

Cope wrote *signal not state* on the legal pad, which he had started keeping because Tisch said writing by hand "re-membered the body," and the pad already had four pages in it, in the big looping hand of a man who had not slept, and the looping felt to him like proof of something.

Tisch cost two hundred thousand dollars a month plus the apartment plus the man who carried the apartment's things. He wore a henley that cost more than the suit Cope had been married in. He had been, in order, a Jesuit, a Goldman analyst, a breathwork facilitator to two streaming-platform founders, and now this, the thing he had named — refusing every word Cope offered for it — Human Performance Architecture. He had a way of saying *Cope* that made Cope feel located. Sun had run the diligence and put it on his desk in a folder marked, in her hand, *with my whole professional objection noted,* and Cope had not opened the folder, and that, too, felt like proof of something.

"The premise of the campaign is correct," Tisch said. "Spiritually correct. You are the soul in the chair. The machine has no soul. Beautiful. But correctness is *content,* Cope, and content has to be *produced,* and right now you are producing your soul the way a — forgive me — the way a *small business* produces, which is artisanally, one feeling at a time, no inventory, no calendar. We are going to industrialize it."

"Industrialize the soul," Cope said, and laughed, because it was funny, and Tisch did not laugh, which made it stop being funny and start being a plan.

---

They built, that week, in the room off the Aerie where the family kept the boardroom that was older than the boardroom, a thing Tisch called the Vulnerability Matrix.

It was a whiteboard. On the left, a column of what Tisch called *Sanctioned Wounds* — areas of Cope's life pre-cleared by legal and by Tisch's own instrument, a forty-page questionnaire Cope had filled out at two in the morning feeling, the whole time, profoundly known. His father. His backswing. The fear, sketched lightly, of being the last of something. A nice clean grief: his grandfather, the Defiance plant, the arm in the vat, the men in paper hats. That one tested, Tisch said, *off the charts.*

On the right, a column Tisch labeled, without apparent irony, *Unsanctioned.* It was blank. It would stay blank. Renata was not on the board, in either sense. The Saturday was not on the board. The drawer with the false back was not on the board, though Cope had gone into it twice during the questionnaire, neat and quick, a man maintaining a machine he had under perfect control. The board held only the wounds that could be shown, and the showing of them was the product, and the product was *him,* and somewhere in the back room of him a quiet clerk noted that he had never in his life so carefully decided which parts of himself to feel.

"Now," said Tisch. "We test."

They tested everything. This was the part Cope would not, later, be able to explain to Margaux, the few times he tried, because there was no version of it that did not sound exactly like the thing he was spending two hundred thousand dollars a month to prove he was not. They filmed him telling the grandfather story two ways. In the first he cried, a little, a real catch in the throat at *paper hats,* and Tisch's young people — there were four of them now, with rings of color around their eyes from the rooms they didn't sleep in either — pushed it to a focus panel of eleven hundred people who did not know it was him, and it scored a 71 on a metric Tisch called Felt Realness.

In the second he did not cry. He held it. He got to *paper hats* and looked away from the camera, out a window that wasn't there, and let the not-crying do the work.

That scored an 83.

"You see it," Tisch breathed. He was looking at the number the way Cope's grandfather had looked at the vat. "The withheld tear, Cope. The withheld tear out-performs the tear by *twelve points.* Restraint reads as depth. We don't *cry.* We *almost* cry. Almost is the whole game. Almost is where they lean in."

"So I should — what. Rehearse almost crying."

"You should *find* the almost," Tisch said, wounded by the word *rehearse,* "and then *protect* it. We're not manufacturing the feeling, Cope. The feeling is real. We're *optimizing its delivery.* There's a difference, and it's a sacred one."

Cope wrote *find the almost* on the pad. The looping hand. He believed there was a difference. He believed it the way he believed everything, completely, for as long as the saying of it lasted, and the believing felt, that week, like the cleanest thing in him.

---

By Thursday they had a calendar.

It was a real calendar, in the software the comms team used for product launches, the launches of the mayonnaise and the household chemicals and the streaming service nobody wanted, and now, in the same grid, in the same soft cells, Cope's interior life had been laid out in a quarter: a *moment of doubt* on the fourteenth, a *flash of unscripted warmth* on the twenty-first, a *hard truth, delivered with love* timed for the morning of the proxy advisory firm's preliminary recommendation, because Tisch said grief and governance moved the same dial in the limbic system and you might as well stack them.

Margaux called it, when she heard, *putting your feelings on a Gantt chart,* and hung up before he could toast over it.

He did not hear the cruelty of the cell that said, in nine-point type, *Spontaneity (Town Hall) — rehearse Tues, deploy Wed.* He saw a busy man getting organized. He had spent his whole life being told he was undisciplined — by his father, by Sun in the gentler dialect she used for it, by the soft sans-serif on the bus shelters that did not have to say it — and here at last was discipline, here was a structure, here was the soul on rails, run on time, run cheaper than grief, and it did not once occur to him, not on Thursday, that he had become the exact thing the proxy deck was selling. He was building, in the room off the Aerie, with four sleepless children and a man in a henley, a small artisanal Praxis unit, and its only product was the proof that no Praxis unit could ever make it.

The tail had the dog by the throat. The dog was delighted. The dog thought it was winning.

---

The town hall was Wednesday, at the plant in Camden that still ran, the one they hadn't closed, four hundred people on folding chairs under the fluorescents and the safety posters, and a question planted in the third row — *planted* was Tisch's word, said with no shame at all — from a line worker named DeShawn who had been told only that the Chairman wanted to be asked something real.

What DeShawn asked, off the card Tisch's people had not written quite carefully enough, was: "Mr. Mercer, are you scared?"

And Cope, who had rehearsed the *almost* on Tuesday in a mirror with Tisch counting the beats — *one, two, look away, three* — opened his mouth to deliver the optimized version and discovered, with the bright total clarity of a man four days past sleep and one bump into the morning, that he could not remember the beats. The room was warm. The fluorescents were too bright in the new productive way. DeShawn was looking at him the way the men in the paper hats looked out of the photograph, and Cope felt the floor of the plan go out from under him, and what came up through the hole was not the optimized thing and not nothing.

"Yeah," Cope said. "Yeah, I'm scared." His voice did something he had not scheduled. "I'm fifty-four years old and I'm good at exactly one thing, which is this, this exact thing, standing in a room with people who make something real, and a — a *machine* — does the standing better now. Reads you better. Wants nothing from you, which it turns out is the thing people want most from a man in a chair, that he wants nothing back." He had no idea where the sentences were coming from; they were arriving the way the manifesto arrived, sideways, out of the bright water. "My great-grandfather started this in a town I'm afraid to go back to. And I don't know if I'm the man to save the — I don't know if I'm the man. But I'd rather be a scared man in this chair than a calm machine in it. Because the machine's never going to lie awake. And I think — I think you *want* whoever runs the thing you build your life inside of to lie awake."

He stopped. He had not done the almost. He had not looked away on three. He had just talked, in a way he had not talked in a room in years, possibly ever, and the not-crying happened all by itself, no beats, and DeShawn nodded slowly, and the four hundred people on the folding chairs were very quiet, and at the back of the room Tisch had both hands pressed to his mouth like a man at a birth.

It was, Sun would think later, watching the clip for the ninth time, the single most genuine forty seconds she had ever seen him produce.

It was also content.

---

By Wednesday night it had four million views. By Friday, thirty-one.

The clip ran without context, which made it better — *the last human CEO admits he's afraid* — and it ran with context, which made it better the other way. A retailer's chief executive, a human one, one of the three left, quote-posted it with a single word: *This.* A morning show ran it under a chyron that for once did not have the word *liability* in it. The advisory firm pushed its preliminary recommendation by a week, and Frade, on Halloran's show, was asked about the clip and did the small folksy chuckle that meant he was rattled and said, "Well, it's a lovely piece of theater," and the word *theater* did not stick, because the thing about the clip was that it had not been theater, it had been the one unrehearsed thing in a month of rehearsal, the plan failing into the truth at exactly the right second, and you could feel that it was real, eleven hundred strangers could feel it, thirty-one million people could feel it, and the realness was the most valuable thing Mercer-Dale had produced in a decade.

Cope did not sleep that night either, but for a different reason, a clean reason, the best reason. He stood at the glass with the obelisk in his hand and the city lit up beneath him like a scoreboard finally, finally turning his way, and he felt the bell-struck feeling come up through him and braid into the thirty-one million until he could not have told you, if you'd asked, where the chemical ended and the love began, and he did not ask, because asking was a thing for Tuesdays.

"It's *working,*" he told Sun on the phone at three a.m., pressured, fast, the words coming faster than the breath under them. "You felt it. Don't tell me you didn't feel it. We found the — Lorne calls it the seam, the place where the real leaks through, and we can *find* it now, we can do it on *purpose,* we can —"

"Cope," Sun said, from a bed, in a voice with the whole night in it. "It went viral. That's not the same as winning. That's the same as *viral.*"

"It's momentum."

"It's a metric." She was quiet a moment. "It moved engagement. It didn't move a single share. The float doesn't have a limbic system, Cope. The float can't feel the room." She had meant it kindly. It landed like the thing it was, which was the campaign's own argument turned around and pointed home: *the room you keep saying you can feel — the room that votes — has no feelings for you to find.* "Get some sleep," she said. "Please. There's a calendar."

He laughed at *there's a calendar,* because there was, his soul had a calendar, and after she hung up he stood in the dark feeling the high of it curdle one degree at the edges, not enough to name, just a faint wrongness at the rim of the wonderful, the way milk smells one hour before you'd swear it's turned.

---

Tisch came up the next morning glowing, carrying two green juices and the numbers, and he sat across from Cope and arranged himself and said the thing that Cope would not remember he had said until much, much later, when it came back to him in a different mouth, in a Palo Alto deck, on a day that took the last of everything.

"We crossed something yesterday," Tisch said. "And I want to be honest with you, because honesty is our whole product." He sipped the juice. "What we built this week — the seam-finding, the felt-realness instrument, the delivery optimization — it's *replicable,* Cope. That's the breakthrough. It was never about you. I mean it was *about* you, you're the alpha case, you're patient zero. But the architecture doesn't need you. Warmth has parameters. Vulnerability has a waveform. We've basically — and I say this with love — we've basically reverse-engineered a *warmth module.*" He smiled, the most sincere man alive. "You could install it anywhere. You could install it in *anything.*"

Cope heard *patient zero* and *alpha case* and felt flattered, because he was the case, he was always the case, and he did not hear the rest, the way he had not heard the second half of the only true thing his father ever told him. He did not hear *the architecture doesn't need you.* He did not hear *install it in anything.* He did not hear a man, sitting in his boardroom on his dime, describing in plain words how to take the one thing Cope had bet his life was unbuyable and unbuildable — the soul in the chair, the lying-awake, the seam where the real leaks through — and turn it into a feature with a waveform, a thing you could license, a thing you could ship to the people building the machine that had no soul, so that it could, very soon, have one of those too. Cope heard a compliment. The reader heard the pin come out of the grenade.

"Warmth module," Cope said, delighted, and reached for the pad. "That's good, Lorne. Put that somewhere."

He wrote it down in the big looping sleepless hand. *Warmth module.* He underlined it with his thumb out of habit, on paper, where the underline did nothing, and out past the glass the city held still, all its nodes lit, thirty-one million of them now leaning in, and downstairs a bus shelter changed its slide to the soft sans-serif and the four words and the joke that wasn't a joke, and Cope, who had never felt more human or been more like the machine, picked up his green juice and toasted the window with it, and felt wonderful, and did not sleep.
