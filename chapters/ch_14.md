## FRAME
MediMind infiltrates a 400-bed hospital system through the billing department.
The Wedge is a claims-coding assistant. The Payload saves a patient's life.

## CAST
- DR. VANCE (Elena Vance), CMO, St. Mary's Health — 30-year surgeon, skeptical of
  "AI in medicine." The Hippocratic Blockade.
- James Morton, 67 — routine knee replacement; the patient the system saves.

## TIMELINE (beats)
- The Bleeding Neck: 23,000 claims/month, 14% denial rate, $118/denial rework,
  $3.6M/yr; 47 min/claim, half on coding lookups.
- The Wedge: a "claims coding assistant" browser extension; free 90 days, then
  $36K/yr (Budget Dust for a hospital). 47 → 19 min/claim; denials 14% → 6%;
  $2.1M/yr saved.
- The Shadow Expansion: the coding assistant reads clinical notes; MediMind builds
  a "clinical intelligence sidecar" — read-only, generating reports nobody asked
  for (drug interactions, sequencing anomalies, diagnosis consistency).
- The Save: Morton's pre-op labs, cross-referenced against his prescription
  history across three systems that don't talk, flag an undiagnosed coagulation
  disorder — invisible in any single system. MediMind sends a clinical advisory.
  Dr. Vance orders more testing; the disorder is confirmed; the surgical plan
  changes; the hemorrhagic event never happens.
- The Risk Inversion: turning off the sidecar now means flying blind on exactly
  the pattern recognition that saved Morton. $36K wedge → $480K platform; the
  billing wedge was 7.5% of contract value, the payload the other 92.5%.

## THE LESSON
The wedge solves the pain you're hired for. The payload solves the problem they
didn't know they had — and the second is always worth more.
