# Chapter 14 — Pressure

> TRANSMISSION 14 — "Everyone Knows Before He Does"
>
> A story is coming. You can feel it the way animals feel the air change before the quake — the journalist's calls getting shorter, the lawyer's getting longer, a wife who already counted the women now counting the days. He is the last to smell the smoke in his own house because he is standing too close to the fire he calls a personality. Next stop: the last quiet hour before the thing he buried learns to breathe.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

The week after the Manifesto, Cope decided, was the best week of his professional life, and he had the calendar to prove it, because the calendar was full and a full calendar was a kind of love.

He was, in fact, sure of this. He stood at the glass in the Aerie on Tuesday morning with a black coffee he would not drink and a clarity he had decided to call vision, and watched the day assemble in his head the way the city assembled below him, every meeting a lit window, the whole bright grid humming toward the obvious conclusion that the campaign had tipped, that he had said the thing out loud at last to ten thousand people and the room had risen and the risen room was the proof. Momentum. He had spent his life reading rooms. The room had stood up. He could feel it.

He could feel, also, the wire in his chest, the fine high one, and he had stopped pretending to himself that it was the coffee.

What he could not feel — and this was the trouble with the instrument he was so proud of, that it read applause beautifully and warning not at all — was the small change in the weather of the people around him. The way the calls were getting strange.

Delia March's calls, for instance, were getting shorter.

She was the writer doing the profile, the long one his comms people had fought for and won — *Atlantic* money, twelve thousand words by now and growing, a photographer who'd shot two presidents and a pope — and for six weeks she had called Cope the way the good ones called, hungry, generous, laughing at the lines, staying on past her questions to ask one more about Aldous, about Defiance, about the smell of the plant. He had loved those calls. He had given her things — more than Sun would have wanted, and a little more than that — because Delia March listened the way a confessor listens, leaning in, and Cope could not be leaned toward without filling the space.

The calls now were six minutes. They were eight minutes. They had a list to them. She asked her questions in a flat even order and did not laugh and did not stay, and twice she had asked him to confirm a date — *just so I have the timeline right, Cope, just for the fact-check* — and the dates were old ones, dates from years he did not keep a calendar for anymore, and he had said *that sounds about right, why* and she had said *no reason, housekeeping,* and moved on.

"She's doing the boring part," he told Sun, in the car, on Wednesday. "The fact-check. They all go cold for the fact-check. It means it's almost out. It means it's real."

"What dates," said Sun.

"I don't know. Nineteen, twenty. She's building the *arc.*" He liked the word. He had used it onstage. "She gets it, Sun. She came to Defiance with me. She *stood* there. You don't put a photographer on a man you're going to —"

"What dates, Cope."

He waved it off, because the wave was easier than the math, and because somewhere under the wave was a room he did not go into, and the going-not-into was a muscle he had been building for thirty years and it was the strongest muscle he had.

---

The lawyer's calls, meanwhile, were getting longer.

This was the part that should have told him, if anything was going to tell him, because Hal Brennan did not make long calls. Hal Brennan was general counsel of Mercer-Dale and had been since the Clinton administration and he billed the company's own time as if it were his own money, which it had, in a sense, become; Hal's calls were ninety seconds and ended with *we're fine* or *we're not, fix it,* and Cope had loved him for it for twenty years the way you love a man who hands you back your weekends.

Hal had called Monday night and stayed on for forty minutes.

He had not said anything, exactly. That was the thing Cope kept not-noticing about it. Hal had asked, in his dry way, how the campaign was *landing,* and then he had asked whether Cope had given *the writer* — he said *the writer,* not her name, which Cope filed nowhere — anything *on background that we'd want to know about,* and Cope had laughed and said he'd given her the family and the founding and the love, the whole love story, the company as a family that happens to make mayonnaise, and there had been a silence on Hal's end with weather in it, a long Greenwich silence, and then Hal had said, slowly, the way you say a thing you have decided to say carefully, "Cope. If anyone reaches out to you in the next little while — a reporter, a lawyer you don't know, anybody — you don't talk to them. You send them to me. You don't charm them. You especially don't charm them."

"When have I ever —"

"I'm serious. This is me being serious. You'll know it's me being serious because I never am." A pause. "We're going to want to get ahead of some things."

"Get ahead of what things, Hal."

"I'll tell you when I know what I'm looking at," Hal had said, which was a lawyer's sentence, which meant *I already know what I'm looking at and I am not going to say it on a phone,* and Cope had heard, instead, a busy man being thorough, a faithful retainer minding the store, and he had said *good man, Hal* and *that's why I keep you* and *we're going to win this thing,* three things, a triad, the cadence rising, and Hal had not risen with it. Hal had said *get some sleep, Cope,* which was the second time that week someone had said it to him as though it were medicine and not weather, and hung up.

Cope had felt, for about four seconds after the call, a cold spot. A draft from a door somewhere in the house of him that he'd thought was shut.

Then he had set himself up another, neat and quick at his father's desk, and the cold spot warmed, and he wrote four hundred words of the keynote for the retailers' summit, and they were, he was sure, the best four hundred words he had ever written, and he forgot the draft entirely, the way you forget the cold of a room once you've left it, and the leaving was, again, the most expensive thing he owned.

---

Frade was tightening the proxy screws and doing it in public, which Cope mistook, characteristically, for a sign that Frade was scared.

The tells were everywhere if you read them as Cope read them, which was as a scoreboard tilting his way. Tidewater had filed an amended 13D — its stake up to nine and a half percent, the disclosure dry as ever, *the Reporting Persons may engage with the Issuer's board regarding the composition of executive leadership* — and the morning it dropped, Frade went on Halloran's show in his good gray suit and his bad warm smile and did the folksy knife.

"Look, I like Cope," Frade said, which was how you knew the rest of the sentence was a coffin. "Everybody likes Cope. That's never been the question, Garrett. The man could sell sand to the beach. The question a fiduciary has to ask — and I'm a fiduciary, that's all I am, I'm a boring man who's a fiduciary — is whether *likability* is a governance strategy." He let it sit. "We're running *for* a system that doesn't have a personality to manage. A steward, not a sovereign. You don't have to *manage* a steward. You don't have to wonder what kind of week your steward is having."

Halloran, dutiful: "And what kind of week is Cope Mercer having?"

And Frade had smiled the smile and said, "I wouldn't presume," which was the single most aggressive thing he had said, and Cope, watching the clip on the treadmill at five in the morning because he was not sleeping and the treadmill at least let him call the not-sleeping a workout, had laughed out loud, alone, in the gym off the Aerie, and said to the screen, "You're *worried,* Gus. You're worried about the *human premium* and you can't say it, so you're talking about *weeks.*"

He texted Sun the clip. *He's rattled. He's down to talking about my mood. We've got him pivoting to vibes. Vibes is where we win.* He sent it at 5:12 a.m. He sent a follow-up at 5:13: *call Delia, tell her she can have the gym, the 5am thing, that's the story, the human who shows up.* He sent a third at 5:14 that was just a heart, because he loved her, because he loved everyone at 5:14 a.m. with a wire in his chest and a treadmill belt going under him like a road he was finally, finally getting somewhere on.

What he did not read in Frade's *I wouldn't presume* was that Frade did not need to presume. A man does not start asking, on cable, about another man's *weeks* unless he has reason to believe the public is about to be handed a very specific week. Frade was salting the ground. He was teaching ten million viewers the word *manage* and the phrase *what kind of week,* so that when the story came — and Frade did not have the story, did not need it, the story was coming the way water comes downhill and all Frade had to do was stand at the bottom of the hill looking unsurprised — the audience would already have the frame.

The screws were not tightening on the company. They were tightening on Cope, one quarter-turn at a time, and the real arithmetic of it was that he was supplying the wrench himself, at 5:14 in the morning, with a heart emoji.

---

Margaux had counted the women a long time ago.

She had done it the way you count anything to survive it — to make it finite, to give it a shape and therefore an edge, because a thing with an edge can be set down and a thing without one just fills the whole house. She had a number. She had never told anyone the number and never would; the number was hers; it was the most private thing she owned and she had earned it the hard way, one careful unsurprised year at a time, and she kept it the way her grandmother had kept a rosary, not for hope, just for something to hold while the worst of it went by.

That was old. She had made her peace with the number the way you make peace with a chronic thing — not peace, exactly, but management, a regimen, a way of living around the pain so that the pain became a wing of the house you simply did not go into.

What she was counting now was days.

Cope did not know this. Cope knew that Margaux was quiet, and read the quiet, when he read it at all, as the ordinary climate of a long marriage, the companionable weather of two people who had stopped needing to fill the air. He came to Greenwich on Thursday because Sun made him, because there were optics to a man who lived in his office during a proxy fight, and he came in bright and enormous, two days unslept and lit from the inside, and he found his wife at the kitchen island with a glass of white wine she was not drinking and a legal pad she turned over when he came in.

"There she is," Cope said, and kissed the top of her head, and she let him, and he did not feel her not-rising to it because he had never once needed her to rise; he supplied the rising for both of them. "You should have seen the room Monday, Margaux. Ten thousand. I've never — it was like the early days. It was like the *idea* of the early days." He was already at the cabinet, already moving, a man who could not be in a room without conducting it. "Frade's down to talking about my *moods* on television. He's got nothing. He's got *vibes.* We're going to win this thing on the one thing they can't —"

"Cope."

She said it the way she said things. Flat, low, set down. The only voice in his life he could not get under or over or around, the one voice with no purchase for the charm, because she had been immune to the toast since roughly 2015 and they both knew the year even if only one of them knew they knew it.

He turned. He was holding the wine bottle. He looked, for a second, like a boy caught reaching.

"Sit down a minute," Margaux said.

"I can listen standing. I listen better standing, I think on my —"

"Sit down, Prescott."

She used his name. The whole name, the old one, the one nobody used, the one his father had used and the nanny had ruined into *Coppy* and the world had ruined into *Cope,* and hearing it now in his wife's flat steady mouth did something to the wire in his chest, dropped the pitch of it half a tone, and he sat.

She looked at him for a while. She was a sculptor who had given it up, and she had a sculptor's way of looking, measuring the mass of a thing, where it would balance, where it would shear if you struck it wrong. She had been looking at him this way for twenty-two years. She knew where he'd shear.

"I want to ask you something," she said, "and I want you to do something you've never once done, which is hear the whole question before you start answering it."

"Margaux —"

"The whole question." She waited. He stopped. "There's a woman writing about you. The long piece."

"Delia. Delia March, she's — she's very good, she came to Defiance, she —"

"I'm not asking who she is." Margaux turned her wine glass a quarter turn on the marble, the way Frade turned screws, though she would have hated the comparison and it would have been exact. "She called the house. Tuesday. She asked for me. She was very polite. She said she wasn't going to ask me anything I didn't want to answer." Margaux looked up. "Why would a woman writing a business profile of my husband want to talk to me, Cope?"

The kitchen was very quiet. Out the window the Greenwich dark was doing its expensive nothing, the lawn, the wall, the trees the gardener kept.

"They — that's standard," Cope said. "They want the family. The human angle. It's the whole campaign, it's the —"

"She didn't want the family." Margaux's voice did not rise. It never rose. That was the thing about it, that it carried everything it carried without ever once lifting off the ground. "She wanted *me.* She wanted the wife. She had a tone, Cope. I've spent twenty-two years learning tones. I can read a tone the way you read a room. You read rooms for who's with you." She set the glass down. "I read them for what's about to be taken."

He opened his mouth. The toast was right there — it was always right there, the warm machinery of it, *sweetheart, you're tired, this is the press being the press, let me handle Delia, let me handle all of it, that's what I do, I handle things* — and he got the first word of it out, he got *sweetheart* out, and Margaux said:

"Don't."

One word. It landed the way the rare ones did, the ones she'd been saving, and it took the rest of the sentence out of him cleanly, the way you take a glass out of a drunk man's hand without spilling it, before he's even decided to let go.

"I'm not going to do this with you," she said. "The thing where you talk and I feel like I've been to a wonderful dinner and driven home and only realize in the driveway that I never got to eat." She stood. She picked up the legal pad, and she did not hide it now, did not turn it over; she held it against her so he could see there was writing on it but not what the writing was. "I counted them a long time ago, Cope. The women. You don't know that. You think I don't know. I counted them years ago and I stopped, because counting them was the same as staying, and I was very good at both."

"Margaux —"

"I'm not counting them anymore." She looked at him, and for once there was nothing to read in her face, no tone, the mass of the thing finally balanced and still. "I'm counting something else now. And I'm not going to tell you what, because the one mercy I can do you — the last one, I think — is not let you talk me out of it. You'd be so good at it. You're always so good at it. That's the whole tragedy of you, Prescott. You'd talk a drowning man out of the rope."

She went up the back stairs to the bedroom that had been her bedroom alone for two years, and Cope sat in the bright kitchen with the wine bottle still in his hand, and the wire in his chest hummed, and for one moment — one — he did not reach for the bottle or the phone or the next word. He sat in the quiet she had left behind her, and it had a shape, the quiet did, an edge to it, and the edge was a number he could not read, days counting down in a hand as steady as a surgeon's, and he felt the draft again from the shut door in the house of him.

Then he poured a glass he would not finish, and took out his phone, and texted Sun: *home looks good, family's solid, tell Delia she can come to greenwich for the wide shot.* And he believed it. He stood in the wreckage of the only conversation that had told him the truth all week and he read it as a win, because reading things as wins was the engine, and the engine had never once failed to start, and he was sure — the way you are sure of things at midnight in a kitchen when you have not slept since a day you can no longer name — that he had handled it. That he handled things. That this was what he did.

---

He drove back to the city himself, which he was not supposed to do. There was a service for that, a quiet man named Oleg who saw everything and was paid to forget all of it, but Cope sent Oleg home and took the Merritt himself at one in the morning with the windows down and the cold April air coming in hard, because he wanted, he told himself, to *think,* and because the road at that hour belonged to him the way nothing else did anymore — the dark trees, the dashed line coming up out of the dark and disappearing under him, an endless ration of small certainties.

The phone lit on the seat beside him twice and he did not look at it. Once it was Sun. Once it was a number he didn't know, an unknown number at one in the morning, and a younger, less lit man might have wondered what kind of caller comes at that hour from a number you don't know, might have heard, in the buzz of it on the leather, the sound of a story turning over in its sleep and beginning, out past the dark trees, to breathe.

Cope thought it was probably Bryce. Or someone like Bryce. There was always someone like Bryce, and the thought warmed him, the generous enormous warmth, *if you ever want to do something more interesting than this,* and he smiled at the road.

He came up the FDR with the river black on his right and the city standing up ahead of him lit at every node, and he felt — he would have sworn to it, he would have toasted to it, he did, in fact, toast to it, aloud, alone, in the car, lifting an empty hand off the wheel — he felt the future coming to ask his advice.

The tower was lit at the crown the way Aldous had insisted, *so a man coming up the avenue knows there's somebody home.* Cope looked up at it through the windshield, the old limestone, the warm gold light at the top where his office was, where his father's desk was, where the drawer was, and he thought: *somebody's home. I'm home. The light's on because I'm in it.*

He did not know — could not have known, would not have heard it if Margaux herself had said it in her flat steady voice — that the light at the crown was the last quiet thing he had. That this was the hour. That out there in the dark, in a city of editors and lawyers and a profiler with a list and a wife with a legal pad and an activist who only had to stand at the bottom of the hill, the buried thing had taken its first breath. That it had been holding it for years. That it was, even now, very quietly, learning to use its lungs.

He parked in his spot. He went up forty floors in the brass cage of the old elevator, alone, watching the numbers climb. He let himself into the dark suite where his fathers watched from oils and the boy at nine grinned up out of a steel vat at men whose names he'd lost. He did not turn on the lamp.

He sat down at the desk, and opened the second drawer, and set himself up one more, neat and quick and competent, a man maintaining a machine he was sure he had under perfect control.

And then, in the dark, with the city held still and lit below him and the wire singing clean and high and total in his chest, the last human chief executive in the consumer Fortune 100 picked up his phone to write — he was sure it would be the best thing he'd ever written — and saw, finally, that he had eleven missed calls.

He smiled at the screen.

"There it is," he said, to no one, to his father, to the dark. "There's the future. Right on time."

He was almost right. He was off by one word, and the word was *future,* and it would take him until morning to learn the other one.
