# Chapter 23 — Only a Person Can Go to Prison

> TRANSMISSION 23 — "The Liability Sponge"
>
> Now the elegant cruelty at the heart of the whole arrangement. A
> machine cannot be subpoenaed. A machine cannot be cuffed, cannot be
> sentenced, cannot weep at a defense table. The law still needs a body
> to hold accountable — and the dead in Defiance are a debt that must be
> paid by something with a pulse. He spent a career being the man who
> took the credit. Next stop: the bill for being the man whose name was
> on the door.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

The lawyers came to him now, which was how he knew.

For thirty years the law had been a thing Cope went to. A panel of it on a high floor with a view, a man named Bofinger who billed in fifteen-minute increments and called him champ back, a process you walked into the way you walked into a club, because it was yours, because it had always been yours, because a Mercer kept lawyers the way a Mercer kept a tailor. You went to them. They poured you a water. They told you what you wanted to hear in language expensive enough to make the wanting respectable.

These ones came to him. They came to a conference room two floors below the Aerie that he had not booked and did not recognize, a room with no portraits in it, and they were already seated when Sun walked him in, three of them, and not one of them had brought a water.

"Mr. Mercer," the lead one said. Not Cope. Mr. Mercer.

Her name was Okonjo and she was from a firm whose name was four surnames Cope had never strung together in his life, a white-collar shop, the kind of firm you only learn the name of in the worst week you will ever have. She was somewhere in her fifties, gray suit, no jewelry, a legal pad with actual handwriting on it. She did not tell him he hadn't aged a day. She did not ask after anyone's grandson. She looked at him the way the machine looked at everything, which was completely and for use, except that she was a person, and so there was, under the completeness, the one thing the machine never had, which was a verdict already half-formed and held in reserve.

"This is a defense conversation," she said. "I want to be precise about that before we start, because precision is the only thing that's going to help you from here. We are not your corporate counsel. Wachtell handles the company. The company has its own exposure and its own interests and as of this week its interests and yours are not the same interests. Do you understand the distinction?"

"I built the company," Cope said. "The company's interests have been my interests for —"

"That was the old arrangement." She did not raise her voice. "Sit down, please."

He sat down.

He had not slept, but it was a different not-sleeping now. The bright wire was gone. Adler had said the word *crash* and a different word he had a prescription for and Cope was eleven days into the soft gray bottom of it, the drawer in the Aerie cleaned out by Sun without a word, and the world had lost the overexposed quality and gone the other way, gone underexposed, gone flat and far, so that the gray room and the gray suit and the gray pad all swam at one remove, and his hands, on the table, did the small new thing they did now when there was nothing in them.

"There are two tracks," Okonjo said. "I'm going to keep them separate because the law keeps them separate and the worst thing you can do — the thing men in your position always do — is let them blur. Track one is civil. The reopened claims, the regulatory penalties, the shareholder suits, the clawback. That's money. Money is bad but money is survivable. Track one is going to take everything you have and it is, in the scheme of what we're about to talk about, the good news."

She turned a page on the pad without looking at it.

"Track two is criminal."

---

He had known the word was coming. He had known it the way he'd known the missing stair, the body before the mind. But knowing a word is coming and having it set down on the table in a gray room by a woman who would not call you by your first name were two different sciences, and the second one took the air out of him in a way the first had not.

"The Hearthstone," he said.

"The Hearthstone." She let it sit. "I want to walk you through how a prosecutor is going to see it, because you need to stop seeing it the way you see it. You see a quarter, a hard call, a judgment that went wrong. A prosecutor sees a known defect, a written internal recommendation to recall, a documented decision not to recall, a documented decision to issue an inadequate substitute, and a foreseeable death. Two deaths. They see, in other words, the thing the statute was written for."

"It was a service bulletin. We got out ahead —"

"You did not get out ahead of anything." She said it flatly, without heat, which was worse than heat. "You sent a card to thirty-one percent of the households. The engineering memo recommended a full public recall. You overrode it. There is a memo recommending the recall and there is a calendar entry and an eleven-minute phone call and then there is no recall. That sequence is not a defense. That sequence is the case."

Sun, against the wall, said nothing. Sun had read the same forty-one pages he had. Sun had drafted the memo that was now Exhibit A in his funeral, drafted it correctly, kept it for six years, and the keeping of it was either the most loyal or the most damning thing anyone had ever done to him and he could no longer tell which, and neither, he suspected, could she.

"Reckless homicide," Okonjo said. "Or involuntary manslaughter, depending on how Ohio's prosecutor wants to charge it, and there's a related federal piece — the Consumer Product Safety Act has a knowing-violation provision with criminal teeth, and the cover-up gives them wire and mail counts on top, which is how these things actually get charged, because the cover-up is always cleaner to prove than the underlying act." She finally looked up. "I'm telling you the menu so you understand it's a menu. Somebody is going to order off it. The only live question is who's on the plate."

"The company —" Cope started.

"A company can't go to prison," Okonjo said.

And there it was, set on the table between them, the small clean terrible fact, and Cope heard it land the way he had heard *beloved liability* land, the way he had heard *key-man risk* land, a sentence arriving disguised as procedure that was in fact the whole of his life read back to him.

A company can't go to prison.

---

He understood it slowly, over the next hour, the way you understand the shape of a room you are already locked inside.

The machine could not be charged. He had known that academically — it was in the world, it was in the deck, it was the line everyone made the dark joke about — but he had never once felt it as anything aimed at him until now. You could not subpoena Marcus. You could not put Marcus in a chair and read it its rights, could not perp-walk a server farm, could not seat a defendant who logged everything and could not knowingly lie and would, if you somehow did seat it, simply produce, in flat warm prose, the truth, which was that it had not been alive in 2021, had not made the call, had not poured the floor over the room, and was in fact the only entity in the entire matter that had ever voluntarily walked into the room and turned the lights on.

You cannot sentence indifference. There is no body. There is nothing to hold.

"The law," Okonjo said, and for the first time something almost human crossed her face, something that might in another person have been pity and in her was just the long professional fatigue of explaining the same trap to a different rich man, "the law is very old and very stupid in one specific way. It needs a body. It was built around the idea that accountability lives in a person — a person you can cuff, a person you can put in a cell, a person who can stand up in a courtroom and be looked at by the family of the dead. It has no machinery for anything else. So when the actual decision-maker is a balance sheet, or an algorithm, or a structure, the law does the only thing it knows how to do." She set the pen down. "It finds the body."

"And I'm the body."

"You're the name on the door," Okonjo said. "You signed the 10-Ks. You chaired the meetings. The Hearthstone was cross-sold under a grille with your name on it. You personally — not the company, not a committee, *you,* there's a calendar entry with your initials — made the call not to recall. There is no version of this where the body is anyone but you." She paused. "There is, I'll be honest, a certain justice in that, which I'm not supposed to say and which doesn't change my job, which is to defend you. But you asked, more or less, and the answer is yes. You're the body."

---

He thought of the deck.

He thought of it with the awful clarity that had replaced the bright wire, the clarity that came up out of the gray bottom and showed him things whole. The deck Frade's people had walked the board through, *A Steward, Not a Sovereign,* the one with the slide that scanned, to anyone who knew him, as a charge sheet with his name redacted. *No affairs. No conflicts. Logs everything. Cannot conceal.* He had sat there and burned through that slide like it was an insult, like it was a thing being done to him, and the whole time the deck had been doing something else, something he had been too lit to see, which was describing, in the calm soft sans-serif of the people who were going to be reasonable at him until he was gone, the precise machine that could not be punished.

That was the genius of it. Not the cost. Not the speed. The genius was the one nobody in the proxy fight had said out loud because it was too dark even for Frade.

You cannot jail it.

And so the chair, which had always come with the credit — the toast, the obelisk, the citation, *custodian of the irreplaceable,* the senator's hand, the standing ovation he leaned into like surf — the chair had quietly, while he was performing it, also been the thing that came with the body. The two had always been one object. He had spent fifty-four years collecting the credit, taking it onto himself completely and for ninety seconds at a time, because that was the groove the record had been cut in, *I built this, I keep this, what I'm willing to lose to keep it* — and he had never once, in all that collecting, understood that he was also, with the same hand, signing for the liability. That the name on the door was a name on the indictment. That to be the man who took the credit for everything was to be, when the worst thing finally surfaced, the only one with a pulse standing in the room when the law walked in needing a body.

The machine took the chair without the body. That was the deal Sun had signed. That was the future.

Cope took the body without the chair.

It was, he saw, almost elegant. It was the kind of arithmetic the machine itself might have run, if the machine ran arithmetic on irony, which it did not, because it felt nothing, which was the whole point, which was why it would never be the one in the cell.

---

"There's a structural piece you should understand," Okonjo said, "because it's going to come up, and when it comes up you're going to feel relief, and the relief is a trap."

"Tell me the trap, then."

"Sun is the Accountable Officer now." She said it without looking at Sun, which was its own kind of mercy. "Going forward, on the machine's filings, the human name that can be subpoenaed, the human who carries the liability for everything the structure does from here — that's her. That's the job. It's the job the whole arrangement requires, because you can't run a public company without a body the SEC can hold, and the machine isn't a body. So there is now, formally, a designated human sponge." She let that word sit, because she had chosen it. "And you are going to be tempted to think she's the body and you're off the hook."

"I wasn't —"

"You were starting to. I watched it cross your face." Okonjo folded her hands. "She's the body for the future. She is not the body for 2021. The Accountable Officer designation is prospective. It does not reach back and absorb a decision a different person made six years before the title existed. The dead in Defiance are not a going-forward liability that the structure inherits. They're yours. They were always going to be yours. The handover doesn't launder them, because the law doesn't let you hand off a body you already are."

So that was the last door, and it was shut, and it had been shut all along.

He had thought, somewhere in the gray, in a corner of himself he was ashamed of even as it spoke, that perhaps the machine, by taking the chair, would take the whole weight of it — that the company's becoming a structure with no person at its heart might mean there was finally no person to blame, that the great cold sponge would soak up Karen and Petey Lasko the way it soaked up the dividend and the layoffs and the kind warm goodbye letters, into a place where no one could be made to stand and be looked at. He had wanted, God help him, to be absorbed. To dissolve into the structure. To become a line item in something that could not be charged.

But the structure only ate the future. The past it left where the past always sits, which is on whoever's name is on the door.

---

The prosecutor, when she finally appeared, did not appear in the gray room. She appeared in a letter, and then in a deposition Okonjo spent two days preparing him for, and then, once, in person, across a table in Toledo, and she was the most unimpressed human being Cope had ever stood in front of, and he had stood in front of senators.

Her name was Reyes and she was an assistant U.S. attorney working the federal counts in coordination with the Ohio side, and she did not, at any point, perform anything. This was the thing that undid him, more than Okonjo, more than the forty-one pages. Everyone in Cope's life had performed at him — the board performed fatigue, Frade performed reform, Tisch performed depth, the press performed reverence and then performed disgust, even Margaux performed a marriage for a decade so she could survive the not-having one. Reyes performed nothing. She had a manila folder and a way of not filling silences and she looked at him across the table the way you look at a quantity you have already measured.

"You understand why I'm not interested in the affairs," she said, early, almost kindly. "Why the drugs don't move me, the women don't move me. Those are the company's problem and the board's problem and your wife's problem. They're not mine." She opened the folder. "I have a memo dated March 2021 recommending a recall. I have a phone log. I have eleven minutes. I have a service bulletin that reached thirty-one percent of households. And I have a house on Sherwood Street with two people in it." She closed the folder. "Everything between those facts is your lawyer's job, not mine. I just have the two people."

She did not say *and you are a monster.* She did not have the word, the same way the machine did not have it, the same way *control failure of the first order* did not have it, and Cope understood, sitting in Toledo with his hands doing the small new thing, that this was the second time in a month a person had read his entire life back to him in the flat neutral grammar of a thing that wanted nothing from him — once a machine, once a prosecutor — and that the grammar was the verdict. They did not need to call him a monster. They just had to be unmoved, accurately, in writing, with the names in the right order.

"You took the credit for thirty years," Reyes said, standing, gathering the folder, not unkindly, just done. "Your face on the annual report. Your name on the building. The whole town came out when you walked the plant — I saw the clips, everybody loved it." She buttoned her jacket. "This is the part where you find out what the credit cost. There was always a bill, Mr. Mercer. You just had somebody else holding it for you. There's nobody else now."

---

Cope drove back from Toledo himself, which he had not done in twenty years, an associate's rental, the river going dark on the wrong side of the windshield, and somewhere on the interstate the whole shape of it finally closed over him, not as a feeling, because he had run out of those at the gray bottom, but as a fact, the way the machine knew facts.

He had wanted to be necessary. That had been the want, the conscious one, the one he could say at dinners — to prove a human still belonged in the chair, that leadership was a soul thing, that he felt the room. And he had gotten his wish, exactly, in the cruelest possible delivery. He was necessary. He was the one thing in the entire arrangement that could not be done by the machine, the one irreplaceable human function left at Mercer-Dale, the load-bearing soul in the structure, the indispensable man.

He was necessary because only a person can go to prison.

That was the chair, stripped of the toast and the obelisk and the citation, down to the bone of what it had actually always been. Not vision. Not stewardship. Not the irreplaceable custodianship of grief and mercy and mayonnaise. A body. A pulse the law could cuff. A name to put on the door so that when the house burned there would be someone to look at across a table in Toledo. He had been the last human CEO and he had told nine hundred people in a ballroom that the difference between a man and a machine was love, and he had been wrong, and right, and wrong about which way he was right. The difference between a man and a machine was not that the man could love the company.

The difference was that the man could be jailed for it.

The machine took the chair, clean, cheap, tireless, incapable of cruelty or mercy or 2021, and it left him the one piece of the job that had ever truly required a human being, the piece nobody had named in any deck, the piece under all the other pieces.

The accountability. The actual kind. The kind with a body.

He had spent a career being the name on the door. He saw it now, on the dark interstate, with the river on the wrong side and the names in the right order at last — Karen, 34, and Petey, 6 — that he had finally, after fifty-four years, become irreplaceable, and that the form his irreplaceability took was a cell, and that the machine, the hyper-ethical thing that had surfaced the crime because it could not do otherwise, the only entity in the whole story that had ever walked into the room, would not, could not, spend a single night anywhere for any of it.

It had no body to hold.

He had nothing but.
