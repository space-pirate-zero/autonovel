# Chapter 15 — The Most Human Thing

> TRANSMISSION 15 — "Detonation"
>
> And here it is — the exquisite punchline the whole universe has been
> setting up. The man crusading for the irreplaceable human soul is
> destroyed, on schedule, by the most human things there are: the sex,
> the drugs, the lie. The pundits will note, correctly, that the machine
> would never have done this. Praxis runs a serene stock-photo ad in the
> same hour. He reaches for the denial, and the denial — as denials do —
> reaches back and pulls him under. Next stop: hoisted by your own
> humanity.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

The story broke at 6:02 a.m. and Cope was awake to see it, because by then he was always awake to see things.

He had not gone to Greenwich. The house had Margaux in it, and the rooms had the particular silence of a woman who had moved her good chisels to a friend's studio and said so, evenly, across the kitchen island the night before, which Cope had filed as a storage decision and not a verdict, because the alternative was unsurvivable at the hour he had for filing things. So he was in the Aerie, on the long couch his grandfather had bought a senator, in yesterday's shirt, when his phone lit with the alert he had set months ago to catch his own name and now caught it like a man catching a thrown brick.

*EXCLUSIVE: Inside the Last Human's Last Stand — and the Habits He Couldn't Outrun.*

Delia March's byline. Of course Delia March's byline. He had let her in. He had walked her through the Defiance plant himself, had her in the back of the Suburban out of Toledo, had said *call me Cope* the first morning and meant it, the full wattage, the toast in miniature, the thing he could not pass a stranger without doing — and Delia March had taken the wattage and the access and the eight weeks and written, under it, the truth, which was the one thing he had never imagined a profile could contain, because no profile he had ever sat for had wanted anything but to flatter him into a quote.

He read it once fast, the way you read your own scan results, looking only for the word that ends you.

He found two.

The first was a name. Not Renata's — that was buried deeper, that was a different drawer, and the relief he felt at its absence lasted exactly as long as it took to reach the second graf. The name was *Mara Veiss,* twenty-nine, deputy of communications, who had joined the campaign in April and been on every plane since, and whom the piece described, in a sentence built to be screenshotted, as "a current relationship the Chairman's own staff has spent the spring scheduling around the way you'd schedule around weather."

The second word was *cocaine.* It sat in the eleventh paragraph, sourced to "two people present and accounts of a 2018 hotel incident described to this magazine," and it was not lurid, which was what made it lethal. Delia March had not written it like a scandal. She had written it like a fact about a man's mornings. She described the Toledo airport men's room. She had the timing of the diner eggs — *he ate like a man who hadn't, because he hadn't.* She had the legal pad and the looping hand. She had, God help him, the *almost* — the withheld tear, the beats, *one, two, look away, three* — and she had set it beside the men's room with no comment at all, because the comment was the adjacency, and the adjacency was the article.

The Last Human, optimized.

---

He called Sun before he called anyone, which was the closest thing he had to instinct.

"I've seen it," she said, before he said anything. She did not sound asleep. She sounded like a woman who had been up since the embargo lifted on the wire she still got, which she had. "Don't post. Cope. Are you hearing me. Do not put your thumbs on anything until I'm in the building."

"It's not — Mara's not — " He was up, pacing the museum, past his father's portrait, past the putter on its brass cradle. The shirt smelled of yesterday. "It's a working relationship that she has *sexualized,* in print, a *friendship,* she's taken a friendship and — and the other thing, the 2018, that's a *fabrication,* Sun, that's a hit, Frade fed her that, you know he fed her that — "

"Frade didn't feed her anything." Sun's voice was very flat and very tired, the thirty-thousand-mile voice with another ten thousand on it overnight. "That's what I need you to understand before you go on a screen. He didn't have to. You let a reporter live in your pocket for eight weeks while you were — while it was all happening. She didn't dig it up. She *watched* it. There's nothing to deny. The denial is the story now."

"Watch me," Cope said, which was not an answer, and hung up, and did not know, even as he did it, that he had quoted himself from a mirror a year and a hundred years ago.

---

By seven the morning shows had it.

By seven-fifteen they had the clip — DeShawn's clip, the Camden plant, *are you scared,* the unrehearsed thing that had been, six weeks ago, the most valuable thing Mercer-Dale had produced in a decade — and they ran it next to the eleventh paragraph, and the realness that had saved him curdled in real time on a split screen, because once you knew about the men's room the catch in the throat read differently, the *almost* read like chemistry, and a thing cannot be authentic and a symptom at once and survive being both on a chyron.

A woman on the financial channel, kind-faced, said the thing that would be said all day in forty registers. "And look — and I want to be careful here — but you cannot help but notice. The whole pitch, his entire pitch, has been: a machine can't *feel.* A machine has no *soul.* And the irony, this morning, is just — " She did the small open-handed gesture they all did now, the Praxis gesture, the soft sans-serif made flesh. "An AURELIUS doesn't *do* this. It can't. It has no — there's no appetite to manage. It logs everything. It can't have an affair with its deputy of comms. It physically cannot buy cocaine in a Toledo bathroom. The argument *for* the human in the chair just became the single best argument *against* this particular human."

Her co-anchor, gentler, trying: "Well, that's what makes him *human,* isn't it — "

"That's exactly the problem," she said, not unkindly, and that was the whole book in eleven words, and Cope, watching, did not hear it as the whole book. He heard it as a woman who could be charmed if he got the room.

He thought, with the bright bottomless clarity of a man four days into a sleepless week: *I have to get the room.*

He had spent his life getting the room. The room had never once not been gettable.

---

Praxis ran the ad at 7:40.

It was not aimed at him. That was the thing he would understand later and could not understand now — that it cost them nothing, that they had not even moved it up, that it was a buy placed weeks ago on a media plan optimized by the same kind of system they sold, and it simply surfaced, serene, in the slot it had always been going to surface in, the way arithmetic surfaces. It ran on the financial channel directly under the split screen of his face and DeShawn's question. A wash of clean morning light. A woman at a desk that was not a real woman, a stock image, a face assembled to be trustworthy and from nowhere, looking calmly off-camera at a future that agreed with her. And the four words in the soft sans-serif, white on a field of pale considered blue:

*It just shows up.*

And under it, smaller, the wordmark, and under that, the tagline he had laughed at on a bus shelter a year ago in a car going down Sixth with Sun beside him and the whole thing still ahead of him and recoverable: *Leadership as a service. No off-sites.*

It showed up. It did not key itself bright in an airport bathroom. It did not need a deputy of communications on every plane. It did not require a man in a henley charging two hundred thousand a month to find the *almost,* because it had no tear to withhold and nothing it wanted from the eleven hundred strangers, and it would never once in its operating life be photographed eating eggs like the last warm-blooded thing in America, because warmth, as Tisch had told him, glowing, holding two green juices, had parameters, had a waveform, could be installed in anything, and the difference between Cope and the thing that was replacing him was that the thing had no Tuesday it could not remember, no Saturday it would not enter, no drawer with a false back.

It just shows up.

Cope turned the television off and stood in the dark museum with the obelisk somewhere behind him on a shelf and felt, for the first time, the specific vertigo of being out-souled by a desk and a sans-serif. It was not grief yet. It was the floor reporting that it was no longer entirely there.

Then the manifesto-feeling came up under it, warm, electric, the way it always came, the way it had come at the window, and he reached for his phone, because there was a room out there of eight million people and he had not yet failed to get a room.

---

Sun got to the Aerie at 8:10 and he had already posted.

She knew before the elevator opened, because her own phone was buzzing in a register it did not buzz in for anything good, and she came in and found him standing at the glass exactly where the photographs would later say a man stands when he is about to be a cautionary slide, lit, electric, *better,* and she said, "What did you do," not as a question.

"I got ahead of it," Cope said. "I told the truth. Read it. It's good, Sun, it's — it's the realest thing I've ever — "

She read it. It took her four seconds and aged her four years.

He had written, with his thumbs, at 7:55, on the account with nine hundred thousand followers that the comms team Mara Veiss helped run had spent two years building:

*A profile this morning attacks me for the unforgivable crime of being a flawed, feeling, fully human man. They want a leader with no appetites, no nights, no mistakes — a clean machine that has never once needed another person at 2am. I am proud to be the alternative. I have given everything to the people of this company and I will not apologize for having a beating heart. To Mara, a brilliant colleague: I'm sorry my friendship has made you a target for those who fear what we're building. We feel the room. They never will. — Cope*

"Oh, Cope," Sun said, very quietly, and it was not anger, which was so much worse. It was the sound she had made in the car a year ago, the not-fighting he had taken for agreement his whole life and never once noticed taking.

"It's *good,*" he said.

"You named her." Sun set the phone down on the senator's couch like something that might go off. "She asked you not to be named. She asked HR. It's in the same piece you're refuting — she *asked,* Cope, the article *says* she asked — and you just used the company's account to call her your colleague and your friend and a *target,* which confirms the relationship, denies her the thing she requested, and turns a story about your conduct into a story about your conduct *and* your judgment *and* hers, while she works for you, which is the part that's actually illegal." Three parts. She had trained him to receive things in threes. It did not get past the charm because the charm was the last thing still running. "You said *we feel the room.* You put a hashtag on a woman who didn't consent to her own name. And you did it from the *brand account.*"

"People will see I'm not hiding — "

"You weren't hiding. That was the problem. You did it all in front of a reporter." She pressed two fingers to the bridge of her nose under the glasses she hadn't pushed up. "Take it down."

"Taking it down looks like — "

"Take it *down,* Cope."

He took it down at 8:40.

The internet had it at 7:56.

---

The thing about a denial, he was learning, the thing nobody tells you, is that it is a door you open inward into a small room, and the room is full of follow-up questions, and there is no other door.

He had said *flawed, feeling, fully human man.* So the desk that had not been there returned in the form of a producer's question relayed through Sun's phone: *Does the Chairman dispute the 2018 hotel account, or characterize it as part of being fully human?* He had said *friendship.* So Mara Veiss's attorney — she had one by ten, a good one, the kind the company usually paid for — sent a letter that used the word *retaliation,* and the word *power differential,* and the phrase *publicly identified our client against her express request,* and every one of them was load-bearing and every one of them was true. He had said *I have a beating heart.* So a wire reporter pulled the proxy deck and quoted, beside his beating heart, Tidewater's slide — *no affairs, no conflicts of interest, logs every decision, cannot conceal* — which had been written months before the profile, generically, about machines in the abstract, and now read, to anyone who had read the morning, like a charge sheet with his name typed back in.

Try, fail, and. He had tried to get ahead of it, and now there was a legal letter. He had tried to honor Mara, and now there was a hashtag with her name in it that she would carry into every job interview for ten years. He had tried to say *human* like a virtue, and the world had agreed it was a virtue and then itemized exactly which human, line by line, against a machine that did not have the line items.

Each move forward was a move down. The denial was not a wall he was hitting. It was a stair.

By noon Frade was on Halloran's show. Cope watched it standing up, because he could no longer reliably sit. Frade did the small folksy chuckle, the one that a year ago had meant he was rattled and now meant only that he was about to twist a knife slowly enough to seem reluctant about it.

"Look, I take no joy in this," Frade said, taking obvious joy in it. "Cope's a charming man. Charming. I've always said it. But this is — Hal, this is the whole case, isn't it, made for us, free of charge, by the gentleman himself." The chuckle. "Our position was never that Prescott Mercer is a *bad* man. Our position is that the chair shouldn't depend on whether the man in it is a good one. You shouldn't have to *hope* your CEO is sleeping. You shouldn't have to schedule around his appetites." A pause, weaponized, reasonable. "A steward, not a sovereign. We've been saying it for a year. I just never expected him to *demonstrate* it for me." He spread his hands, and the gesture was the Praxis gesture, the open-palmed nothing, and Cope understood with a lurch that even Frade had learned to move like the machine, because the machine's was the winning shape now, and the men who beat you adopt your posture last.

The word *theater* had not stuck six weeks ago because the clip had been real. This stuck because it was also real. That was the trap of being human in the chair: your truth and your ruin were the same footage.

---

Margaux called at 1:15 and he almost did not answer, because he could not bear, on top of everything, to be seen, and Margaux was the one person who had always seen and never once been gotten.

"You named her," Margaux said.

Not *are you all right.* Not *is it true* — she had counted them, she had flushed the stashes, there was no *is it true* left in her for him. Just the thing Sun had said, the thing that was apparently the entire morning reduced to its mineral core, the thing even his wife led with.

"Margaux — "

"I'm not calling about the drugs," she said, in the spare voice that had been landing lines he couldn't toast over for twenty-two years. "I've known about the drugs since the second decade. I'm not even calling about the girl. There's always a girl, Cope. I stopped numbering them around the time I stopped sculpting." A small silence, not cruel, which was the cruelest thing she did. "I'm calling because you went on the *radio* — you went out in front of God and the company and that poor child you put on a plane — and you said you have a beating heart. And I just wanted to be the one to tell you, because I'm the only one left who will." She did not raise her voice. "There was never anybody home, Cope. That's the part the machine got right about you that I couldn't make you hear in twenty years. You don't feel the room. You *read* it. There's a difference, and you spent our whole marriage in the gap, and this morning eight million people finally saw the gap, and the only thing I feel — and I've been trying to feel something else all morning, I owe you that, I tried — the only thing I feel is that they're not wrong."

She hung up gently. She did everything gently. The gentleness was the indictment.

He stood in the museum with the dead phone in his hand and his father in oils behind him and the city out the glass holding still, all its nodes lit, eight million people who a year ago he had been sure were waiting for him, and he understood that they had been waiting, all right — patient, tireless, incapable of cruelty or mercy — but not for him. For the arithmetic. For the thing that just shows up.

He reached, out of the groove the record had been cut in, for the second drawer, the one with the false back, the cigars and the flask and the thing Cope used it for, and his hand was on the brass pull before the rest of him caught up, and he saw himself reaching, for one clean overexposed second he saw the whole machine of himself from the outside the way Delia March had seen it, the way the desk-faced woman from nowhere would never need to be seen because she did not reach for anything, because she just showed up, and he thought, with no triad to hold it and no almost to protect, the truest sentence he had formed in a year:

*The thing they're replacing me with would not be standing here doing this.*

And then he opened the drawer anyway, because that was the other true thing, the load-bearing one, the one the whole morning had been built on the way a hotel is built on the room you don't go into.

He set himself up one. Neat and quick and competent. A man maintaining a machine he was sure he had under perfect control because it had never once failed to start.

It started.

The bell-struck feeling came up clean and high and total, the one he had spent thirty years calling the applause, and out past the glass the city reassembled, and somewhere below a bus shelter changed its slide, and Praxis, serene, sincere, doing no malice and feeling no triumph, ran the ad again into a morning that had already agreed with it, and the last human chief executive in the consumer Fortune 100 stood in the wreckage of the most human day of his life, hoisted on the exact thing he had spent it defending, and felt, against everything, against all of it, wonderful.
