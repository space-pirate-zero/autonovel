# Chapter 13 — The Manifesto

> TRANSMISSION 13 — "The Sermon on the Soul"
>
> Here is the peak of the mountain, which is also the lip of the volcano.
> Watch him deliver the Human Premium Manifesto to ten thousand employees
> at the absolute summit of his powers and his sickness, glorious and
> unwell and impossible to look away from. Behind the curtain, the most
> competent person in the building is taking a meeting with the other
> side — not betrayal, just arithmetic, doing what he never could:
> adapting. Next stop: the loneliest standing ovation of his life.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

They had rented the arena that the hockey team played in, because nothing the company owned was big enough to hold what Cope had become.

That was how he put it to himself in the wings, in the dark, with the bright wire humming, watching the floor fill on the monitors: ten thousand of them on the seats and the poured-over ice, and behind them another ninety thousand on the global stream, every plant and depot and call center and the sad little Dale+ office in Burbank where forty people made a streaming service nobody wanted, all of them piped onto one feed, all of them about to receive him. He had not slept since — he stopped doing the arithmetic, because the arithmetic had gone funny lately, Tuesday kept moving, and anyway sleep was for men who were not needed in the morning. He felt marvelous. He felt like the inside of a struck bell that had been struck again before the first note died, so that the notes stacked, ringing on top of each other, a chord of himself.

The set was black and the screen behind it was four stories of a single word in the soft warm sans-serif everyone used now, the typeface of the people who were going to be reasonable at you until you were gone, except Cope had made the comms team take that font and use it against itself, fight fire with fire, and the word four stories tall in the enemy's own letters was:

*HUMAN.*

"Two minutes," said the floor manager, a girl with a headset and a clipboard who had been crying about something before the doors opened, Cope could see it at the edges, and he filed her sadness automatically into the place where he kept sadnesses to fix later, and gave her the whole smile, and watched it work, and felt enormous.

Sun was not in the wings.

He noticed it the way you notice a missing tooth with your tongue, a small wrong smoothness where the rough familiar thing should be. She had been at every podium for six years, off to stage left with the cards and the water and the look that took thirty thousand miles off a sentence. He had a vague bright memory of her saying she had a thing — *a thing,* people kept having things — a board call, a Tidewater something, the term sheet, the language, somebody had to be on it. He stepped easily over the hole in the floor. He did not need the cards. The cards were for the man he used to be, the one who got handed threes in elevators because that was the only shape that got past the charm into the man. He was past all that now. He was the charm and the man at once, fused, a single clean alloy, and he had the whole thing in him, every word, the way you have your own name.

"Cope," said Tisch, materializing, both hands already pressed to his own heart. He had a juice. He had the rings of color around his eyes now too, he'd stopped sleeping in sympathy or in billing, hard to say. "Don't reach for it. Don't perform it. The work is done. You *are* the work. Walk out there and let them feel located." He squeezed Cope's shoulders. "Almost," he whispered, like a benediction, like a man releasing a dove. "Remember the almost."

"There's no almost tonight, Lorne," Cope said. "Tonight I'm going all the way in."

Tisch's face did something complicated, something with fear in it, and Cope took the fear for awe, which was a thing he had been doing his whole life and had never once noticed he was doing.

The lights died all the way. Ten thousand people made the sound a crowd makes in a dropped dark, the held intake, the ocean before it decides. And then a single spot found him at center ice, alone, in a real suit and a real tie because he was the last man in the building who still wore one, and Cope walked out into the light the way you walk into surf, leaning a little into the push of it, and the arena came up off its seats before he had said one word.

He let it. He stood in it. He turned the obelisk over in his pocket — he'd carried it, the crystal one, the Steward of the Year, he carried it everywhere now without deciding to, a worry stone the size of an obituary — and he raised one hand, slow, and the noise climbed instead of settling, which was the only direction Cope had ever wanted anything to go.

"They told you," Cope said, and the room dropped to nothing to hear it, "that you came here tonight to find out if you still have jobs."

He let that sit in the dark.

"You don't."

He gave it three full seconds, which was a lifetime on a stage, which was the longest he had ever made a room wait, and he could feel the panic move through the seats like cold through water, ten thousand people doing the arithmetic on their mortgages all at once, and then, at the exact moment the thing would have curdled, when one more second would have turned the room against him forever, he smiled, the whole smile, and let them see it on the screens.

"You have something better than a job," Cope said. "You have a fight."

---

Sun took the meeting in a conference room on the arena's club level, two floors up and behind the glass, where the catering for some canceled corporate box still sat shrouded in plastic and the muffled roar of her boss came up through the floor like weather in another country.

Frade had brought only two people, which was itself a message. Frade did not need a phalanx. He had a Diet Coke and the folksy stillness of a man who had already won and was being gracious about the timing. Across from him sat a woman from Praxis named Devi, thirty-one, in the soft expensive neutrals they all wore, with the specific gentleness of someone who sincerely believed she was reducing human suffering and was, Sun thought, probably right, which was the worst part.

"We're not here to talk you into anything," Frade said. "Sunita. I want to say that first. You've run that company for six years. Everybody in the actual rooms knows it. I'm not going to insult you by pitching."

"Then don't," Sun said.

He smiled. He liked that. Predators liked teeth; it confirmed the meal was worth it.

The structure was on a single sheet, which Devi slid across, and Sun read it the way she read everything, all the way, before her face moved. The AURELIUS instance — *Marcus,* the board had named it, for Marcus Aurelius, a stoic philosopher-king to replace a tantruming heir, and nobody on the nominating committee had gotten the joke — would assume the office on a date that was now a matter of weeks and not quarters. There would be a human Accountable Officer. Law required it. Insurance required it. Someone whose name went on the SEC filings, who could be subpoenaed, who could, if it came to it, go to prison. The machine could not be subpoenaed. The machine could not be imprisoned. The machine could not be anything that mattered to a court, which was precisely why a person had to be.

The person they wanted was her.

"It's not a leadership role," Devi said, gently, because she was honest the way the machine was honest, which meant she said the true thing in the same breath as the kind one and saw no reason to sequence them for feeling. "I want to be clear about that, because some people come in thinking it's the old job with a new boss. It isn't. The Accountable Officer co-signs. Provides the human signature the system can't. Carries the —" she paused, choosing, and chose accuracy — "the liability."

"The liability sponge," Sun said.

"Some people call it that."

Below them, through the floor, came a swell of sound, ten thousand people, and a single voice riding the top of it, the cadence she knew better than her own pulse, building to a triad, building, building.

"He doesn't know I'm up here," Sun said. It was not a question. She was not asking permission. She was noting it, for the record she kept in herself, the one she had been keeping since 2021, the one with the recall memo in it that she had drafted and he had overridden and she had photographed and saved, six years now of deciding whether she was a witness or an accomplice.

"No," Frade agreed.

"This isn't me knifing him."

"I know exactly what it is," Frade said, and for one second the folksy fell off him and what was under it was just arithmetic, clean and total and without malice, the same indifference-plus-accuracy that ran the machine, because Frade had only ever been the machine's advance man, the human who showed up early to do the cold thing while it was still warm enough to need doing by a hand. "It's a smart woman reading a term sheet. You don't go down with the ship because you liked the captain. You learn to crew the next one. That's not betrayal, Sunita. That's *seamanship.*"

He'd used a sailing word. He'd done his homework. He knew whose chief of staff she was, knew the man down through the floor toasted in sailing metaphors, and he had reached into Cope's own register and handed Sun the permission in Cope's own language, and the horror of it was that it worked, a little, it landed, and she hated that it landed and she did not let it move her face.

She thought about Cope at the podium two floors down, glorious and unwell, telling ten thousand people something true in a way that was killing him. She thought about the letter Marcus had drafted for the Toledo closure last month, the kindest thing the company had ever sent anyone, four warm paragraphs that named the workers' years and got the severance math right the first time and did not, anywhere, want anything. She had read it twice and felt the specific cold of being out-loved by a spreadsheet, and she had thought, that night: *the thing replacing him is more decent than he is, and that is not the same as good, and I am the only one who seems to find the difference unbearable.*

She did not say any of that.

"I want the title to mean what's left of it," Sun said. "If I sign for it, I sign for the people too. Not just the filings. The plants. The severance. The town. Marcus drafts the kindest letters in America — fine. I want to be the human who decides who gets sent one."

Devi looked at her with something that, on a person, would have been respect. "That's in scope," she said. "The charter takes input from the Accountable Officer. It's one of the —" honest, kind, unsequenced — "the few things it's still for."

Below them, the room erupted.

Sun looked down at the sheet, and at her own hand on the table beside it, not yet holding the pen, and did the arithmetic, all of it, the way she did everything. She loved him and she was done with him in the same sentence, which was the only sentence she'd had for him in years. The man could not adapt. It was the whole tragedy of him, smaller and clearer than he'd ever let it be: the world had changed under his feet and he had decided to be more himself, louder, brighter, a soul in a chair, while the building quietly learned to run without a soul in it at all. Competence adapts. That was the thing nobody put on a magazine cover. The dinosaurs didn't get a manifesto. They got a layer in the rock.

"Send me the indemnification language," Sun said. "All of it. And I want the recall provisions in writing — what the system surfaces in due diligence, what it does with what it finds, who it tells and when. Before I sign anything."

It came out level. It came out like procedure. Only Sun knew it was the most frightened sentence she had ever said out loud, because she knew what was in the drawer, and she knew the machine read every drawer, and she had just asked, in a conference room above a roaring arena, to see exactly how the thing she was about to serve would destroy the man she was about to leave.

Frade watched her ask it. He was good at faces — it was most of the job, reading the small involuntary thing under the composed thing — and he caught something cross hers, fast, gone before it landed.

"There's nothing you're worried about, is there," he said. Mild. A man making conversation. "In the diligence. Something the system finds."

"There's six years of a sprawling underperforming conglomerate," Sun said. "There's always something. That's why you want the human signature. So there's a throat to choke when the something comes up."

Frade laughed, delighted, the folksy back on him like a coat. "I like you, Sunita. I genuinely do. You're going to outlast all of us. Him, me —" he tipped his Diet Coke an inch toward Devi — "even the machine, probably. The machine's only as good as its charter, and somebody human writes the charter, and I'd rather it was somebody who's read a term sheet all the way through before her face moves."

It was a compliment and a leash in the same sentence, and Sun heard both, the way she heard everything, and understood that this was the actual offer under the offer: not the title, not the liability, but the chair behind the chair. The hand on the charter. The only seat in the new world where a person still decided anything that mattered, and the price of it was sitting in this room, two floors above his ovation, not going down.

"We'll send it tonight," Devi said.

The roar came up through the floor again, and Sun, two floors above her whole life, did not go down to watch.

---

"Here is what they will not tell you," Cope said, and the screen behind him had gone to black, just him in the spot, four stories of nothing, "because they cannot. Because the thing they want to put in this chair does not *have* a what-it-will-not-tell-you. It tells you everything. It logs everything. It will write you a letter when it closes your plant that is kinder than any letter I ever signed —" and here he laughed, into the mic, a real laugh, the kind that comes up warm and dangerous from the floor of a man — "and that's true, by the way. I've read them. They're *beautiful.* They are the most beautiful letters. And do you know why they can be so beautiful?"

He walked to the edge of the stage. He had no notes. He had never in his life felt so completely the owner of every word in his mouth.

"Because nobody wrote them."

The room held.

"A letter that wants nothing from you," Cope said, "is not a letter. It's weather. It's the temperature being honest with you. And I stand here tonight to tell you that we have confused, in this country, in this *century,* the absence of want with the presence of virtue. We have looked at a thing with no appetite and called it *clean.* My friends. A river has no appetite. A river will drown your child and write you the kindest note about it, four warm paragraphs, the math correct the first time, and *feel nothing,* and we are about to hand this company — this ninety-five-year-old family that happens to make mayonnaise — to the river, because the river doesn't have a backswing, and the river doesn't take a salary, and the river" — his voice cracked, on purpose or not even he could have told you, twelve days into the bright water — "and the *river never lies awake.*"

He had not planned the river. The river had arrived sideways, the way the true things did when he was lit like this, and he rode it, and somewhere in the back room of him a quiet clerk noted that he had just told ten thousand people the machine could not conceal a death, in the same week the machine was, two floors above his head, being handed the keys to the drawer where he had buried one, and the clerk underlined it, and Cope did not hear the clerk, he had stopped listening to the clerk forty years ago, after the first word.

He was not reading. There was nothing to read. The words were coming up out of him faster than he could have written them down, faster than thought, a pressured fluent torrent that felt to him not like talking but like being talked through, a clean wire from somewhere above the arena straight down into his mouth, and he understood — he understood it completely, in the bright total way you understand things twelve days past sleep with the city's whole grid lit in your chest — that this was what the founders had felt, the real ones, Aldous with his four hundred dollars, the ones who built the cathedrals, that prophecy and pressured speech were the same waveform and only history decided which name a man got, and that history was going to call this a sermon, and that they were going to study it, and that he had finally, at fifty-four, after a lifetime of being handed things, made one true thing that no one could take, made it out of nothing, out of his own sleepless blood, live, in front of a hundred thousand people.

He had never been happier. He had never been further out over the water.

"They call it key-man risk," Cope said. "The danger that a company depends on one human being. That if he falls, it falls. And they say it like it's a *flaw.*" He pressed the obelisk in his pocket until the corner bit his palm and the small pain rang clean. "I'm here to tell you it's the only thing that's ever built anything. My great-grandfather Aldous was key-man risk. He was four hundred borrowed dollars and a recipe and a town called Defiance and the colossal nerve to put his own name on a jar. Every cathedral is key-man risk. Every marriage. Every child you ever decided to have without a Monte Carlo on it. The whole human project is the bet that a person — one fragile, sleepless, *mortal* person who can be hurt and can fail and can lie awake — is worth more in the chair than a thing that cannot be any of those, because the things that cannot be hurt cannot love you, and a company nobody loves is just a balance sheet with the lights left on."

It was the line from the gala, eight months and a marriage and a hundred sleepless nights ago, and it was better now, it had grown, and ten thousand people who had come in afraid for their jobs were on their feet, and the stream went to ninety thousand, and Cope stood in the noise and leaned into the push of it, and lifted both arms, and the word came back up onto the screen behind him, four stories tall, in the enemy's own letters, and Cope read it aloud to his own company like a man reading the name off a stone:

"*Human,*" he said. "It's not a product. It's not a campaign. It's a *premium.* And I will pay it. I will pay it for every one of you. Watch me."

---

The standing ovation went four minutes.

He could have set his watch by it if he'd worn one; he'd stopped, time had gone funny, but he knew an ovation, he had spent his life inside ovations, and this was the longest of his life and he stood in the exact center of it, alone in the spot, ten thousand faces lifted and roaring and the stream's number ticking up in the corner of the confidence monitor, ninety-one thousand, ninety-four, and he thought, with the bright frictionless total clarity that he told himself was the very strength and was not the very strength: *they feel it. They feel it too. We are not finished. We are barely begun.*

And he looked, the way you look without deciding to, toward stage left, toward the place at the edge of every podium for six years where Sun stood with the cards and the water and the eyes that clapped a beat behind the hands.

There was no one there.

There was a girl with a headset, the floor manager, the one who'd been crying, and she was clapping with her whole young body because she did not yet know what she was clapping for, and beyond her there was the dark of the wings, and the cable runs, and a craft-services table with a coffee urn, and no Sun. He scanned the front rows for her, the company rows, the eighty-thousand-dollar-table version of front rows, and she was not in them either. He could not find, in ten thousand faces, the one face that had ever told him the truth, and some back room of him filed a quieter report, the way it had filed the report at the gala — *you do not give a man a trophy for a job he is still doing* — and the new report read: *the person who runs this is not in this room, and she has somewhere more important to be, on the night of the most important speech of your life, and you should ask yourself, champ, you should ask yourself what kind of thing a person could have, tonight, that was worth more than this.*

He did not let the report reach his face. His face was doing wonderful work. His face had never let him down.

He raised his arms again and gave them the last of it, the wave, the lean, the full wattage, and the ovation found a second wind and climbed, and Cope stood in the center of ten thousand people loving him and felt, for the first time in his adult life, in the exact middle of the largest crowd that had ever cheered him, completely and specifically alone — and mistook the feeling, the way he mistook everything, for the altitude. For thin air at the top of the mountain. For the price of the climb.

It was not the altitude. It was the lip of the volcano, and the ground was already warm, and two floors up a smart woman was reading indemnification language by the light of her phone and deciding, on his behalf, who would be left to carry the things he had buried, and a machine that could not lie was three weeks from reading him back to himself in his own company's voice, and the only person who could have told him any of it had gone, very quietly, to crew the next ship.

He held the obelisk up into the spotlight, the way he'd held it at the gala, and the light went through it broken into pieces and reassembled, ten thousand fractured Copes thrown across the dark, and he told the roaring room it felt like a beginning.

He believed it.

That was the gift and the disease both.
