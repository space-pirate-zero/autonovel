# Coda — Defiance, After

> TRANSMISSION ∞ — "The Negative Space"
>
> One year on, in the founding town, on an ordinary afternoon. The
> machine runs the company well. Better, even — cleaner books, kinder
> letters, no scandals, no dead. And yet the broadcast picks up
> something it cannot name as either sentiment or superstition: an
> absence, shaped exactly like the thing we spent three hundred pages
> deciding we were better off without. We automated the corruption. We
> automated the appetite. We may have automated something else, too. This
> is Space Pirate Zero. We don't have the answer. We just have the
> signal. Keep it tuned.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

A year on, the apostrophe is still gone.

Nobody put it back. The machine that runs Mercer-Dale now had, in its first quarter, two hundred and forty open questions about the Defiance property — taxes, easements, the cost of demolition against the cost of a roof — and it answered all of them in the warm flat font that wanted nothing, and not one of the answers was about the missing letter on the dried-blood brick near the roofline. MERCER S. A tooth knocked out of the word, and no instrument in the council of subsystems had a slot for it, because a thing that feels nothing does not notice a thing that is gone. That is the whole trouble with absence. You need the soul to miss it, and the soul was the line item they retired.

The town is fine. That has to be said plainly, because the broadcast is not in the business of pretending otherwise.

It is, by every number the machine logs, more fine than it was. The plant did not reopen — the math was never close, it was never going to be close, the math was the entire point — but the fund did what the man's apology never could, and the fund is run by no one, which is to say it is run perfectly. It does not skim. It does not call a press conference. It does not arrive in the curbside door of an SUV and let a boy with a gimbal walk a slow arc around its grief. It wires the money. It got the severance math right the first time and then it got the next thing right and the next, a clinic on Clinton Street, a line item for the credit union the front office used to hold, scholarships paid out in full and on time to kids whose grandmothers ran the second-shift relish line. Donna's boy graduated Bowling Green. The fund paid the back half without being asked, because the machine reads everything, including a partial scholarship, and saw no reason to make a person ask.

It is the kindest thing the company has ever done for the town.

It is also the first thing the company has done for the town that no one in the town can thank.

---

The memorial is still down on the river walk, where Clinton gives out into the flat green nothing along the Maumee.

The maple is taller now. Past the staked years, into the years where a tree just stands. Karen Lasko, 34. And her son. Petey Lasko, 6. *Taken home too soon* — the town's words, cut into the fieldstone before the town knew what Cope knew, before the plea put the memo and the Saturday and the eleven minutes into a record, before *taken* turned out to have a hand attached to it. The town knows now. The town left the words anyway. There is a kind of mercy in a stone that does not update.

Someone still leaves a pinwheel. Blue, cheap foil, staked in the dirt at the foot of the maple, and when the breeze comes off the water it turns and throws the light, on and off, on and off, to whoever is on the walk, which on an ordinary afternoon is no one. Nobody knows who replaces it. It is not the fund — the fund does not have a budget line for pinwheels; the fund priced the reconstructed structure and the reopened claims and the regulatory penalties to the dollar and had no field for this. So it is a person. Somewhere in Defiance is a person who buys a blue pinwheel when the last one goes ragged and walks it down to the river and presses the stick into the ground and stands a minute and goes home, and that person is doing the one thing in this whole town the machine cannot do, and it costs them nothing anyone could quote, and they do it anyway, which is either the smallest thing left or the only thing that was ever real, and the broadcast cannot tell you which.

The bronze man still holds out his hand in the triangle park where Clinton meets Second. Offering you something. A jar, a future. *Made by hands, for homes.* The granite has not changed. The hands are gone.

---

Cope is alive.

That has to be said too, and said carefully, because the broadcast knows the shape of the story you want and will not hand it to you. He is not redeemed. There was no scene where the town forgave him, because the town did not, and the broadcast will not stage one for your comfort. He pleaded to what a person can plead to — only a person can plead to anything; that was the whole cruel architecture of it — involuntary manslaughter, two counts, the concealment folded in, and the State of Ohio gave him thirty months in a low brick facility outside Marion, ninety minutes from the stone with the two names on it, and he is in it now. Do the arithmetic; the town did. Thirty months comes out to about a day and a half for every day the memo sat in the drawer — or, if you prefer the other denominator, fifteen months per Lasko — and the broadcast reports the number without comment, because the comment would be redundant. The law needed a body. It took the one on offer. It did not need much of it. He does not run anything. He is not the name on any filing. He refused the one title they offered him on the way out, the liability sponge, the human signature for the machine, and refusing it was the second-cleanest thing he ever did, and like the first it saved nothing.

He sends money to the fund. What a man can send from a commissary account is, on the fund's scale, nothing — a rounding error's rounding error — and the machine logs it anyway, accurately, the way it logs rain. Quietly, which is new for him, quiet being the one register he spent fifty-six years unable to find. It is not enough. He knows it is not enough; that knowledge is the closest thing to a soul he has left and it is finally, too late, awake. The fund accepts it the way the fund accepts everything, accurately, without mercy and without malice, and logs it, and applies it to a clinic he will never visit, in a town that does not want his face, for a debt that does not have a number, and that is correct, and it is not nearly enough, and both of those are true, and the machine sees no reason to sequence them for feeling.

He is not the point. He was never quite the point. He just thought he was, which was the disease, and the disease is in remission now, which is not the same as cured, which is not the same as good.

---

Here is what the broadcast picks up, on the ordinary afternoon, standing over the town like a specimen under glass.

The books are clean. The letters are kind. Nobody settled a woman this year and struck her from an org chart; nobody sat on a memo for ten months while a heater ticked in a stranger's bedroom; nobody poured a floor over a room with two people in it. There is no drawer with a false back anymore, because there is no one with a reason to build one. The thing in the chair does not get high, does not get hard, does not get bored, does not need to be loved at two in the morning by someone it outranks. We took the appetite out and the corruption came out with it, clean, the whole rotten flywheel, and the company is better, and the town is better, and the dead are exactly as dead as they were, and on all of this the arithmetic and the broadcast agree.

And still.

There is an absence the instruments register and cannot name. Not a ghost — the broadcast does not deal in ghosts, the horror here was always mundane. It is shaped like the thing the whole book spent its length deciding we were better off without. We automated the corruption. Good. We automated the appetite. Good, and overdue, and itemized. But the man who buried the recall was also, on a counter stool at the Maumee Diner, the man who knew inside a minute that Donna's boy wrestled, and meant it, and the meaning was real even when the man was a fraud, and the machine that would never bury the recall is also the thing that will never, not once, want the boy to call. We took out the hand that hid the memo. The same motion took out the hand that buys the pinwheel. We are not sure those were two different hands. We have looked, and we cannot find the seam, and that is the signal, and we are not going to pretend we have resolved it for you on the way out.

So we hand it to you, the way the verdict was handed and not answered, the way the whole broadcast has been handed and not answered.

Was something lost.

Not the man — keep him exactly as guilty as he is. Not the chair; the chair never needed a heartbeat in it, he was right about that and wrong about everything that mattered more. Something smaller and harder to file. The thing that buys a blue pinwheel for a stranger's child and presses it into the ground for no one to see. The thing that has no line in the ledger because it has never, in all the machine's tireless reading, found anyone willing to write it down. Maybe it was never worth its cost. Maybe it came bundled, inseparable, with the appetite and the lie, one switch for both, and we threw the switch and got exactly what we asked for and a little less than we knew. The broadcast does not know. The broadcast is a pirate signal from a salvage ship, not an oracle.

Down on the river walk the pinwheel turns. The light comes off the water and goes on and off, on and off, to no one, all afternoon.

This is Space Pirate Zero.

We don't have the answer.

We just have the signal.

Keep it tuned.

*— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*
