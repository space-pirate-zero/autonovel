# Chapter 25 — No NDA

> TRANSMISSION 25 — "The Toast He Can't Give"
>
> Watch a man who has talked his way out of everything walk up to a truth he cannot toast over, cannot triad, cannot charm. The lawyers beg him to take the settlement, the silence, the soft landing. For once — too late to save a single thing he wanted to save — he opens his mouth to say exactly what happened and exactly who did it. Next stop: the difference, finally, between a performance and a confession.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

The settlement was sixty-one pages and it loved him almost as much as the machine did.

That was the thing Cope kept coming back to, in the conference room on the fourteenth floor of Kessler Brandt that had no name on the door because the rooms at that altitude of lawyering did not get named for bankers, they got numbered, and this one was 14-C, and the coffee was the good coffee, and the table was the long kind, and across it sat four people who were paid more by the hour than Desmond made in a season, and all four of them wanted, with a unity that was almost moving, to save his life. The document in front of him was a kindness. It was built like a kindness. Read it the right way and you would think a great many smart and expensive people had spent a long weekend trying to find a version of the world in which Prescott Mercer IV walked out of all of this with his liberty and a number and his name.

There was just the one clause.

"It's a mutual non-disparagement and confidentiality provision," Levin said, for the third time, in the voice you use for the third time. Levin was the senior of the four, sixty-some, white hair he kept long enough to suggest he had once been interesting. "Standard. Boilerplate, honestly. The company doesn't characterize the events, you don't characterize the events, the record is sealed, the Defiance matter resolves through the fund, the fund issues a statement, and the statement uses the word *tragedy* and does not use the word *recall* and does not, anywhere, use the word *you.*"

"It uses the word *Hearthstone,*" Cope said. He had read it. For once in his life he had read the actual pages.

"It uses *the product line.*"

"Right." Cope turned a page he had already turned. "And the two people."

A small silence with money in it.

"The decedents are referred to," Levin said carefully, "as the decedents."

---

He had not slept, and this time it was just not sleeping, the ordinary kind, the kind a person does in a hotel near a courthouse when there is a thing the next day, no folded paper involved, nothing in any drawer because there were no drawers left that were his. Eleven days clean, which Adler kept saying like it was an accomplishment and which felt to Cope like being skinned. The world had a flat true color to it now. No clean high overexposure. Just the room, lit the way rooms are lit, and the faces in it lit that way too, and he could see, in this new flat light, exactly how good the deal was and exactly what it cost and that those were the same fact.

He understood the deal completely. That was the horror of being sober: he understood things now, all the way down, and could not un-understand them.

If he signed, the sealed record stayed sealed. The Hearthstone stayed a *thermal event profile.* The two people in the house in Defiance — a mother and her six-year-old, the house on Sherwood Street, a January night in 2021, the space heater with the Mercer name on the grille doing the one thing the memo on his desk had told him it might do — stayed *the decedents,* stayed a number in a fund, stayed a *tragedy* in a press release written, he had no doubt, more humanely than he could write it himself.

And he, Cope, stayed a man who had *gotten out ahead of a recall scare.* That was the version that survived if he signed. He had told it at dinners. People had nodded. Protecting the brand was a virtue in the rooms he stood in.

The fund was real money and would do real good. He believed that. Adler had taught him to notice when he was using a true thing to cover a buried thing, and he noticed it now, and made himself keep the true thing true *and* keep looking at what it covered. Both. At once. It was the hardest thing he had ever done and it was just sitting in a chair reading.

"What happens to me," Cope said. "If I sign. Walk me through the soft landing one more time. I want to hear it out loud."

Levin brightened, because this was the part where he was good. "You resign for health reasons, which are documented and real. The company indemnifies you under the existing officer agreements for the governance matters. The Defiance settlement is civil; with the seal, the criminal exposure"—he tipped his hand, a man weighing fog—"becomes a question of what a prosecutor can build without the record, and the record is what builds it. You keep the Greenwich house. You keep, after the clawback, a number that is still a number most people would—"

"How much is left."

A glance among the four.

"After the clawback and the fund contribution and the divorce," said the woman to Levin's right, whose name was Okonjo and who was the one Cope had decided he trusted, "you are looking at a low eight figures. Protected. Yours." She did not soften it and he loved her a little for that. "You would not be rich the way you were rich. You would be rich the way a dentist who sold three practices is rich. You would be fine."

Fine. He turned the word over the way he had turned the obelisk over, and the room went through it broken into pieces and did not reassemble.

"And if I don't sign."

---

Okonjo took that one too, because Levin's face had started doing a thing.

"If you decline the confidentiality provision and make a full statement on the record—a real one, the one you keep saying you want to make—then you are not characterizing the events. You are *establishing* them. You're handing a prosecutor the timeline, the memo, the Saturday, your own decision in your own words. The indemnification almost certainly voids, because indemnification doesn't cover a knowing—" She stopped, recalibrated, gave him the clean version. "It doesn't cover what you'd be admitting. The clawback goes from a negotiation to a foregone conclusion. The fund stays, but it stops being a gesture and starts being evidence. And the criminal—" She held his eyes. "Reckless homicide, or involuntary manslaughter if the prosecutor is feeling gentle, depending on what the record shows about the eleven minutes. You would very likely go to prison, Mr. Mercer. Not the kind with tennis. The other kind. And you would do it broke, because the statement that sends you there is the same statement that strips the indemnity that was paying for me."

"So I'd be paying you to lose."

"You'd be paying me to do the thing correctly," Okonjo said. "Which is not the same as winning. I want to be very clear, because everyone in this room except possibly you thinks I'm describing a catastrophe." She closed her folder. "I am. It's a catastrophe. I'm not going to dress it as anything else. I went to law school to keep clients out of exactly the room you'd be walking into."

Levin found his voice. "Cope. I've known your family thirty years. I sat at your father's funeral. Pres III did not raise a martyr. He raised a Mercer." He said it the way you play a trump. "There is no version of this where telling the truth saves you. There is no jury that gives you back your life for being honest. The honesty is a *cost,* not a strategy. You'd be spending everything—everything—to buy a feeling."

And here it was: the place where, a year ago, a month ago, Cope would have stood up. Found the room. Taken the Mercer Inhale and said something with a triad in it and three generations of brass behind it, something about grit and gratitude and guts, something that made the catastrophe feel like a backswing, and watched the four expensive faces decide not to fight him, and taken the not-fighting as agreement.

He could feel the toast assembling in him even now. That was the thing nobody warned you about getting better — the machinery did not stop. The toast came up like reflux. *Gentlemen. Ms. Okonjo. My great-grandfather Aldous used to say—*

He shut his mouth on it.

He sat in the silence where the toast would have gone, and it was the loudest silence of his life, and the four of them watched him not perform, and did not understand what they were seeing, because they had only ever seen the other thing.

"Aldous never said anything," Cope said. "I want you to know that. I made him up. Forty years of him and he never said a word. I needed a dead man to agree with me so I made one and put a recipe in his mouth." He looked at the document. "I'm very good at putting words in the mouths of people who can't argue. The decedents, for instance."

---

He went down in the elevator alone, which Sun would never have allowed, except Sun was not his anymore in the way she had been his. She was the Accountable Officer now, or about to be — the title sat on her like a coat one size too heavy — and there were things she could not be in the room for, because she had her own name to keep clean now, her own filing to be liable for, and one of the cruelties of the new arrangement was that the person who had spent twelve years catching the second card out of his hand could no longer afford to be standing next to his hand.

She was in the lobby anyway. Of course she was.

"They told you the number," she said. Not a question. She had her glasses up in her hair. The lobby of Kessler Brandt slid gray and expensive across the empty lenses.

"Low eight figures. Fine, like a dentist."

"And the other thing."

"Prison, broke, the bad kind." He found he could say it lightly, not as a toast, just as a fact a man carries through a lobby. "Or I sign, and the house on Sherwood Street stays a *thermal event profile,* and two people stay decedents, and I stay a man who got out ahead of a recall scare, and I keep Greenwich and the dentist money and my name, and the fund does some real good, which it does, Sun, it does, I'm not pretending it doesn't."

"It does," she agreed. She had drafted the recall memo in 2021. She had kept a copy. She had spent six years deciding whether she was a witness or an accomplice, and the document upstairs would let her stop deciding — would seal her copy in with the rest, would make her, finally and officially, neither. The settlement was a kindness to her too. He understood that now, in the flat true light. Everyone in 14-C was being saved. The deal was a machine for saving everyone, and the only thing it cost was the truth about who killed the people in Defiance, which was a thing the dead could not spend and so would not miss.

"You kept the memo," he said.

"I kept the memo."

"If I make the statement, the seal doesn't happen. Your copy comes out. You—" He stopped. He was doing it again, the thing where he found the reason it would hurt someone else so he could call his silence mercy. He had done it for thirty years. He made himself finish the sentence the true way. "You'd be exposed. Because I overrode you. The memo shows you were right and I was the one who—it shows you tried. It shows you tried, Sun." He heard his own voice do something it had not done in the building in years. "I'd be the only one telling a jury you tried."

Sun pulled her glasses down. She looked at him for a second too long, the way she had in the car the night they gave him the obelisk, except the thing in her face was different now, because the man she was looking at was different, smaller, finally here.

"You think I want that," she said. "To be saved by your seal. To get the title and never have to know which one I was." She let it sit. "You spent twelve years not noticing what I'd settle for. Don't start now by deciding it's the silence. That one's mine to choose, Cope. Not yours to give me."

It was the most anyone had ever refused him, and she did it in three parts, because she had trained him to receive things in threes, and the third part got past the charm because there was no charm left to get past.

---

That night he did not go up high to think.

There was no Aerie to go up to — there was, the lease ran another nine years, the family refused to sell, but it was Marcus's now in every way that the word *now* meant anything, the museum of Mercer men kept on as a kind of period room, and Cope did not have the stomach for being an exhibit in it. He went back to the hotel near the courthouse, the Marriott, a real one, carpet you could describe to a stranger and they'd know it, and he sat on the end of the bed in the lamp light with the sixty-one pages on the bedspread and a legal pad on his knee and he did the thing he had avoided his entire life.

He went into the room where it was kept.

He had built a cathedral of metaphors so he would never have to. *Thermal event profile.* *Got out ahead of it.* *Protect the brand.* Adler had taken the doors off one by one over eleven flat sober days, patient, incapable of being charmed, saying *the word is recall, Cope, say the word,* saying *they have names, do you know their names,* and he hadn't, that was the obscene part, he had killed two people to protect a quarter and never learned their names, had filed them under *the decedents* the way the lawyers did because the lawyers had learned it from men like him.

He knew them now. He had made himself learn them. He wrote them at the top of the legal pad, first, before anything else, in his own hand, the hand that had underlined a word in the dark the night of the gala.

Then under the names he wrote what he had done.

Not the version. Not *we got out ahead of a recall scare.* He wrote the Saturday. He wrote the memo Sun sent him and the eleven minutes and the number he had been protecting and the window of time he had decided was worth the *thermal event profile,* which was a phrase he now wrote out in full and then crossed out and replaced with *the heaters catching fire,* because that was what it had meant, that was what he had decided to risk, in his best-thinking eleven minutes, on his best-thinking day. He wrote that the board had not known because he had not told them. He wrote that the concealment, not the heater, not even the eleven minutes, was the thing — that a machine doing nothing but reading every memo it was given had found in an afternoon what he had spent six years building rooms inside himself to not find, and that this was not the machine being cruel, the machine could not be cruel, it was the machine being the thing they had bought it to be, which was a thing that could not look away, which was a thing he had never once been.

He wrote: *I am the strongest argument for my own replacement. I would like that on the record too.*

He stopped. He read it back, the way he read everything back. There was no bell-struck feeling. There was no high. There was a man on the end of a hotel bed at two in the morning, sober in every sense, with two names at the top of a legal pad, and the pages were not good, were not even well-made, had no triad in them and no Aldous and no soul-in-the-chair, and would buy him nothing — not his liberty, not the dentist money, not Greenwich, not the name, not Margaux, who would read about it like everyone else, not the chair, which was gone, not the company, which was Frade's now in everything but the slow paperwork, not even forgiveness, because the only people who could forgive him were the two at the top of the pad and they were exactly as dead as he had decided they could afford to be.

It would not buy back a single thing he had spent his life wanting.

He understood that completely. He sat in the understanding for a long time. And then he did the thing that the four expensive people in 14-C and Levin who had sat at his father's funeral and Frade who had already won and the patient tireless machine that had read him back to himself in his own company's voice could none of them have predicted, because they had only ever seen the performance and this was the other thing, the thing the performance had been built for forty years to hide the absence of.

First, around one, he found himself with the phone in his hand and a number on the screen he had not consciously summoned — no name on it, because you do not put a name on that name — and his thumb hung over it for a long time. That was the new horror the rooms with the bad coffee had not advertised: you got to watch yourself reach. The wanting did not arrive with trumpets; it arrived as logistics, a practical thought about a practical man who could be at a Marriott in Wilmington inside forty minutes, and the room was very quiet, and the carpet was describable, and his heart was going wrong in his chest like a bird in a bag. He pressed the side button until the screen went black and sat in the dark holding the dead phone like a railing. It did not feel like a victory. It felt like standing in a doorway, counting. He understood, in the long minute before his pulse came back down, that he would be doing this exact thing, in some room or another, every night for the rest of his life, and that the rooms called it recovery because the accurate word would empty the rooms.

Then he picked up the pen and he signed the legal pad. Not the settlement. The legal pad. The statement. His own name, the whole of it, Prescott Mercer IV, at the bottom of the worst sentences he had ever written and the only true ones, and he did it with the same hand that had given a card to a crying coat-check girl in a beautiful men's room a lifetime ago without registering the doing, except this time he registered it, this time it was a decision, the first real one he had ever made alone.

Then he put the sixty-one pages back in their envelope unsigned and set them on the desk where the next person would have to deal with them, and he called Okonjo, because she was the one who would do the thing correctly, and it was 2 a.m. and she answered on the second ring like someone who had been waiting up for exactly this and dreading it.

"No NDA," Cope said. "I'm making the statement. The full one. With the names in it. Tomorrow, on the record, no seal." His voice did not perform. It just carried the fact. "I know what it costs. You told me twice and you were right both times. I'd like you in the room if you'll still have me as a client, and I understand if a lawyer who went to law school to keep people out of that room doesn't want to walk one in."

A silence on the line. Then the small sound of a pen finding paper, because she was already working.

"You'll have my appearance as personal counsel by eight," Okonjo said, "and a revised engagement letter by nine, because the man who has been paying me is about to stop existing and I bill the man who exists." A breath, nothing in it he could use. "I'll be in the room, Mr. Mercer. Not because it's right. Because somebody has to keep the record exact while you do this, and exact is what I sell."

He hung up. He did not sleep, and it was the ordinary kind, and out the Marriott window there was no circuit diagram of his own genius, just a parking structure and a strip of orange sky over the courthouse where in the morning he would say exactly what happened and exactly who did it, and lose everything it was still possible to lose, and not get a single thing back, and do it anyway, for its own sake, in the rubble, with two names at the top of the page.

He had spent fifty-four years learning which sentences to leave in the kitchen.

In the morning he was going to bring out all of them.
