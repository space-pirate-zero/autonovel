# Chapter 1 — The Premium Blend

They gave him a crystal obelisk for being the last of something, and Cope held it up to the light and told the room it felt like a beginning.

The room loved that. The room was nine hundred people in the Conrad Vance Ballroom of a hotel that had, eleven months earlier, replaced its own chief executive with a Praxis unit and seen its stock go up eighteen percent on the news, and the ballroom was named for a banker whose firm no longer employed a single human officer above the rank of greeter, and not one of the nine hundred found any of this strange, because Cope was up there in a real bow tie he had tied himself, saying the word *beginning* the way his great-grandfather used to say grace, and the chandeliers were doing what chandeliers are paid to do, and the lamb had been good.

"My great-grandfather Aldous used to say a man's worth is three things," Cope said. "What he builds. What he keeps. And what he's willing to lose to keep it." He let the obelisk catch the light again. He had not, in fact, slept since Tuesday in any way a doctor would sign off on, and it was Thursday, and the not-sleeping had given the whole evening a clean overexposed quality, like the world had been color-corrected one stop too bright, and he liked it. He felt marvelous. He felt like the inside of a struck bell. "Aldous started this company in a town called Defiance with a recipe for mayonnaise and four hundred borrowed dollars, and tonight a very kind committee has decided to give me a paperweight for being the only one of us left who has to *sleep.*"

Laughter. The good kind, that comes up warm from the floor.

He did not say: who has to sleep, and lately can't. He had learned forty years ago which sentences to leave in the kitchen.

The award was real. *The American Enterprise Institute for Leadership* — Cope had never been able to keep the institutes straight, there were so many now, each one a little louder than the last about the disappearance of the very thing it gave dinners to honor — had named him their Steward of the Year, and the citation, which a woman with a lanyard had read aloud before they handed him up the steps, called him "the last human chief executive in the consumer Fortune 100, a custodian of the irreplaceable." Custodian. Irreplaceable. He had heard the words as a crown. It was only now, holding the cold glass thing, eight hundred miles into a night with no bottom to it, that some back room of him filed a quieter report, which was that you do not give a man a trophy for a job he is still doing. You give him a trophy on the way out. The citation was an obituary, beautifully set, and the lamb was the wake.

He did not let the report reach his face. His face was doing wonderful work. His face had never let him down.

"People ask me," Cope said, "they ask me, Cope, aren't you afraid? The machines run thirty-one of the hundred now. They run them — and I'll be honest with you, my friends, they run some of them *well.*" A murmur; he rode it. "They don't take a salary you could feed Toledo on. They don't have affairs. They don't, God knows, have a backswing like mine." The backswing got him the room again. "And every quarter another good board does the arithmetic and decides the chair doesn't need a heartbeat in it. So they ask me — aren't you afraid?"

He set the obelisk down on the lectern. He took, here, the pause he had taken in a thousand rooms, the one Sun called *the Mercer Inhale*, and out past the lights he could see the round tables going on forever, and at the nearest one his own table, Mercer-Dale's table, eighty thousand dollars of table, and the seat to the right of his empty chair was empty. Margaux had said she had a thing. She had not said what kind of thing a person could have, on a Thursday, that was worth more than watching her husband be the last of his species. The empty chair had a little reserved card on it with her name in calligraphy and nothing behind the card.

"I'm not afraid," Cope said, and meant it, and that was either the truest or the most dangerous thing he said all night. "Because a machine can run a company. I've seen it. It can run it on rails, run it on time, run it cheaper than a man. But it cannot *love* one. And a company nobody loves is just a balance sheet with the lights left on. I have loved this one since I was nine years old and my grandfather walked me through the Defiance plant and let me put my whole arm up to the elbow in a vat of our mayonnaise." He spread his hands. "Some things you cannot put a machine in charge of. Grief. Mercy. A christening. *Mayonnaise.* And so help me, leadership."

Standing ovation. Of course a standing ovation. He stood in it the way you stand in surf, leaning a little into the push of it, and he thought, with the bright frictionless clarity that he told himself was the very tiredness and was not the very tiredness: *they get it. They feel it too. We are not finished. We are barely begun.* And down at the table Sun Rao was clapping with her hands but not her eyes, and the empty chair stayed empty, and somewhere in the kitchen of him the quiet report sat unread on a desk.

He gave them four more minutes at the table — a hand on a senator's shoulder, the senator's wife told she hadn't aged a day, a photograph he leaned into like weather — and then he excused himself the way he always excused himself, with a joke about his prostate and his age, both of which were lies, and he went to find the men's room off the coat check where the attendant knew him.

It was a beautiful men's room. Marble the color of good butter, an attendant named Desmond who had worked the Vance for thirty years and got a Mercer-Dale ham every Christmas because Cope remembered things like that, remembered them the way the machine remembered things, which was to say completely and for use. Cope tipped him a hundred and asked after his grandson and went into the far stall and stood for a moment with his back against the door, and the noise of the ballroom went to a hum, and he took out the little folded paper he kept folded the particular way his guy in the '90s had taught him to fold it, *so it can't open in the pocket, Cope, a gentleman doesn't spill,* and he did what he had been thinking about doing since the second course, which was the real reason he had stopped eating the second course.

After, the world came back up to pitch like an orchestra finding the note. The bell-struck feeling, clean and high and total, the one he had been calling, all night, *the applause.* He looked at himself in the good mirror. Fifty-four and the camera still loved him and the jaw was still the jaw. *You'll get the company because you're a Mercer,* his father said, from oils, from everywhere. Cope smiled at the mirror until the smile was right and said, very quietly, to no one, "Watch me," and could not have told you, if you'd asked, what he meant to do or to whom.

On the way out the coat-check girl was new and young and had been crying about something before her shift in the way you could still see at the edges, and Cope, who could not pass a sadness without trying to fix it onto himself, leaned on the marble counter and got her laughing inside ninety seconds — the toast in miniature, the full wattage, *what's a face like that doing handing out other people's coats* — and got her name, Bryce, and gave her a card, the personal one, *if you ever want to do something more interesting than this, you call me, I mean it,* and meant it, the way he meant everything, completely and for ninety seconds. He did not register doing it. It was not a decision. It was the groove the record had been cut in. He was three feet down the corridor feeling generous and enormous before Sun appeared at his elbow out of the architecture the way she did, and took the second card out of his hand before he could give that one to anyone too, and said, "The car's up," in a voice with thirty thousand miles on it.

---

In the car he was electric and Sun was tired, which was the usual division of labor.

"You moved a hand," Cope said. "On *cheaper than a man.* I saw it. That was you wanting to cut it."

"It was me wanting to cut it," Sun agreed. She had her glasses up in her hair and the city slid orange across the lenses as they went down Sixth. Forty-one floors of it ahead of them, the old limestone tower lit at the crown the way Aldous had insisted on in 1961, *so a man coming up the avenue knows there's somebody home.* "Frade's going to use that clip. 'Cheaper than a man.' You handed him a chyron."

"Let him have it. People are sick of cheaper. People are starving for *better.*" He had his phone out and was thumbing at it too fast, the screen too bright in the dark car, and he was already somewhere past this conversation, three turns ahead, the way he got. "What if that's the whole thing, Sun. What if the entire play is just — say it out loud. Stop hiding from the word. *Human.* Lead with it. A premium product for a premium —"

"You haven't slept."

"I slept."

"You told me on Tuesday you'd cracked the back nine of consciousness. That's not a thing people who sleep say."

He laughed, delighted to have been quoted to himself. He did this — he loved his own lines best when they came back in someone else's mouth, it proved they had legs. "The back nine of consciousness. That's good. Put that somewhere." He went back to the phone. Outside, a bus shelter slid by with an ad on it, a Praxis ad, just the wordmark and four words in that soft sans-serif they all used now, the typeface of the people who were going to be reasonable at you until you were gone: *Leadership as a service.* And under it, smaller, the joke that wasn't a joke: *No off-sites.* "They're advertising on bus shelters now," Cope said. "To whom. Who's the buyer at a bus shelter. The driver?"

"Boards read bus shelters. Boards' wives do." Sun pulled her glasses down. "Cope. Monday. We have to talk about Monday before Monday."

"Monday's a good day. I love a Monday."

"Tidewater's at four and a half percent. They'll cross five this week and then they have to file, and when they file it's not a rumor anymore, it's a 13D, and the 13D is going to have a name in it, and the name is going to be a machine, and you are going to want to have already decided who you are when that happens instead of deciding it live on Halloran's show." She said it in three parts because she had trained him, years ago, to receive things in threes; it was the only shape that reliably got past the charm and into the man. "Decide it this weekend. Please. Not Monday."

He had heard about a third of it. The good third, the part with the fight in it. "Renata used to say I do my best thinking on Saturdays," he said, not because it mattered, just because the name had floated up out of the bright tired water of him, the way names did when he was like this, and he watched it land in the car and saw Sun go very still in the front seat in a way he was too lit to read.

"You shouldn't say that name in a car, Cope," she said finally, evenly. "You shouldn't say it anywhere. We've talked about this."

"Said what name?" He was already gone again, back in the phone, back in the manifesto that did not exist yet but was beginning to assemble itself in him the way weather assembles, a pressure, a charge. *The Human Premium.* He could see it on a stage. He could see it on a cover. "Drop me at the office," he said. "I'm not going to Greenwich tonight, the house is — Margaux's got a thing." He said *thing* with no weight at all, a man stepping easily over a hole in the floor. "I'll work. I think better up high."

Sun looked at him in the mirror for a second too long. He smiled at her in it, the whole smile, the one that had closed the Pelletier deal and married Margaux and gotten three generations of board members to vote against their own arithmetic, and it still worked, he could see it work, he could see her decide not to fight him tonight, and he took the not-fighting as agreement, which was a thing he had been doing his whole life and had never once noticed he was doing.

---

Up in the Aerie at one in the morning the city was a circuit diagram of his own genius.

That was how it looked to him, anyway, with the lights of his office off and the lights of the world on. Every lit window a node. Eight million people down there, and somewhere in the math of them was the sentence — he was sure of it the way you are sure of things at one a.m. with a fine bright wire humming in your chest — the one sentence that would make all of them understand why the chair needed a man in it. Not *a* man. A man who *loved* it. He stood at the glass with his bow tie undone and the crystal obelisk in his hand because he had carried it up without deciding to, and he turned it over and the city went through it broken into pieces and reassembled.

Behind him the suite was a museum and he was its last exhibit. His father's portrait, Pres III, in oils, looking out over the conference table he'd died at the head of — not at, *at the head of,* there was a difference and the family observed it. His grandfather's putter on its little brass cradle. And the photograph Cope loved best and never explained: himself at nine, in short pants, grinning, one arm buried to the elbow in a steel vat at the Defiance plant, mayonnaise to the bicep, and behind him a row of men in paper hats who had made the thing he was drowning his arm in, men whose names he had known once and lost, men whose town it was.

There had been a heater. The thought arrived sideways, the way the true things did when he was lit like this, slipping in under the manifesto. The Hearthstone. A good little product, the insurance arm had cross-sold it, a space heater with the Mercer name on the grille, and there had been a — what was the word the lawyers had liked — a *thermal event profile,* and a memo, and a Saturday, his best thinking day, and a decision he had made about a quarter and a number and a window of time, and the decision had been the kind you make in eleven minutes and live inside for the rest of your life without ever quite going into the room where it's kept. He told a version of it at dinners sometimes — *a recall scare, we got out ahead of it, you've got to protect the brand* — and people nodded, because protecting the brand was a virtue in the rooms he stood in, and the version he told had no town in it and no Saturday and no house. He did not, standing at the glass, go into the room. He almost did. The tiredness had taken the doors off their hinges and for a second the hallway to it stood open and dark. Then the manifesto came back, warm, electric, *The Human Premium,* and he turned from the window toward his desk, and the moment closed over like water over a thrown stone, and he would not think of it again for a long time, and the not-thinking was the most expensive thing he owned.

He sat down at his father's desk and opened the second drawer, the one with the false back his grandfather had built for cigars and his father had used for a flask and Cope used for the thing Cope used it for, and he set himself up another, neat and quick and competent, a man maintaining a machine he was sure he had under perfect control because it had never once failed to start. He did not turn on the lamp. He wrote, in the dark, with his thumbs, on the small bright screen, the first line of the thing that was going to save him, and it was very good, he was sure of it, he was sure of it the way you are sure of things at one a.m. when you have not slept since Tuesday and they have just handed you a trophy for going extinct:

*They will tell you a company is a machine. I am here to tell you a company is a family that happens to make mayonnaise, and you cannot fire your way to a family, and you cannot compute your way to a soul.*

He read it back twice. The bell-struck feeling rang through him, clean and high and total. Out past the glass the city held still, all its nodes lit, eight million people who did not know yet that they were waiting for him. He thought: *this is the best I have ever felt.* He thought: *I will sleep when this is finished.* He thought: *Margaux should see me now.*

The chair behind him, his father's chair, the one with the head of the table, sat empty and turned slightly out, as if someone had just risen from it and stepped away, and behind that, in oils, Pres III watched his son begin to write his way out of the world and said the thing he had said when Cope was twelve and never said again, which Cope had spent forty years failing to hear the second half of: *You'll get the company because you're a Mercer. Not because you're good.*

Cope, in the dark, underlined a word with his thumb.

He had stopped listening after *Mercer.* He always had. That, in the end, was the whole story, though it would take the machine — patient, tireless, incapable of cruelty or mercy or mayonnaise, reading every memo ever filed because it could not do otherwise — another nineteen months to read it back to him in his own company's voice.

Downstairs, a bus shelter changed its slide. *No off-sites.*

Up in the Aerie the last human chief executive in the consumer Fortune 100 wrote until the windows went gray, and felt wonderful, and did not sleep.
