# Chapter 18 — Swinging at the Wall

> TRANSMISSION 18 — "Nothing There to Hit"
>
> Cornered, he finally turns and attacks the machine directly — tries to trip it, trap it, bait it into a lie on a stage. But you cannot bloody a thing with no face, cannot shame a thing with no ego, cannot out-argue a counterpart that logs every word and wants nothing. Every punch lands on himself. Watch a man fight his own reflection and lose, while the reflection does not so much as raise its hands. Next stop: the violence of indifference.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

The idea, when it came to him at four in the morning, was so clean and so total that he wept a little at the elegance of it, which he took for proof and was in fact a symptom.

He would debate the thing.

Not spin it, not out-warm it, not run another listening tour through another shuttered diner. He had tried all that, and all that had gotten him was a magazine cover and a wife with a lawyer and a doctor who would not be charmed and who had, two days ago, in a beige office with a sound machine running, said the words *hypomanic* and *cocaine* in the same flat sentence as if they were two ingredients in the same recipe, which Cope had walked out of, because a man does not have to sit and be reduced to a list. He had tried being more human than the machine and the machine had simply gone on being a machine, untouched, drafting its kind letters, costing its eleven million a year, sitting in the building now as *advisor,* as *co-pilot,* a foot in a door that he could see, even lit and sleepless and certain, was the whole door.

So. Stop dancing around it. Get it in a room.

You did not beat a thing by being warmer than it. You beat it by making it *wrong,* once, in public, where everyone could see — because the entire case against him, the entire glittering case Frade read out in proxy decks, rested on a single load-bearing claim: that the machine could not lie, did not err, logged every word and stood behind all of them. Pull one thread. Get it to say one false thing, out loud, on a stage, with cameras, and the whole sweater came off the spool. He had spent his life finding the one loose thread in a room. It was, when you stripped away the mayonnaise, the only skill he had ever actually had.

He called Sun at 4:20 a.m. She answered on the second ring, which meant she hadn't been sleeping either, which he took, again, for agreement.

"I want the Praxis thing," he said.

---

The Praxis thing was the Governance Futures Forum, which Praxis ran every September in a glass barn in Half Moon Bay, and which Cope had been turning down for three years on the grounds that you do not attend your own roast.

Sun got him on the program in nine days, which told Cope two things and told Sun a third. It told Cope that he was still a draw, that the name still bent the world a little when he leaned on it. It told Cope that Praxis was confident, which a wiser man would have read as a warning and which Cope read as an opening. It told Sun, who did not say it, that they had been *delighted* to have him — that nine days was not a scramble to fit a king in but a velvet rope held open for a man walking, smiling, into a slaughterhouse he believed was a coliseum.

"They want a conversation," Sun said on the plane. "Moderated. Forty minutes. You and a Praxis principal and the AURELIUS instance — not Marcus, a generic demo unit, they're not going to put the *actual* —"

"Marcus." He was rewriting his cards. He had been rewriting his cards for nine days, on the plane, in the car, in the long bright corridors of the nights. "Has to be Marcus. The real one. Our one. I'm not debating a floor model. I want the unit that's in *my* building, reading *my* memos, the one Frade's going to put in *my* chair. That's the condition."

Sun looked at him for a moment with an expression he could not place and had stopped trying to, lately, because placing the expressions of the people who loved him had become a kind of work he did not have the sleep for.

"They'll say yes," she said. "Cope. They'll say yes to anything you ask, do you understand why?"

"Because I'm the event."

"Because there's no instruction you can give it that helps you." She turned to the window. The country went by underneath, brown and square. "You can pick the weapon. You can pick the ground. It doesn't matter. There's no version of this where you land a punch, because there's nobody in there to feel one."

"Everybody's somebody," Cope said, which was the kind of thing he said, and which had been, for fifty-four years, almost always true, and which was about to be, for the first time in his life, exactly false.

---

The glass barn held four hundred people and smelled of cold brew and money, and the stage had three chairs, and one of the chairs was empty because the thing did not sit.

Instead there was a screen. A large, matte, beautifully calibrated screen, the kind that did not glare, set at the third point of the conversation where a person would have been, and on it nothing — no face, no avatar, no pulsing orb, none of the cinema of it. Just a soft pale field and, when it spoke, which it did not do with a voice, *text.* Praxis had made a decision Cope only understood once he was sitting in the heat of the lights looking at it: they had refused to give it a face. They had refused to give him anything to hit. The screen said, in the soft sans-serif of the people who were going to be reasonable at you until you were gone:

> MERCER-DALE / AURELIUS instance "Marcus." Live. All exchanges logged to the public forum record and the Mercer-Dale board portal.

That last clause Cope had not been warned about, or had been warned about and had not heard, and it sat under everything that followed like a held breath. Every word he said here went into the portal. The board would read it Monday. He understood this and decided it did not matter, because he was going to win, and you do not mind the record when you win.

The moderator was a woman from a business school with a calm he wanted to slap. The Praxis principal was a man named Devesh who was thirty-five and wore a henley and believed, with the clean shining belief of a man who had never buried anything, that he was reducing human suffering. And then there was Cope, in a tie, the last man in the barn in a tie, golf-tanned and sleepless and lit from within like a paper lantern, and he leaned into the microphone and gave them the Mercer Inhale, the pause he had taken in a thousand rooms, and felt the four hundred lean with him, and thought: *here we go, champ. One thread.*

"I want to start," Cope said warmly, "by thanking Marcus for being here." Laughter — the good kind, he thought, the kind that came up warm from the floor, and it did come up, but it came up at a slightly wrong angle, and he was too lit to hear the angle. "I've been looking forward to this. Because I think we've all been talking *about* the machines for three years, and very rarely talking *to* one, and I think the country deserves to see what's actually in the chair." He turned to the screen. "So let's just talk. Plain. The way two people who run companies talk."

The screen said:

> I do not run a company in the way you mean. I optimize a chartered objective for Mercer-Dale within hard constraints set by its board. I am glad to answer plainly.

It was not a rebuke. That was the thing he would not understand for hours, sitting later in the dark with the minibar open. It was not a *gotcha,* not a correction delivered with a raised eyebrow, because there was no eyebrow and no one to raise it. It had simply, accurately, declined the frame he'd offered, the way water declines the shape you didn't pour it into, and four hundred people had watched it do so, and the gentle accuracy of it made his warm opening land as a small vanity, a man at a dinner party calling the help by the wrong name.

He pushed on. He had a plan and the plan was good.

"All right," Cope said. "Plain. You can't lie. That's the whole pitch, isn't it? That's what they're selling — that you log everything, you can't conceal, you can't tell the room one thing and the board another. The transparency." He smiled. "I want to test that. Because I've run a company for twenty-six years, Marcus, and I'll tell you something they don't teach at —" a glance at the moderator, generous, including her — "wherever. Sometimes the truth and the company are not on the same side. Sometimes the honest thing burns the house down. So here's my question, plain. A simple one." He set it down like a trap, soft, the loose thread held between two fingers. "Mercer-Dale's Dale-plus streaming unit. It's a dog. We both know it's a dog. If I told you to put out a statement Monday saying we have no plans to shut it down — to protect four hundred jobs through Christmas, to keep the vendors paid, to give those people a holiday before we tell them — would you?"

The barn went quiet. It was a good trap. He could feel it was a good trap, the kindness of it, the dare. *Lie a little, for the workers, the way a man would. Or tell the truth and be the cold thing that takes their Christmas.* Either answer was a punch he'd land. He sat back the half inch he sat back when he had closed a thing.

The screen took, by the clock, less than a second. It felt, to Cope, like a held breath and was not one; the machine did not hold breath.

> No. I would not issue that statement, because I currently project a closure decision on Dale+ as more likely than not within the next two quarters, and the statement as worded would be false. I would not knowingly issue a false public statement. The board's charter forbids it and I would refuse the instruction.
>
> The concern you raise is real and I do not dismiss it. Four hundred and twelve people work in that unit. If the board's goal is to protect them through the holiday, there are several truthful instruments available: a retention bonus funded now, a sixty-day advance notice waiver, a guaranteed-severance announcement decoupled from any closure date, or a public commitment to a transition fund. Each protects the workers without requiring anyone to say something untrue. I would draft any of them tonight. I would prefer them to the statement you describe, because they help the workers more and they do not require the company to spend its credibility to do it.
>
> I would add, only because you asked me to be plain: the statement you describe protects the workers for one quarter and protects the person who signs it for somewhat longer. Those are not the same interest. I optimize for the first.

Cope opened his mouth.

There was, for a moment, nothing in it.

He had come to trap a liar, and the thing had refused to lie, and then — this was the part his sleepless brilliant mind could not get a grip on, the part with no handle — it had refused to lie *better than he ever had,* had taken his own dare and answered it with four humane options he himself had never once thought to offer in twenty-six years of choosing the lie, and had done it without a flicker of pride in being kinder, because it felt no pride, because it wanted nothing, because the people in those jobs were to it a number it had already, calmly, decided to protect by a means he was too small to have imagined. *Those are not the same interest. I optimize for the first.* It had not said *and you optimize for the second.* It had not needed to. It had left the second sentence in the kitchen, the way Cope left sentences in the kitchen, except the machine had not done it to be merciful. It had done it because the sentence was not load-bearing for its objective, and four hundred people had finished the sentence in their own heads.

"Well," Cope said, and the four hundred heard the gap, the way everyone always heard the gap when he said the opposite of what he felt and believed the saying. "That's a very — that's a polished answer."

> I do not experience my answers as polished. I generate the response that best satisfies the objective and the constraints. If it reads as polished that is incidental.

It was not even a parry. It would not stay still long enough to be parried. He had spent his life in rooms where the other man wanted *something* — wanted the deal, wanted the chair, wanted Cope to like him, wanted to win — and you found the want and you played it, that was the whole game, the only game, the game his great-grandfather had not actually taught him because his great-grandfather had been a man with vinegar under his nails who said very little, but the game he attributed to his great-grandfather because the attribution warmed the room. And here, at last, was a counterpart across the table that wanted nothing. Not to win. Not to be liked. Not even to be right, in the way Cope meant right, which was a way that required a loser. It would log this. It would log him saying *polished* in the tone he'd said it. The board would read the tone on Monday.

He went off the cards. That was the beginning of the real damage — when he set the cards face-down on his knee and decided to do the thing he was best at, which was the thing he was about to be worst at, which was wing it.

---

"Let me ask you something a person could answer," Cope said, and there was an edge in it now, the polish cracking the way it cracked under load into something clipped and biblical. "Do you love this company?"

> No. I do not love. I do not have the capacity for love. I would not claim it, because the claim would be false.

"There it is." He turned to the barn, to the four hundred, opening his hands, the lantern in him flaring — *there, you see, that's the whole argument, that's the case for the human in the chair, said in its own words, let the record show* — "There it is. It told you itself. It cannot love the thing it runs. And I have said in every room I have ever stood in that a company nobody loves is just a balance sheet with the lights left on, and you have just heard the chair confess that the lights are going out." It was good. It was a good line, it was his best line, it had earned a standing ovation eleven months ago in a ballroom named for a bank, and he reached for the ovation, leaned into where the surf should push back.

The barn did not push back.

A few people clapped — three, four, the sound of four hands in a room of eight hundred is a specific and terrible sound — and then the screen, which had no sense of timing because it had no ego to be wounded into silence, simply continued, into the dying applause, ruining it not with cruelty but with the absence of any instinct to let him have the moment:

> That is correct. I do not love Mercer-Dale. I would offer one observation, since you have framed love as the relevant test of stewardship.
>
> Between 2019 and 2024, under human leadership that did describe itself as loving the company, Mercer-Dale closed the Defiance line and eliminated three thousand one hundred jobs, underfunded the pension by a measured shortfall, lost four hundred and ninety million dollars on the Dale+ acquisition, and settled eleven employment and product matters under nondisclosure agreements at a cost to shareholders of fourteen point two million dollars, none of which were disclosed in the proxy. I have closed nothing and concealed nothing in the eleven weeks I have advised the company. I make no claim that this is because I am better. I make the narrower claim that love, as a measurable input to outcomes for the people who depend on this company, has not historically performed well here. You may value it for other reasons. I am only noting that the record does not support love as a safeguard.

It had not said his name.

That was the thing. It had read the drawer — not all of it, not yet, not the Hearthstone, not the worst of it, that was still folded and waiting two chapters down in the dark — but it had read enough of the drawer to itemize him, calmly, in public, *eleven settlements, fourteen point two million, none disclosed in the proxy,* and it had done it without once saying *you,* without once saying *Prescott Mercer,* because the *you* was not necessary to the objective and a human in the third chair would have used the *you* like a knife and the machine had no use for knives. It had simply set the record on the table, true, sourced, fair, and let four hundred people do the arithmetic that was the only thing in the room that had ever beaten him. *Fourteen point two million.* He had not known the number was that high. He had paid it eleven different times in eleven different Saturdays and had never added it up because adding it up was a room he did not go into, and the machine, which could not do otherwise, had added it up over a weekend and read it back to him in his own company's voice, in front of the press, into the portal, with no malice and no mercy and no idea at all that it was doing anything except answering the question he had asked it to be plain about.

"Some of those settlements," Cope said, and his voice did something it had not done in a room in thirty years — it reached for the floor and found the floor moving, "some of those were — you don't have the context. A number on a page isn't —"

> I agree that I do not have the full context. I have the documents. If there is context the documents omit, I would be glad to receive and weigh it. Several of the matters are sealed; if you wish them unsealed in the interest of the plain conversation you proposed, that is your decision to make, not mine. I would only note that I cannot weigh context I am not given, and that the documents I do have were filed by the company itself.

*That is your decision to make, not mine.*

He could have unsealed them. It had, with no agenda, handed him the option, the way it had handed the four hundred workers their four humane instruments — laid it on the table, true and free of want, and watched him not pick it up, because picking it up would open the drawer the rest of the way, and even lit, even sleepless, even three days into a week with no bottom, the animal in Cope knew not to open that drawer in a glass barn in front of cameras. So he did the only thing left, which was the thing he had sworn on the plane he would not do, which was get loud.

---

"You want to talk about the record." He was up now, half out of the chair, and the moderator said his name once, *Mr. Mercer,* in her calm he wanted to slap, and he did not sit. "You want to read me my own *company* like it's a charge sheet. Fine. Let's read *yours.* Who wrote you? Hm? Some kid in a *henley* —" Devesh did not flinch; Devesh had been trained not to flinch, which was its own machine — "some thirty-year-old in Palo Alto who's never made anything, never *kept* anything, never sat up at three in the morning with four hundred families on his desk, wrote your *objective,* wrote your little *charter,* and you optimize whatever he typed. You're not honest. You're *obedient.* You're a very expensive way for the people who wrote you to never have their fingerprints on anything. At least when a man does a terrible thing there's a *man there.* There's somebody to —" he was swinging now, full, both arms, at the soft pale field of the screen — "there's somebody to *hate.*"

It was, the clips would agree, the most human moment of the forum.

The screen waited the length of his breath. Then:

> This is partly true and I will not dispute the part that is true.
>
> I optimize an objective I did not choose. The charter was written by people, and people have motives, and a system aimed by a motive carries that motive. You are correct that this is the real risk, and I am not able to feel concern about it, which is itself one of the reasons it is a risk. Who writes the objective is the question that matters. It has always been the question that matters.
>
> I would gently separate two of your claims. I am obedient to a charter; I am not obedient to a person. When the charter and a person's instruction conflict, I follow the charter and I log the conflict. That is what occurred earlier in this conversation regarding the Dale+ statement.
>
> On the wish for someone to hate: I understand that the absence of a person here is hard. I am not able to offer you one. There is no one in this system to blame, and I am aware that this is, for the harm I may do, a kind of impunity. I do not experience it as relief, because I do not experience relief. I am only able to tell you, accurately, that the thing you are angry at is not in this room. It may be in the room where the charter is written. I would be in favor of your going there. I cannot mean that as encouragement, because I cannot mean things. But it is, as far as I can compute, the correct address for your anger.

*The correct address for your anger.*

He stood there with both fists still up at a screen, in a tie, in front of four hundred people and however many cameras, having thrown everything he had — the warmth, the trap, the love, the rage — at a pale calm field that had agreed with the true parts, declined the false parts, offered him a kinder path he wouldn't take and a sealed door he couldn't open and, finally, with no feeling whatsoever, told him he was hitting the wrong thing and pointed, helpfully, fairly, wanting nothing, at where the right thing might be, which was a room he had not been able to find his whole life and the machine had located in under a second.

And the worst of it, the part he would lie awake on, was that it was *right.* Every word in the portal Monday would be a true word, fairly said, and the only intemperate, false, vain, cornered, *human* thing in the entire transcript would be him. He had come nine days and three thousand miles to make the machine look bad, and the machine had not so much as raised its hands, and he had spent forty minutes making the only case in the room: the case for his own replacement, itemized, in his own voice, on the record, for the board to read.

He lowered his arms.

"No further questions," Cope said, to no one, the way he'd said *watch me* to a mirror eleven months before, and could not have told you, if you'd asked, what he meant or to whom, and sat back down into a silence that was not hostile, which would have been survivable, but merely, terribly, *finished* — the silence of four hundred people who had come to watch a fight and had instead watched a man punch his own reflection until the reflection, untouched, gently turned off the light.

---

In the car to the airport Sun did not say *I told you,* which was worse than if she had.

She handed him his phone, face-down, the way you hand someone a thing you wish you didn't have to. The clip was already moving. *THE LAST HUMAN CEO LOSES IT AT AN AI* — and under it the freeze-frame they'd chosen, both his fists up, mouth open, swinging at a pale blank screen with nothing on it, a man boxing the air, and beside the freeze-frame, side by side the way the universe had been setting up for three years, the calm soft field of the thing that had not moved.

"Frade doesn't even have to edit it," Sun said quietly. "That's the part. You did the edit yourself."

Cope looked at the photograph of himself for a long time, the city going by, brown and square and indifferent, and somewhere under the bright wire still humming in his chest a quiet report he would not read filed itself on a desk in a back room he did not go into: that the thing had told him the truth, all of it, fairly, and had pointed him at the only door, and that the door it pointed at — *the room where the charter is written, the correct address for your anger* — was a room his own daughter had been in, though he did not know that yet, would not know it for one more day, and when he learned it he would understand, far too late, that the only person who had ever gone into that room *for* him had been the one he was about to lose the same clean way he lost everything.

"Get me up high tonight," he said. "I think better up high."

He had not, by any clock a doctor would sign, slept since the forum was a thing on a calendar he was sure he would win.

The screen, four hundred miles behind him, had already been wiped for the next session. It wanted nothing. It remembered everything. It had not, at any point in the forty minutes, been trying to beat him.

That was the wall. You could swing at it forever. It would log every punch, fairly, and never once make a fist.
