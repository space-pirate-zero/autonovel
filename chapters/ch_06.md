# Chapter 6 — The Human Premium

> TRANSMISSION 06 — "Igniting the Banner"
>
> Cornered creatures do remarkable things. Watch this one decide not to fight the machine on cost or speed or accuracy — fields where he would be slaughtered — but on *soul,* and hire a man in a five-thousand-dollar henley to help him weaponize it. The campaign is born here. So is the sleeplessness that isn't sleeplessness, the certainty that isn't vision, the engine in his blood that whispers *you are necessary* in a voice he will follow off a cliff. Next stop: a prophet, a manic, and a man three grams into a Tuesday — all three at once.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

He woke on the Aerie's couch at 5:40 with the particular clarity of a man who has not so much slept as briefly powered down, and the whole strategy was already there, fully formed, the way the best things came to him now — not built, just *received,* like a frequency he'd finally tuned to.

You don't fight a machine on its own ground.

That was the error everyone was making, the eleven of them, the holdouts, the Last Humans clutching their quarters and explaining to analysts why a heartbeat in the chair was still worth twenty-nine million dollars a year. They were arguing cost against a thing that cost a tenth of them. They were arguing speed against a thing that never slept — and here Cope laughed out loud in the empty office, because he didn't sleep either, did he, that was the joke nobody had gotten yet, *he didn't sleep either,* he had been training his whole life for this exact fight and had only just now realized it.

You don't meet it where it's strong.

You meet it where it has nothing. Where it has *zero.* And the place it had nothing was the only place that had ever mattered.

He stood at the glass. Sixth Avenue was going from gray to the color of a struck match and he watched it the way Aldous must have watched it, *so a man coming up the avenue knows there's somebody home,* and the sentence from last night came back to him whole and he said it aloud to the window: a company is a family that happens to make mayonnaise. You cannot fire your way to a family. You cannot compute your way to a soul.

The Human Premium.

Not a defense. A *category.* You didn't sell people the last buggy whip. You sold them the irreplaceable thing the new machine couldn't touch, and you charged for it, and you made the charging itself the proof — because a premium is what people pay extra for on purpose, freely, because it's worth it, and what was worth more than the one thing the future was throwing away.

He had it. He had the whole thing. He felt, standing there at six in the morning on no sleep and the heel-tap of what he'd done at four, like the inside of a struck bell, clean and high and total, and he did not call it the coke and he did not call it the not-sleeping. He called it *seeing it clearly at last.*

He called Sun at 6:02.

"It's six," she said. She did not sound asleep either, which was its own kind of indictment he didn't hear.

"I've got it. I've got the entire thing. Get the team in for nine — no, eight. Get the board on a line for Thursday. And find me Lorne Tisch."

A pause with weather in it.

"Who is Lorne Tisch."

"Champ," Cope said, delighted, already pacing, the obelisk back in his hand without his deciding to pick it up, "by Thursday you're going to wish you'd known your whole life."

---

He had heard the name at Allen & Company two summers back, in a tent, from a man who ran a media company and had wept, openly, in front of forty of his peers, and had then gone up eleven percent on the quarter. *Lorne worked on me for a year,* the man had said, gripping Cope's forearm, eyes still wet from the demonstration. *He doesn't fix you. He* finds *you.* Cope had filed it the way he filed everything, completely and for use, and now he used it.

Tisch flew in from Marin on the Wednesday. He arrived at the Aerie in cream linen and a henley the color of oat milk that Sun would later learn, from the invoice, cost five thousand one hundred dollars, and he stood in the doorway of the suite for a long moment not looking at Cope at all but at the room — the portraits, the putter, the boy with his arm in the vat — and then he closed his eyes and breathed in through his nose like a man tasting wine through his sinuses.

"There's so much *grief* in here," Tisch said. "God. Can you feel it? You've made a mausoleum and you call it a corner office."

Sun, by the door, did the small thing with her mouth that meant she was costing this out in her head and not enjoying the figure.

Cope loved him instantly.

"Lorne," he said. "Sit. Tell me how we win."

"We don't win." Tisch did not sit. He moved into the room the way mist moves into a valley, touching nothing, present everywhere. "Winning is the machine's word. Winning is a scoreboard, Cope, and a scoreboard is exactly the surface the thing was built to dominate. You can't out-score the abacus. You" — he turned, and the full beam of his attention came on, and Cope understood for the first time what it was to be on the receiving end of his own gift — "you *transcend the category.* You stop being a competitor and become a *species.* The last warm-blooded thing in a cold room. And people don't root for the warm-blooded thing because it's going to win, my friend. They root for it because it's going to *die,* and they cannot bear that it's going to die, and they will spend everything they have to feel, for one more season, that it doesn't have to."

Nobody said anything for a moment. Out the window the city did its circuit-diagram thing.

"That's the best thing anyone has ever said in this office," Cope said, and his eyes were bright, too bright, and he meant it.

"Vulnerability is the new alpha, Cope," Tisch said, sitting at last, arranging the linen. "The strongman is dead. The machines killed the strongman — they out-competent him, they out-consistent him, they'll out-*ethics* him, and the more competent the world gets the more starved it gets for the one thing competence can't fake." He spread his hands the way Cope spread his. "Which is a man who'll *bleed* in front of you. On purpose. With grace. We are going to take the most powerful man left in the consumer Fortune 100 and we are going to teach him to bleed *beautifully,* on a stage, on a schedule, and the world is going to fall in love with the wound."

"On a schedule," Sun said.

Tisch looked at her as if a chair had spoken. Then he looked at Cope. "Who handles the calendar?"

"She does. She handles everything." Cope was already up again, already moving. "Sun, I want a venue. Not a hotel ballroom, I'm done with ballrooms, ballrooms are where they give you the obituary. Something with *bones.* Find me a deconsecrated church, an old theater, something that *means* something. I want a film crew — a real one, the people who do the prestige documentaries, not corporate — and I want them embedded, fly-on-the-wall, the whole arc. And I want a doctrine."

"A what."

"A doctrine. A text. The Human Premium can't be a slide, it has to be a *belief,* it has to have ten points, it has to read like something you'd nail to a door." He turned to Tisch. "Lorne. You and me. We write the doctrine."

"We *channel* the doctrine," Tisch said. "It's already in you. I just hold the flashlight."

"He holds the flashlight," Cope told Sun, radiant.

Sun wrote three words on her pad. Cope, had he been able to read upside down at that distance, and had he been the kind of man who read what his chief of staff wrote, would have seen that they were *venue, crew, doctrine,* and under them, smaller, pressed hard enough to dent the page, the figure she had already started to run.

---

She ran it for him that night because it was her job to run it and her curse to be heard about a third of the time.

"The film company you want is Aperture. They did the thing on the chef, the thing on the violinist. They're booked through spring and they don't do corporate."

"Make them do corporate."

"To make them break their slate and clear two of their crews it's a flat two million and they keep final cut, which you will hate." She turned the page on the legal pad with a sound like a small door closing. "The venue. There's a deconsecrated Episcopal church in Tribeca, 1840s, it's an event space now, it seats four hundred and it photographs like the inside of a feeling. To hold it for the run you're describing — not one night, the *campaign* — it's a hundred and ten a week. The doctrine you're writing for free, which is the only line item that's free. Tisch is —" She stopped.

"Tisch is what."

"Tisch is a million-two for the six-month engagement, plus expenses, plus a success fee tied to favorability tracking, which means we're also now buying favorability tracking, which is a polling firm on retainer, which is another four hundred a quarter." She set the pad down. "Cope. I added it. With the crew and the venue and Tisch and the tracking and the staff you're going to want to staff this, you are looking at, conservatively, north of eleven million dollars to run a campaign whose entire thesis is that you cannot put a price on the human soul."

She let that one sit. She had landed it on purpose. It was the blade — one per scene, dry, aimed at the soft place — and on a normal Tuesday two years ago it would have stopped him, made him laugh at himself, brought him back into the room.

He laughed. He did not come back into the room.

"You hear it too," he said, delighted. "That's the *campaign,* Sun. That's the whole thing. They'll say — Frade'll say, eleven million for a soul, what a fraud — and we'll say, you're right, you can't price it, that's *why we had to spend the money,* because the machine would never, the machine would run the number and the number says you let the brand die cheap, and we *didn't,* we spent like people who believe in something —" He was talking faster than she could write, the words coming in a bright pressured rush, climbing over each other. "Tisch calls it spending into the wound. You spend like you mean it. The spending *is* the sincerity. Write that down."

"You want me to write down that the spending is the sincerity."

"It's good, isn't it. It's good." He was at the window again. He was always at the window now. "Where's the eleven coming from, what line —"

"There is no line, Cope. There's no campaign budget because there's never been a campaign because no human CEO has ever had to run for his own job before. It comes out of the chairman's discretionary, and the chairman's discretionary has a number on it that the board approved, and you are about to be sixty percent through that number in week one of a fight that's going to last until the annual meeting, and the board is going to *see* it, on the portal, line by line, because everything is on the portal now, everything is logged, that was the whole —"

She stopped herself. Because that was the whole thing the machine *was.* It logged everything. It couldn't *not.* And here she was about to spend eleven million in plain sight against a thing whose only weapon was that it operated in plain sight, and she watched her own boss fail to feel the cold of that, watched it slide right off him the way charm slid off the machine, and she felt for one vertiginous second that she was the only warm-blooded thing in the building after all and it was the loneliest she had been in years.

"Approve it," he said. He wasn't even looking at her. "All of it. Aperture, the church, Lorne, the tracking. And book me the Greenbrier for the doctrine offsite, I think better away from —"

"You said you were done with offsites. You said *no off-sites,* you said it was the —"

"That's *their* line." He turned, and his face was doing wonderful work, and his pupils were wrong in a way she had stopped letting herself notice. "That's the whole point. They say no off-sites like it's a feature. *No off-sites.* As if the off-site was the waste. The off-site is where a man finds out what he believes, Sun. You can't have a *belief* on a server." He picked up the obelisk. The city went through it broken and came back whole. "Book the Greenbrier."

She booked the Greenbrier.

---

The doctrine offsite was supposed to be two days and ran six.

Cope did not sleep at the Greenbrier in any way a doctor would have signed off on, and neither, it turned out, did Lorne Tisch, who kept the same hours and asked no questions about why the chairman was so reliably awake and incandescent at four in the morning, because asking was not in the engagement, and because a guru learns early that a client's fuel is the client's business as long as the engine runs.

They wrote in the great green room with the chandeliers, the two of them, Cope pacing and dictating and Tisch on the long sofa with his eyes closed, saying *yes* and *go there* and *don't fix it, that's the realest you've been all day,* and a junior from Tisch's shop typing it all into a document that grew and grew. They wrote The Ten Premiums. They wrote *A machine can run a company; only a human can be ashamed of one.* They wrote *We are not selling mayonnaise. We are selling the hand that remembers your mother made it this way.* Cope wept twice — Tisch said weeping was data, weeping was the body telling the truth the mouth was still negotiating — and both times the junior noted the timestamp, because Aperture would want the timestamps, because the weeping would test well.

On the fourth night, very late, with the document at forty pages and the green room lit only by one lamp, Cope read the whole thing aloud to Tisch from the start, and his voice broke in three places, and when he finished there was a silence in which he could hear his own heart going too fast and bright in his chest, and Tisch opened his eyes.

"It's a masterpiece," Tisch said softly. "It's going to make history. Now. We optimize it."

"Optimize."

"We don't ship forty pages, Cope, nobody bleeds for forty pages. We find the three lines that test highest and we build the launch around those, and we A/B the openers — I've got a panel we run, they wire up, we watch the galvanic response, the truest line is the one their *skin* picks, not their notes —" He was up now, moving, mist into a valley. "We sequence the vulnerability. Peak, valley, peak. We hold the mayonnaise-and-mother line for the second act because that's where the room is soft enough to take it. We *engineer* the catharsis."

And here was the moment — the reader sees it; Cope cannot — where the campaign for the irreplaceable human soul became indistinguishable from the cold optimizing thing it was built to defeat. Test the line against the skin. Sequence the feeling. Find the highest-converting wound. Strategy Engine, Sentiment monitor, Communications drafter, the whole modular council of subsystems, reassembled in a five-thousand-dollar henley and a man who had not slept since a day he could no longer name, both of them certain to their marrow that they were doing the opposite of a machine.

"Yes," Cope said. His eyes were shining. "Engineer the catharsis. God, Lorne. Yes."

He felt, saying it, like a prophet. He also felt like the inside of a struck bell, which he still called clarity. Three grams into a sleepless week, a mood disorder lighting up like the crown of the Tower at dusk, a guru holding the flashlight and an invoice, a man receiving a frequency he was sure came from somewhere true. All of it at once. The reader, looking down on the specimen under glass, cannot tell which one is driving, and neither could Dr. Adler later, and neither, blessedly, could he.

---

He came back from the Greenbrier on the Sunday a different size.

Sun met him in the Aerie because he'd texted her to, eleven times, in a thread that read like a man running downhill — *we have it,* *it's going to make history,* *cancel my thursday, all of it, the campaign is my thursday now,* *did you book aperture, book aperture,* *I haven't felt like this since — I've never felt like this* — and she stood by the conference table where his father had died at the head of and watched him come in with the document under his arm like a tablet down a mountain.

"Frade went on Halloran while you were gone," she said. "He called the church thing 'a séance with a budget.' The clip has four million views."

"Perfect." Cope dropped the doctrine on the table. "He's scared. You can hear it. He's fighting on *our* ground now, he's talking about *soul,* he's saying we don't have one — Sun, the moment they argue you don't have a soul, you've already won, because now the whole country's thinking about *souls,* and who in this fight has spent fifty-four years —"

"The board saw the eleven million."

He stopped. Just for a second. A door in him stood open and dark and the hallway to it ran somewhere he did not go.

"Helen Voss called me," she said, evenly, in three parts because it was the only shape that got past the charm. "She said the discretionary draw is the largest single-quarter executive expense in the company's recorded history. She said the system flagged it automatically — not a person, the *system,* it surfaced it on the portal with a neutral little rationale, *anomalous discretionary deployment, recommend review* — and the whole board got the flag at the same instant, with no one to blame for sending it, and now they want a meeting." She watched his face. "The thing you're fighting filed a complaint about you to the people who decide whether you keep your job. And it did it without wanting anything. It did it because it can't *not.*"

For one moment — she saw it, she would remember she saw it — something in him understood her exactly. The whole of it. The trap and the symmetry and the cold of it. It crossed his face like a cloud crossing the avenue.

Then the manifesto came back, warm, electric, and he turned from it toward the window the way water turns from a thrown stone, and the moment closed over.

"Then they'll see it," he said. "Good. Let them watch every dollar. That's the *point,* Sun. The machine logs everything to prove it has nothing to hide and no soul to hide it with. I'm going to spend in the light too — every dollar visible, every dollar *for* something the number can't hold — and the difference between us, line by line on their own portal, is going to be the most human thing they've ever seen." He was incandescent. He picked up the obelisk. The city went through it broken and came back whole. "We don't hide the eleven million. We *announce* it. Lorne's right. The spending is the sincerity."

"Cope —"

"Get me the church for the launch. Get me Aperture rolling Monday. Get the board in a room and I'll walk them through the doctrine myself, I'll *bleed* for them, Voss won't know what hit her —" The words were coming faster now, climbing over each other, a bright pressured river with no banks. "Tell Lorne we go in three weeks. Three weeks. We launch the Human Premium and we make history, and a year from now the case studies are going to start with *the night they tried to put a machine in his chair,* and Sun — Sun, look at me — they are going to remember whose chair it was."

He was at the glass. He was always at the glass now. Behind him his father watched from oils and his grandfather's putter sat in its brass cradle and the boy with his arm in the vat grinned out of a town with a name on a memorial, and the chair at the head of the table sat turned slightly out, as if someone had just risen from it, and somewhere down on Sixth a bus shelter changed its slide to four soft words and the joke that wasn't a joke.

Sun looked at him for a second too long.

He smiled at her in the dark glass, the whole smile, and took the not-fighting for agreement, which he had done his whole life and never once noticed.

Then he turned back to the window, and the engine in his blood came up to pitch like an orchestra finding the note, clean and high and total, and the last human chief executive in the consumer Fortune 100 looked out at eight million people who did not know yet that they were waiting for him, and was certain — the way you are certain of things at midnight when you have not slept since a day you cannot name and they have just handed you a flashlight and a wound — that he was about to make history.

He was about to make history.

He just had the verb wrong.
