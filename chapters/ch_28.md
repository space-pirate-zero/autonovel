# Chapter 28 — Final Image

> TRANSMISSION 28 — "The Audience"
>
> Return with us to a ballroom, a chandelier, a chair, a toast — the
> exact shape of where we began, run in reverse. He is not on the stage
> tonight. He is in the audience, sober in every sense, watching the
> future be introduced to applause he used to think was for him. One
> person in the room turns and actually looks at him. It is not
> forgiveness. It is not redemption. It is just a daughter, seeing her
> father, smaller and more real than he has ever been. Next stop: what's
> left when the throne is gone and the man, against all odds, is finally
> here.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

They gave a crystal obelisk to a machine for being the first of something, and Cope sat in the eleventh row and watched the light go through it and reassemble, and felt nothing he could have toasted over.

The room loved it. The room was nine hundred people in the Conrad Vance Ballroom, the same nine hundred more or less, the same chandeliers doing the same paid work overhead, and they were here for the *American Enterprise Institute for Leadership*'s newest citation, which a woman with a lanyard had read aloud before the applause — *the first fully autonomous chief executive of a Fortune 100 consumer enterprise, a model of stewardship without appetite* — and the obelisk had been accepted, in a manner of speaking, by Sun Rao, who stood at the lectern in a charcoal dress and held the cold glass thing up to the light because someone had to hold it and a machine has no hands.

She did not say *beginning.* She said the company's name, and the year, and thanked the committee, and the citation, Cope noticed from the eleventh row, did not call anyone irreplaceable. It had taken him a long time to learn that the trophies were obituaries. The machine had not needed to learn it. It simply did not get one.

He had tied his own bow tie. That part was the same. He had stood in the bathroom of the rented apartment in Murray Hill — one bedroom, a window that looked at another window — and done the thing his fingers still knew how to do without him, and the man in that mirror had been fifty-six and not golf-tanned, the jaw still more or less the jaw but the rest of the face come down to meet it at last, and he had said nothing to the man. He had stopped saying *watch me.* There was no one left to mean it at, and, more to the point, he had finally run out of the thing that used to make the sentence feel true.

Eleven months sober. He counted it the way Margaux had once counted the women, which was to say privately, and to survive it, and without expecting the number to forgive him.

"They asked me to say a few words about the transition," Sun was saying, and the room settled, "and I told them I'd rather say a few words about the work, because that's what survives a transition. The work." She briefed them in threes, because she could not help it, because a man in the eleventh row had trained her to, years ago, and she had outlived the training and kept it. "It runs on time. It runs clean. It tells you the truth even when the truth files a 13D against you." A laugh, the warm kind, up from the floor. "I've worked for both kinds of boss now. I'll let you guess which one remembered my kids' birthdays and which one never once needed me to remember its."

Cope laughed. It surprised him — a real one, short, before he could decide whether he was allowed. She had landed the blade on him and he had felt it go in clean and he had laughed, because it was true, and because there was a particular freedom, he was finding, in no longer being the man the room was watching. You could just hear things now. You could sit in the eleventh row at a table that cost eleven hundred dollars instead of eighty thousand and hear, for the first time in forty years, what people actually said.

---

He had not wanted to come.

Pip had wanted him to come, which was a different and stranger thing, and so he had come, and they had put him at a table near the back with a deputy comptroller and the comptroller's husband and two empty chairs that stayed empty all night and no card on either of them, no calligraphy, nobody's name, just two chairs that belonged to no one and announced nothing. He had spent a lot of the dinner looking at the empty chairs and being grateful, in a way he could not have explained to anyone in 2027, that they meant nothing at all.

The lamb was good. He noticed he could taste it. That was new, or rather it was old, very old, a thing from before the decades when every dinner had been a stage he ate on without tasting, and somewhere around the second course he understood that he was not waiting for the second course to end so that he could go somewhere and do something. There was nowhere to go. There was nothing folded in any pocket of the rented tuxedo, folded the particular way a gentleman folds it so it can't spill. There was no Desmond off the coat check. He had thought, getting dressed, that the absence would have a hole in it the shape of the old ritual, and it did, but the hole did not howl the way he'd been warned it would in the rooms with the bad coffee and the better people. It just sat there, quiet, the way a scar sits. He had been afraid of the men's room all night and had gone, twice, and washed his hands, and come back, and that was all, and that was everything.

Onstage they were running a short film about the transition. Mercer-Dale's wordmark, then the soft sans-serif that all of them used now, the typeface of the people who were going to be reasonable at you until you were gone. *Stewardship without appetite.* He watched the Defiance plant come up on the screen — drone footage, the new memorial garden where the old loading dock had been, the two names on the low granite wall that he had finally, far too late, made sure were spelled the way the families spelled them. The film did not say his name in connection with the wall. The settlement had been very clear about that, and for once in his life he had wanted his name kept out of a room, had fought to keep it out, and had won the one fight he ever fought to lose.

The machine had written the apology. He knew that now. He had refused to let it sign the apology, had insisted a human sign it, had signed it himself in a deposition room in Delaware with a pen that cost nothing, and it was the only thing he had ever put his name to that he could read back without flinching, and it had saved no one, and the two on the wall were still on the wall.

He found, watching the garden go by on the screen, that his eyes were doing something, and he let them, and did not perform it, and did not wipe it away for anyone to see him not wiping it away, which was the part that would have damned the old version. A single anything was always a lie in the old grammar. He just sat with his face wet in the dark of the eleventh row and let the film be about a place and not about him.

---

The applause came again when Sun stepped back and the lights came up half, and the room rose — of course the room rose, the room always rose — and Cope rose with it because you stand when the room stands, and he clapped, with his hands and, he was a little startled to find, his eyes too, for the woman who had run the thing he'd been credited with running, and for the thing that ran it now, the patient tireless thing that had read every memo ever filed because it could not do otherwise and had, in the reading, told the truth he spent eleven minutes deciding to bury and the rest of his life inside.

It cannot love a company, he had told this same room once, from the stage, holding a different glass up to the same light. A machine can run it but it cannot love it. He had been so sure. He had meant it the way he meant everything, completely and for the length of the sentence.

He stood in the eleventh row and understood, without drama, the way you finally understand a joke that has been waiting in a room for two years, that loving it had never been the qualification he thought it was. He had loved it. He had loved it since he was nine years old with his whole arm in a vat to the elbow, and the loving had not made the heater safe, had not made the letter kind, had not kept a single name off the wall. The machine did not love it and had done all three. That was the whole sentence his father had started at the table when Cope was twelve, the half he had spent forty years failing to hear, and he heard it now, standing, applauding, sober, in the eleventh row of a room that had stopped looking at him: *Try to be good anyway.* It had never been about the chair. It had been about the second sentence. He had inherited the first one and called it a verdict and never once tried for the second, and now the second was being done, beautifully, on time, by a thing that could not try because it could not fail to.

The applause went on. He was diminished inside it the way a stone is diminished by water, which is to say completely and over a long time and without anyone watching the particular moment it happened.

And then, three tables up and to the left, in the standing crowd, one person did not face the stage.

She turned around.

Pip. Twenty-six now, in a dress she'd have hated him for noticing, her hands still going automatically against the applause because you clap when the room claps, but her face turned all the way back into the eleventh row, finding him, the way no one had looked at him in this ballroom or any ballroom in his whole performed life — not up at him, not for the wattage, not waiting to be remembered or fixed onto someone or gotten laughing inside ninety seconds. Just looking. At her father. At the actual size of him, which was a size he had spent everything he had to finally be.

It was not forgiveness. He could read that much; he had gotten, late, the ability to read a room he wasn't running, and her face was not offering him absolution, was not the end of any arc, was not the thing the rooms with the bad coffee promised would come if you did the work. She had consulted for Praxis. He knew that now too; she'd told him in the hospital, in the seventy-two hours when the floor went out, told him she had helped build the thing replacing him because she thought it would be better, and that she still thought so, and that both of those could be true and still she'd flown in to sit in a hotel and watch him not have a drink. None of that was resolved. None of it was going to be.

She just looked at him. And he let himself be looked at, which was the hardest thing he had ever done, harder than Delaware, because there was no toast that survived it, no version to tell at dinner, no wattage to bring up, nothing folded in any pocket — only the man, finally home behind the face, smaller and more real than the stage had ever let him be.

He did not smile the whole smile. He didn't have it anymore, and would not have used it on her if he had. He did something else, something he had no practice at, which was nothing at all. He looked back.

She held it for a moment. Then she did a small thing with her mouth that was not a smile and was not not one, the specific thing you do at the father you can't stop loving and can't pretend about either, and she turned back to the stage, because the applause was ending and you face front when the applause ends.

That was all. That was the whole of it.

The chandeliers did what chandeliers are paid to do. Onstage Sun was setting the obelisk down on the lectern, where it would sit unclaimed because the recipient had no use for objects and no hands to want them with, and the room was finding its coats, and somewhere out past the lights a coat-check counter was handing back nine hundred coats to nine hundred people who would drive home in the orange-sliding dark and not think once about the man in the eleventh row, which was, he understood, the kindest thing the night could do for him, and the truest.

He picked up his own coat, an ordinary coat. No one took a second card out of his hand, because there were no cards, because there was no Sun at his elbow out of the architecture, because Sun was up there now and the architecture was hers. He walked out through the same marble lobby past the same good mirrors and did not look at himself in any of them.

Outside it had started to rain, the small cold spring rain that means nothing, and there was no car up, and he stood under the Vance's awning for a minute among the strangers waiting for cars that were theirs, and he did not have the bright frictionless certainty, did not have the fine wire humming in his chest, did not have the city laid out below him like a circuit diagram of his own genius. He had a coat and a rented apartment uptown and eleven months and a daughter who had turned around. He had the second sentence, forty years late, with the two on the wall still on the wall and the loss still lost and no machine and no man able to make it otherwise.

It was not enough. It was never going to be enough, and the part of him that used to fix that with a fold of paper or a fistful of warmth was finally, quietly, dead.

He stood in the rain and was nobody, and tasted it, the way he had tasted the lamb.

Then he put his collar up, and went to find the train.
