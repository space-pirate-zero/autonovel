# Chapter 21 — Dark Night

> TRANSMISSION 21 — "The Engine Cuts Out"
>
> Every flight that climbs on a chemical eventually meets the part of the
> sky with no more chemical in it. The mania's floor drops away; the
> hospital lights are the wrong kind of bright; and for one terrible
> clear hour — the clearest of his life — he sees himself with the
> machine's own pitiless accuracy and none of its indifference. No
> applause down there. Just the man, and the ledger, and the dead.
> Next stop: sober, in every sense, for the first time in thirty years.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

He did not remember the lobby.

He remembered the desk and the two lines on page eight, and he remembered standing up from his father's chair with a plan that had felt, for one bright second, like a manifesto — he would go to Defiance, he would go *now,* he would stand on the river walk and say the true thing into a camera and the saying would be the redemption, the redemption would be the campaign, the campaign would be the chair — and then he remembered the bright second guttering out somewhere around the elevator, the way a match goes out when you carry it too fast, and after that there was a gap. The gap had a marble floor in it. There was a doorman in the gap, the morning doorman, Hector, whose daughter was at Fordham on a thing Cope had quietly made happen, and Hector was saying *Mr. Mercer* in a voice that was not the voice you used for a man who was fine, and the avenue was the wrong temperature, and then there was no avenue at all.

He came back under a light he could not turn off.

That was the first fact, the governing fact, the one the body filed before the mind woke up to countersign it: the light. It came from a long tube in a recessed ceiling and it had no source and no warmth and no off, and it lay on everything at once, on the rail of the bed and the back of his own hand and a blood-pressure cuff sighing itself full on his arm, and it was, he understood, lying very still under it, the exact opposite of the light he had been living in. The Aerie light had been the city's, gold and chosen, a circuit diagram of his own genius. This light had been chosen by no one to flatter anything. This light was actuarial. It was the light a thing would build if it wanted to see, and did not care what it saw, and never blinked.

There was a band on his wrist with his name on it. Not Cope. *MERCER, PRESCOTT.* A barcode. A date of birth that made him, the barcode had decided, fifty-four.

Somewhere a machine counted his heart, and the count was wrong, too fast, and a softer machine answered it, and the two of them talked all night in beeps about a man who was not in the conversation.

---

The crash, when it came, did not come like weather.

He had thought of it as weather his whole life — the highs a front rolling in, the lows a system passing through, things that happened *to* the sky rather than things the sky paid for. He had been wrong about that the way he had been wrong about most of the load-bearing things. This did not roll in. This was a subtraction. There had been a wire humming in him since a Tuesday he could no longer date, a fine bright total wire, the one he had called the applause and the vision and the edge and discipline, and the wire was simply gone, pulled out of him at the root, and what it left was not pain exactly but the place where pain would have been if he had still had the current to feel it.

Dr. Adler had told him, weeks ago, in the calm flat voice she used for the things he most wanted to argue with, exactly what this would be. She had drawn it for him, even, on the back of a prescription pad — a line climbing, the cocaine and the mood disorder feeding each other up a slope that felt like flight, and then the part of the curve he had not let her finish, the part where the borrowed went back to the lender with interest. *You are not buying energy,* she had said. *You are borrowing it from a version of yourself that will present the bill. The mania is the credit. This is the collateral.* He had nodded and toasted over it. He had heard about a third of it, the good third, the part that still had a fight in it.

He was reading the rest of it now off his own arm.

His skin hurt. Not a wound; the surface itself, as if some membrane that stood between him and the room had been billed and repossessed. His jaw ached from a clench he could not remember authorizing. His thoughts came slow and gray and one at a time, single file, and each one had to be lifted by hand, and behind them, where the manifesto used to assemble itself out of pure pressure, there was a flat white silence, no weather in it at all, the worst silence he had ever stood in, because it was the silence of a man hearing his own ordinary mind for the first time in thirty years and finding it small.

He wanted it back. That was the obscene part, the part Adler had also predicted and he had also not believed. Two people were dead and the company was lost and the wife was gone and the daughter was somewhere and a barcode said his name, and the loudest thing in him, louder than all of it, was a low animal want, located precisely behind the bridge of his nose, for the bright wire to come back and make him able to stand it. He understood, lying still under the light, that this was the thing itself. Not the powder. The *wanting.* The engine the powder had only ever been the fuel for — the thing in him that could not, would not, had never once been able to sit alone in a quiet room with Prescott Mercer and no audience and no chemical and no next thing. He had built a whole life so that he would never have to. He had a tower for it. He had appetites for it. He had a nickname for it; they had named him, at three, for coping, and he had spent fifty-one years proving the name a lie by never once doing the thing, by climbing instead, charming instead, keying instead, flying instead, anything, anything at all, rather than cope.

The room would not let him climb. The room was the size of the room. That was its whole cruelty.

---

A 72-hour hold, a nurse had said, or someone had said, in the gap. Involuntary, then voluntary, the paperwork sorting itself out somewhere he was not, Sun's hand in it probably, or Margaux's, or Pip's — one of the women, it was always one of the women, the calling-in of the favor at four in the morning, the someone who knew which sentences to leave in the kitchen. Seventy-two hours. He did the arithmetic, because doing arithmetic was a thing his hands still knew how to do when nothing else worked. Three days. The board would settle inside three days; they had had it written for weeks. He would come out of this room into a company that no longer had a chair in it for him. The timing was not even cruel. The timing was just the machine's, which was to say no one's: both things were true, the hold and the handover, and the universe saw no reason to sequence them for his feeling.

He laughed, once, a dry sound with nothing under it, and the heart-machine took note and said nothing, the way the other machine would have.

---

And then, with no warning and no chemical and no permission, the hour came.

He would try, later, for the rest of whatever was left, to describe it and fail, because the only instrument he had for describing things was the toast, and the toast was the one tool that did not work in here. It was not a vision. Nothing glowed. No tear rolled down any cheek; he was too dry for that, hollowed, a husk under a light. It was simpler and worse than a vision. It was an hour of being awake. The first genuinely sober hour — sober in the blood and sober in the self, both at once, the powder gone and the wire gone and the audience gone and the story gone, the whole apparatus of not-looking offline at last — that he had stood inside since he was twenty-four years old and a man in a bathroom on Wall Street had taught him a particular way to fold a paper so a gentleman doesn't spill.

He saw himself the way the machine had seen him.

That was the thing about the hour, the unbearable engine of it. The machine had read him perfectly — *control failure of the first order,* the women in a table, the dead in the same font as the dividend — and it had felt nothing while it read, and its not-feeling had been the source of its accuracy; mercy blurs, self-preservation blurs, love most of all, and the machine had none of these to blur with. And now, lying under a light built on the same principle, Cope found he could do the machine's reading too. He could see the ledger. He could see it whole, all of it at once, the way he had never let himself see anything whole — but he could see it *with the soul still in,* with the very thing he had stood in a ballroom and called irreplaceable, the feeling, the room-sense, the heartbeat in the chair. He had the machine's eyes and his own nerves both, at last, in the same hour, and it turned out that was not a gift.

It turned out that was hell. That was the actual design of it. Pitiless accuracy and total feeling, married, with no high to divorce them.

He saw Renata as a person and not a row. He saw a young woman handing out coats, crying about something before her shift, and a card pressed into her hand by a man who experienced his own appetite as generosity. He saw Margaux flushing a stash down a toilet in Greenwich and refilling her own prescription and counting, the way you count anything to survive it. He saw Sun at four in the morning calling a man who owed the family since the eighties, knowing without being asked, and he understood for the first time that he had not been a man who inspired loyalty. He had been a man who manufactured accomplices and called the manufacturing love.

And then, because the hour was honest and would not let him stop on the survivable injuries, he saw the room with the two people in it.

He had never gone in. He went in now. There was nowhere else the hour would let him stand. He saw a downstairs bedroom in Defiance on a January night and a heater with his family's name on the grille and a thermostat with a failure mode the engineers had named in clean language and the lawyers had renamed in smooth language and he had renamed, at a dinner, in language he had rather liked. He saw the eleven minutes. He felt them from the inside the way he had felt them on the dead plant floor — the quarter mattering because the quarter was the thing the arithmetic watched, a recall a number you could not toast over, a bulletin quiet, a card mailed to people who do not register space heaters because no one registers a space heater. He had made the decision completely, the way he made everything, and then poured a floor over the room and gotten so good at the not-going-in that when the room came with a child in it he already knew the way not to look.

Karen Lasko, 34.

Petey Lasko, 6.

A boy who was six. Who would be twelve now, the age Cope had been when his father told him the only true thing anyone ever told him and he stopped listening after the first half. A boy who would be the age to put his whole arm up to the elbow in a vat at the Defiance plant, grinning, if the plant were still open and the boy were still alive, and the plant was not open and the boy was not alive, and the line between those two facts ran through a Saturday and eleven minutes and a man who needed, more than he needed to be good, to protect a number so that a board would keep telling him he was necessary.

He did not weep. He wanted to, the way you want a drink — as relief, as performance, as the thing that would discharge the unbearable into something you could hand a room and have the room absolve. The hour would not let him have it. The hour stood him in the room with the two of them and the light that did not blink and made him do the one thing he had spent thirty years and a fortune and a tower arranging never to do, which was to feel exactly what he was, fully, with the soul he had bragged about, and no audience, and no exit, and no high.

He was a man who had killed two people to protect a quarter and then accepted a crystal obelisk for having a heart.

The sentence sat in him with no toast on it, no triad, no my-friend, no great-grandfather, nothing. It was just true. It was the first thing he had thought in thirty years that he could not improve.

---

The door opened a crack of the wrong-colored light and a person stood in it, small against the corridor, and for a confused beat the part of him that still ran on habit got ready to perform — to sit up, to find the wattage, to fix a sadness onto himself and get someone laughing inside ninety seconds, the groove the record was cut in — and then the part of him that was awake in the hour saw that he had no wattage left to find, and was, for the first time, glad of it.

It was Pip.

She did not come all the way in. She stood in the door with the corridor behind her and looked at him under the light that flattered nothing, and he understood she was seeing him without any of the apparatus for the first time in her life, the dinosaur dismantled, just the man and the band on his wrist and the barcode that had made him fifty-four. She had her mother's stillness. She did not say *Dad* and she did not say the technical fast funny merciless thing and she did not, thank God, say anything kind, because kindness would have been a wattage he could have leaned into and she knew it, she built alignment for a living, she knew exactly which inputs produced which performances and she gave him none of them.

"They said an hour," she said. "I'm not staying. I just had to see it was you."

"It's me," Cope said. His voice came out wrong, unfooded, a stranger's. "What's left."

She looked at him a moment longer. Out in the corridor a cart went by, a wheel ticking. He thought she might come in. He found he did not want her to, not because he didn't want her — he wanted her so much it was a second animal want behind the first one — but because he could not stand for her to be near him in the hour, in the room with the two of them, where he finally was; he did not want to teach her the way not to look by demonstrating, one more time, the charm that filled a doorway so no one could see past it. He had practiced the not-looking on himself until he could do it on a child. He would not do it on this one.

"Pip," he said. The true thing was right there. The hour had walked him all the way to it, dragged him through everything else to set him down in front of the one thing, and it sat in his mouth, plain, no figure beside it, the only un-hollow sentence he had ever assembled in his life. *There is something I have to do and it will cost everything and it is the only thing I have left that is mine to do and I am going to tell the truth about Defiance, on the record, with my own name, no NDA, and let it cost what it costs, and it will not save anything, and I am going to do it anyway.*

He had it. He held it. It was the manifesto, the real one, the one that had been trying to assemble itself in him for nineteen months under all the noise, and here it finally was, and it was four sentences long and it had a town in it and a Saturday in it and a house in it, all the things the dinner version had left in the kitchen.

He did not say it.

Not because he had lost his nerve. Because the hour, in its last mercy and its last cruelty, would not let him hand it to her the way he had handed nine hundred people the word *beginning* and believed it as it left him. He had toasted his whole life over true things and watered them down to applause in the saying. This one he would not water. This one he would not say in a hospital room to his daughter's stillness to make her look at him a certain way; he had spent a career mistaking being looked at for being loved and he was done, in the hour, with the spending. He would say it where it cost. He would say it on a record, to the people he had answered to and lied to, in the wrong-colored light, with a stenographer instead of an ovation. He would do it. The doing, not the saying, was the whole of it. He had finally found the one sentence that needed an audience of none.

So what he said, instead, the only thing he let out of the room, was:

"I'm glad it's you."

Pip held the door a second more. Then she nodded, once, the smallest possible motion, a thing you would miss if you were looking for wattage, and she stepped back into the corridor and the wrong-colored light closed over the gap, and Cope lay alone under the tube that never blinked, sober in every sense for the first time in thirty years, with the one true thing assembled and held and not yet acted on, and the heart-machine beside him counted, and was, at last, almost right.
