# Chapter 8 — Defiance

> TRANSMISSION 08 — "The Ground You're Standing On"
>
> Every empire has a founding town it visits for the photographs and a reason it can't look at the photographs too long. Watch him walk the closed plant where four hundred dollars and a recipe became a fortune, shaking the hands of the people the fortune left behind, standing — and this is the part he doesn't know yet, but you're about to — directly on top of the thing that will end him. There is a name on a memorial here. Remember it. Next stop: the difference between a homecoming and a crime scene.
>

---

The first thing Cope did in Defiance, Ohio, was get out of the wrong door of the SUV on purpose.

Tisch had told him to. "The man who arrives," Tisch had said on the plane, doing the thing with his hands like he was smoothing a sheet over the future, "is not the man we want. We want the man who *returns.*" So Cope swung out of the curbside door into the early heat off the parking lot of the closed plant, and stood, and let his face do the thing a face does when it is moved, and let the boy with the gimbal rig walk a slow arc around him catching it, and felt nothing in particular except the clean bright hum that meant he'd stopped at the Toledo airport men's room and the hum was holding.

It was a beautiful morning to come home to a place you had never lived.

He had been to Defiance, by his own count, four times in fifty-four years. Once at nine, the vat, the photograph. Once at thirty for his father's idea of a leadership offsite, a weekend of golf two counties over and an hour at the plant for the local paper. Once in 2014 for the eightieth-anniversary thing. And once, in a way he did not count because he had never actually come — once in 2021, by phone, from the Aerie, on a Saturday, for eleven minutes. He did not think of that as a visit. He did not think of it at all. It was simply not among the times he had been to Defiance, the way a held breath is not among the things you've said.

The plant sat at the end of Clinton Street where the town gave out into the flat green nothing along the Maumee, a long brick building the color of dried blood with MERCER'S in white block letters near the roofline, the apostrophe gone, just MERCER S now, a tooth knocked out of the word. They'd closed the line in 2019 and moved the mayonnaise to a facility outside Columbus that ran with forty people instead of four hundred, and the building had done what buildings do when you take the people out, which is get quietly enormous. Pigeons in the high windows. A loading dock with grass coming up through it. And above the main entrance, where his great-grandfather had stood in a photograph Cope had seen ten thousand times, the cornerstone: ALDOUS MERCER 1933, and under it the line they'd put on everything for ninety years: *Made by hands, for homes.*

"God," Cope said, to the camera, to Sun, to the morning. "Look at her."

He meant it. That was the trick of him and always had been — he meant it while he was saying it, completely, the way the gimbal needed him to. His great-grandfather had turned four hundred borrowed dollars and a recipe his wife wrote on the back of a feed receipt into all of this, and standing here in the lot Cope could feel the whole length of it run through him like current down a wire, ninety-four years of Mercers ending, for now, in him, and he found the feeling enormous and clean and entirely about himself.

Sun was beside him with the run-of-show on a folded card. "Forty minutes here," she said. "You walk the floor with Hap Dengler — he ran the line thirty years, he's the one in the flannel, be warm but don't keep him, he's seventy-nine and the heat. Then the diner, the statue, the river walk, then wheels up by two." She fed him the day the way she fed him everything, in shapes that got past the charm and into the man. "The diner's the set piece. That's the one that travels."

"And the camera's invisible to them?"

"The camera's a documentary about American work," Sun said. "That's what we told the town. That's what's true enough."

Hap Dengler had hands like a catcher's mitt and would not quite look at Cope.

That was the first thing — not unfriendly, Dengler shook the hand and said it was an honor and called him Mr. Mercer, but the eyes went past Cope's shoulder to the dead line, the way you'd look at the bed of somebody who'd just died in it. He walked Cope down the silent floor where the conveyors still stood, scaled with old grease, the air cool and vast and smelling faintly, even now, of vinegar and steel, and Dengler talked about the line the way men talk about a war they were in, the speed of it, the million-jar week, the names — Pugh, Castellano, the two Marias — and Cope nodded and touched a conveyor rail with two fingers, reverent, and the boy with the gimbal got it from below, the cathedral light coming down through the high dirty glass onto the last human chief executive communing with the work.

"My grandfather brought me here when I was nine," Cope said. "Put my arm in a vat of it. Up to here." He touched his bicep. He had told the story so many times it had worn smooth as a stone in a pocket, and it landed now the way it always landed, and Dengler almost smiled.

"That'd be the old number-four batch tank," Dengler said. "We took her out in '11. Sold her for scrap to a fella in Findlay." He looked at the empty bay where she'd stood. "Three hundred dollars."

There was a thing in the way he said the number. Cope heard it and chose, smoothly, the way he chose past a hundred such things a day, not to.

They walked on past the bottling end, where the labelers stood in a row like organ pipes, and Dengler told him the building had been more than the line — the softball team, the credit union in the front office, the Christmas in the sixties when Cope's own father had come out and handed every man a turkey by hand, by *hand,* Dengler said, looking at him, and known that Dengler's father had pitched the no-hitter in '61. "Your dad knew my dad's name," Dengler said. It was not quite an accusation. It was a man laying a thing on the scale and watching where the needle went. Cope said his father had been a great man, which was true and meant nothing, and Dengler nodded at the floor.

When the insurance arm started cross-selling appliances out of the plant store in the back — small stuff, the household line, toasters and fans and the heaters in the winter, Mercer on the grille — Dengler had run the loading for that too, boxes going out on the same dock the jars used to. "Wasn't really us," Dengler said. "But it had the name on it, so." He shrugged, a man who had loaded what he was told to load. Cope said, *mm,* and touched a labeler, reverent, and they moved on toward the daylight at the end of the floor, and somewhere under the concrete they were walking on, under all of it, the buried thing lay where it lay and did not stir, because that is the entire point of a floor.

---

The Maumee Diner had been the Maumee Diner since 1948 and had a pie case that turned.

They'd cleared the back booths — "regulars," the manager kept saying, apologetic, "these are the regulars, they don't move for nobody," and Tisch had said *perfect, keep them, that's the texture* — so Cope worked the front of the house, the part that would travel. He was good at this. There was no version of Cope that was not good at this. He slid into the counter stool next to a man in a Defiance Bulldogs cap and ordered, before the man could be awkward about it, exactly what the man was having — eggs over with sausage and white toast — and ate it, all of it, with the appetite of a man who had not eaten because he had been keying himself bright in a Toledo bathroom, and he asked about the Bulldogs and the man unclenched and they were off.

He remembered the cards Sun gave him and he didn't need them. He never needed them, not for this; this was the one true gift, the only thing his father had been wrong about. He knew the waitress was Donna inside a minute and that her boy was at Bowling Green on a partial scholarship for wrestling, and he told her the boy should call his office, and he meant it, the way he meant everything, the full wattage poured out across the laminate, and Donna lit up and the camera caught it and that, Sun would say later, was the shot.

It was a good forty minutes. He bought the house's checks. He let a woman take a picture and told her she hadn't aged a day, which made no sense, he'd never met her, and she laughed anyway because the not-making-sense was part of it, the warm fraud of it, the way the toast at a wedding doesn't have to be true to make the room cry.

He told the vat story again, the arm in the mayonnaise to the elbow, and a younger man two stools down said his own grandmother had worked the line, the second-shift line, the one that did the relish, and Cope said relish was the unsung hero of the American sandwich, and got a laugh he hadn't earned and took it anyway. He was generous with the room and the room was generous back. This was the thing he believed about himself that happened, just often enough, to be true: that he could walk into a place that had every reason to hate the name on its dead building and have it eating out of his hand inside the hour, not because he tricked them but because some real warm current did pass between him and a room, did make Donna's whole tired face open like a window. He felt the room. He always felt the room.

And there was one man at the end of the counter who did not laugh.

Cope clocked him the way he clocked everything, peripherally, expertly, a heavyset man maybe sixty in a windbreaker with a coffee he wasn't drinking, watching Cope work the room with an expression Cope had no slot for. Not a fan. Not a heckler — a heckler was just a fan who needed more attention, and Cope would have walked down there and gotten his name and broken him open like an egg. This was something else. The man looked at Cope the way Dengler had looked at the dead line. Like he was looking at the bed.

"Who's the gentleman at the end," Cope said to Sun, low, still smiling at the room.

Sun followed his eyes and something crossed her face, fast, gone. "Local," she said. "We don't need the end of the counter. Wave to Donna and let's get you to the statue."

"He looks like he wants to say something."

"He doesn't," Sun said, and it was the *doesn't*, the flatness of it, the way she put her body a half-step between Cope and the end of the counter as she said it, that told some back room of him that filed the report and did not deliver it. Cope let himself be steered. On the way out he passed close enough to the man in the windbreaker that he could have stopped, could have put out the hand, *Cope Mercer, good to meet you,* and the man's eyes came up and met his and held, and there was no heat in them at all, which was the thing — just a kind of patient terrible knowing, the look of a man who has carried something so long it has stopped being heavy and become simply his, and Cope felt the back of his neck go cold in the August heat and smiled and kept walking and was out the door into the brightness before he had finished deciding not to think about it.

The hum had thinned. He noticed it the way you notice a song has ended.

"Two minutes," he told Sun. "Before the statue. I want to look at my notes."

He did not have notes. He went around the side of the diner where the SUVs were and stood between two of them out of the camera's eye and did, quickly and competently, the thing that brought the hum back up to pitch, and the world resolved into its clean overexposed brightness, and the cold spot at the back of his neck warmed and closed, and he came back around the corner with the wattage full again, and the man in the windbreaker was a thing that had happened to someone else.

---

The statue of Aldous Mercer stood in the little triangle park where Clinton met Second, eight feet of bronze gone the green of old pennies, the founder in his shirtsleeves with one hand out as if offering you something, a jar maybe, a future, and behind him the same line off the cornerstone, *Made by hands, for homes,* cut into the granite base.

This was the easy part. Cope knew how to stand at the foot of his own family. He gave the camera the upward angle, the founder and the heir in one frame, ninety-four years compressed into a sightline, and said a few words to the small crowd that had gathered — there is always a small crowd, even for the last of something — about hands, and homes, and the difference between a company that makes a thing and a company that just moves a number, and the crowd, which had worked with its hands and lost the work, gave him the warm ground-up applause he lived on, because he was, whatever else he was, *theirs.*

A man near the front had brought his daughter, maybe six, up on his shoulders to see, and the girl waved a small flag somebody had handed out, and Cope pointed at her and said the future was watching, the future had a better view than any of them, and the crowd laughed and the father beamed up at his kid, and Cope felt the surf-push come up under him, the warm total certainty, and did not think about a different child, because there was no reason a child would have come to mind at all, and none did, and the not-coming-to-mind was a clean cool floor he stood on and gave his speech from.

"He started with four hundred dollars," Cope said, "and the one thing a machine will never have, which is somebody to come home to." It didn't quite parse. It didn't need to. Tisch, off to the side, touched two fingers to his own heart: *that's the one.*

It was on the river walk that he saw the house, and the small bright thing beside it, and did not let himself see either.

The walk ran along the Maumee behind the old residential streets, routed for the water and the light, and they were maybe a hundred yards along it, the camera ahead and walking backward, when Cope's eye snagged on a house up the slope. A nice house. Newer than the ones around it, brick and pale siding, a little raw at the edges the way a new thing is among old things, the landscaping not grown in yet. It sat in a row of older houses like a tooth that had been replaced. *Rebuilt,* some part of him said, before he could stop it, the part that had grown up in real estate, that read a streetscape the way Dengler read a line.

And at the foot of the slope, where the river walk passed the bottom of the property, there was a small low marker. Not a grave. A memorial, the kind a town makes — a flat granite plaque on a fieldstone base, a young maple planted behind it, staked, still in its first years, and at its foot somebody had left a child's pinwheel, blue, turning a little in the breeze off the water, the cheap foil of it catching the sun and throwing it back, on and off, on and off.

Cope's gait did not change. That was the thing about Cope; the gait never changed. But the camera was on him and the camera was hungry and the boy with the gimbal, who knew nothing, who thought this was a documentary about American work, drifted the lens toward the pretty turning pinwheel and the little stone because it was a beautiful sad image and the morning light was on it, and Sun said, instantly, "Stay on Cope, stay tight on Cope," in a voice that had a thing in it the boy had never heard from her, and the lens came back.

Cope read the plaque. He could not not read it; it was there, twelve feet away, the granite catching the same sun as the pinwheel. He read it the way you read a sign in a dream, the words arriving without weight, refusing to assemble:

IN LOVING MEMORY — KAREN LASKO — AND HER SON PETEY LASKO — 2021 — *taken home too soon.*

A name on a memorial. Two names. A mother and her boy.

Something in Cope went very still, the way the air goes still before the part of the storm that matters, and for one half of one second the tiredness took the doors off their hinges the way it had once before, at a window, at one in the morning, and the hallway stood open, dark, leading back to a Saturday and a memo and a window of time and a word the lawyers had liked, *thermal event profile,* and a thing he had decided in eleven minutes and lived inside ever since by the simple method of never going into the room where it was kept —

and then he did the thing he always did, which was charm.

"Beautiful spot," Cope said, to Sun, to the water, to nobody, turning his shoulder to the slope, turning away from the little stone the way the body turns from a flame before the mind has named it heat. "You can see why he built here. The light off the water." He clapped his hands together once, briskly, a man closing a meeting. "I'm getting hungry again, can you believe that, after all that. Tell me we have time for Donna's pie."

"We don't," Sun said. She was looking at him the way she had looked at the end of the counter, and the way she had looked at him in the rearview on the night of the gala, a look with too much in it, a look doing arithmetic. "Wheels up. You did great. You did really great today, Cope."

"I did, didn't I." He was already moving, already up the walk toward the cars, his back to the river and the house and the staked young maple and the blue pinwheel turning and turning behind him, and he did not look back, and the not-looking was effortless, was a thing he had been doing his whole life and had never once noticed he was doing. "I felt it today, Sun. Did you feel it? They feel it. We're not finished. We're barely begun."

He needed the bathroom on the plane. He told himself it was the coffee.

---

On the climb out of Toledo the town tilted under the wing and lay flat and green and small, the river a brown thread through it, the dried-blood brick of the plant, the little triangle where the bronze man held out his hand, all of it shrinking into the patchwork until it was just one more place that had given everything to a name, and Cope watched it go with the hum back at full pitch and a glass of something he wasn't drinking sweating on the tray.

Tisch was in the facing seat, scrubbing through the footage on a tablet, narrating his own genius. "The diner is a *cathedral,*" Tisch said. "Donna is — Cope, Donna is the whole thesis in an apron. The machine doesn't know Donna's kid wrestles. The machine can't *want* the boy to call." He turned the tablet around. There Cope was on the little screen, eating eggs at the counter, lit from the window, and he did look, Cope had to admit, like the last warm-blooded thing in America. "This is more than a cover. This is the spread."

"It's good," Cope agreed. He felt the rightness of it move through him, the win of it, the same surf-push he had leaned into at the gala. The campaign was working. He had gone to the founding town and the founding town had loved him, had given him its hands to shake and its eggs to eat and its applause to stand in, and that was the truth, that had really happened, and Cope let it be the whole truth because he was very, very good at letting a true thing be the whole thing.

And under it, under the win, in some sealed back room with the door shut and the floor poured over it, there was a thing he would not have called dread because he would not have called it anything, because to call it anything he would have had to go in and read the name on it, and he had spent six years and a settlement and a voluntary service bulletin and a great deal of money making sure there was a floor between him and that room — a good floor, a plant floor, vast and cool and smelling of vinegar and steel.

The pinwheel. He saw it once more, behind his eyes, blue, turning, throwing the light, and then he put the glass to his lips and did not drink and watched Ohio disappear under the cloud and thought, with the bright frictionless certainty he told himself was strength, *I felt the room today. A machine could never have felt that room.*

He was right. That was the unbearable part, the part the machine would itemize for him in nineteen months in his own company's voice, reading every memo because it could not do otherwise, opening the room because it had no instinct for mercy or self-preservation to make it look away. He had felt the room. He had felt all of it — Dengler's eyes going past him to the dead line, the man at the end of the counter with his patient terrible knowing, the pinwheel turning at the foot of a staked young tree. He had felt every bit of it, perfectly, completely, the way only a man with a soul in him can.

And then he had charmed it, and keyed it bright, and turned his shoulder, and flown away.

The plane leveled off. The seatbelt sign went dark with a soft warm chime, the kind of sound the machines were teaching everything to make now, the sound of a thing being reasonable at you until you were gone.

Sun came up the aisle and sat across from him and did not pick up a tablet, which was unusual, and looked at him for a moment in the cabin light with the day still on her face. She had something she was deciding whether to say; he had seen the look before and never once learned to read it as anything but admiration that needed time to surface.

"Big day," he offered.

"Cope." She stopped. He waited, generous, ready to receive the praise. The cabin hummed. Below them the cloud closed over Ohio like a lid. "Nothing," she said finally. "It was a good day. Drink some water." She got up and went back to her seat and her screens and the thing she had drafted in 2021 and kept a copy of, and Cope took that, too, as agreement, which was a thing he had been doing his whole life and never once noticed he was doing.

"Get some sleep, champ," Tisch said, not looking up. "Big week."

Cope smiled and said he would, and did not, and somewhere far below and behind him a small blue pinwheel turned in the breeze off the Maumee in front of a house that had been built twice, in front of a stone with two names on it, throwing the light on and off, on and off, to no one, all afternoon.
