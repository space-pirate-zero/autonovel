# Chapter 16 — Renata

> TRANSMISSION 16 — "She Was Never a Story"
>
> Every powerful man's scandal has a woman he has cast, in the privacy of
> his own legend, as either the temptation or the redemption. Watch her
> walk in and refuse both parts. She was not a love story. She was not a
> headline. She was an employee whose career he spent, and she is here,
> unbitter and unbreakable, to remind him — and the board — that the
> firing offense was never the affair. It was the lie he told the people
> he answered to. Next stop: the precise governance crime, served cold.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

He had imagined the scene so many times, over so many years, that when it finally came it had the slightly used quality of a suit worn to too many funerals.

In the imagining, it was always a doorway. Renata in a doorway, evening light behind her, the years having done to her what years did, and Cope rising from somewhere — a chair, a hospital bed, in one version a courtroom — and saying the thing. He had a few versions of the thing. The good one started *I never deserved* and got better from there. In all of them she forgave him, which he experienced, in the privacy of the daydream, not as forgiveness but as a kind of verdict overturned on appeal, the record sealed, the man restored. He had cast her, without ever once using the word to himself, as the person who would someday tell him he had not been the worst version of the story he was in.

She did not come in a doorway. She came in a conference room on the nineteenth floor of a building that wasn't his, at four in the afternoon, in a gray blazer she had clearly chosen because it would not photograph as anything, carrying a single legal folder and a paper cup of the bad coffee from the machine in the hall, and the first thing she said to him, before the lawyers had finished arranging their pads, was:

"You look tired, Cope."

And he heard, under it, with the bright wire in his chest going very still, that she did not mean it kindly and did not mean it cruelly. She just meant it. The way you'd note the weather.

---

The room had been arranged by people whose job was arranging rooms, which is to say it had been arranged to be deniable. Not Mercer-Dale's offices, not Tidewater's, not Renata's firm's, but a neutral floor a relocation company rented by the hour to companies that needed to be in the same room as a thing they did not want to be seen near. There were eleven people in it. Cope had counted, the way he counted rooms, and then had stopped counting because he did not like the number he was getting on his own side.

His side: Cope. Sun, who had not wanted him here and had said so in three clean parts in the elevator and been overruled by the one part of him that still believed a room was a thing he could win. Two lawyers from Brandt Michelson, a man and a woman, both of whom Cope had met four times and could not have named under oath. The general counsel, Hollis, gray as a filing cabinet, with the specific stillness of a man calculating his own exposure. And a woman from the special committee — a director Cope barely knew, Adaeze Okafor, retired audit-firm partner, brought onto the Mercer-Dale board fourteen months ago for exactly this, for the day a thing came up that the regular directors could not be in the room for without becoming part of it. She did not have a pad. She had a phone, face-down, and her hands folded on top of it, and she watched Cope the way you watch a structure you have been told is unsound.

Her side — Renata's — was just Renata and one lawyer, a tired young man named Ferro who looked like he billed by the syllable and had decided to spend none of them.

It curdled something in Cope, the arithmetic of it. He had brought five. She had brought one and a half. He had spent his whole life understanding that the size of your delegation was the size of your fear, and here he was with a delegation, and there she was with a coffee.

"Renata," he said. He had decided in the car to use her name early, warmly, to take it back from the lawyers, to make it a human room. "Thank you for —"

"Let's not do thank-yous," Ferro said, without heat, turning a page. "My client agreed to be in the room. She didn't agree to be grateful in it."

---

Here is what the press already had, by that Tuesday: a name. Not Renata's — they had a *current* name, wrong, a woman in corporate communications who had never been within a marriage of Cope and who was now, because of a leaked calendar and a stupid lunch, being doorstepped at her son's school. And they had the shape of an older thing. A 2019 settlement. A VP who had left "to pursue other opportunities," which was the phrase the company used the way the Hearthstone documents used *thermal event profile*, a phrase built specifically to be a wall. Reuters had the docket number of a thing that had been sealed and was now, in the way of sealed things during a proxy fight, being un-sealed by people who had decided the public had an interest.

Cope had told Sun it was survivable. He had told her it was 2019, ancient history, a private matter between consenting adults, that the *current* story was a nothing because it was a nothing, and that Renata — he had actually said this, in the Aerie, at two in the morning with the wire singing — Renata had loved him, that whatever the paper said, the truth of it was that two people had cared for each other inside a situation that made caring impossible, and that if it came to it, if it ever truly came to it, she would not want to hurt him.

He had built, in other words, a small cathedral, and cast her as the light through the window.

She had asked for the meeting. That was the part he kept holding up to the light, the way he'd held the obelisk. *She* had asked. Through Ferro, through Hollis, a request: a room, the right people, an hour. He had read it as the daughter of his daydream walking back up the avenue. Sun had read it correctly, which was why Sun had not wanted him in the room.

---

"I asked for this," Renata said, "so I could say a few things once, to the people who need to hear them, and then never say them again. I'm not recording. You can record. I assume someone is." She glanced, neutrally, at Hollis. "I'd like to start before the lawyers tell me which parts I'm allowed to feel."

"Renata —" Cope started.

"I'm going to talk to her, actually," Renata said, and turned a few degrees in her chair, toward Adaeze Okafor and the face-down phone. "If that's all right. You're the board."

"I'm the special committee," Okafor said. "But yes."

"Then you're who this is for."

Cope sat with the strangeness of being, for the first time in possibly thirty years, the second-most-important person in a room with his name on the building. He had a sentence ready — he always had a sentence ready — and he could feel it going stale in his mouth, the *we cared for each other* sentence, the cathedral sentence, because she had walked past it without looking, the way you walk past a busker.

"Mr. Mercer hired me in 2016," Renata said, to Okafor, in a voice with no tremor and no edge, a voice reporting. "I ran the Hearthside division — small appliances, the cross-sell stuff. I was good at it. I want that in the room, because the story that's going to get told outside this room is going to be that I was a pretty girl he noticed. I was a VP who turned a dying category eleven points of margin. He noticed *that* first. I have the decks. I bring this up not because my pride needs it." She paused. "It does, a little. But mostly because the firing offense in here" — she tapped the folder, once — "has nothing to do with how I looked. And I don't want anybody in this room comforting themselves later that it did."

"Renata, no one thinks —" Cope said.

"You did," she said. She did not raise her voice. "You told me I was the smartest person in the building and then you spent four years making sure that wasn't the sentence anyone remembered about me. That's not an accusation. It's a personnel record."

---

Cope had a backswing for moments like this. He had charmed a senator's widow at his own table; he had gotten a board to vote against its own arithmetic on the strength of his face. He leaned forward now and put both forearms on the table and brought the whole wattage up, slow, the Mercer Inhale, the pause he had taken in a thousand rooms.

"Can I just — as a human being. Not as a — Hollis, let me." He spread his hands. The hands had never let him down. "What we had, whatever its — whatever you want to call it. It wasn't *nothing*, Renata. I won't sit here and let a roomful of lawyers turn two people into a deposition. There was real —"

"There it is," Renata said. Quietly. Almost to herself. And then, to Okafor, not unkindly: "Watch this part. This is the part he's good at. He's about to make it a love story, because in a love story the worst thing he did was love someone he shouldn't have, and that's a *forgivable* thing, that's a *human* thing, men have written symphonies about that thing." She looked back at Cope, and for one second something passed behind her face that was not anger, was older and more tired than anger, was the specific exhaustion of having to explain to a man, again, the inside of his own conduct. "It wasn't a love story, Cope. I know that's the version you've kept. I've thought about you keeping it. It's not the version that happened."

"I never —"

"You slept with someone whose career you controlled, and when it got complicated you handled it the way you handle a recall." She let that sit. She had no idea, Cope realized with a small cold drop, how exactly that word would land, and that was almost worse — that she'd reached for it as a figure of speech. "You assessed the exposure. You found the cheapest number. You bought a wall and you put my name behind it and you told yourself a story where I was a chapter in your life instead of the end of mine, at that company, doing the thing I was good at. I didn't leave to pursue other opportunities. There weren't any. You'd made sure of the references without ever once being unkind to my face. You were *never unkind to my face.* That's the part I couldn't explain to anybody for about three years."

---

The young lawyer, Ferro, had stopped turning pages.

Sun was looking at the table. Cope wanted, badly, for Sun to look up, to give him the room with her eyes, to do the thing she did out of the architecture, and she did not look up, and he understood she would not, and a small structural beam in him went.

"This is —" Cope said, and heard his own voice find the register it went to under pressure, clipped, a little biblical, and could not stop it. "I want to say something, and I want the committee to hear it as what it is, which is a man — a flawed man, I have never claimed otherwise, I stood up in a ballroom and told nine hundred people the only thing a machine can't do is *err* —" He had not said that. He had said *love.* He did not notice the swap. "I'm a flawed human man. That's the whole — that's literally the entire thing I've been saying for two years. And now the same people who want to hand my chair to a calculator are going to take the most human failing there is and put it on a chyron and call it a *governance crisis.* It's a witch hunt. It's a witch hunt against the last man in the building who still has a pulse, and the pulse is the evidence, that's the — do you see what they've built? A world where the disqualifying thing about a leader is that he's a *person.*"

He had said it the way he said things. It had a triad in it somewhere, almost. It had, for about four seconds, the shape of something he could have toasted with.

And then it just sat there, on the deniable table, in the rented room, and curdled.

Because Renata was looking at him with something that was not even contempt. It was patience. It was the patience of a woman who had done the reading.

"You think the firing offense is the affair," she said.

"I think the —"

"It isn't." She opened the folder, not to read from it — she didn't need it, he could see she didn't need it, she had it the way he had Aldous and the mayonnaise — but to put her hand flat on it, to make it a thing in the room. "I've had a long time to understand exactly what you did wrong, Cope, and it took me about two years to stop thinking it was the bedroom, because that's where you wanted me to think it was. The bedroom's between us. It was wrong and it was ours and it's not why any of these lawyers are here." She turned, fully now, to Okafor and the special committee and the audit partner's folded hands. "You're not here because the chairman slept with a VP. You're here because of what came after."

---

She laid it out the way you'd lay out a quarter.

In 2019, when the situation she would not call a love story became a liability the company could be sued over, Cope had a duty. Not a moral one — Renata waved the moral one away, almost impatient with it, it was the least interesting duty in the room. A *governance* duty. He sat on a board. He answered to a board. And the board's compensation committee, the audit committee, the general counsel sitting right there gray as a cabinet, had a settlement come across their work with a number on it and a category attached to it, and the category said *commercial dispute, departing executive,* and the category was a lie, and Cope knew it was a lie, and Cope had personally — she had the email, it was in the folder, Ferro had it filed and tabbed — personally directed that the matter be characterized in a way that kept it off the board's radar. Kept it out of the disclosure to the directors. Kept it from the people he was, by charter, by law, by the entire load-bearing fiction of the corporation, *accountable to.*

"You can sleep with whoever you want," Renata said. "I mean that almost literally — the board can't fire you for the affair, your own lawyers will tell you that, it's tawdry but it's not a crime. What they can fire you for — what they have to fire you for, if they want to be a board and not a portrait gallery — is that you looked at the people you answer to and you decided they didn't get to know. You made yourself unaccountable. You took a thing that belonged to the people who own the company and you put it in a drawer with your name on the lock." She closed the folder. "Easterbrook didn't lose a hundred million for the affair. He lost it for the second interview, when he sat across from his own board and didn't tell them the whole list. The cover-up. The concealment. That's the offense, Cope. Not the sin. The lie you told the room you answered to."

It was so quiet in the room that Cope could hear the bad coffee machine in the hall finish a cup for someone who wasn't there.

He understood, sitting in it, that he could not answer her. Not that he chose not to. That there was, in the entire warm inventory of him — the toasts, the triads, the face that had never let him down, Aldous and the four hundred borrowed dollars and the arm in the vat to the elbow — there was no sentence. She had taken the one door he reached for, the *flawed human man,* and shown him it opened onto the very thing that was hanging him: not that he was a man, but that he was a man who *hid.* The machine, he thought, with a lurch he did not have a name for, the machine couldn't have hid this. The machine logged everything. The machine would have put the settlement in the board portal with a transparent rationale by Wednesday and it would have been awful and it would have been *clean,* and the awful clean thing was the thing the board had paid ten figures less than him to get.

He had spent two years arguing that a machine couldn't love a company.

She had just shown him, in a gray blazer, with a folder, that the company had never asked him to love it. It had asked him to *tell it the truth,* and that was the one human thing he had withheld.

---

"That's all," Renata said. She stood. She had not raised her voice once, and she did not raise it now, and that, more than anything, was the thing that made Cope feel the floor of himself drop a level he had not known was there. "I'm not going to the press. Ferro will tell you the terms, which haven't changed, which is that I want nothing, which I know you can't understand. I came so that when this is told — and it's going to be told, you've made sure of that, the whole world's going to get the version with the pretty girl in it — at least one room would have heard the real shape of it. From me. Once."

She picked up the coffee cup she had not drunk.

"You weren't the love of my life, Cope. You weren't even the villain of it. You were just my boss, and you were careless with me the way you've been careless with everything, and then you were careful — God, you were so *careful* — about making sure it cost you nothing. That's the whole story. There's no machine in it and there's no soul in it. There's just a man who could've told the truth to four people in a conference room in 2019 and instead spent six years and however many dollars making sure he never had to."

She paused at the door. Not the doorway of the daydream, the evening light, the verdict overturned. Just a fire door, in a rented hall, with a coffee cup in her hand.

"I hope somebody gets you some sleep," she said, and it was the kindest thing anyone said to him that whole season, and it was not kind, and she left.

---

The room reorganized itself around her absence the way rooms do. Ferro gathered his syllables. The Brandt Michelson lawyers began, in low murmurs, the work of converting a human catastrophe back into a manageable legal one. Hollis was already on his phone. Okafor turned her own phone over, looked at it, and then looked at Cope for a long moment with an expression he could not charm because it was not asking to be charmed; it was the expression of a person finishing the arithmetic, and the number was not in his favor, and they both knew she had been brought onto the board to be a person who would do the arithmetic anyway.

"Mr. Mercer," she said, and stood, and that was all she said, his name and a courtesy, and it landed on him like a sentence with the second half left off.

Sun stayed. Sun always stayed. She came around the table and stood near him, not touching, in the way she had of being available to a man who could not be helped, and she did not say *I told you,* and she did not say *we'll fix it,* and the not-saying was the truest brief she had ever given him.

Cope sat at the deniable table in the room that wasn't his.

He was fifty-four and the camera still loved him and the jaw was still the jaw, and somewhere far down the avenue a bus shelter was changing its slide, and up in the Aerie a manifesto waited that he had been sure, at one in the morning, would save the world from the loss of him. He had walked into the room a flawed human man and he had liked that phrase, he had polished it in the car, it had a pulse in it, it had a kind of grandeur. He understood now that it had been the last large thing he was going to be able to say about himself.

He felt, sitting there, smaller than he had ever in his life let himself be — smaller than the obelisk, smaller than the chair, smaller than the four people in 2019 he had decided did not get to know — and the smallness was not a feeling he could toast over or fold into a paper or set up neat and quick in a drawer with a false back. It just sat in him, exact and quiet, the size of one withheld sentence.

He did not reach for a line. For once in his life, there wasn't one.

The coffee machine in the hall finished another cup for nobody.
