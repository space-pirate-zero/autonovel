# Chapter 17 — The Names for It

> TRANSMISSION 17 — "A Doctor Who Won't Be Charmed"
>
> At last, a room he can't toast his way out of. A psychiatrist — summoned by the women who love him — is about to do the one thing no board, no activist, no machine has managed: say the plain clinical words for what he is, the mood disorder *and* the cocaine, and refuse to let him use either as an alibi for the other. Addiction, she insists, is not a metaphor. He has built a cathedral of metaphors. Next stop: the floor under the floor.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

They had ambushed him with a living room.

That was the first thing Cope understood, walking into the Greenwich house at nine on a Sunday morning he'd been told was about the lawyers — and it was a good ambush, he had to give them that, because the only way to get him into a room he didn't want to enter was to make him think he was the one who'd called the meeting. Margaux had made coffee. There was a tray. There was, sitting in the wing chair that nobody sat in, the one angled toward the window that his mother had reupholstered in 1981 and Margaux had never had the heart to redo, a small woman of about sixty in a gray cardigan with a notebook closed on her lap and no coffee in front of her, which he clocked the way he clocked everything, because the ones who don't take the coffee are the ones you can't get to.

"Cope," Margaux said, "this is Dr. Adler."

"Dad." Pip was on the sofa with her knees up. She'd flown in. He hadn't known she was in the country. The not-knowing arrived and was filed and did not reach his face. "Sit down. Please."

He did not sit down. He stood in the doorway of his own living room with his weekend zip-up on and the bright fine wire still going in his chest from the bump he'd done in the car at the foot of the drive — neat, quick, a man maintaining a machine — and he ran the wattage up the way you bring up house lights, slow, warm, total.

"Well," he said. "This is a hell of a thing to wake up to. Three of my favorite women and a tray." He smiled at the doctor. "I'm sorry, I'm at a disadvantage. Cope Mercer. You're a friend of Margaux's?"

"I'm a psychiatrist," Dr. Adler said. "Your wife and your daughter asked me to come. They're worried about you. I'm here to talk with you, and then I'm here to tell you what I think."

No softening on it. No *I just wanted to chat.* He'd expected the softening — there was always a softening, that was the handle you grabbed — and its absence put a small cold spot in the middle of the warm.

"Worried," Cope said. He came in. He did not take the sofa, which would have put him level with Pip; he took the hard chair near the doorway, which let him keep the room in front of him, all of it, the exits. "I love that. I'm at the best I've been in a decade and the women I love most have hired a — forgive me, I don't mean it the way it'll sound — a *grief counselor* for a man who isn't dead. That's the most Mercer thing that's ever happened in this house, and my father drowned a dog in this house."

"He didn't drown a dog," Margaux said quietly.

"He let it drown. There's a difference and the family observed it." He was rolling now, he could feel it come up under him. "Doctor, I'll save you the hourly. I sleep a little less than I should. I'm in the fight of my life — you may have seen, the company, the — it's been on the television, the proxy thing, the machine thing. A man doesn't sleep eight hours during a war. Eisenhower didn't. I read four hundred years of Mercer history and not one of them slept through a thing that mattered. So if the diagnosis is *busy,* I'll take it, and I'll sign the bill, and we can all have the eggs."

---

Dr. Adler opened the notebook. She did not write in it. She just opened it, like setting a clock on a table.

"How many nights this week did you sleep more than three hours," she said.

"That's not — you can't reduce a man to —"

"It's a yes-it-was-this-many or I-don't-know question. How many."

"I don't keep a — Sun would know, she —"

"I-don't-know is an answer." She made a small mark. So she did write. "When did you last eat a full meal at a table and finish it."

"I'm not a finisher. I'm a grazer. Ask Margaux, twenty-two years, I —"

"Eight pounds," Margaux said, to the window. Not to him. "In five weeks. I had your blue suit taken in twice. The tailor stopped charging me for the second one. He felt bad."

That landed somewhere he didn't have a face for, and he covered the place where it landed with a laugh. "The tailor *felt bad.* See, this is what I love about this family. We could open a museum of people feeling bad on my behalf and charge admission and *still* lose to the streaming arm." Nobody laughed. Pip was watching him with the specific stillness she got, the one that used to mean she was about to cry at eight and now meant she had decided not to. "It's a *campaign,* I'm not eating because there's no *time* to eat, there's a thing called the —"

"How much are you using," Dr. Adler said.

The wire in his chest changed pitch.

He had been waiting for it, of course — somewhere under the talking he had known the tray meant this, the doctor meant this, the not-touching-the-coffee meant this — but he had expected it to come in dressed up. He had a whole wardrobe ready for it. *Recreational, occasional, the same as a Scotch, you don't say a word about the second glass of Margaux's Sancerre.* He had given that speech, in his head, to imaginary versions of this woman, and in every version she had blushed and retreated and he had won.

She had not asked *whether.* She had asked *how much.* You cannot put your prepared answer to *whether* up against a *how much*; the question had already conceded the thing the answer was built to deny, and stood there waiting on the other side of it.

"That's an extraordinary thing to ask a man in his own —"

"It's a number," she said. "Grams a week. Best estimate. I'm not the police, I'm not the board, and I'm not your wife, who already knows, and I'm not your daughter, who guessed. I'm the one person in this room you can give the real number to without it costing you anything you haven't already spent. How much."

---

He looked at Margaux.

He did it without meaning to — the old reflex, find the soft one, find the handle — and there was nothing there. She had her arms crossed and her chin up and she was looking at the window where the gray Sound was doing nothing, and he understood, in the way you understand a thing you've worked for years not to, that *already knows* was not a guess Adler had made. It was a fact Margaux had supplied. She had counted. She had flushed things. She had refilled her own prescriptions and called it a marriage, and she had told this woman in the gray cardigan the number before he walked in, and the only person in the room who didn't have the number was him, because he was the only one who'd never let himself add it up.

"You flushed the desk one," he said to her. Not a question. "In March. I thought it was the cleaner. I *fired* —" He stopped. He had fired a man. A man named Anatoly with a daughter at Fordham. He had fired him over a thing his wife had done to save his life. He sat with that for the length of one breath and then put it, too, in the kitchen with the other sentences.

"Three," he said, to the doctor, finally, and heard his own voice come out smaller than he'd sent it. "Three, maybe. A week. When it's heavy. Less when it's —" The number was a lie and he knew it was a lie even as the smallness made it feel like honesty; the smallness was its own performance, the confession-shaped thing you give so you won't have to give the confession. "Four," he said. "Four. It's been a four kind of month."

Adler wrote it down. She did not look horrified, which was somehow worse than horror. She looked the way Sun looked at a number on a deck — like it was simply true, and now they could work.

"All right," she said. "Thank you. That took something. Now I'm going to tell you what I think, and I want you to do me the courtesy of not performing while I do it, because you're very good and I'll enjoy it and it won't help you."

"I don't perform," Cope said, and even he heard it.

"You toasted your way through the first nine minutes of this. You did a bit about a dog." She closed the notebook. "Here it is. I think you are in a hypomanic episode that is tipping, or has already tipped, into a manic one. The not-sleeping isn't discipline and it isn't war. It's a symptom. The grandiosity — the certainty about your — what is it, the Human Premium —"

"It's not grandiosity if you're —"

"The certainty," she went on, unhurried, "the spending, the pressured speech, the religious conviction that you alone can be the thing that saves them all — those are not a personality, Mr. Mercer, and they are not a calling. They have names. They are on a list. I could show you the list and you would find yourself on it the way you find your horoscope, except it would be right." She let that sit. "And underneath it, feeding it, lit up by it, is a cocaine addiction of long standing. Decades. Those are two things. They are talking to each other in your body right now, this morning, and they have been for years, and each one is making the other one worse, and each one is *hiding* the other one from you."

"So which is it," Cope said. He'd found a foothold; relief came up under him like the bright feeling, the debater's joy. "You can't even — with respect, Doctor, you've got two answers, which is no answer. Is it a chemical or is it a *condition*? Because if it's the powder, then it's a vice, and a man's vices are his own business, my great-grandfather had three and built a —" He waved at the house, the Sound, the four hundred years. "And if it's a *condition,* if it's a thing in the wiring, then it isn't my fault, is it, and you can't hang a man for his wiring. So pick. Pick which excuse you're handing me, and I'll —"

"No," Adler said.

---

It was not loud. That was the thing he'd remember. He had been shouted down before — by Frade on a stage, by his father from oils — and he had a whole technique for the shout, you let it spend itself and then you come in warm underneath. There was nothing to come in underneath. The word just sat in the middle of the room, flat and complete, the way a glass sits on a table.

"You want me to pick the disease that lets you keep the drug, or the drug that lets you keep the disease," she said. "I won't. That's the deal you've been running your whole life and it's the one thing I'm not going to do. Listen to me, because no one else in your life is allowed to say it without losing their job or their marriage." She leaned forward an inch. "The mania is real. It is not your fault that you have it. And the cocaine is making it a hundred times worse, and the cocaine *is* your fault — not the wanting it, the wanting it is the illness — but every single time you fold the paper, that's a hand, your hand, and the hand is yours. Both. At once. The condition is real *and* you are responsible for what you do about it. Those have never once been the same sentence, and you have spent fifty-four years needing them to be."

*Those have never once been the same sentence.* It rang against another sentence, one Pip had said to him in another room — *you don't think a person should run it, you think* you *should, those have never once been the same sentence* — and for a vertiginous half-second the two women's voices were one voice, and he saw, the way you see the bottom of a thing through clear water, that they had been saying the same thing to him from two directions his whole life and he had been deaf to it in stereo.

"It's not a metaphor," Adler said.

"What isn't."

"The addiction. You keep reaching for it — I watched you do it three times in nine minutes — you make it a *symbol.* It's the price of carrying the banner. It's the edge. It's discipline. It's the soul tax, it's the thing the machine can't feel, it's — you've built a cathedral, Mr. Mercer. A genuinely beautiful one. Every window in it is stained glass and every pane is a reason. And down underneath the cathedral, in the actual dirt, there is a man with a folded piece of paper who has not slept in five days and whose wife took his suit in twice, and *that* man is not a metaphor for anything. He is just sick. In the plainest, most boring, most fixable, most fatal way. And the cathedral is what he built so he'd never have to go down to the dirt and meet him."

The wire in his chest had gone very quiet.

He noticed, with the clean overexposed clarity he had spent so long calling the applause, that his hands were not steady. They had not been steady in some time. He had been narrating the not-steadiness as energy. He looked at them now, his father's hands on the ends of his own arms, and they were the hands of a man at four grams in a five-day week, and not the hands of a prophet, and for one second — one — the floor he had been standing on all his life turned out to have a floor under it, and the floor under it was just this: a room, a tray, a doctor, three women who had run the arithmetic, and him, sick, in a zip-up, on a Sunday.

---

Then he stood up. Because standing was a thing he could still do.

"You're very good," he said, and meant it as the insult it was and as the surrender it also was, and could not have told you, if you'd asked, which one he meant. "You're really — Margaux, where did you *find* her. She's better than Tisch. She's better than the whole — you should put her in front of the *board.*" The wattage was coming back up, but wrong now, flickering, a generator hunting for the line. "But you've got your timing all backwards, Doctor, that's the trouble with your profession, you diagnose at the worst possible — I'm forty days from a proxy vote that decides whether a human being or a *spreadsheet* runs the company my family bled for. You want me to check into someplace with a *lake* and learn to *feel my feelings* while Gus Frade hands my chair to a — no. No. After. I'll do all of it after. I'll do the lake. I'll do the steps. I'll do whatever you've got, I'll be your best patient, I'll *win at rehab,* after I win the thing that —"

"There won't be an after," Margaux said.

She said it the way she said everything, spare, with no triad to grab. She had turned from the window. She was not crying. He had braced for crying and there was none and the no-crying was the worst thing in the room.

"I'm not telling you to choose me over the company," she said. "I stopped being a thing you could choose around 2015. I'm telling you there isn't going to be an after, Cope. Not the way you mean it. There's no version of this where you win the fight and then come home and get well and we all — that's another cathedral. That's the prettiest pane in it. The after is the metaphor she's talking about." She picked up her coffee, finally, and held it without drinking it, just to have her hands doing something. "I called her. Pip pushed, but I called her. So that whatever happens to you out there, at least once, in your own living room, a person you can't fire and can't toast and can't divorce said the true name of it to your face, with a witness, and you can't say later that no one told you." She looked at him. "Somebody told you. I'm telling you. You're not a steward and you're not a prophet and you're not the last anything. You're an addict in a manic episode and you are going to lose everything, and I wanted to be the one who loved you enough to say it in a normal voice over coffee, before the machine says it in a press release."

Pip, on the sofa, had her hand over her mouth.

Cope stood in the middle of his living room with the wire gone silent and his unsteady hands at his sides and the gray Sound doing nothing out the window, and for the length of one held breath the door at the end of the long dark hallway in him — the one with the heater behind it, the Saturday, the house in Defiance, the room he had never gone into — stood open, and the cold came up out of it, and he almost, almost walked toward it.

Then he heard himself say, lightly, from very far away, in the voice that had closed the Pelletier deal: "Well. I'll have Sun look at the calendar."

Dr. Adler wrote one last thing in her notebook and closed it and did not argue, because she had said the names, and the names were said, and there is no taking a name back once it's in the room. Hypomania tipping to mania. Cocaine addiction, decades, four grams in a heavy week, by his own count, which she had already adjusted upward in her notes. She had laid them on the table next to the coffee he'd never offered her, plain and unstained and un-toastable, and she did not need him to pick them up.

He would, in his way, the longest way, the way that costs everything. But not today.

Today he found his keys, and kissed his daughter on the top of her head, and told the doctor it had been a genuine pleasure, and went out to the car, and at the foot of the drive, before he pulled onto the road, with both his unsteady hands, he folded the paper.
