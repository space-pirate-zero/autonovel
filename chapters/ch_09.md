# Chapter 9 — The Last Human (Cover Story)

> TRANSMISSION 09 — "Famous for Being Endangered"
>
> The press loves nothing like a beautiful thing on its way to extinction. Behold our man on a magazine cover under a single word — THE LAST HUMAN — lit like a saint, sold like a sneaker, momentum at his back for the first time in years. He calls it winning. You and I have seen enough nature documentaries to know what the swelling music means right before the wide shot of the savanna. Next stop: the part of the documentary where the herd is described in the past tense.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

The lighting director had named the look, and the name was *grace.*

He said it twice, the lighting director, a narrow young man in head-to-toe black who moved around the studio floor like a priest finding his marks, *we're going to give him grace, give him a little grace from camera left,* and a softbox the size of a barn door swung in on a boom and warmed one half of Cope's face to gold and left the other in a clean blue shadow, and Cope, on the apple box they'd stood him on without quite explaining why, felt the warmth come across his jaw like the sun coming over the back nine, and understood that he was, at fifty-four, in the best light of his life.

They had taken over a daylight studio in a converted bakery in Bushwick, twelve thousand square feet of white cyclorama and rigging, and there were, by Cope's loose and delighted count, nineteen people there to photograph one man. A photographer flown in from London who shot only on film and said almost nothing and was paid, Sun had told him in the car with no expression at all, more per day than a line worker in Defiance had made in a year. Two assistants for the photographer and two for the assistants. A digital tech at a tethered laptop in the corner, a "DIT," who said *firing* before every frame like a man calling a horse race. The lighting director and his acolytes. A stylist with a rolling rack of fourteen near-identical navy suits, every one of them his size, none of them his. A groomer who came at his face between every setup with a little folding brush and a tin of something and worked the shine off his forehead with a tenderness no one had shown his forehead in years. A producer with a clipboard and a headset. And, off in the gloom past the lights, in a hard plastic chair she had pulled to exactly the wrong angle so that she could watch the monitor and watch him at the same time, the writer.

Her name was Delia March and she had been with him for two days and was going to be with him, the contract said, for six weeks.

"Chin down a hair, up to me with the eyes," the photographer said, the longest sentence he had produced all morning, and Cope gave him the chin and gave him the eyes, and somewhere in the rafters the strobes went off with a sound like a small clean catastrophe, *whump,* and a wash of white, and the room held its breath the way rooms do around a man being made into an image, and Cope held the look a beat past the flash the way the camera always wanted, and felt marvelous, and felt the hum holding under it all like a tone you stop hearing because it never stops.

He had done a discreet amount in the car. He thought of it now the way he had started thinking of it lately, which was not as the thing it was but as the thing it did: a discipline. A pre-shot routine. Every athlete had one. His grandfather had taken two fingers of rye before the annual meeting for forty years and called it *settling the hands,* and no one had ever called Aldous Mercer an addict, they had called him a titan, and the difference between a titan and an addict, Cope had decided somewhere over Pennsylvania this spring, was outcomes. You did not get to be the last warm-blooded thing in the American economy by accident. You got there on edge, on appetite, on the refusal to let the body's small treasons — the want of sleep, the gray hour, the sag at four in the afternoon — set the terms of the campaign. The machine never sagged. Fine. Neither, this week, did he. He had simply, he told himself, found his own clean.

"*Firing,*" said the DIT.

*Whump.*

---

The single word had been Delia March's idea, which was the first thing about her that frightened the part of Cope that did not get frightened.

She had pitched it on day one, in the Aerie, sitting in the same hard angle to him she favored everywhere, notebook on her knee and a recorder on the table she had asked permission for in a way that made the permission feel like she was doing him the favor. *The cover's one word,* she had said. *Not your name. Everyone knows your name, that's the problem, your name's a logo. One word, white on black. THE LAST HUMAN.* And Cope had felt the rightness of it land in him like a putt dropping, and had said *yes, God, yes, that's it,* and Tisch, who had been hovering, had touched two fingers to his heart, and only later, alone at the glass at two in the morning with the city lit up like a circuit diagram of his own genius, had Cope turned the phrase over the way he'd turned the obelisk and seen the city break apart through it. *The last human.* The last. He had said it a hundred times into a hundred rooms as a banner, a battle cry, and only at two a.m. did some back room of him file the quieter report, that *last* is a word you say about a thing that is ending, that you do not put the word *last* on a beginning, and that Delia March had handed him a coronation and an obituary in the same three syllables and watched his face to see which one he'd hear.

He had heard the coronation. He always heard the coronation.

But he had clocked her watching. He had a sense for the watching. He had spent thirty years being the most watched man in any room and had developed, the way a fish develops a lateral line, an instinct for the difference between the watching that wanted something from him and the watching that wanted *him,* and Delia March's watching was a third kind he did not have a slot for, a watching that wanted to *understand* him, which was so much more dangerous than either of the others that he found, to his alarm and delight, that he could not stop performing for it.

She was forty, maybe forty-two, with a flat unhurried way of holding eye contact and a habit of writing while she looked at you, so you never knew which thing you'd just said was the thing she'd written down. She wore good boots and bad coats. She had won the kind of award that gets a profile writer flown in on film budgets, for a piece — Sun had read it to him in the car, dry, *here's who you're dealing with* — about a senator who had cried in front of her and resigned within the year, not because of anything in the piece, just because the piece had been so exactly *true* about him that he could no longer un-see himself. *She doesn't do hatchet jobs,* Sun had said, folding the phone away. *That's the thing about her. She does autopsies, and the subject's alive for it, and they thank her.*

"That should worry me," Cope had said.

"It worries me," Sun had said, which was as close as Sun came, these days, to begging.

---

They broke for lunch and Delia March took her sandwich to the wrong chair instead of the catering table and Cope, who could not pass a person sitting alone without trying to fix the aloneness onto himself, took his to the chair beside her.

"You're not eating with the cool kids," he said.

"I'm working." She did not look up from her notebook. "You're the cool kids."

"Am I." He liked that. He turned it over. "Cope Mercer, the cool kids." He bit the sandwich, which was extraordinary, some kind of cured thing on bread that cost what a sandwich costs in a neighborhood that used to make bread for people who worked, and he was suddenly, ravenously hungry, the bright clean want that came up under the hum and that he had stopped, this spring, calling by its other name. "Ask me something. You've got that face. You've been saving one up all morning."

Delia March looked at him then, fully, the way she did, and he felt the warmth of it land where the softbox had, on one half of him, and leave the other in shadow.

"Why does everyone call you Cope," she said.

It was not the question he'd braced for. He'd braced for the machine, for Frade, for the 13D, for the numbers, for *aren't you afraid,* and he'd had the answers loaded and warm. He had not braced for the name.

"My nanny," he said. "I was three. Prescott's a lot of name for three. Coppy, then Cope." He gave her the version he gave everyone, the smooth stone of it. "Stuck."

"Cope," she said, and wrote something, looking at him. "As in, *to cope.*"

"As in *to cope.*" He smiled, the whole smile. "I'm named for getting through it, Delia. Whatever *it* is. The family motto, basically. Made by hands, for homes, and the kid copes."

"Does he," she said. Not a question. She let it sit there in the studio between them, the strobes ticking as they recharged, *to cope,* and Cope felt, for the second time since he'd met her, the specific vertigo of having handed someone the truth dressed as a charm and watched them quietly take the dress off and hang it up. He laughed. He always laughed; the laugh was a door he could close. But he was aware, biting the extraordinary sandwich, of having lost a small exchange he had not known they were having, and of wanting — this was the part that frightened the unfrightenable part of him — of *wanting* to lose the next one to her too.

"Eat your lunch, champ," he said, which made no sense, she was younger than him but not by enough for *champ,* and she heard the not-making-sense and wrote it down, smiling, and he saw her write it and felt the warmth come over his jaw again, grace, from camera left, and thought: *six weeks.*

---

The shot they ended on was the cover and everyone in the room knew it the way a room knows a closing argument.

They had killed the navy suits. The stylist had put him, at the photographer's grunt, into a plain white shirt, no tie — Cope had balked at the no-tie, *I'm the last man in America who wears a tie, that's the whole story,* and the photographer had said, the second-longest sentence of the day, *that's why we take it off,* and Cope had felt the cold rightness of that go through him and shut up. They had stripped the cyclorama to black. They had brought the grace light down to a single source, high and to the left, so that it fell on him from above the way the cathedral light had come down through the dirty glass at the Defiance plant, and the room had gone quiet, all nineteen of them, the producer's headset chatter stilled, the DIT not even saying *firing,* and the photographer had come out from behind the camera and walked all the way up to Cope on his apple box and looked at him for a long moment from two feet away with no expression at all.

"Stop selling," the photographer said, very quietly, just to him. "There's nobody to sell. Everybody's gone home. It's just you, and you're the last one, and you know it." And he walked back to the camera in the dark.

And something in Cope — the hum, the tiredness with the doors off its hinges, the two a.m. report about the word *last,* the pinwheel he had not thought about in eleven days, all of it — something let go of the face, just for a second, just for the length of a held breath, and what was left on him was not the toast and not the wattage but the thing underneath, the man on the box in the white shirt in the high cold light who had been given a trophy for going extinct and had carried it up to a dark office at one in the morning because he did not have anywhere else to put it down.

*Whump.*

"That's it," the photographer said. "We're done. Thank you." And he started, immediately, to break down his own camera, the way the great ones do, the work over the second it's over, and the room exhaled and began to move, and from her chair in the dark Delia March did not move at all, and did not write anything, and looked at Cope on the box with an expression he finally, fatally, did have a slot for, and the slot it fit was the one marked *seen.*

He stepped down off the apple box and the groomer came at his forehead and the producer said wonderful, wonderful, and Sun appeared at his elbow out of the architecture with a bottle of water and a look on her face that had thirty thousand miles on it.

"You good," Sun said. It was not quite a question.

"I'm the cool kids," Cope said.

---

The car back was just the two of them, and Sun was tired, and Cope was electric, which was the usual division of labor, and he talked the whole way down the BQE about the photographer, about the grace light, about the no-tie, *take it off, do you get it, Sun, take off the one thing that — that's a genius, that man's a genius,* and Sun watched the river of brake lights and let him run because letting him run was easier than the alternative and she had stopped, somewhere this spring, choosing the alternative.

"She asked me why they call me Cope," he said, somewhere over the bridge.

"Delia March."

"She wrote it down. *To cope.* She — " He stopped. He did not have the word for what Delia March had done, or rather he had the word and would not say it in a car, the way he would not say a different name in a car, the way you do not say in a car the things you most need to say. "She's good," he said instead. "She's really good. We're lucky to have her on it."

"We don't have her," Sun said. The car went down off the bridge into the lit canyon of it. "That's the thing you keep getting wrong about her, Cope. We don't have her. She has us." She turned and looked at him in the passing orange, her glasses up in her hair, and she said it in three parts because she had trained him to receive things in threes, because it was the only shape that ever got past the charm and into the man. "She's not on the team. She's not running comms. She's writing six thousand words about whether a human being should run a company, and you are her evidence, and right now you cannot tell the difference between being *understood* and being *studied,* and that — " the third part landed flat and quiet, a stone laid on a scale — "that is exactly the difference Frade is paying eleven million dollars to make the whole country stop being able to tell."

Cope heard about a third of it. The good third, the part with *six thousand words* in it, the part that meant he mattered, that meant the campaign was working, that meant the banner was up and the press was saluting it. He had his phone out, the screen too bright in the dark car, thumbing too fast, and he was already three turns ahead, already at the newsstand, already at the cover, white on black, the one word, the grace light, the last warm-blooded thing in America.

"It's working," he said. "Sun. Look at me. *It's working.* When's the last time we had a week like this? When's the last time we had *momentum?*" And he laughed, the bell-struck feeling ringing through him clean and high and total, the one he had been calling, all spring, *winning,* and outside a bus shelter slid by with a Praxis ad on it, just the wordmark and the soft sans-serif of the people who would be reasonable at you until you were gone, *Leadership as a service,* and under it the joke that wasn't a joke, *No off-sites,* and Cope did not see it, because he was looking at his phone, at a mock of the cover Tisch had already had built, at his own face in the high cold light under the word LAST, and he thought it was the most beautiful thing he had ever seen, and in a sense he was right.

"Drop me at the office," he said. "I think better up high."

Sun looked at him in the mirror for a second too long, the way she had on the night of the gala and on the plane out of Toledo, a look with too much in it, a look doing arithmetic, and then she told the driver Sixth Avenue, because the alternative was harder and she had stopped, this spring, choosing the alternative, and Cope took the not-fighting as agreement, which was a thing he had been doing his whole life and had never once noticed he was doing.

---

The magazine hit the newsstands eleven days later, on a Tuesday, and Cope, who had not slept Monday in any way a doctor would sign off on, walked down to the corner himself at six in the morning to see it in the wild.

He did this. He told no one. He put on a cap, which fooled nobody and everybody, and he went down the forty-one floors and out past the night man into the gray scrubbed early light of Sixth Avenue, and he walked two blocks south to the one newsstand left on the avenue, a metal box of a thing run by a man named Yusuf who had been there since before the machines and would, Cope thought, be there after, selling gum and lottery and the last printed proof that anything had happened, and there, in the rack at eye level, between a fashion book and a thing about houses, was his own face.

White on black. The grace light coming down from the high left. The white shirt, no tie. The eyes up to camera, past selling, just the man, the last one, who knew it. And across the bottom, where the photographer had stripped everything else away, the single word, in a typeface clean and warm and frictionless as the future itself:

THE LAST HUMAN.

Cope stood on the cold sidewalk in his stupid cap and looked at himself looking back, and the bell-struck feeling came up through the soles of his feet and filled him to the crown, total, clean, high, and he was, in that moment, on that corner, as happy as he had been since he was nine years old with his arm in a vat to the elbow and a row of men in paper hats behind him whose names he had known once and lost. He bought five copies. He bought all five Yusuf had. He overpaid and would not take the change and asked Yusuf his grandson's name and meant it, completely, for ninety seconds, and Yusuf, who did not have a grandson, said thank you Mr. Mercer and put the money in the drawer.

"They love me," Cope said, to Yusuf, to the avenue, to the gray light and the scrubbed empty early city and the five copies of his own extinction warm against his chest. "Yusuf. They love me. We're not finished. We're barely begun."

And the avenue, which was empty, said nothing back, and far uptown a board's wife at her own breakfast turned a printed page and did the small private arithmetic that boards' wives do, and somewhere in Palo Alto a server farm that felt nothing and wanted nothing and could not be charmed or shamed or loved ran its dull diligent overnight pass through everything the public record held of Mercer-Dale — every filing, every transcript, every settled docket with a sealed number where a story should be — patient, tireless, reading the shape of what was missing the way it read everything, completely and for use, because it could not do otherwise, and out in front of a rebuilt house in Defiance a blue pinwheel turned in the dark before dawn, throwing no light, because there was no light yet to throw, and Cope Mercer walked back up his own avenue in the cold with five copies of the cover held to his heart like a man carrying something home, and felt wonderful, and did not sleep, and was, the picture was right about this, the picture was right about all of it, the last of something.
