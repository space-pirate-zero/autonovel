# Chapter 10 — The Listening Tour

> TRANSMISSION 10 — "Discipline, He Calls It"
>
> The banner needs feeding and so does he. Watch him run an unscripted tour of his own goodness with a journalist embedded close enough to become the next entry in a pattern he experiences as generosity. The powder is discipline now. The affair is *really seeing people.* Every appetite the machine doesn't have, he is indulging in the exact week he campaigns on having a soul the machine lacks. Next stop: the place where the cure and the disease share a hotel key.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

The tour was unscripted, which meant it had taken nine days to script.

It was Tisch's idea and then, within an hour of being Tisch's idea, it was Cope's idea, the way good ideas migrated in the Aerie, crossing the rug from the man in the henley to the man at the desk and arriving sourceless and warm, like weather. *The Last Human Listens.* That was the working title, until legal flagged that you could not trademark *listens,* and then it was just the Listening Tour, six cities in eight days, no podium, no prepared remarks, the Chairman of Mercer-Dale simply *out among the people,* hearing them, holding their hands, being — Tisch's word, said with two fingers on his own sternum — *available.*

The people were pre-vetted. The hands had signed releases.

"Spontaneity," Tisch had said on the first plane, "is a *production value.* The most expensive thing in the world to make look free." And so there was a manifest, and the manifest had columns, and one of the columns was named, in the comms team's product-launch software, *Unscripted Beat,* and under it, for Tuesday, in nine-point type: *Cope helps a stranger. Find stranger Mon.*

Cope did not read the manifest. He had people for the manifest. He read the room, which was the one thing, he was certain, no manifest could do for you.

The room, that first morning, was a union hall in Pittsburgh that had been a union hall when there were unions, and Cope walked into it on no sleep and a clean bright hum and felt the whole place tilt toward him the way iron filings tilt. He was good at this. He had always been good at this. He moved through the folding chairs touching shoulders, getting the names — Sun had stopped feeding him the cards somewhere around Camden, he didn't need the cards anymore, the names came up out of him like the names of his own children — and he crouched, actually crouched, knees popping, in front of a woman named Deb who had run a Mercer's mayonnaise line in a plant they'd closed in '09, and he held both her hands in both of his and looked at her with the full wattage, and he meant it, he meant every second of it, and the boy with the gimbal walked a slow arc catching the meaning of it, and Deb cried, and Cope's eyes shone but did not spill, because he had learned in a room off the Aerie that the withheld tear out-performed the tear by twelve points, and the withholding had become, somewhere in the last three weeks, indistinguishable to him from restraint, and the restraint indistinguishable from depth, and the depth from love.

He stood up feeling enormous. He had given Deb something. He was sure of it. He could not have told you what.

---

Delia March was on the second plane, and the third, and by the fourth she had the seat across the little table from him without anyone deciding she should, which was how Cope's tables had always filled.

She was forty-one and wrote long pieces for the kind of magazine that still ran them, eight thousand words with a photographer attached, and she had been embedded since the cover — since *THE LAST HUMAN,* since the spread, the deal Sun had argued against in a folder he hadn't opened. Three weeks of access. *Total access,* the editor had said, and Tisch had laughed and said total access was the most controlled access there was, you just had to give them so much they drowned in it and surfaced grateful. Delia had not drowned. She took her notes on paper, in a small hand, and she did not flatter him, which Cope experienced, after three weeks of being flattered professionally by people he was paying, as a kind of cold water he kept walking back into.

"You crouched a long time with Deb," she said somewhere over Ohio, not looking up from the paper. "Your knees."

"My knees are a casualty of caring," Cope said. "Put that in. My great-grandfather Aldous used to say a man who won't get down on the floor with people has no business standing up over them."

"Did he say that."

"He says a lot of things." Cope grinned. "He's been dead ninety years. It's a very productive relationship."

She wrote something. He watched her write it and felt the specific pull he had spent thirty years not naming, the pull toward a person who was not yet his and could be — not a sexual thing, he would have said, if anyone had been crude enough to ask, though it became that, it nearly always became that; it was a *seeing* thing, a hunger to be looked at by someone whose looking hadn't already been bought. Delia looked at him like a piece of work she hadn't decided about. He found he could not stand to leave the question open. He had never in his life been able to leave that particular question open.

"You don't like me," he said, delighted.

"I don't know you," Delia said. "Those aren't the same. Your daughter said something like that, didn't she. In the piece you wouldn't let me read."

It landed somewhere it shouldn't have. He covered it the way he covered everything, with more of himself. "Pip's smarter than all of us. Builds the things that are going to bury her old man and calls me to ask if I'm eating." He leaned in over the little table, the lean that had closed the Pelletier deal. "Ask me anything. That's the deal, isn't it? Total access. Ask me the thing you've been saving."

Delia looked at him for a moment with her pen still.

"What are you so afraid of staying still for," she said.

The hum went very clean and high. Outside the window Ohio went by underneath, the founding state, somewhere down there a town he did not think about, and Cope laughed the warm laugh and said, "Lunch. I'm afraid of lunch. Let's get this woman a sandwich," and stood up too fast, and went to the back of the plane where the lavatory was, and did the thing he had started calling, in the privacy where he named things, *the discipline.*

---

The powder was discipline now. He could have walked you through the logic and it would have sounded, the whole way, like sense.

A man carrying the human banner for eight million people did not have the luxury of being tired. The machine did not get tired; that was the whole game; you could not go up against a thing that ran on rails and warm sans-serif and never once needed to be loved at two in the morning, you could not meet that on the field of *endurance* and bring a body that demanded sleep and food and forgiveness. So you disciplined the body. You took the edge off the edge. You did the small neat competent thing in the lavatory and came back up to pitch like an orchestra finding the note, and you were *present,* you were *available,* you could crouch with Deb and feel it, all of it, on command, which was the job, which was the product, which was the soul on rails run on time. His guy in the '90s had folded the paper a particular way so a gentleman didn't spill. Cope had refined the philosophy. A gentleman didn't spill, and a leader didn't flag, and the line between maintenance and need was a line he had never once had to look at because the machine he was maintaining had never failed to start.

Dr. Adler — Margaux's idea, then Pip's, a name on a card he had not called — would have had words for it. Adler had plain words for everything, Cope suspected, which was exactly why he hadn't called. The plain word for the lavatory and the plain word for the not-sleeping and the plain word for what was building in his chest like good weather, like a charge, like a sermon. Cope did not want the plain words. He had built a cathedral. You do not bring a man with a clipboard into a cathedral.

He came back up the aisle clean and high and total, and Delia was watching him come, and he gave her the whole smile, and she did not write anything down, which he took, the way he took everything, as the beginning of something.

---

Cleveland was the hospital, which tested off the charts. Then Detroit, the auto-parts retiree, the firm handshake, the cap. Then the manifest said *Unscripted Beat — Cope helps a stranger,* and the stranger turned out to be a kid named Marcus.

"No," Cope said, on the curb, when the comms lead told him. "No. Find me a different stranger."

"He's nineteen, he changes the tires at —"

"His *name,*" Cope said, and for a second the hum dropped out and something underneath it showed, "his name is the thing they're putting in my chair." He looked at the comms lead, twenty-six, sleepless, terrified, holding a clipboard with a stranger's name on it, and Cope's face did a thing it almost never did, which was nothing, went blank and bright and far away, and then it came back, it always came back, his face had never once let him down. "It's fine. Different Marcus. The world's full of them now. Find me a Steve."

They found him a Steve. Cope changed Steve's tire in the heat with his jacket off and his sleeves rolled, a fifty-four-year-old Fortune 100 chief executive on one knee in a gas station while a boy walked a slow arc with a gimbal, and it played, later, beautifully, eleven million views, *this is what they want to replace* over a swelling string cue, and what no one watching could see was that Cope's hands were shaking very slightly on the lug wrench, fine and bright, and that he had needed, halfway through, to go into the station men's room and take the edge off the edge, and that he had come out and finished the tire feeling generous and enormous and entirely sure he was the most alive man in Ohio.

Delia saw the shaking. She was forty-one and had embedded with three campaigns and a war. She wrote nothing down.

---

The hotel in Columbus was a good one, old, the kind the family used to stay in, brass and dim and a bar that closed when the last person left it.

They were the last people in it. This, too, was a groove the record had been cut in: the day's work done, the crew sent up, the one person left who hadn't been bought yet, and Cope at his most enormous, at the top of the hum, leaning in across a small table with a drink he wasn't drinking, *really seeing* her. He told Delia about his father. He told her the second sentence — *try to be good anyway* — which he had never told a journalist and rarely told himself, and he told it well, the catch in the throat real this time, no beats, no Tisch counting, and it was real, that was the thing he would have wanted understood, the feeling was always real, it was only the use of it that was the product. Delia put the pen down. That was how he knew. The pen went down on the brass and stayed down, and the looking changed, and Cope felt the whole evening tilt toward him the way the union hall had, the way every room had, iron to a magnet.

He did not experience it as a decision. It was never a decision. It was warmth. It was the groove. It was *really seeing people,* which was the one thing the machine could not do, and so doing it — here, now, with the elevator's brass doors and the room key already warm in his pocket — felt less like a betrayal of the banner than a defense of it. He was being human. Floridly, generously, irreplaceably human. He was proving the thesis with his body.

The cure and the disease shared a hotel key. He carried both up in the same pocket and did not feel the weight of either.

In the room, after, he was talkative and bright and could not stay still, and he keyed himself one more neat small competent line off the marble of the bathroom counter while Delia watched from the bed without expression, a woman doing arithmetic she would not show her work on, and Cope, catching her watching in the mirror, smiled the whole smile and said, "Discipline," and held the rolled thing up like a toast, "the human banner is *heavy,* my friend," and believed it, and felt that he had let her see him, the real him, the soul in the chair, and that she understood.

She understood completely. That was the thing he had wrong. She understood completely, and she still had eight thousand words to write.

---

His phone had been ringing on the nightstand. He saw it after, the missed call, and called back without thinking, still bright, still up at pitch, generous toward the whole world including the wife he had not been home to in eleven days.

Margaux answered on the second ring, which meant she had been holding the phone.

"You called," he said. "I was — it's a tour, it's nonstop, six cities, you should see it, Margaux, the *people,* I wish you could —"

"Where are you."

"Columbus. The old Hartwell, they still have the —"

"I know the Hartwell." A pause, and in the pause he could hear Greenwich, the house, the particular quiet of a house she had not left all evening. "I called to tell you Pip got the Hauser fellowship. She wanted you to know. She didn't want to call you herself."

"That's *wonderful.* That's — God, that's wonderful, I'll call her, I'll —"

"Don't call her tonight."

He stopped. The hum kept going but something under it slowed. "Why not tonight."

There was a long silence on the line, and it was not an empty silence, it was a silence with twenty-two years in it, a silence that had counted things and flushed things and refilled its own prescriptions and called it a marriage, and into that silence Cope said, too brightly, "Margaux? You there?" and across two hundred miles of fiber his wife listened to the specific pitch of her husband's voice at midnight in a hotel he was not staying in alone, the pitch she could read like a sculptor reads a flaw in the stone, the over-bright frictionless ring of a man two days past sleep and one line into the small hours, and she did not ask. She had stopped asking somewhere around 2015. She knew everything the silence needed to know.

"Goodnight, Prescott," she said.

She only called him Prescott when she was very far away.

The line went dead. Cope held the phone for a second, frowning at it, and then the frown passed, because the frowns always passed now, the tiredness took the doors off their hinges and the hard things slid through and out the other side before they could be felt, and what came back up to fill the space was the warm enormous certainty, the charge, the sermon assembling itself in his chest. Pip got the fellowship. His daughter, brilliant, building the future, and she'd thought of him, she'd wanted him to know. They all wanted him to know things. He was the kind of man people wanted to tell things to. He stood at the dark window of the Hartwell with the city of Columbus laid out below him like nodes, like a circuit diagram of his own goodness, and behind him on the bed Delia March lay with her eyes open in the dark composing sentences he would not read for nineteen days, and Cope felt the bell-struck feeling ring through him clean and high and total, and thought: *they get it. They feel it too.*

He thought: *I have never been more myself.*

He thought, looking out at the lit and sleeping town and the dark founding country beyond it where a house had once burned with two people inside it on a quarter he was protecting: *Margaux should see me now.*

He did not call his daughter. He had a thing in the morning. He went back to the bed feeling generous and enormous and entirely unaware, a man at the very top of the only mountain he could see, with no idea at all that it was the lip of the volcano, and somewhere far below him a thing with no appetites and no need to be loved read every memo ever filed, because it could not do otherwise, and did not yet know his name well enough to say it back.
