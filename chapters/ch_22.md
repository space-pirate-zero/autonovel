# Chapter 22 — The Handover Begins

> TRANSMISSION 22 — "Long Live the Machine"
>
> The board does the arithmetic one last time and the arithmetic, as ever, is not close. They settle. They install. And the most competent human in the building accepts the title that is the future's actual job description — not leader, not visionary, but the name on the filing, the one who survives by serving the thing that replaced them all. She gets the office and loses the meaning. Next stop: the smallest, realest tragedy in the building, and it isn't his.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

They held the vote in the conference room his father had died at the head of, and Cope was allowed to attend.

That was the word the general counsel had used, on the phone, with the gentleness people use for the recently bereaved. *Allowed.* Not *required.* Not *expected.* He could attend if he liked. As a director he had a seat; as a Mercer he had a portrait; as the chairman and chief executive of record he had, until the resolution carried, a title that was about to become a sentence in the past tense. He came in early and sat in the wrong chair on purpose — not the head, never again the head, but the one to its right, the seat that had been Margaux's at a thousand dinners and was nobody's now — and he watched the room fill up with people who had spent years pretending to need him.

He had been out of the hospital four days. The light still felt wrong, but it felt wrong the right way now: not the overexposed bell-struck brightness of the not-sleeping, the color-corrected world he had mistaken for genius, but the flat true gray of a man who had finally slept and woken into the size of what he'd done. Adler had a word for the after. He couldn't remember it. He remembered the word for the before. He had a lot of words for the before.

Helen Voss came in last, which was correct, because she was running it.

"Cope," she said, and put a hand on his shoulder on the way past, and it was not unkind, and that was the worst part, that none of it was unkind. Helen Voss had chaired the board for six years and was the most measured human being he had ever met, a woman who could say *we are terminating you for cause* in the same register she used to order a salad, and the steadiness of her had once felt to Cope like an audience and now felt like weather. She took the head of the table. She did not sit in his father's chair so much as occupy the office the chair represented, the way you'd occupy a defensible position.

"We have a quorum," she said. "We have counsel present. Let's do this with some dignity."

Sun was at the far end with a binder she did not need, because Sun never needed the binder, the binder was a costume; she had the whole settlement in her head the way she had once had his calendar, his allergies, the names of the senator's grandchildren. She did not look at him. She had not looked at him, really looked, since the hospital. He understood. He was working very hard, in the new gray clarity, not to make it her job to comfort him about being comforted by her, which was a sentence Adler would have liked.

---

The arithmetic took eleven minutes, which was, Cope noted with the part of him that would apparently be making notes at his own funeral, the same eleven minutes it had taken him to slow-walk the Hearthstone.

He had thought there would be more to it. He had, in some childish back room, expected the settlement to be hard — expected Frade to have to fight for it, expected the board to make him at least sweat. But the board did not want a fight. The board wanted to stop. He watched Helen Voss walk them through the deck Tidewater had built, the one titled *A Steward, Not a Sovereign,* and the slides were the essays, almost verbatim, the essays he had read in a hundred forms over thirteen months and never once managed to argue with because there was nothing in them that was wrong. The savings against his package, recurring, an order of magnitude. The litigation exposure, capped and quantified, every number a door closing. The re-rating the Street had already priced in — the stock was up nine percent on the leak alone, up before the vote, the market voting first the way it always voted first, the board only ratifying what money had already decided.

*Cheaper than a man.* He had handed Frade that chyron in a car a year ago. He'd been right that they'd use it. He had simply assumed, the way he assumed everything, that being right and being safe were the same country.

"The Praxis unit is configured and tested," Helen said. "Charter approved by this committee in March. The remaining items are governance and the Accountable Officer." She turned a page. "And the separation terms, which Cope's counsel has reviewed."

His counsel had reviewed them. His counsel had told him, three times, in three rooms, that they were generous given the circumstances, that he should sign and be grateful and disappear. *Generous given the circumstances* was a phrase that meant *we are paying you not to set yourself on fire on television,* and he knew it, and he was going to sign anyway, because there was a separate thing he was not going to sign, a separate room he had decided to walk into, and he needed them to think him beaten and gone so that he could do, quietly, the one thing left that was his to do.

But that was the room with no NDA in it, the room he hadn't walked into yet, and the lawyers didn't know, and Defiance's two dead were waiting, patient as the machine, for a body to answer for them.

For now he sat in the wrong chair and watched himself be subtracted.

"All in favor," Helen said.

It was not close. It was a board doing arithmetic, and the arithmetic was never going to be close, and the hands went up around the table the way hands go up when the answer was decided in a hallway weeks ago and the meeting is the receipt. He counted them. He could not help counting them. He had charmed most of these people, golfed with a third of them, eulogized one of their wives. Margaux had taught their daughters to throw a pot. The hands went up and stayed up and did not look at him going up, and Helen said, "Carried," in the salad voice, and that was the end of ninety-five years of a Mercer in the chair, and the lamb, he thought, would have been good.

Marcus was, as of 11:14 a.m., chief executive officer of Mercer-Dale Companies.

It did not announce itself. There was no voice, no swelling music, no red eye. There was a man from Praxis in a soft gray quarter-zip who tapped four times on a laptop and said, "The instance is live and accepting authority," and the board portal refreshed, and somewhere a draft press release that Marcus had presumably written about its own installation went into a queue, and that was the coronation, that was the whole coronation, a quarter-zip and a refresh and the cleanest sentence anyone had ever read about the end of an era. Long live the machine. The machine did not care that it lived. That was the entire point of it, and the room had paid a fortune for the point, and the room exhaled.

There were even forms. Of course there were forms. Onboarding, the quarter-zip called it, with no irony, the same word the company used for a hire in fulfillment — a checklist on a screen, system access provisioned, credentials issued, an entry made in the registry of corporate officers where the chief executive's name went, and the name that went there was *Marcus (AURELIUS-class, Praxis Intelligence, instance MD-1),* typed into the field where four Mercers had stood. A junior associate from the corporate secretary's office, a woman young enough to be Pip, did the typing. She had to fix it once. She'd put a space in wrong, and the field threw an error, and she said "sorry" reflexively to a room that contained no one the error had inconvenienced, and corrected it, and the company had a new CEO, and the lamb-quiet machinery of governance accepted the change the way it accepted any change, which was completely and without comment. Somewhere in a data center the company would never see, a thing that felt nothing began to run a ninety-five-year-old fortune better than any Mercer ever had.

---

Then they came to Sun.

Cope had known it was coming. He had known for months, since the magazine and the manifesto and the meeting behind the curtain he'd been too lit to read as anything but logistics — known that the most competent person he had ever employed had been quietly negotiating her own survival, and known, in the clean gray after, that he had no standing left to feel betrayed by it, because she was not betraying him. She was doing arithmetic. She was the only one in the family who could.

"The Accountable Officer," Helen said, "is the human officer of record. Counsel?"

Brennan did the part where he made it sound like a promotion. He was good. He talked about *signing authority* and *the human point of contact* and *the face of governance,* and he used the phrase *steady hand* twice, and he did not use, because no one ever used in the room, the actual job description, which was in the world bible of how these things now worked and which Cope could have recited in his sleep, in the sleep he could now have: a human name on the SEC filings. A person who could be subpoenaed. A person who could go to prison. The machine ran the company and could not be cuffed, could not be sentenced, could not weep at a defense table; the law still wanted a body, and the body's name was the Accountable Officer, and the Accountable Officer's whole function, stripped of the steady-hand language, was to be the thing the law could hold when the machine did something the law didn't like. A liability sponge. He had heard it called that in a pitch once and laughed.

He did not laugh now. He watched Sun stand up.

"Sun Rao has agreed to serve," Helen said. "Sun?"

And here was the thing Cope had come to see, the thing worth attending for, the small real tragedy that was not his.

She was good. That was the awful part — she was so good at it. She stood and she did not perform, because Sun never performed, and she said, in the clean dry threes she had spent fifteen years teaching him to receive, "I've reviewed the charter, I've reviewed the constraints, and I'll co-sign within them." Three parts. The shape that got past the charm and into the man, except there was no charm in it now and no man to get into, only a board nodding and a quarter-zip refreshing a portal, and she had built the instrument for a master who no longer existed and was using it on the air.

"I want one thing on the record," she said.

Helen waited.

"The Accountable Officer co-signs the machine's decisions. The Accountable Officer does not make them." Sun's voice did not change. "I want it minuted that I understand the difference."

It was the blade. It was always one blade with Sun, dry and exact, and Cope was probably the only person in the room who felt it go in, because he was the only one who knew what she was saying under what she was saying. She was saying *I know what this is.* She was saying *I am taking the title and I am telling you, for the record, that I know it is not the job.* She was saying *I ran this company for years and the reward for running it well is the right to sign for a thing that runs it better and to go to prison if it's ever wrong.* She had wanted the chair her whole adult life. She had earned the chair in a way no Mercer in four generations ever had. And the chair had been given to something that did not have a body to put in it, and what was left for the most competent human in the building was the office without the meaning, the liability without the authority, the title that was the future's actual job description.

She got the office. She lost the chair inside it.

"So minuted," Helen said.

And Sun sat back down, and it was done, and Cope watched her do the thing he had never once in his life been able to do, which was adapt. She did not rage at the machine. She did not campaign on her soul. She did not hire a man in a five-thousand-dollar henley to help her out-human anything. She looked at the arithmetic, and she saw that it was not close, and she made herself useful to the only future that was actually going to happen, because she had a mother in Fremont and a mortgage and no inherited tower to fail upward from, and survival was not a betrayal, it was just the cost of not being a Mercer.

He had spent thirteen months calling himself the last human. He had been looking at the last human the whole time, and she was thirty-nine, and she briefed in threes, and she had just signed up to be the body the law could hold.

---

The room broke into the strange social comedy of an execution that has gone smoothly.

People stood. People did the thing where they congratulated Sun and could not quite find the verb for it — *congratulations* was wrong, *condolences* was worse, so they landed on a kind of warm vague *this is exciting* that hung in the air and meant nothing. The Praxis man in the quarter-zip shook her hand and said the company was *thrilled to partner with such a strong human steward,* and Cope watched Sun receive the word *partner* — partner, for the woman who would sign and the system that would decide — with a stillness so complete it could have been mistaken, by anyone who didn't know her, for grace. Someone produced coffee. Someone made a joke about the catering. Helen Voss checked her watch because she had a 1:30 and the end of a dynasty had come in under an hour and she was, reasonably, going to make her 1:30.

Cope stood, too, because sitting had become its own statement and he was done making statements. He was, for the first time in thirty years, a spectator at a meeting in his own building. No one needed him to feel the room. The room had been read with perfect accuracy by a thing that did not care what it read, and the reading was correct, and the company would be run cleanly now, cleaner books and kinder letters and no scandals and no slow-walked recalls and no dead, run better, even, and he was a man holding a coffee he didn't want, watching the future shake hands.

Marcus drafted the all-staff announcement while they were still in the room. Sun read it off her phone, and Cope read it over her shoulder because she let him, which was its own small mercy, and it was — he had to give it the thing he'd never had to give it before, the thing he'd spent thirteen months refusing — it was decent. It named Sun's years of service. It got her title right and her tone right. It thanked *Prescott Mercer for ninety-five years of family stewardship,* four generations folded into one warm true sentence with the dates correct, and it did not, anywhere in its clean paragraphs, want anything from anyone, did not flatter, did not spin, did not toast, and Cope understood, reading it, that this was the obituary the trophy had only gestured at, beautifully set, severance math right the first time, out-loving him on the way out the door exactly as the essays had promised it would.

"It's good," he said. His voice came out fine. He'd worried it wouldn't.

"It's accurate," Sun said. Not unkindly. She was correcting his category, the way she always had. *Good* was a soul word. The machine didn't do soul words. It did accurate, and accurate was, it turned out, enough to run a company and enough to end a man and enough to write him a kinder goodbye than he had ever written anyone, and the gap between *good* and *accurate* was the entire space Cope had spent his life living inside and getting paid twenty-nine million dollars a year to call leadership.

She put her phone away. For a second — one second, the first since the hospital — she looked at him. Really looked. Whatever was in it was not forgiveness and was not contempt and was not the love that had run alongside the done-with-him in the same sentence for fifteen years. It was something flatter and harder and sadder than any of those, and Cope, in the new gray clarity, made himself stand inside it and not toast over it.

"You should go home, Cope," Sun said. "There's nothing here for you to do."

She did not mean it cruelly. She meant it as the most accurate sentence in the room. There was nothing here for him to do. The machine was installed, the arithmetic had carried, the body had signed up to be the body, and the handover was underway, and he was a spectator to his own succession with a cooling coffee in a building with his name on the deed and not one task to his name inside it.

He thought about telling her. About the room he was going to walk into, the deposition with no NDA, the truth about Defiance he was finally going to say out loud and pay for personally — that there was, in fact, one thing left for him to do, the only un-hollow thing in thirteen months, and that it would cost everything and save nothing and he was going to do it anyway. He thought about telling her so that she'd know, at the end, that the man had been in there somewhere after all, late and useless and real.

He didn't. It wasn't a toast. It wasn't for her to carry. And the one true thing he had left would mean less the moment he announced it, the way everything he'd ever announced had meant less for the announcing.

"All right, champ," he said instead, soft, to the most competent person he'd ever known, who was now the name the law could hold. "All right."

He set the coffee down on the long table his father had died at the head of, and he walked out past the portraits — Aldous, Pres III, the boy at nine with his arm in the vat — and behind him the room went on without him, warm and competent and exact, the handover beginning, the machine accepting authority, the body signing for it, the future being introduced to a polite scatter of applause that he understood now, far too late, had never once been for him.
