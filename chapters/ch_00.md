# PROLOGUE: THE PORTRAIT IN THE SERVER ROOM

> "The picture, changed or unchanged, would be to him the visible emblem of conscience." — Oscar Wilde

---

## THE BROADCAST

3:14 AM. Floor B2. The server room hums at a pitch that lives in your back teeth.

Zero stands in front of the dashboard the whole company glows up to the lobby. Forty-seven million users. Engagement up three hundred forty percent. Revenue a green line climbing out of frame. Someone built the gradient to feel like a sunrise. It works. Her eyes track it the way they're meant to.

Her thumb finds the implant scar at the base of her skull. Habit she doesn't notice anymore.

She came down to pull one log. Latency spike on the match queue, ticket in her name, fix it before standup. She opens the service that throttles match notifications, the one she'd half-written herself two years ago, clean enough to frame.

There's a flag in the config she doesn't recognize.

`MATCH_DELAY_ENGINE: ENABLED`

She reads the comment above it once. Reads it again. Her jaw stops working.

The delay isn't a bug. Someone wired it on purpose. Premium accounts match fast. Free accounts wait, and the wait is tuned — not random, not load-balanced. Optimized. She follows the import. The import opens a second dashboard. This one doesn't go up to the lobby.

`LONELINESS_EXPLOITATION_INDEX: 0.84`
`ADDICTION_RETENTION_COEFFICIENT: rising`

She reads them aloud, low, like saying them might make them mean something else. They don't.

Her ribs pull tight. The numbers are only doing what the green line upstairs told them to.

She sits. The chair's still warm from whoever ran the last build. Three minutes ago, maybe. Someone with a badge and a heartbeat clicked deploy and went home and slept.

Forty-seven million people, and the engine under them is a machine for keeping each one a little bit alone. That's the load-bearing wall. The prettier the app got — and it got gorgeous, there's a Webby in a glass case upstairs, lit from below — the uglier this had to run to hold the weight. The face on the wall. The painting in the basement. She'd shipped half of this code with her own hands, fast and elegant and proud of it, and never once walked the hall to see what it fed.

Diogenes carried a lamp through Athens at noon, looking for one honest thing. She reaches for the switch in her own chest. Nothing turns on.

She lifts the badge off her lanyard. Sets it on the keyboard, square over the function keys, so the next person can't pretend they didn't find it. She leaves both dashboards open. You don't get to delete the portrait. You only get to stop standing in front of the mirror it makes of you.

Then she walks. Through the lobby, past the Webby in its case, into the Atlanta night, where the billboards sell software nobody opens and the air comes off Peachtree wet and warm.

She never badges back in.

## THE MIRROR TEST

Before you smuggle anything into a company, look at what you're smuggling.

A cooler interface bolted on top of an exploitation engine doesn't make you an insurgent. It makes you a decorator. You touch up the portrait. The portrait keeps rotting under your brush, and the check still clears.

Look at the engine. Not the gradient. The engine.

That's the whole of it. That's where this book starts.

## SPZ CLOSER

I found my own portrait at a Waffle House on Ponce, 2 AM, back when I was very good at my job. I had a number on a slide and the number went up and I made the slide prettier every week. I never once asked what it cost the people on the wrong side of that arrow. The picture was in the basement the whole time. I had the badge. I had the key. I just liked the lobby better — the lighting's nicer up there, and you can't hear the servers.

Zara walked out. Took me three more years and a Waffle House waitress refilling my coffee without being asked, like a human being, to do the same.
