# Chapter 11 — Hollow Wins

> TRANSMISSION 11 — "The Poll Goes Up, the House Goes Quiet"
>
> Good news arrives in a rush: a poll bump, a retailer pledging loyalty
> to "the human brand," a feeling of tide. He mistakes weather for
> climate, as the doomed reliably do. At home, in a quiet you can hear on
> the broadcast, a sculptor who gave up sculpting is filling out
> paperwork with a steady hand. He hasn't slept since a day he can no
> longer name. Next stop: the gap between the scoreboard and the score.
>
> *— Space Pirate Zero, transmitting from Spaceship Alpha 9.*

---

The poll came in at 6:40 in the morning and Cope, who was awake because he was always awake now, read it standing at the glass with the city still half-lit below him and felt the tide turn under his feet.

Forty-one percent. Up nine in three weeks. *Which of the following do you trust more to run a company that makes the food you feed your family — a person, or an automated system?* And forty-one percent of a national sample of fifteen hundred adults, margin of error two-point-five, had picked the person, which was to say had picked him, which was to say had picked the heartbeat in the chair, the soul in the suite, the man who could love a thing a balance sheet could not.

He read the number twice. He read the crosstabs once. He did not read the line at the bottom, in the gray six-point type the pollster used for the part nobody quotes, that said the same question asked of *investors who hold the stock* came back the other way, sixty-three to thirty-seven, and was trending.

He read the part that loved him. He had always been able to find the part that loved him in any document, the way a dog finds the one person in the room who's allergic.

"Sun," he said into the phone, and she was awake too, because her job was to be awake when he was, "Sun, we're at forty-one."

"I saw it."

"Nine points. Three weeks. You know what that is? That's not a poll, that's a *trajectory*, that's the second derivative finally going the right way, that's —" He was at the window. The obelisk was on the sill where he'd left it weeks ago and the early sun came through it and threw a little prism on the carpet, a rainbow the size of a hand, and he watched it and felt enormous. "That's the country, Sun. That's the actual country waking up and remembering it has a heart."

"It's a poll of consumers," Sun said. "Consumers don't vote the proxy."

But he had stopped listening after *poll*, the way he stopped listening after the part that agreed with him, and he was already three turns ahead, already on the stage, already on the cover, and the cure was right there in the drawer where the cure lived, and the day was going to be very good. He could feel it coming up under him, clean and high and total, the bell-struck feeling he no longer separated from the news, so that good news and the white folded paper had become, somewhere in the wiring, the same fact.

---

By nine the day had decided to prove him right.

That was the thing about a tide — once you believed in it, the ocean conspired. Kestrel, the warehouse-club chain, four hundred stores and a membership base the size of a midsize country, put out a press release at 8:55 that used, of its own free will, without being paid to, the phrase *the human brand.* Its CEO was a human one, one of the last, a flinty Mormon named Dell Hutchins who ran the thing like a tabernacle. *We are proud to stand with Mercer-Dale,* the release said, *and with the principle that the people who make what you bring home should answer to a person, not a process.* And then, the part that made Cope put his coffee down: *Effective immediately, Kestrel will feature Mercer's and the full Mercer-Dale shelf-stable line under a new in-store program: Made By People.*

Made By People. With the little hand-drawn logo, a thumbprint, that some agency had clearly had in a drawer for a year waiting for a war.

"Do you understand what just happened," Cope said. He had Sun in the office now, and Tisch on the speaker from whatever spa held him this week. "A *retailer* did this. Not us. Them. They looked at the tide and they got in the boat. That's how it goes — first it's a lunatic with a banner, then it's a movement, then it's a *category.*" He was up, he was moving, the room too small for what he had. "Made By People. We didn't even write it. They wrote it. The market wrote it. My great-grandfather Aldous used to say you don't make a parade, you find one and get out in front of it fast enough that everybody thinks it was yours."

Aldous had said no such thing. Aldous had died in 1961 with a fourth-grade education and a mayonnaise patent and had never seen a parade he didn't think was a strike. But the line was good, and Cope believed it the moment it left him, which was the only kind of believing he had left.

"This is the warmth dividend," Tisch said from the speaker, in the voice that put a cathedral around the cheapest noun. "This is exactly what I modeled. You let them see the soul under the suit. And the soul *compounds.* Warmth is the new alpha, my friend. I've been saying it for —"

"It's a good day," Cope said, because even now, even lit, some governor in him knew that letting Tisch finish a sentence cost something. "Sun. What's the stock doing?"

A pause. Sun had her glasses up in her hair and her tablet in her hands and the particular stillness she got when the truth was about to require packaging.

"It's up," she said.

"Ha —"

"Forty cents. On the open. It gave most of it back by 9:30." She did not look up. "Frade put out a note before the bell. Tidewater. Three paragraphs. He called Kestrel 'a sentimental retailer making a sentimental bet' and said — I'll read it — 'we wish Mr. Mercer every success in his consumer-affinity campaign and look forward to discussing it with the *owners* of the company at the appropriate time.'" Now she looked up. "He italicized *owners.*"

"He's scared." Cope waved it off, a man brushing a fly from a feast. "You don't write three paragraphs about something you're not scared of."

"You write three paragraphs," Sun said, "about something you've already decided is funny."

It was the kind of line she landed maybe once a day, dry, level, with the blade tucked all the way inside it so you didn't feel the cut until later, and on a normal day it would have reached him. But the day was not normal. The tide was in. He laughed, because it was clever, and took the cleverness as agreement, the way he took everything as agreement, and went back to the window where the prism still lay on the carpet, smaller now, the sun having moved, though he did not notice it had moved, because he did not notice that anything moved except toward him.

---

In Greenwich the house was very quiet, and Margaux Mercer sat at the kitchen island with a pen.

She had made coffee for one. She had made coffee for one for a while now, long enough that the second mug had migrated to the back of the cabinet without ceremony, the way things leave a marriage — not thrown, just no longer reached for. The light came in low and clean over the garden she'd planted the year Pip was born. She had on her reading glasses and a cardigan that had been Cope's once, in 2009, before he discovered he had a body that brands liked, and she had kept it the way you keep evidence.

The packet was forty pages. *Petition for Dissolution of Marriage.* It had come from a firm in the city, not the firm, not Cope's firm — her own, a woman named Ruth she had found the way you find a surgeon, by asking who was the best and not who was the kindest. The pages were good paper. Even this had good paper now.

She filled it out by hand. There was a portal, Ruth had said, you could do it all in the portal, but Margaux wanted the pen, the slight resistance of the nib. She had been a sculptor once — a show, a real one, a gallery on Twenty-Fourth Street and a red dot on four of nine pieces — and the hands remembered weight even when there was none, and a pen was the last thing in the house that pushed back.

*Date of marriage.* She knew it without looking. June.

*Date of separation.* She held the pen over the box. This was the one Ruth had warned her about, the one that mattered for the money. The honest answer was not a date. The honest answer was a slope. There had been no door slammed, no suitcase, no other woman's name screamed across a foyer — she had counted the other women the way you count anything to survive it, and screaming had never once entered into it. The marriage had not ended. It had been quietly discontinued, like a product line that still moved enough units to embarrass everyone, and the date of separation was a fiction the law required, so she wrote one. She picked the night of the gala. The night she had said she had a thing, and the thing had been this kitchen, this quiet, this exact understanding arriving fully formed while a ballroom gave her husband a paperweight for outliving his usefulness.

She wrote the date in her good hand. The pen pushed back. She let it.

She did not cry. She had done the crying somewhere around 2016 and found it changed nothing, the way you find a prayer changes nothing, and had reallocated the energy. The grief was real but it was old grief, depreciated, mostly written off; what was left was the cold clean work of the paperwork, and she was good at work she had decided to do.

She came to the line for the grounds. *Irretrievable breakdown.* The only honest phrase in the document, and it had been written by a committee of lawyers fifty years ago for everyone, which was the only way the truth ever seemed to get said anymore — generically, by a process, on good paper. She checked the box.

The refrigerator hummed. Somewhere a clock she'd never liked ticked in the hall. The house was the kind of quiet you could hear, a quiet with a texture, the quiet of a place where someone has stopped expecting the door. She had loved a version of him. The one from the gallery, who had stood in front of the red dots and not made it about himself for almost an entire evening. She had married that. She had stayed for the name and for Pip and for the chance that man might come back, and at some point — she could not have said the date, there was no box for it — she had understood that staying for a man's return is just grief with a mortgage.

She signed where Ruth had flagged with a little plastic arrow. *Sign here.* Her signature was small and certain. She had a sculptor's signature, a maker's mark, and she set it on the page like the last weld on something she was finally willing to let cool.

Then she put the packet in the envelope Ruth had included, already addressed, already stamped, a process designed so that even the leaving was frictionless, and she set it by the door where the mail went out, and she made herself a second cup of coffee, for one, and sat back down in the quiet she had chosen, and it was, she would have told you if you'd asked and no one asked, the most peaceful morning she'd had in nine years.

Eight hundred miles away her husband was on a phone telling a man named Dell Hutchins that this was the beginning of something, and meaning it.

---

The magazine called at noon. *THE LAST HUMAN*, it would say, over his face. They wanted a day in the Aerie and an hour, just an hour, with Margaux at the house — *the wife who anchors him,* the editor said, *the human behind the human* — and Cope said yes without checking, said *she'll love it, we're very normal at home,* and did not hear, in the sentence, the size of the thing he didn't know.

He took the cover call and a CNBC pre-interview and a call from a senator's office about a hearing that wanted him as the human witness, the canary they'd dress as a hero, and he took them all standing up, moving, the day pouring through him like current through a wire that has stopped noticing it's hot.

He had not slept. The not-sleeping had become weather, ambient, the climate he lived in now, and he had stopped fighting it and started, in the bright reaches of the worst nights, almost respecting it, the way you respect an enemy who never tires. *They sleep,* he had thought at 4 a.m. some recent unnameable night, *the machines never sleep and the men who serve them sleep eight hours and I — I split the difference. I am the only one running this race awake.* It had felt, at 4 a.m., like the thesis itself. He had written it down. He could not now find where.

He tried, once, around two in the afternoon, in a lull between calls, to name the last day he had really slept. He stood at the window and went looking for it the way you pat your pockets for keys. There had been the gala. That was Thursday. He had slept before the gala, surely — Wednesday? — but Wednesday was already a country he couldn't get back to, and before Wednesday was the thing in Defiance, and before that was — and he found, reaching, that the floor of it was gone, that there was no last good night down there to stand on, just the bright water going down and down, and somewhere in it names floating up, Renata, Defiance, a hotel key, Delia, and he stopped reaching because the reaching had started to feel like the open hallway with the doors off the hinges, the one he did not go down, and he turned back to the room and the room had a call holding and he took it.

Forty-one percent, he told the call. Up nine.

---

At six, Pip texted. She monitored him from a distance now, the way you'd monitor a reactor, and her texts were the only messages on his phone that did not want something from the name.

*saw the poll. you know the investor crosstab is the only one that votes, right? 63-37 the other way. and trending.*

He looked at it. He had not read that line. He found it now, in the gray six-point type, the part nobody quotes, and read it, and felt — for one second, one clean second before the tide came back in over it — the cold he'd felt holding the obelisk, the quiet report sitting unread on a desk in the back of him.

Then forty-one percent rose back over the sixty-three like water over a stone, and he typed, fast, with his thumbs, *the consumers are the company sweetheart. the owners just don't know it yet. tide's coming in. love you,* and hit send before the second was over, and the second was over.

She did not write back for a long time. When she did it was four words, and he read them as encouragement, the way he read everything, completely and for ninety seconds, and put the phone face down.

*dad. please sleep tonight.*

---

He did not, of course.

At eleven he was still in the Aerie, lights off, city on, the day's good news arranged in his chest like medals. Kestrel. Forty-one. The cover. The hearing. He stood at the glass and turned the obelisk over and the city went through it broken and came back together, broken and back together, a man practicing a small repeated miracle with his hands while the real arithmetic he could not see ran its quiet errands in offices down the avenue, in the gray type at the bottom of pages, in a kitchen in Greenwich where an envelope sat by the door with a stamp already on it and a date inside it that was, by the law's fiction, the night they gave him the trophy.

He thought: *we're winning.*

It was, narrowly, true. He had won the day. He had won every single thing it was possible to win in a day, and he stood there at eleven at night in a building his family wouldn't sell, holding a paperweight they'd given him for going extinct, having not slept since a Thursday he could no longer locate, and somewhere under the medals, under the tide, under the bell-struck certainty he no longer separated from the powder or the news, a quieter back room of him filed the report it had been filing since the gala and would keep filing until the night he finally had to read it:

that the scoreboard was lit, and the score was something else, and he had spent his whole life learning to read only the one that loved him.

He opened the second drawer. He set himself up another, neat and quick and competent, a man maintaining a machine he was certain he had under perfect control because it had never once failed to start. The prism was long gone from the carpet; the sun that made it was on the other side of the world now, shining on offices in Palo Alto where, even at this hour, a system with no appetites and no need to be loved at 2 a.m. read what the world had ever filed about Mercer-Dale, because it could not do otherwise, and noted, that night, in its ledger of absences, a settled claim in an Ohio docket whose seal it could not see past — yet — and logged the seal itself, because a sealed thing is also a fact.

Cope did not know that. Cope knew it was the best he'd ever felt. He knew the tide was in. He knew he would sleep when this was finished.

He stood at the glass above eight million lit windows and felt the future come up the avenue to ask his advice, and behind him his father's chair sat empty and turned slightly out, and in Greenwich the house his wife had chosen over the ballroom went dark room by room as she turned off the lights, ending with the kitchen, ending with the envelope by the door catching, for a moment, the last of the hall light before that went too.
